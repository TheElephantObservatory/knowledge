# Political Polarization as Broken Opponent Processing Between Real Moral Perspectives

The tension was supposed to hold us together.

---

> Left and right political orientations each detect real but partial features of moral reality. Their tension should function like opponent processing — driving better collective calibration — but when the dialectic breaks down into mutual negation, the system generates crude rebalancing forces like populist nationalism.

There is a deep and underappreciated connection between global political polarization and the cognitive architecture of relevance realization. Jonathan Haidt's moral foundations theory shows that different political orientations are not simply errors — they are perspectival emphases on genuinely different features of moral reality. The progressive orientation prioritizes care and harm reduction, extending the moral circle outward. The conservative orientation prioritizes loyalty, authority, sanctity, and the maintenance of social order — which, from an autopoetic standpoint, reflects sensitivity to the conditions required for a self-organizing system to persist.

Neither perspective alone captures the full picture. What John Vervaeke and others describe as 'opponent processing' — dynamic tension between competing frameworks driving the system toward better calibration — may be exactly what a healthy political ecology looks like. The left perceives real suffering at the margins that the right's inward focus misses. The right perceives real fragility in the organizational substrate that the left's outward focus ignores. The tragedy is not that these perspectives exist in tension; it is that the tension has become so extreme that opponent processing breaks down into mutual negation rather than productive dialectic.

The recent global political contraction — the rise of nationalist and populist movements across very different cultural contexts — can be read not as mere regression but as a systemic correction signal. When the center is neglected long enough, when people cannot afford heating and food while political discourse focuses exclusively on distant causes, the system generates a rebalancing force. Understanding this as a structural response of a self-organizing system — rather than as irrationality or moral failure — opens the possibility of a more sophisticated response than simply condemning it.

## Apprentice

Progressive and conservative political orientations are not simply right or wrong — each one picks up on something genuinely important that the other tends to miss. The left notices real suffering at the margins of society and pushes to include more people in the circle of moral concern. The right notices that the social structures holding everything together are fragile and need active maintenance. Both of these observations are true at the same time.

In a healthy political system, this tension between perspectives would work like a productive argument — each side correcting the other's blind spots, pushing the whole system toward better answers. But when the tension becomes too extreme, the two sides stop learning from each other entirely and just try to cancel each other out.

This framework offers a different way to understand the recent global rise of nationalist and populist movements. When large numbers of people struggle with basic needs like food and heating while political conversation focuses on more distant concerns, the system pushes back. Rather than dismissing this as ignorance or moral failure, it can be understood as a correction signal — a sign that something real was being neglected. Recognizing this opens the door to more thoughtful responses than simply condemning the reaction.

## Adept

Jonathan Haidt's moral foundations theory reveals that progressive and conservative orientations represent perspectival emphases on genuinely different features of moral reality rather than simple errors of reasoning. The progressive emphasis on care and harm reduction extends the moral circle outward, detecting real suffering at the margins. The conservative emphasis on loyalty, authority, and sanctity reflects — from an autopoietic standpoint — a sensitivity to the conditions required for self-organizing social systems to persist. Neither perspective alone achieves adequate coverage of the moral landscape.

What John Vervaeke describes as opponent processing offers a powerful lens here: dynamic tension between competing cognitive frameworks drives a system toward better calibration. A healthy political ecology would function precisely this way — left and right as opponent processes, each correcting the other's attentional blind spots. The pathology of contemporary polarization is not that these perspectives exist in tension but that the tension has escalated past the threshold where productive dialectic collapses into mutual negation. The opponent processing mechanism itself has broken down.

The recent global rise of nationalist and populist movements across culturally diverse contexts can then be read as a systemic correction signal rather than mere regression. When the autopoietic center — the basic material and social conditions that sustain the system — is neglected long enough, the system generates a rebalancing force. People who cannot afford heating while discourse focuses on distant abstractions are registering a real signal about organizational fragility. Understanding this structurally, as the behavior of a self-organizing system under stress, opens more sophisticated response possibilities than moral condemnation alone permits.

## Magus

Haidt's moral foundations theory, reframed through the lens of relevance realization, reveals political orientations as competing attentional policies over a shared but irreducibly complex moral landscape. The progressive configuration maximizes sensitivity to suffering at expanding perimeters of moral concern; the conservative configuration maximizes sensitivity to the autopoietic boundary conditions — loyalty, authority, sanctity — upon which the persistence of self-organizing social systems depends. These are not ideological preferences but genuinely different relevance-realization profiles, each achieving salience on features the other systematically backgrounds.

Vervaeke's opponent processing framework provides the critical structural insight: cognitive systems achieve optimal calibration not through the triumph of one processing mode but through the dynamic tension between competing modes. Applied to political ecology, this suggests that left-right tension is not a defect to be resolved but the very mechanism by which collective intelligence navigates the trade-off space between expansion and consolidation, between extending moral concern and maintaining organizational coherence. The contemporary crisis is precisely a breakdown of this opponent processing — a phase transition from productive dialectic into reciprocal inhibition, where each pole's signal becomes the other's noise.

The global political contraction — populist and nationalist movements emerging across radically different cultural substrates — acquires new intelligibility as an emergent correction within a self-organizing system whose autopoietic core has been systematically under-attended. When material preconditions for system persistence are neglected while attentional resources flow toward peripheral extension, the system generates endogenous rebalancing pressures. This is not irrationality but relevance realization operating at the collective scale, reasserting salience on neglected structural dependencies. The sophisticated response is neither to celebrate this correction nor to condemn it, but to recognize the broken opponent processing that necessitated it and to work toward restoring the dialectical tension that permits genuine collective calibration.
