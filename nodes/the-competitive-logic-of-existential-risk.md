# The Competitive Logic of Existential Risk

A race where the winner loses everything

---

> Every competitor in the modern world feels forced to take dangerous risks because falling behind seems worse than the danger itself — and the weapons available to those caught in this trap are growing exponentially more destructive.

At the core of Game A is a logic that has now become existentially dangerous: the logic of rival risk. Every actor in the system — whether a nation-state, a corporation, or a research lab — is ultimately competing for the capacity to wield power in some domain. This creates a game-theoretical trap where even actors who are fully aware that their choices are reckless feel compelled to continue, because the cost of falling behind is perceived as worse than the risk of proceeding. The AI arms race is a perfect illustration: researchers at the frontier openly acknowledge the dangers of heedless acceleration, yet feel they cannot slow down because a competitor might not. What makes this moment historically distinct is not just the trap itself — that trap has always existed — but that the footprint of destruction available to actors caught in that trap is now expanding exponentially. Nuclear weapons were once the exclusive province of superpowers. CRISPR-enabled biological warfare is now approaching the threshold of large criminal enterprises. Cyber attacks on smart grids are available to an ever-widening range of vectors. And crucially, as civilization becomes more technologically integrated, its fragility increases in parallel — the same infrastructure that amplifies capability also amplifies the cascade consequences of any perturbation. So the trap is not static. The stakes of the trap grow with every iteration of the technological curve, and Game A contains no mechanism — no institutional design, no cultural norm, no feedback loop — that is adequate to interrupt this dynamic. The system is, by its own internal logic, careening toward a threshold it was never built to navigate.

## Apprentice

Imagine a race where every runner knows the track ends at a cliff, but no one can stop because stopping means losing to someone who keeps going. This is the basic trap built into how modern civilization organizes competition — between countries, companies, and research labs. Everyone can see the danger, but the fear of being left behind feels more immediate than the fear of the cliff.

What makes this moment different from any previous era is not the trap itself — that has always existed — but the size of the weapons available to those caught in it. Technologies that once required the resources of entire governments are now accessible to much smaller groups. Biological tools, cyberattacks, and other capabilities are spreading to more and more actors. At the same time, our civilization is becoming more interconnected, which means any serious disruption can cascade further and faster than ever before.

The troubling conclusion is that the current system contains no built-in mechanism to interrupt this dynamic. No institution, no shared agreement, no cultural habit is adequate to the scale of the problem. The logic of competition keeps accelerating the race even as the stakes grow with every new technological development.

## Adept

The concept of 'rival risk' identifies a structural feature of competitive systems: actors who perceive their security or relevance as contingent on relative standing are trapped in a defection-dominant equilibrium. Even when the aggregate outcome of universal defection is catastrophic, no individual actor can unilaterally cooperate without accepting asymmetric disadvantage. The AI frontier makes this dynamic unusually visible — leading researchers publicly acknowledge the risks of unchecked acceleration while simultaneously feeling unable to decelerate, because the competitive landscape punishes restraint.

What distinguishes the present historical moment is not the game-theoretic structure, which is ancient, but the exponential expansion of destructive footprint available to actors caught within it. Nuclear deterrence once required state-level industrial capacity. CRISPR-based bioweapons are approaching accessibility by sub-state actors. Offensive cyber capabilities targeting critical infrastructure are proliferating across an ever-wider threat surface. Simultaneously, deep technological integration increases systemic fragility: the same interdependencies that amplify productive capacity also amplify the cascade potential of any perturbation.

The argument here is that this is not a static trap with stable stakes — it is a dynamic one whose danger compounds with each iteration of the technological curve. Existing institutional architectures, whether international treaties, regulatory frameworks, or professional norms, were designed for a world where destructive capacity scaled with state power. They contain no adequate feedback mechanism for a world where that capacity is rapidly democratizing. The system is, by its internal logic, accelerating toward thresholds it was never architected to survive.

## Magus

The logic of rival risk constitutes a generalization of the Hobbesian security dilemma into a multi-domain, multi-actor competitive landscape in which the expected utility of restraint approaches zero as the number of defecting agents increases. The Nash equilibrium of such a system is universal defection — not because actors are irrational or malicious, but because the informational and strategic conditions make cooperative deviation individually irrational even when collectively catastrophic. The AI arms race instantiates this with unusual clarity: the epistemic acknowledgment of existential risk coexists with behavioral acceleration, a conjunction that falsifies naive rational-actor models and confirms the structural compulsion embedded in rival-risk dynamics.

The historically novel variable is not the game-theoretic topology but the scaling relationship between technological iteration and destructive footprint. Classical deterrence theory assumed a near-monopoly on existential-scale capability by a small number of state actors, permitting coordination through bilateral or multilateral treaty regimes. That assumption is being systematically invalidated. The democratization of dual-use biotechnology, the proliferation of offensive cyber capability, and the expanding attack surface of hyperconnected critical infrastructure collectively describe a trajectory in which the minimum organizational threshold for catastrophic-scale action declines monotonically. Crucially, systemic fragility scales superlinearly with integration density — the same network effects that generate civilizational productivity simultaneously amplify cascade vulnerability.

The deeper structural claim is that 'Game A' — the ensemble of competitive institutions, incentive architectures, and cultural grammars that constitute modernity — contains no endogenous corrective mechanism adequate to this dynamic. Existing governance instruments are calibrated to a world of slower diffusion curves and higher capability thresholds. The absence of an adequate feedback loop is not an implementation failure but a design feature: the system was never architected to navigate the convergence of exponential capability growth with rival-risk compulsion at civilizational scale.
