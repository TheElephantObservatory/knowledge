# How Medieval Theology Secretly Shaped Modern Materialist Thought

God left the building, but his logic stayed.

---

> Medieval nominalism — the idea that universals like 'twoness' are just names, not real entities — was originally designed to protect God's absolute power. Most modern materialists are unwitting heirs of this theological doctrine, having kept its structure while forgetting its motive.

The rise of nominalism in the medieval period represents a totally new epoch of human thought. Before nominalism, universals — things like the color yellow, or the mathematical concept of twoness — were understood to have an independent existence, a kind of heavenly quality. There was an intelligence out there in the cosmos almost aiding human thinking. The nominalists humanized thought by saying these universals are just concepts in your head, derived from your experience of particulars. They don't exist in a different order of being; they're just names we give to classify many particulars. Interestingly, this shift had theological motivations: the nominalists wanted to preserve the absolute power of God. If one plus one equals two in some eternal, independent sense, then God is subject to that mathematical truth and cannot override it. But if universals are merely human constructs, then God's power remains unconstrained — God decides everything, even logical relationships. What's remarkable is that most contemporary materialist and physicalist thinkers are nominalists without realizing they've inherited a theological doctrine originally designed to protect divine omnipotence. This is a stunning example of how a metaphysical position can migrate across centuries, shedding its original motivation while retaining its structural consequences.

## Apprentice

Before the medieval period, most thinkers believed that abstract concepts — like the color yellow, or the idea of "twoness" — had a real, independent existence somewhere beyond the physical world. There was something out there in the cosmos that made two things genuinely share a quality. Then a group of medieval thinkers called nominalists argued that these abstractions are really just names we invent to group similar things together. They exist in our heads, not in some higher realm.

What makes this story fascinating is why the nominalists made this move. They weren't trying to be skeptical or scientific — they were trying to protect God's unlimited power. If mathematical truths exist independently and eternally, then even God would be bound by them. But if "one plus one equals two" is just a human label, then God remains truly all-powerful, free from any constraint.

Here's the twist: today's materialists and physicalists — people who believe only physical matter is real — are almost all nominalists. They reject the independent existence of universals. But they rarely realize they've inherited a position that was originally a piece of theology. The original motivation fell away, but the intellectual structure survived. It's a striking example of how ideas can travel across centuries, losing their original meaning while keeping their shape.

## Adept

The emergence of nominalism in the late medieval period marks a genuine rupture in the history of Western thought. Prior to figures like William of Ockham, the dominant frameworks — whether Platonic realism or moderate Aristotelian realism — held that universals possess some form of mind-independent existence. The color yellow, the property of twoness, mathematical relations: these were understood as participating in an intelligible order that transcended individual minds. Nominalism dismantled this architecture by relocating universals entirely within human cognition. They became names — convenient classifications derived from encounters with particulars, possessing no ontological standing of their own.

The theological motivation behind this shift is crucial and often overlooked. The nominalists were driven by a commitment to divine omnipotence. If logical and mathematical truths exist independently and necessarily, they constitute constraints on God — truths that even God cannot violate. By denying universals any independent reality, nominalism preserved God's radical freedom: the divine will is answerable to nothing, not even the principle of non-contradiction. This was a deliberate metaphysical strategy in service of a specific theology of absolute sovereignty.

The irony is that contemporary physicalism and materialism are overwhelmingly nominalist in their metaphysical commitments, yet almost entirely unaware of the theological genealogy of that position. The structural consequences of nominalism — the denial of abstract objects, the reduction of universals to linguistic conventions — persist intact, while the original motivation has been completely forgotten. This represents a remarkable case of philosophical migration: a metaphysical framework designed to safeguard divine power now underwrites a worldview that has no place for the divine at all.

## Magus

The nominalist revolution of the fourteenth century constitutes what can only be described as an epochal discontinuity in the metaphysics of intelligibility. The prior consensus — spanning Platonic participation, Augustinian illumination, and Aristotelian moderate realism — maintained that universals possess ontological traction independent of any particular mind. Whether as subsistent forms, divine ideas, or real essences instantiated in particulars, universals anchored a cosmos in which thought was understood as receptive to an intelligible order not of its own making. Nominalism's intervention was radical: universals are post rem, mere nomina — classificatory artifacts generated by the mind's encounter with discrete particulars, possessing no extra-mental reality whatsoever.

The genealogy of this move is theologically precise. The nominalist program, particularly in its Ockhamist formulation, was motivated by a voluntarist commitment to the potentia Dei absoluta. If universals and necessary truths — logical, mathematical, moral — enjoy independent existence, they function as constraints external to the divine will, rendering God subject to an order God did not author. Nominalism dissolves this problem at its root: if there are no mind-independent universals, there are no necessary structures to which omnipotence must defer. God's sovereignty becomes genuinely unconditioned.

The philosophical irony is profound. Contemporary physicalist and eliminativist programs inherit nominalism's full structural apparatus — the rejection of abstract objects, the deflationary treatment of universals, the empiricist reduction of concepts to generalizations from particulars — while remaining almost entirely ignorant of its voluntarist-theological provenance. A metaphysical architecture engineered to secure divine omnipotence now serves as the unexamined foundation of a worldview that considers the divine question settled in the negative. The doctrine migrated; the motivation evaporated; the consequences remain fully operative.
