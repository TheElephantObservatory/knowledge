# How People and Institutions Shape Each Other Through Reciprocal Emergence

The spiral turns both ways

---

> People and institutions shape each other in a spiral that can run upward or downward. Since the 1960s it has been running down. The only viable response is to build slightly better institutions that help people grow, so that improved people can build yet better institutions — gradually turning the spiral upward.

The personal-institutional spiral is one of the core structural insights of Game B, and Rutt notes that it is currently running in reverse. Institutions are built of people, and the kinds of institutions you can build depend on the kinds of people you have. But reciprocally, the kinds of people you become depend on the kinds of institutions you inhabit. These two things form each other in what Rutt calls reciprocal emergence — a dynamic that operates in both directions and can spiral either upward or downward. Since roughly 1965, the spiral has been running down: people are getting worse, institutions are getting worse, and the institutions are making the people worse still. The Game B response is not to try to fix the institutions first and then fix the people, or vice versa, but to do both simultaneously and gradually. You cannot force people arriving from Game A into accords that are too radically different from what they know — they simply will not voluntarily enter them. So the new institutions must be ones that people from the current culture can see, aspire to, and recognize as better than what they have. And critically, those institutions must be designed from the outset with the explicit purpose of helping people become better — more courageous, more honest, more capable of genuine collective sensemaking — so that as people improve, they become capable of building yet better institutions, and the spiral turns upward. This is why Game B cannot be imposed from above and cannot be rushed. It is also why the vision is not a sudden new man — the socialist man, the blood-and-soil man — but a gradual, anthropologically grounded evolution of who we are, within the range of what humans have actually demonstrated they can be across the full diversity of cultures.

## Apprentice

There is a feedback loop between the kind of people a society produces and the kind of institutions those people build. Good institutions help people develop virtues like honesty and courage, and those better people go on to create even better institutions. But the loop works in reverse too: failing institutions produce cynical, fragmented people, who then build even worse institutions. Jim Rutt argues this downward spiral has been accelerating since roughly 1965.

The temptation is to pick a starting point — fix the institutions first, or fix the people first — but neither works in isolation. You cannot drop people shaped by today's culture into radically different arrangements and expect them to thrive. They will simply walk away. Instead, the proposal is to design new institutions that are only a step or two ahead of what people currently know, but that are visibly, recognizably better.

Critically, these institutions must be built with the explicit goal of helping their members grow — becoming more honest, braver, better at thinking together. As people improve inside these structures, they become capable of creating the next generation of institutions, and the spiral begins to turn upward. This is necessarily gradual. It rejects the fantasy of a sudden new kind of human and instead works within the real, demonstrated range of what people across diverse cultures have actually shown they can become.

## Adept

Jim Rutt identifies the personal-institutional spiral as a core structural insight within the Game B framework. Institutions are constituted by persons, and the design space of viable institutions is constrained by the developmental capacities of the people available to inhabit them. Reciprocally, the developmental trajectory of persons is shaped by the institutional environments they occupy. Rutt calls this reciprocal emergence — a bidirectional dynamic that can compound in either direction. His diagnosis is that since approximately 1965, the spiral has been running downward: degraded institutions produce less capable, less virtuous people, who in turn build further-degraded institutions.

The strategic implication is that neither an institution-first nor a person-first approach can succeed in isolation. Game B rejects both technocratic institutional reform that ignores human development and utopian visions of a transformed humanity that ignore structural constraints. Instead, the approach is to construct new institutional forms — accords, in Game B terminology — that are close enough to existing cultural norms that people shaped by Game A can voluntarily enter them, yet designed from the outset to cultivate greater honesty, courage, and collective sensemaking capacity in their participants.

This creates the conditions for an upward spiral: as people develop within these transitional institutions, they become capable of designing and sustaining more demanding ones, which in turn accelerate further development. The gradualism here is not a concession but a design principle rooted in anthropological realism. The goal is not the sudden emergence of a new type of human — a move that historically collapses into ideology — but an incremental evolution within the empirically demonstrated range of human cultural possibility. This is why Game B cannot be imposed, cannot be rushed, and must be entered voluntarily at every stage.

## Magus

Rutt's articulation of the personal-institutional spiral constitutes a claim about the generative grammar of social order: institutions and persons stand in a relation of reciprocal emergence, each constraining and enabling the other's possibility space. The spiral is not merely correlational but constitutive — the ontological status of both terms is co-dependent, such that neither can be treated as an independent variable in any viable intervention strategy. Rutt's periodization locates the inflection point around 1965, after which the spiral's sign reversed: institutional decay and anthropological regression entered a mutually reinforcing downward cascade.

The Game B response refuses the standard reformist dichotomy between structural and cultural intervention. Neither institutional redesign imposed on unreconstructed agents nor moral exhortation absent structural scaffolding can gain traction against a self-reinforcing degenerative dynamic. The operative constraint is voluntarism under conditions of path dependency: new institutional forms — accords — must be legible and attractive to persons formed within Game A, while simultaneously functioning as developmental environments that expand participants' capacity for honesty, courage, and genuine collective sensemaking. The gradient must be navigable; the gap between current and aspirational forms cannot exceed the adaptive range of incoming participants.

This yields a theory of change that is explicitly anti-eschatological. It repudiates the revolutionary archetype — the socialist new man, the völkisch subject — as anthropologically naive and historically catastrophic. Instead, it operates within the empirically attested envelope of human cultural variation, treating the full ethnographic record as evidence of latent but real developmental possibilities. The upward spiral is bootstrapped incrementally: each generation of institutions produces persons capable of sustaining the next, slightly more demanding generation. The temporality is geological rather than political, and the mechanism is emergent rather than designed from a fixed endpoint.
