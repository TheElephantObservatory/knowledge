# Reconciling Ancient Biology with God-like Technology

Paleolithic hearts steering the lightning

---

> Humanity is running ancient emotional hardware, medieval institutional software, and god-like technological power simultaneously — while both civilizational progress and civilizational collapse accelerate at once. No framework yet exists to make sense of this at the speed it demands.

E.O. Wilson, the Harvard biologist, offered a pithy summation that captures our civilizational predicament perfectly: we have Paleolithic emotions — our neurophysiology and root psychology are roughly 50,000 years out of date — we have medieval institutions, and we have god-like technology. We are in a period of exponential change, but no one has articulated a framework for exponential meaning to help us make sense of all this and steer it. And that mismatch would probably have overtaxed both religion and modern liberalism even at their healthiest, fullest versions. But we're now encountering this challenge in a period of decline, decay, and collapse. What makes this especially disorienting is a frame that Daniel Schmachtenberger first shared: things are getting exponentially better — that's the Steven Pinker, Matt Ridley, Hans Rosling view, the under-reported uptick in the benefits of the Enlightenment experiment — and simultaneously, undeniably, things are also getting exponentially worse. Both of these things are happening at once. And typically, people cannot hold that tension. We glom onto whichever one resonates with our existing disposition. If you're prone to doom and gloom, you latch onto extinction narratives. If you're prone to optimism, you cling to the good news. But the fairly obvious answer is that both curves are real — and when you're trying to map a linear outcome to the intersection of two exponential curves running in opposite directions, it becomes genuinely crazy-making. That is the epistemic and existential situation we are actually in.

## Apprentice

E.O. Wilson, the Harvard biologist, captured something essential when he observed that human beings carry Stone Age emotions, medieval institutions, and god-like technology all at once. Our feelings and instincts were shaped tens of thousands of years ago. The organizations we use to govern ourselves — governments, religions, corporations — are centuries behind. But the tools we wield can reshape or destroy the planet. That gap is not a minor inconvenience. It is a civilizational fault line.

What makes this harder still is a paradox identified by thinker Daniel Schmachtenberger: things are genuinely, measurably getting better — poverty falling, disease retreating, violence declining — and simultaneously, genuinely, measurably getting worse — ecosystems collapsing, weapons proliferating, social trust eroding. Both curves are real and both are accelerating. Most people cannot hold that tension. They pick a side: either optimism or doom, Steven Pinker or the extinction crowd.

But the honest position is that two exponential curves are running in opposite directions at the same time, and no existing religion, political ideology, or philosophical tradition was built to navigate that. The disorientation people feel is not weakness or confusion — it is the accurate perception of a genuinely unprecedented situation.

## Adept

E.O. Wilson's tripartite diagnosis — Paleolithic emotions, medieval institutions, god-like technology — names the core mismatch driving civilizational instability. Human neurophysiology was largely fixed in the Pleistocene; the institutional architectures of the nation-state, the church, and liberal democracy were forged across the last millennium; but the technological leverage available to individuals and states now operates at a scale and speed that neither emotional nor institutional evolution has remotely tracked. The result is a compounding misalignment that would have strained even the most robust versions of modernity's organizing frameworks.

Daniel Schmachtenberger's framing sharpens the epistemic problem: the Enlightenment's empirical gains — the Pinker-Ridley-Rosling data on poverty, violence, and health — are real and defensible, and so are the counter-trajectories in ecological overshoot, weapons of mass destruction proliferation, and institutional legitimacy collapse. These are not competing narratives where one side is simply wrong. They are simultaneous exponential curves running in opposite directions. The cognitive demand of holding both as true, without collapsing into either techno-optimism or catastrophism, exceeds what most existing sense-making traditions are equipped to support.

The deeper problem is not merely psychological but structural: there is no widely shared framework for what might be called exponential meaning — a coherent account of value, purpose, and orientation adequate to a period of simultaneous accelerating gain and accelerating loss. The absence of that framework is itself a civilizational risk factor, because without it, collective navigation defaults to whichever partial narrative aligns with prior disposition.

## Magus

Wilson's diagnostic triad — Paleolithic affect, medieval institutional morphology, Promethean technics — encodes a multi-timescale mismatch that standard modernization theory consistently underweights. The neurophysiological substrate of human motivation was largely canalized in the Pleistocene; the organizational forms through which collective agency is exercised — Westphalian sovereignty, confessional institutions, Enlightenment liberalism — were crystallized across a span of centuries; while the technological systems now operative compress consequence-cycles to timescales that neither affective nor institutional adaptation can track. This is not merely a coordination problem amenable to incremental reform. It is a structural incommensurability between the temporal registers of human nature, social organization, and material power.

Schmachtenberger's contribution is to render the epistemic predicament with unusual precision: the empirical record simultaneously supports the Pinker-Ridley thesis of secular improvement across welfare indicators and the counter-thesis of accelerating systemic fragility across ecological, geopolitical, and epistemological dimensions. These are not symmetrical rhetorical positions — they are orthogonal empirical trajectories, each exponential, each real. The cognitive and motivational architecture most people bring to this situation is adapted for linear environments and single-valenced threat assessment; the demand to sustain genuine dual-register awareness — neither collapsing into meliorism nor into catastrophism — exceeds the load-bearing capacity of most inherited sense-making frameworks, including both religious theodicy and secular progressivism.

What is conspicuously absent is any developed framework for what might be termed exponential meaning: a meta-level orientation adequate to navigating the intersection of divergent accelerating curves without sacrificing epistemic integrity to motivational necessity. The pathological attractors — optimism bias and doom capture — are not merely individual psychological failures but symptoms of a civilizational lacuna in normative and interpretive infrastructure. That lacuna is itself among the most consequential under-theorized problems of the present moment.
