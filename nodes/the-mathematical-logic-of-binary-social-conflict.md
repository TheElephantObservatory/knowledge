# The Mathematical Logic of Binary Social Conflict

A dance on the knife's edge

---

> When people model each other's minds, they naturally cluster into groups sharing the same worldview. Simulations show the only stable outcome is a near-even split between competing groups — which is why major political contests almost always end up at 50/50.

When the observed is herself an observer, something qualitatively new emerges. If my world is constituted by agents like me, I can assume you share my generative model and explain your behavior by asking what intentions I would have needed to produce the same behavior in myself — a ready-made theory of mind. When many agents do this together, they can minimize joint free energy by converging on the same world model: the same ideology, the same theology, the same language. You can mathematically draw a Markov blanket around such a group. But if that blanket becomes impervious, you have created a closed system — an echo chamber — and everything converges to equilibrium. Because Markov blankets are porous, there is always tension between ingroup convergence and outgroup engagement. Simulating this produces a striking result: the only stable non-equilibrium solution is a roughly 50/50 split between groups. If one group is too small it gets absorbed; if one dominates entirely there is no epistemic allure from outside. This is why Brexit, Trump versus Biden, independence referenda — wherever you look — always land near 50/50. It is the only dynamically stable solution.

## Apprentice

When you interact with another person, you instinctively try to understand their intentions by asking yourself: what would I have to be thinking to behave that way? This works because you assume the other person's mind works roughly like yours. Over time, groups of people who do this together end up sharing the same beliefs, language, and way of seeing the world — they converge on a common reality.

But every group has a boundary. Inside that boundary, ideas reinforce each other and the group grows more unified. Outside it, other groups are doing the same thing. The question is: what happens when you simulate many such groups interacting over time? The answer is striking. If one group becomes too dominant, it loses the stimulation of genuine outside challenge and stagnates. If a group is too small, it gets absorbed. The only arrangement that keeps things alive and moving is a roughly equal split between competing groups.

This is why major political contests — Brexit, presidential elections, independence referenda — so reliably end near 50/50. It is not a coincidence or a quirk of polling. It is the only dynamically stable solution when groups of mind-readers compete for shared reality.

## Adept

Active inference offers a powerful lens for understanding social cognition. When an agent's environment is populated by other agents running similar generative models, a shortcut becomes available: to predict another's behavior, simply ask what prior intentions would have produced that same action in oneself. This projection-based theory of mind is computationally cheap and socially potent — it seeds the convergence of beliefs, norms, and ontologies within a group as agents collectively minimize joint free energy.

This convergence can be formalized using Markov blankets drawn around social collectives. Within the blanket, shared inference drives ideological and linguistic alignment; the group becomes an epistemic unit. Yet Markov blankets are never fully impermeable. A tension persists between ingroup coherence — which reduces prediction error internally — and outgroup engagement, which supplies novel signals that prevent the system from collapsing into equilibrium. A fully sealed group is an echo chamber: it reaches stasis.

When this dynamic is modeled computationally, one result stands out. The only non-equilibrium stable state is a near-equal division between competing groups. A minority group below a critical threshold gets absorbed; a group that achieves total dominance loses the epistemic friction needed to sustain active inference at the population level. This predicts — and explains — the persistent 50/50 pattern observed across Brexit, US presidential elections, and independence referenda. These are not statistical accidents; they are attractors of a system governed by collective belief-updating under social Markov blanket dynamics.

## Magus

The extension of active inference to multi-agent settings introduces a qualitative discontinuity: when the object of inference is itself an inferring subject, the generative model can exploit a powerful symmetry assumption. Agents sharing a common model class can perform vicarious inference — attributing intentional states to conspecifics by inverting their own action-generation process — yielding a low-cost, projection-based theory of mind. Iterated across a population, this mechanism drives collective variational free energy minimization, producing convergence on shared priors, shared likelihood mappings, and ultimately shared ontologies: ideology, theology, language.

The resulting epistemic collective admits a natural formalization as a higher-order Markov blanket, with internal states corresponding to the group's shared generative model and active states governing its interface with competing collectives. Crucially, the semi-permeability of this blanket is not a defect but a structural necessity. Full impermeability — the echo chamber limit — drives the system to thermodynamic equilibrium: maximal internal coherence, zero surprisal, epistemic death. Outgroup signals function as a source of non-equilibrium free energy, sustaining the inferential dynamics that constitute the group as a living epistemic entity.

Agent-based simulations of this architecture reveal a striking attractor structure. The only dynamically stable, non-equilibrium configuration is a near-parity partition of the belief landscape between competing collectives. Below a critical mass, a minority group cannot sustain sufficient model complexity to resist absorption; above a dominance threshold, the winning group loses the contrastive signal required to maintain active inference at the population scale. The empirical signature of this attractor — the near-universal 50/50 outcome in Brexit, US electoral contests, and independence referenda — is recast not as a sociological curiosity but as a mathematical inevitability: the fixed point of collective belief dynamics under Markov blanket constraints.
