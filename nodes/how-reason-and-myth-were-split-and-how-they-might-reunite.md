# How Reason and Myth Were Split — and How They Might Reunite

What if the story actually happened?

---

> Modernity split reason from imagination, fact from myth, leaving us unable to find meaning in what we know or truth in what moves us. C.S. Lewis's conversion reveals that this split was never necessary — and that healing it is central to resolving the contemporary meaning crisis.

C.S. Lewis, before his conversion, described his inner life with devastating precision: 'On one side, a many-islanded sea of poetry and myth. On the other, a glib and shallow rationalism. Nearly everything I loved I believed to be imaginary. Nearly everything I believed to be real I thought grim and meaningless.' That bifurcation — that horrible split — is not merely Lewis's personal pathology. It is the epistemological inheritance of modernity. We have created what might be called an epistemological apartheid: on one side, the hard currency of objective, reproducible, verifiable fact; on the other, little bantustans of art and imagination and myth, which are permitted to exist as long as they make no purchase on reality. The healing Lewis found came through a very specific intellectual crisis. He loved the myths of dying and rising gods — Balder, Adonis — but his rationalism told him they were only myths: meaningful stories, not actual events. Meanwhile, mere historical facts without mythic resonance seemed to him to say nothing. Then Tolkien put to him a question he could not escape: what if, on one occasion, the great story — the myth of the dying and rising god — without losing any of its mythic resonance, its deeper charge of what death and resurrection mean at every level, also became historical fact? What if the medium through which a creator chose to enact the myth was not language, as Homer used, but the substance of history itself? For Lewis, this was the moment when the two alienated hemispheres of his mind began to come together — when he could analyze it with reason as historical and verifiable, while it lost none of its mythic power. The lesson is not that everyone must follow Lewis's path. The lesson is that the split itself was false and unnecessary — that reason and imagination, the historical and the mythic, are not enemies but complementary organs of a single knowing, and that healing the meaning crisis requires healing that split.

## Apprentice

C.S. Lewis once described a painful divide in his own mind: everything he loved — poetry, myth, imagination — he believed to be fictional, and everything he accepted as real seemed grim and pointless. This wasn't just Lewis's personal problem. It reflects a broader modern habit of separating hard facts from the things that give life meaning, treating them as though they belong in entirely different categories.

The turning point for Lewis came when his friend J.R.R. Tolkien posed a radical question: what if a myth — the ancient story of a dying and rising god — actually happened in history, without losing any of its deeper meaning? What if one event could be both historically real and mythically powerful at the same time? This idea broke open the wall Lewis had built between reason and imagination.

The broader insight here isn't about following Lewis's specific religious path. It's that the division between fact and meaning, between the rational and the imaginative, is itself the problem. These aren't opposing forces. They are two ways of knowing that work best together. Much of what people today experience as a "meaning crisis" traces back to this unnecessary split — and addressing it means learning to hold reason and imagination as partners, not rivals.

## Adept

C.S. Lewis's pre-conversion self-diagnosis — that he loved the imaginary and found the real meaningless — names something far larger than one man's spiritual crisis. It identifies what might be called an epistemological apartheid at the heart of modernity: the rigid segregation of objective, verifiable fact from the domains of myth, art, and imagination. Under this regime, facts are granted epistemic authority but stripped of meaning, while myth and poetry are permitted emotional resonance but denied any purchase on reality. The result is a civilization that knows more and more while meaning less and less.

The intellectual breakthrough Tolkien offered Lewis was not a retreat from reason but a radical expansion of it. The proposal was precise: what if the archetypal myth of the dying and rising god — Balder, Adonis, Osiris — was on one occasion enacted not through the medium of language but through the medium of history itself? Such an event would be simultaneously available to rational-historical investigation and charged with the full mythic significance that gives death and resurrection their universal resonance. It would be fact that had not been drained of meaning, and myth that had not been exiled from reality.

This framework suggests that the contemporary meaning crisis is not primarily a crisis of insufficient facts or insufficient stories, but a crisis of their artificial separation. Reason and imagination, the historical and the mythic, are not competing epistemologies but complementary organs of a single integrated knowing. The healing Lewis experienced points toward a broader epistemological reconciliation — one that does not require adopting his specific conclusions but does require recognizing that the split between fact and meaning was always a wound, never a discovery.

## Magus

Lewis's autobiographical formulation — 'Nearly everything I loved I believed to be imaginary; nearly everything I believed to be real I thought grim and meaningless' — constitutes a phenomenological diagnosis of modernity's epistemic architecture. The bifurcation he names is not idiosyncratic but structural: it is the inheritance of a post-Kantian settlement in which Wissenschaft claims exclusive jurisdiction over the real while consigning Bildung, mythos, and poetic knowing to a subjective hinterland granted aesthetic toleration but denied ontological standing. The result is what one might term epistemological apartheid — a regime in which facticity and significance are systematically prevented from inhabiting the same epistemic space.

Tolkien's intervention operated at a precise philosophical juncture. The challenge was not fideistic but categorial: what if the mythopoeic pattern — the dying-and-rising god attested across cultures as an archetypal structure of meaning — were instantiated not through the semiotic medium of narrative but through the ontological medium of historical event? Such an occurrence would resist classification under the modern settlement's terms, being irreducibly both Faktum and Mythos, available to historiographical scrutiny without exhaustion by it, saturated with archetypal resonance without reduction to mere symbol. It would constitute what might be called a myth become fact — an event that collapses the fact-meaning distinction by inhabiting both registers simultaneously.

The philosophical yield extends well beyond Lewis's particular resolution. The insight is that modernity's meaning crisis is substantially an artifact of an epistemological partition that was presented as a discovery about the structure of knowledge but functions as a decision about what counts as knowing. Reason and imagination, the verifiable and the numinous, are not rival magisteria but integrated capacities of a single noetic act. Recovering their unity is not nostalgia for pre-critical naïveté but a genuinely post-critical epistemological task — one that the Lewis-Tolkien exchange illuminates with unusual clarity.
