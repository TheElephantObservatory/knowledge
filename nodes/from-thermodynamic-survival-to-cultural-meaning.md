# From Thermodynamic Survival to Cultural Meaning

The ink remembers the paper

---

> Meaning didn't appear with humans — it evolved in layers, from the basic survival signals of physics all the way up to poetry and philosophy, each level building on but irreducible to the last.

Meaning has complexified across cosmic evolution not by replacing earlier forms but by layering new registers of semantic information upon existing substrates — each level dependent upon but irreducible to what came before. Beginning as a purely thermodynamic imperative, the most rudimentary form of meaning is simply what aids an entity in resisting entropic dissolution — what physicist Carlo Rovelli calls 'direct meaning': information with immediate causal bearing on an organism's thermodynamic viability. From this foundation, meaning deepens through biological life into goal-directed behavior — energy capture, self-maintenance, reproduction — then diversifies through the evolution of nervous systems into felt experience, normative valence, and the inner world of subjectivity. Language then performs a double transformation: it reframes meanings from all prior levels while simultaneously opening entirely new domains — self-awareness, theory of mind, philosophy, religion, science — that have no analogue below the cultural threshold. This extraordinary semantic complexity is grounded in what Rovelli calls 'indirect meaning': information relevant not because it directly sustains survival, but because it increases the probability of acquiring more directly meaningful information. Crucially, indirect meanings can themselves generate further indirect meanings, each layer more abstracted from the thermodynamic foundation yet still tethered to it through a long causal chain — a recursive dynamic that bootstraps an entire civilization of meaning from that original survival signal. The crucial philosophical move is the distinction between reduction and foundation: human meaning cannot be reduced to thermodynamic survival any more than the contents of a book can be reduced to the paper it is written on — yet without the paper, there is no book. This gives us a naturalistic grounding for meaning that is neither reductive nor mysterian: a chain of successively more complex semantic registers, each adding articulation to the last, running unbroken from physics to culture.

## Apprentice

Most people assume meaning is a uniquely human invention — something that arrived with language, culture, or consciousness. But this perspective argues that meaning has a much older and more continuous history, stretching back to the very first physical processes that allowed living things to persist in a world that tends toward disorder. At its most basic, meaning is simply information that helps something survive — a chemical signal that triggers a response, a nutrient gradient that guides a bacterium. That's not a metaphor for meaning; it is meaning in its earliest form.

As life grew more complex, meaning grew with it. Nervous systems added the capacity for felt experience — pleasure, pain, desire. Language then performed something remarkable: it didn't just add another layer, it reached back and reframed all the layers below it, while simultaneously opening entirely new territories of meaning that have no equivalent in any non-linguistic creature. Self-awareness, philosophy, science, and religion are all meanings that language made possible for the first time.

The key insight is that none of this makes human meaning less real or less profound. A book cannot be reduced to the paper it's printed on, but without the paper there is no book. Human meaning is grounded in physical survival without being imprisoned by it — a continuous chain running, unbroken, from thermodynamics to culture.

## Adept

Drawing on Carlo Rovelli's distinction between direct and indirect meaning, this framework proposes that semantic complexity has evolved through successive, irreducible layers across cosmic history. Direct meaning — information with immediate causal bearing on an organism's thermodynamic viability — constitutes the most primitive register: the survival signal. Biological evolution elaborates this into goal-directed behavior, homeostasis, and reproduction. The emergence of nervous systems then introduces a qualitatively new dimension: felt experience, normative valence, and the interiority of subjectivity. Each level is dependent upon but not reducible to its substrate.

Language marks a phase transition rather than a mere increment. It performs a double transformation: retroactively reframing meanings from all prior biological and experiential registers, while simultaneously generating entirely novel semantic domains — theory of mind, reflexive self-awareness, metaphysics, science — that have no analogue below the cultural threshold. These new domains operate through indirect meaning: information relevant not because it directly sustains survival, but because it increases the probability of acquiring directly meaningful information. Crucially, indirect meanings can themselves generate further indirect meanings, producing a recursive, self-amplifying architecture of semantic complexity.

The philosophical payoff is a naturalistic account of meaning that avoids both reductionism and mysterianism. The relationship between thermodynamic foundation and cultural superstructure is analogous to that between paper and text: the former is a necessary condition for the latter without determining its content. This preserves the genuine novelty and irreducibility of human meaning while grounding it in an unbroken causal chain running from physics through biology to culture.

## Magus

This framework advances a stratified ontology of semiosis in which meaning undergoes complexification through successive emergent registers, each constituting a genuine novelty irreducible to its substrate while remaining causally dependent upon it. The foundational register is thermodynamic: following Rovelli, 'direct meaning' designates information with immediate causal efficacy for an entity's resistance to entropic dissolution — a biosemiotic primitive that precedes and grounds all subsequent semantic elaboration. Biological evolution iterates upon this foundation through the development of teleonomic organization, metabolic self-maintenance, and reproductive imperatives, while the Cambrian elaboration of nervous systems introduces phenomenal consciousness, affective valence, and the interoceptive construction of a subject-pole — a qualitative transformation that cannot be derived from functional description alone.

Language introduces what might be termed a semiotic fold: it does not merely append a new stratum but retroactively restructures the semantic content of all prior registers through symbolic re-encoding, while simultaneously bootstrapping domains of meaning — recursive self-modeling, intersubjective mind-attribution, propositional theology, formal science — that are strictly unprecedented below the threshold of cultural transmission. The operative mechanism is Rovelli's 'indirect meaning': information whose relevance is mediated through its probabilistic contribution to the acquisition of directly meaningful information. The recursive productivity of indirect meaning — whereby each abstracted layer can generate further abstracted layers — accounts for the extraordinary semantic fecundity of cultural evolution without invoking any non-naturalistic explanatory resource.

The central philosophical contribution is a principled distinction between foundational and reductive relations. Thermodynamic viability is the necessary condition — the paper — without which no cultural inscription is possible, yet the propositional content of that inscription is not derivable from the physical properties of its substrate. This yields a position orthogonal to both eliminative naturalism and explanatory dualism: a continuist yet non-reductive account in which the full hierarchy of meaning, from physics to metaphysics, constitutes a single unbroken but internally differentiated causal-semiotic chain.
