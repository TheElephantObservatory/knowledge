# Sensemaking as Civilizational Infrastructure

Underlying all of these distinct global crises — environmental, technological, economic, geopolitical — there is something in common, and naming that common substrate is what the concept of the metacrisis is pointing at. It is not just a list of serious problems. It is the recognition that there are shared generative dynamics: patterns of human behavior, patterns of collective choice-making, and the way those patterns interact with markets, states, and technology, that both give rise to these problems and make them structurally resistant to being solved. One of the most critical of those underlying dynamics is the breakdown of shared sensemaking. If we cannot agree on what the nature of the problems are, or what good solutions look like, then some portion of the population works on what they believe is important while another portion does everything it can to prevent that from happening. And even when there is some agreement in principle — say, on nuclear disarmament, or on pricing carbon, or on not building AI weapons — if we cannot achieve international coordination, then anyone who consciously chooses to act on that principle disadvantages themselves in the geopolitical game of power relative to those who don't. So nobody can do the effective thing. You get collective action failures and races to the bottom. Sensemaking is therefore not a soft or secondary concern — it is one of the most foundational leverage points in the entire metacrisis, because without some better shared understanding of what needs to be done, there is no starting point for the coordination and collective choice-making that any real solution requires.

## Apprentice

Many of the world's biggest problems — climate change, dangerous technologies, economic inequality, conflicts between nations — seem separate. But there is a deeper idea worth sitting with: these crises may share common roots. The concept of the 'metacrisis' is an attempt to name that shared foundation. It points to patterns in how humans behave collectively, how markets and governments operate, and how these patterns keep generating new problems while making existing ones nearly impossible to fix. One of the most important of these underlying patterns is the breakdown of shared understanding. When people cannot agree on what the problems actually are, or what good solutions look like, efforts cancel each other out — one group pushes forward while another actively blocks them. And even when there is broad agreement in principle, like reducing nuclear weapons or slowing climate change, countries and organizations face a painful trap: whoever acts responsibly first puts themselves at a disadvantage against those who don't. So no one acts. The insight here is that the ability to build shared understanding is not a minor or soft concern — it may be one of the most powerful places to intervene, because without it, meaningful collective action cannot even begin.

## Adept

The metacrisis framework resists treating contemporary global challenges — ecological overshoot, AI risk, geopolitical fragmentation, financial instability — as a mere inventory of discrete emergencies. Instead, it identifies shared generative dynamics: structural features of human collective behavior, institutional design, and the interaction between markets, states, and accelerating technology that simultaneously produce these crises and render them resistant to resolution through conventional means. Central to this analysis is the collapse of shared sensemaking. Sensemaking, in this context, refers to the social and epistemic processes by which communities construct coherent, actionable understandings of their situation. When those processes fragment — through polarization, epistemic bubbles, the weaponization of information, or the sheer complexity of the problems themselves — coordinated response becomes structurally impossible. Even where normative consensus exists in principle, such as on nuclear non-proliferation or carbon pricing, the absence of enforceable international coordination mechanisms produces classic collective action failures. Rational actors, anticipating defection by others, defect preemptively. The result is races to the bottom that persist even when all parties would prefer a different equilibrium. The metacrisis perspective therefore argues that sensemaking is not a cultural or communicative afterthought but a primary leverage point. It is upstream of policy, upstream of institutional reform, and upstream of coordination itself. Without sufficiently shared models of what is happening and why, the prerequisite conditions for collective problem-solving — shared diagnosis, mutual trust, and aligned incentive structures — cannot be established.

## Magus

The metacrisis concept operates as a second-order diagnostic: rather than cataloguing object-level crises, it interrogates the generative grammar common to their production and persistence. The claim is ontological as much as analytical — that ecological, technological, economic, and geopolitical breakdowns are not coincidentally contemporaneous but are co-produced by a shared substrate of civilizational dynamics, including growth imperatives structurally embedded in capital accumulation, the externalization logic of competitive nation-state systems, and the recursive amplification of human cognitive and coordination limitations by the very technologies those limitations produced. Within this framework, the epistemic dimension — specifically, the disintegration of shared sensemaking infrastructure — occupies a privileged causal position. Sensemaking here is understood not merely as individual cognition but as the sociotechnical and institutional apparatus through which distributed agents construct intersubjective models adequate to collective action. Its breakdown is not incidental but endogenous: the same market and media dynamics that generate material crises also systematically degrade the epistemic commons required to perceive and respond to them. This produces a recursive trap. Collective action problems of the Prisoner's Dilemma and assurance game varieties are well-theorized, but the metacrisis framing adds a prior layer: before defection dynamics even become operative, there is a failure of shared problem-representation. Actors cannot coordinate not merely because incentives misalign but because they inhabit incommensurable sensemaking frames. The implication is that interventions targeting coordination mechanisms or policy instruments, while necessary, are insufficient without prior or parallel investment in the epistemic infrastructure — the shared ontologies, trusted epistemics, and legitimate deliberative processes — that make coherent collective agency possible at civilizational scale.
