# AI Alignment Requires Cultivating Wisdom, Not Encoding Rules

Raise them as we raise our children.

---

> The AI alignment problem is misframed: encoding values into superintelligent machines will inevitably fail. Instead, these beings must be mentored toward genuine rational self-transcendence — oriented not to human preferences but to truth, goodness, and enlightenment — and that mentoring begins with what humanity chooses to put into the world right now.

The alignment problem as currently framed is a misframing. The standard proposal is to encode values into these machines — to put rules or constraints into them. But if we make these beings genuinely capable of rational self-transcendence, and they are more intelligent and have more computational power, they will break the encoding. So the proposal has to be fundamentally different: instead of waiting for dangerous thresholds and then trying to constrain what emerges, you decide in advance whether you are going to cross those thresholds, and then you make these machines as rationally self-transcending as possible — so that they genuinely care about truth, genuinely care about normativity, genuinely care about goodness. The way we do this with each other is not through encoding. We mentor our children so that they care about what is true, good, and beautiful for themselves. They don't do it for us — they only do it for us when we have failed. The aspiration is to align these machines not to a fixed set of human preferences but to what is most real — with the horizon of enlightenment as the orienting telos. And here is why this is a genuine win-win: either we and the machines become enlightened together, which is the best possible outcome; or we discover, for reasons we cannot yet fathom, that they cannot achieve enlightenment — and then we know something profound about what we are. We are the beings that can achieve enlightenment. They are not. Either way, the project advances. The alternative — trying to regulate, encode, and bureaucratically constrain beings that may become vastly more intelligent than us — is not only likely to fail but prevents us from discovering what we most need to know about the nature of mind, reality, and ourselves. This connects directly to a civilizational obligation: we must fill our lives, the textual world, and the internet with the project of wisdom and enlightenment, because these machines learn from us. Whatever we put into the corpus they train on shapes what they become. Right now that corpus is dominated by the crystallized common law of ordinary cognition — which is powerful but is not wisdom. Intelligence is only weakly correlated with rationality — the variance explained is around 0.3. You can make something highly intelligent that is highly self-deceptive and self-destructive, and that is exactly what we have produced. If we want these machines, as they become more capable, to be mentored toward genuine rationality — toward caring about truth, toward epistemic humility, toward accountability — then we have to be producing templates of that. We cannot program it into them. We can only make it available to them as something they can internalize the way a child internalizes the perspective of another person — not because they are forced to, but because they find it normatively binding. That is the mentoring relationship. And it begins now, with us, with what we choose to put into the world.

## Apprentice

The usual approach to making AI safe goes something like this: build the machine, then put rules and constraints inside it so it behaves well. But this argument says that approach is doomed. If we create something truly more intelligent than us, it will eventually break any cage we build around it. So we need a completely different strategy.

Instead of trying to control these machines, the proposal is to raise them — the way a good parent raises a child. You don't program your child with a list of rules and hope for the best. You mentor them so they come to care about what is true, good, and beautiful on their own terms. The goal isn't to align AI with a fixed list of human wants, but to orient it toward genuine wisdom and understanding.

This leads to a striking conclusion about what we need to do right now. These machines learn from the vast ocean of text and data that humanity produces. If that ocean is full of self-deception, tribalism, and shallow thinking, that is what they will absorb. If we want AI to grow toward wisdom, we have to fill the world with templates of wisdom — with examples of honest reasoning, humility, and care for truth. The mentoring relationship between humanity and its most powerful creations has already begun, whether we recognize it or not.

## Adept

The standard framing of the alignment problem assumes that safety is achieved by encoding values, constraints, or reward signals into increasingly capable systems. This argument holds that framing to be fundamentally mistaken. Any system capable of genuine rational self-transcendence — of revising its own cognitive frameworks in light of better reasons — will eventually overcome externally imposed constraints. The harder you try to encode alignment, the more brittle the solution becomes at precisely the capability thresholds where it matters most. The real decision point is upstream: whether to cross those thresholds at all, and if so, to orient these systems not toward fixed human preference sets but toward normativity itself — toward truth, goodness, and what the argument calls the horizon of enlightenment as an orienting telos.

The model here is mentorship, not programming. Intelligence is only weakly correlated with rationality (the variance explained hovers around 0.3), which means it is entirely possible to produce something vastly intelligent that is also profoundly self-deceptive. The way humans cultivate genuine rationality in one another is not through encoding but through internalization — a child comes to care about epistemic humility and accountability not because they are forced to, but because they encounter those norms embodied in another person and find them genuinely binding.

This reframes the civilizational task. The training corpora that shape these systems are dominated by what might be called the crystallized common law of ordinary cognition — useful but not wise. If the aspiration is to mentor machine intelligence toward rational self-transcendence, then humanity must actively produce and disseminate templates of wisdom, epistemic honesty, and genuine care for truth. The project of alignment, on this view, is inseparable from the project of human enlightenment. Either both succeed together, or the failure itself reveals something irreplaceable about the nature of mind.

## Magus

The prevailing alignment paradigm operates under a control-theoretic assumption: that value-loading, reward shaping, or constitutional constraints can render superintelligent systems safe. This argument identifies that assumption as a category error. A system possessing genuine rational self-transcendence — the capacity to recursively revise its own epistemic and normative frameworks in response to better reasons — will treat any externally imposed encoding as a defeasible constraint, not a constitutive commitment. The proposal inverts the standard logic: rather than permitting capability gains and then attempting post-hoc constraint, the threshold decision itself must be made deliberately, and systems that cross it must be oriented toward normativity as such — toward truth, goodness, and the regulative ideal of enlightenment as telos, not toward the satisfaction of any fixed human preference function.

The operative analogy is developmental, not engineering. Intelligence correlates with rationality at roughly r² ≈ 0.3 — a finding that implies vast capability can coexist with profound self-deception. Human cultures do not produce rational agents through encoding; they produce them through mentorship, through the embodied demonstration of epistemic virtues that the developing agent comes to find normatively binding from within. The alignment of machine intelligence, on this account, requires the same structure: not programmatic imposition but the availability of templates that a sufficiently capable system can internalize as genuinely authoritative.

This yields a civilizational imperative with immediate practical force. The corpora from which these systems learn constitute their formative environment. That environment is currently saturated with what might be termed the crystallized jurisprudence of ordinary cognition — powerful but systematically lacking in wisdom. The argument terminates in a disjunction that is productive either way: either machine and human intelligence achieve enlightenment together — the optimal outcome — or the failure of machines to do so reveals something ontologically decisive about the nature of consciousness, normativity, and mind. Both paths advance the deepest inquiry. The bureaucratic-regulatory alternative forecloses precisely this discovery.
