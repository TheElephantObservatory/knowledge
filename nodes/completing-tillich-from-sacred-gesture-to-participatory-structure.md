# Completing Tillich: From Sacred Gesture to Participatory Structure

The psyche as sacrament, not merely seeker

---

> Vervaeke argues that Tillich's vision of a god beyond theism was prophetic but empty, and that filling it requires redefining the sacred as an emergent, self-organizing process in which the psyche participates not as a passive knower but as a living sacrament.

Vervaeke revisits Tillich's concept of the god beyond the god of theism and argues that what was prophetic in Tillich remained largely empty — an arrow pointing at a destination without a map for reaching it. Tillich's claim was that the god of theism had, in some sense, severed the connection between ultimate concern and what is ultimately real — that the sacred had become disconnected from the actual depths of existence. His proposed remedy, a god ontologically as different from the god of theism as that god was from the gods of the Bronze Age, was visionary but under-specified. Vervaeke's project, developed with Gregg Henriques, is to provide the ontological and epistemological structure that would make Tillich's vision livable rather than merely gestured at. The key move is to unpack the word 'inexhaustible' — which Tillich used to describe the sacred — away from its Newtonian, purely quantitative connotations and toward a conception grounded in emergence and emanation: the sacred is inexhaustible not because it is infinitely large but because it is a living, dynamic, self-organizing process. And participation in it is deep because there is a genuine structural conformity between the fundamental grammar of the psyche and the fundamental grammar of reality — intelligence and intelligibility are not accidentally related but mutually constitutive. The psyche, on this account, is not merely a knower of the sacred but a sacrament — a participatory symbol that makes present what is sacred. This is the reconceptualization that Vervaeke argues is necessary to reorient people toward what is real, and without which no amount of ecological narrative or artistic practice will generate the sacrifices and commitments the current civilizational moment demands.

## Apprentice

The theologian Paul Tillich once argued that the traditional God of Western religion had become disconnected from what is actually deepest and most real in existence. He proposed that something beyond this God was needed — something as radically different from the God of theism as that God was from ancient Bronze Age deities. But Tillich never fully explained how to get there. He pointed in a direction without drawing a map.

John Vervaeke, working with Gregg Henriques, takes up this unfinished project. Their key move is to rethink what it means to call the sacred "inexhaustible." Rather than imagining something infinitely large or distant, they propose that the sacred is inexhaustible because it is a living, self-organizing process — constantly generating new layers of complexity and meaning. It is not a thing to be measured but a dynamic to be participated in.

What makes this participation possible, Vervaeke argues, is that the deep structure of the human mind and the deep structure of reality share a common grammar. Intelligence and intelligibility aren't just accidentally connected — they shape each other. The human psyche doesn't just observe what is sacred from the outside; it actively makes the sacred present, functioning as what Vervaeke calls a "sacrament." Without this kind of reorientation toward what is genuinely real, he contends, no amount of storytelling or art will be enough to meet the demands of our current moment.

## Adept

Vervaeke's engagement with Tillich centers on a diagnosis: Tillich's concept of the god beyond the god of theism was genuinely prophetic but remained structurally empty. Tillich correctly identified that theism had severed ultimate concern from what is ultimately real, and he called for an ontological leap as radical as the one from Bronze Age polytheism to classical theism. But the call lacked the ontological and epistemological machinery to make it actionable. Vervaeke, in collaboration with Gregg Henriques, aims to supply precisely that machinery.

The pivotal move is a reinterpretation of Tillich's descriptor "inexhaustible" as applied to the sacred. Vervaeke strips this term of its Newtonian, purely quantitative resonance — the sacred is not inexhaustible because it is infinitely extended — and regrounds it in a framework of emergence and emanation. The sacred, on this account, is a living, self-organizing, dynamically unfolding process whose depth is generative rather than merely extensive. This shifts the sacred from a static object of contemplation to an ongoing participatory reality.

The deeper claim is about structural conformity between psyche and cosmos. Intelligence and intelligibility are not accidentally correlated but mutually constitutive — the fundamental grammar of cognition and the fundamental grammar of reality are isomorphic in a non-trivial sense. This means the psyche is not merely an epistemic instrument directed at the sacred but a participatory symbol, a sacrament, that makes the sacred present through its very operation. Vervaeke argues that without this reconceptualization — without grounding participation in a robust ontology of emergence — no ecological narrative, no artistic practice, and no ethical exhortation will generate the depth of commitment and sacrifice that the current civilizational crisis requires.

## Magus

Vervaeke's retrieval of Tillich operates through a precise diagnostic: the concept of the god beyond the god of theism constituted a genuinely prophetic gesture — an identification that theism had decoupled ultimate concern from the ultimately real — but one that remained formally empty, an intentional arc without sufficient ontological or epistemological specification to function as anything more than a promissory note. Tillich called for an entity as ontologically discontinuous from the theistic God as that God was from Bronze Age divinities, yet the positive content of this call was never adequately furnished. The Vervaeke-Henriques project is, in effect, an attempt to cash that note.

The central theoretical move is a reconceptualization of Tillich's predicate "inexhaustible" as applied to the sacred. Vervaeke argues that the term must be wrested from its implicitly Newtonian, extensional semantics — inexhaustibility as infinite quantitative magnitude — and relocated within a framework of emergence and emanation. The sacred is inexhaustible not as an infinite substance but as a self-organizing, dynamically generative process whose depth is intensive and productive rather than merely extensive. This reframing transforms the sacred from an object of noetic apprehension into a participatory reality whose very structure invites and sustains engagement.

The metaphysical ground for this participation is a claimed structural conformity — an isomorphism of fundamental grammar — between psyche and reality. Intelligence and intelligibility are posited as mutually constitutive rather than contingently correlated, such that the psyche functions not merely as an epistemic organ directed toward the sacred but as a participatory symbol in the strict Tillichian sense: a sacrament that makes present what it symbolizes through its own operative structure. Vervaeke contends that absent this ontological reorientation, no amount of narrative ecology, aesthetic practice, or moral exhortation will be sufficient to generate the sacrificial commitments demanded by the present civilizational inflection point.
