# The Anti-Fascist Origins of Systems Thinking

A firewall against the objectifying eye

---

> Gregory Bateson's systems thinking wasn't born in a library — it was a moral emergency response to rising fascism, built on the conviction that treating any living thing as an isolated object is the first step toward dominating it.

Gregory Bateson's foundational contribution to systems thinking was not primarily an intellectual exercise — it was a moral and political emergency response. When Gregory and Margaret Mead were in New Guinea in 1929, both in their mid-twenties, fascism was rising across Europe. They began writing urgent letters to a network of friends — people who would later become the core participants of the Macy Conferences — calling for what they described as 'a new kind of science.' This science would study how the world is put together rather than fragmenting and compartmentalizing it. They didn't yet have the vocabulary or the framework — the letters are earnest, searching, almost fumbling — but the animating passion was clear: they believed that if you could objectify anything, it could be exploited. The act of treating any part of the living world as a discrete, bounded object — separable from its relationships and context — was the epistemological precondition for domination, whether of nature, of peoples, or of cultures. So the entire intellectual architecture that would eventually become cybernetics, systems theory, and the ecology of mind was not born in the abstract. It was born in the specific historical horror of watching fascism rise and asking: what kind of thinking made this possible, and what kind of thinking might prevent it? That origin story matters enormously, because it means the stakes of getting epistemology right are not merely academic — they are civilizational.

## Apprentice

In 1929, Gregory Bateson and Margaret Mead were doing fieldwork in New Guinea when fascism was beginning its rise across Europe. Young, alarmed, and thousands of miles from home, they began writing urgent letters to a circle of friends — people who would later gather at the famous Macy Conferences — calling for an entirely new kind of science. They didn't have the words for it yet. The letters are searching and earnest, feeling toward something they couldn't quite name.

What they were reaching for was a science that looked at how things connect rather than how they can be separated. Their core intuition was this: the moment you treat something — a person, a culture, a forest — as a self-contained object, cut off from its relationships and surroundings, you've already done something dangerous. You've made it available for exploitation. Objectification, in their view, wasn't just a philosophical error. It was the mental move that made domination possible.

This is where cybernetics, systems theory, and what Bateson would later call 'the ecology of mind' actually came from — not from abstract curiosity, but from a specific historical horror and a desperate question: what kind of thinking enables fascism, and what kind of thinking might stop it? That origin changes everything about how these ideas should be read. The stakes were never merely academic.

## Adept

The intellectual genealogy of cybernetics and systems theory is typically narrated as a story of mid-twentieth-century scientific convergence — Wiener's feedback mathematics, Shannon's information theory, von Neumann's computation, all braided together at the Macy Conferences. What this account obscures is the moral emergency that preceded and motivated that convergence. As early as 1929, Bateson and Mead, conducting fieldwork in New Guinea, were already circulating letters among the future Macy participants calling for a 'new kind of science' — one oriented toward integration and relationship rather than analytic decomposition. The proximate cause was the rise of European fascism.

The epistemological argument they were groping toward was precise, even if the vocabulary wasn't yet available: that the act of bounding any phenomenon as a discrete, context-independent object — the foundational move of classical empiricism — was not merely an abstraction but a political act. To objectify is to sever an entity from the web of relationships that constitute it, rendering it available for instrumental use. Bateson would later formalize this as a critique of 'the Cartesian dualism' and develop it through his concept of the 'ecology of mind,' but the moral intuition preceded the theoretical architecture by decades.

This origin story carries significant interpretive weight. It means that the systems-theoretic insistence on context, relationship, and pattern-over-time is not a methodological preference but an ethical commitment — a deliberate counter-epistemology designed to resist the cognitive preconditions of domination. Getting the epistemology wrong, in this framing, is not an academic failure. It is a civilizational one.

## Magus

The standard historiography of the cybernetic moment — Heims's account of the Macy Conferences, Hayles's reading of posthuman information theory, Pickering's 'ontological theatre' — tends to locate the generative pressure of the movement in the institutional and disciplinary conjunctures of postwar American science. What this framing underweights is the prior moral-political urgency that structured the project from its inception. Bateson and Mead's 1929 correspondence, composed during fieldwork in the Sepik River region and addressed to the nascent network that would eventually constitute the Macy circle, articulates a demand for epistemological renovation whose animating force is explicitly anti-fascist. The letters are pre-theoretical in register — the vocabulary of feedback, recursion, and systemic causality had not yet been assembled — but the diagnostic claim is already present: that a particular mode of knowing was complicit in a particular mode of domination.

The argument, reconstructed in its mature form across Bateson's later work, runs as follows: the bounding of phenomena as discrete, context-independent objects — the operationalizing gesture of Cartesian-Baconian method — is not epistemologically innocent. It performs a severance of entity from constitutive relation, producing what Bateson would call 'the epistemology of Occidental civilization,' in which the unit of survival is misidentified as the organism or the lineage rather than the organism-in-environment. This misidentification is not merely a theoretical error; it is the cognitive infrastructure of extractive and dominating praxis, whether directed at colonized peoples, nonhuman natures, or subordinated cultures.

The implication is that cybernetics, systems theory, and the ecology of mind must be read not as value-neutral formalisms subsequently available for ethical application, but as counter-epistemologies — deliberately constructed to interrupt the cognitive moves that fascism, colonialism, and ecological destruction share. The civilizational stakes Bateson attached to epistemological correctness were not rhetorical inflation. They were the founding premise.
