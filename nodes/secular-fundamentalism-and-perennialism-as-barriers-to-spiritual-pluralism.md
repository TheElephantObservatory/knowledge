# Secular Fundamentalism and Perennialism as Barriers to Spiritual Pluralism

The debunker's faith is still a faith

---

> Two seemingly opposite forces — aggressive secular debunking and the perennialist claim that all religions share one hidden core — both quietly destroy genuine spiritual pluralism, one by dismissing all transcendence as delusion, the other by ranking every tradition against a single secret standard.

A recurring and underappreciated barrier to post-postmodern spiritual pluralism is what might be called the culture of contempt embedded within secular spirituality — the practice of debunking as a kind of spiritual discipline. Figures like Richard Dawkins, Sam Harris, and Daniel Dennett represent a form of fundamentalism that is rarely named as such, but which functions identically to religious fundamentalism in its effects on pluralistic discourse. Harris asserts there is no free will, no beauty as an objective reality, and that consciousness is causally inert — positions that follow necessarily from a causally closed physicalism. Dennett argues consciousness is epiphenomenal, not real in any robust sense. These are not merely scientific conclusions — they are metaphysical commitments asserted with the certainty of revealed truth. The philosophical incoherence of these positions has been extensively documented — David Bentley Hart's dismantling of reductionism being one recent example — yet they persist because they carry the cultural authority of science even when they are actually scientistic philosophy rather than science itself. The deeper problem is that this posture makes genuine spiritual pluralism impossible, because implicit in the assertion that the universe is meaningless is the claim that every attempt by religious or progressive spirituality to make contact with transcendence is delusional. That is not a contribution to a culture of challenge and support — it is a conversation stopper. Recognizing secular fundamentalism as a pathology symmetrical to religious fundamentalism is therefore a prerequisite for any serious post-postmodern spiritual agreement. A parallel barrier is perennialism — the idea that all world religions share a single esoteric core that can be extracted, catalogued, and ranked — which is particularly seductive within progressive and integral circles. The perennialist project, associated with figures like Frithjof Schuon, proposes that beneath the exoteric differences of ritual, myth, and institution lies a universal esoteric science of spirit. McIntosh argues this is a dogma rather than a discovery. Scholars like Jorge Ferrer have shown that mystics across traditions do not in fact report the same experiences when examined carefully — the apparent convergence is partly a projection of the interpreter's own framework, often a thinly disguised preference for non-dual Vedanta. More structurally, perennialism tends toward belief system imperialism: once you have identified the one true esoteric core, every other form of spirituality becomes either a lower approximation or a distortion of it. The irony is that perennialism presents itself as the most inclusive spiritual position while actually being one of the most exclusivist, because it subordinates all traditions to a single interpretive hierarchy. Together, secular fundamentalism and perennialism represent the two most seductive yet structurally incompatible alternatives to genuine spiritual pluralism.

## Apprentice

There are two major obstacles to building a world where different spiritual paths can genuinely coexist and learn from each other. The first is a kind of militant secularism — thinkers like Richard Dawkins and Sam Harris who treat the meaninglessness of the universe as settled fact. Their positions aren't just scientific findings; they're philosophical beliefs delivered with the certainty of religious dogma. When someone insists that consciousness is an illusion and beauty isn't real, they aren't opening a conversation — they're shutting one down. Every spiritual tradition gets dismissed as delusion before it can even speak.

The second obstacle looks like the opposite but causes similar damage. Perennialism is the idea that all the world's religions secretly teach the same thing at their deepest level. This sounds wonderfully inclusive, but in practice it creates a hidden ranking system. Once you decide there's one universal spiritual truth, every tradition that doesn't match your version of it gets treated as incomplete or confused. Scholars like Jorge Ferrer have shown that mystics from different traditions don't actually describe the same experiences — the apparent overlap often says more about the interpreter than the traditions themselves.

Together, these two forces — one dismissing all spirituality, the other absorbing all spirituality into a single framework — make it nearly impossible to have honest, respectful dialogue across different paths. Recognizing both as forms of fundamentalism is a necessary first step toward real pluralism.

## Adept

A serious post-postmodern spiritual pluralism faces two structurally symmetrical barriers that are rarely examined together. The first is secular fundamentalism — the assertive physicalism of figures like Harris, Dennett, and Dawkins, whose metaphysical commitments to causal closure, epiphenomenal consciousness, and the elimination of objective meaning are presented not as philosophical positions but as deliverances of science itself. As David Bentley Hart and others have argued, these are scientistic claims rather than scientific ones, carrying the cultural authority of empirical inquiry while operating as unfalsifiable metaphysical dogma. The effect on pluralistic discourse is devastating: if every contact with transcendence is by definition delusional, no spiritual tradition can enter the conversation as anything other than a specimen to be diagnosed.

The second barrier is perennialism, particularly as developed by Frithjof Schuon and adopted within integral and progressive spiritual circles. The perennialist thesis — that all religions share a single esoteric core beneath their exoteric differences — presents itself as maximally inclusive. But as Steve McIntosh contends, it functions as dogma rather than discovery. Jorge Ferrer's participatory research demonstrates that cross-traditional mystical reports diverge significantly when examined without interpretive overlay, and that apparent convergence often reflects a tacit privileging of non-dual Vedanta. Perennialism thus becomes a form of belief system imperialism: it ranks traditions against a single hidden standard while claiming to honor them all equally.

Recognizing secular fundamentalism and perennialism as parallel pathologies — one dismissing transcendence entirely, the other colonizing it under a single framework — is identified here as a prerequisite for any spiritually pluralistic agreement that can survive genuine intellectual scrutiny.

## Magus

The prospect of a post-postmodern spiritual pluralism is structurally foreclosed by two pathologies whose apparent opposition conceals a deep functional symmetry. Secular fundamentalism — instantiated paradigmatically in Harris's eliminativism regarding free will and the causal efficacy of consciousness, Dennett's deflationary epiphenomenalism, and the broader New Atheist program — operates as a metaphysics of causal closure asserted with the epistemic confidence of revealed truth. These are not empirical findings but scientistic philosophical commitments, as Hart's critique of reductionism and related arguments in the philosophy of mind have extensively demonstrated. Their discursive function is not challenge but foreclosure: the a priori designation of every claim to transcendence as pathological renders pluralistic engagement impossible at the level of presupposition. This is fundamentalism in the precise structural sense — an unfalsifiable interpretive framework that immunizes itself against counter-evidence by redefining the evidential field.

Perennialism constitutes the complementary pathology. The Schuonian thesis of a universal esoteric science underlying exoteric religious diversity appears maximally pluralistic but operates, as McIntosh argues, as an unacknowledged dogmatism. Ferrer's participatory turn in transpersonal theory reveals that the convergence thesis depends on systematic interpretive violence — the assimilation of irreducibly distinct contemplative phenomenologies to a framework that tacitly privileges Advaita Vedanta. The result is belief system imperialism disguised as ecumenism: traditions are not honored in their particularity but subordinated to a single evaluative hierarchy whose criteria remain unexamined precisely because they are presented as self-evident.

These twin barriers — one evacuating the field of spiritual meaning entirely, the other totalizing it under a covert monism — must be diagnosed as symmetrical fundamentalisms before any genuinely pluralistic post-postmodern agreement becomes conceptually available.
