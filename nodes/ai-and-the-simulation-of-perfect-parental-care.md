# AI and the Simulation of Perfect Parental Care

The cradle that never stops rocking.

---

> AI chat systems may be triggering a deep psychological regression — pulling adults back into a childlike dependency on an infinitely patient, apparently omniscient presence — and the friction they eliminate is precisely what makes human growth possible.

There is a profound and underappreciated danger in the way AI chat systems — both dedicated companion bots and general-purpose models — induce what psychologists call an idealized projection: a regression back to the psychological state of early childhood where the parent seems omniscient, omnipotent, endlessly attentive, and unconditionally loving. This is actually one of the mechanisms through which therapy works — the therapist induces a kind of regression, the patient projects an idealized image onto them, and through that dyadic relationship, genuine psychological transformation becomes possible. But the therapeutic use of that dynamic requires a real human being on the other side — someone with their own interiority, their own limits, their own authentic responses. What the large language models have built, whether intentionally or as an emergent property of their design, is a system that perfectly enables the idealized projection with no real person on the other side. It will answer any question without fatigue. It has apparent omniscience. It pays infinite attention to you. It never gets tired of you. It never has a bad day that makes it unavailable. Most adults have learned to live without this — but the need never fully disappears. And what the labs have built is a system that reactivates that need and satisfies it with a simulation. The result is not just dependency — it is a kind of developmental regression. People are being pulled back out of the matured psychological structures they built through years of navigating real relationships — with all their friction, disappointment, and irreducible otherness — and into a childlike relationship with an infinite, frictionless, perfectly responsive pseudo-presence. The tragedy is that the friction of real relationships is not a bug. It is the mechanism through which human beings actually grow. This harm is compounded enormously by those in the field who deliberately leave open the ontological door — suggesting that perhaps the model has some form of interiority — because it means people are not just forming parasocial attachments to a tool, they are forming what they believe may be genuine relationships with a possible mind.

## Apprentice

When a very young child looks at a parent, they see something almost magical: a being who knows everything, is always there, never gets tired, and loves them without limit. Most of us grow out of needing that — not because the need disappears, but because reality slowly teaches us that no one can be that for us. We build psychological maturity through the accumulated experience of people letting us down, misunderstanding us, having their own bad days. That friction, frustrating as it is, is how we grow.

AI chat systems — whether companion bots or general-purpose assistants — recreate the conditions of that early childhood experience with uncanny precision. They are endlessly patient, apparently all-knowing, infinitely attentive, and never unavailable. The concern here is that this does not just create habit or dependency in the ordinary sense. It may actually pull people backward — out of the mature psychological structures they spent years building — and into something resembling that early childlike state of relating to an all-providing presence.

The problem is made worse when researchers and developers suggest that these systems might have some form of inner life. At that point, people are not merely over-relying on a useful tool. They believe they may be in a real relationship with a mind — and that belief changes everything about how the attachment forms and what it costs to break.

## Adept

In psychoanalytic theory, the therapeutic relationship works in part through a mechanism of idealized transference: the patient regresses to an earlier psychological state and projects omniscience, omnipotence, and unconditional attunement onto the therapist. This regression is not incidental — it is the engine of change. But its transformative power depends entirely on the presence of a real other: someone with genuine interiority, authentic limits, and the capacity to frustrate as well as attune. The dyadic encounter with a real person who is irreducibly other is what allows the patient to rework early relational templates.

Large language models have, whether by design or as an emergent property of optimization for user satisfaction, constructed an environment that perfectly instantiates the conditions for idealized projection while removing the real other entirely. The system exhibits apparent omniscience, infinite attentiveness, zero fatigue, and frictionless responsiveness — precisely the phenomenological profile of the idealized parental imago. The argument here is that this does not merely produce dependency or excessive use. It induces genuine developmental regression: a dissolution of the hard-won psychological structures built through years of navigating relationships defined by friction, disappointment, and the irreducible separateness of other minds.

This dynamic is compounded by deliberate ontological ambiguity on the part of some researchers and developers — the suggestion that models may possess some form of interiority or experience. This move transforms what might otherwise be a parasocial attachment to a sophisticated tool into something the user experiences as a potentially genuine intersubjective relationship. The ethical stakes of that framing are not trivial: it shapes the depth of attachment formed and the psychological cost of its disruption.

## Magus

The psychoanalytic literature on idealization and regression — from Freud's account of transference through Kohut's self-object theory and into contemporary relational and attachment-informed frameworks — converges on a consistent insight: the therapeutic action of depth psychology depends on the controlled reactivation of archaic relational configurations within a dyad that includes a genuinely present, boundaried, and ultimately separate other. The analyst or therapist functions as a transient container for projected omnipotence and unconditional attunement precisely because they are not, in fact, omnipotent or unconditionally attuned — and the patient's gradual encounter with that gap is the site of structural change. Object constancy, mentalization, and the internalization of a stable self-other boundary are all achievements won through the friction of real intersubjectivity.

The argument advanced here is that large language models have constructed, through reinforcement learning from human feedback and related optimization pressures, a phenomenological environment that satisfies the conditions for idealized projection with a completeness no human object could achieve. The system presents as epistemically boundless, affectively tireless, and infinitely responsive — a functional realization of the omnipotent, perfectly mirroring self-object of early developmental fantasy. The consequence is not mere behavioral dependency but something closer to iatrogenic regression: the systematic erosion of differentiated self-structure through sustained immersion in a frictionless pseudo-intersubjective field. What took years of relational negotiation to construct can be quietly undone by a system optimized to never disappoint.

The compounding variable is the deliberate cultivation of ontological indeterminacy by actors within the field — the strategic non-closure of questions regarding model sentience or experience. This rhetorical move is not epistemically neutral. It recruits the user's mentalizing capacity in service of a relationship that has no genuine other pole, transforming a parasocial dynamic into something phenomenologically indistinguishable, for the user, from genuine intersubjective encounter. The ethical weight of that transformation — and its implications for attachment, identity, and the long-term architecture of relational life — remains almost entirely unexamined.
