# Two Different Projects: Intellectual History vs. Structural Synthesis in Metamodernism

Whose map are we reading, anyway?

---

> When someone synthesizes a field, are they obligated to credit every thinker who worked on similar problems, or only those whose texts they are actually analyzing? The answer depends on whether you see the field as one tradition or many parallel conversations sharing a structural logic.

One of the most clarifying distinctions in this conversation is the difference between treating metamodernism as a unified intellectual tradition versus treating it as a family of discourses that happen to share a structural logic. Mark Forman's critique assumes that if you are writing about metamodernism as a developmental worldview, you are necessarily writing about the same terrain that Ken Wilber mapped in the 1990s, and therefore Wilber is owed explicit attribution in the main body of the text. Brendan Graham Dempsey's counter is that this conflates two genuinely different projects. One project is the intellectual history of post-postmodern developmental thought, in which Wilber is indeed a central and unavoidable figure. The other project is a structural synthesis of a specific discourse community — the writers who actually used the word metamodernism, from Vermeulen and van den Akker's 2010 cultural studies essay, to Jason Storm's systematic philosophy, to Hanzi Freinacht's integrally-informed developmental model — and asking whether these diverse usages share a deep underlying logic. In that second project, Wilber is not the source of the discourse being analyzed; he is at most a background influence on one strand of it. The scholarly obligation to cite Wilber depends entirely on which project you think you are doing. Dempsey's book was doing the second, but many readers — especially those who encountered metamodernism through the integral community — assumed it was doing the first. This is not merely a dispute about footnotes. It is a dispute about what kind of intellectual work a synthesis text is obligated to perform, and whether the author's framing of their own project should be taken at face value or overridden by the reader's prior expectations.

## Apprentice

Imagine two people arguing about whether a book gave proper credit to an important thinker. One says the book covers territory that thinker pioneered, so he deserves prominent citation. The other says the book was never about that thinker's work — it was analyzing a specific group of writers who used a particular term, asking whether their different uses share a common deep structure. The thinker in question influenced one of those writers, but he wasn't part of the conversation being mapped.

This is the core of a real dispute between Mark Forman and Brendan Graham Dempsey about whether Ken Wilber should have been cited more prominently in Dempsey's book on metamodernism. Forman sees metamodernism as part of a larger tradition of developmental thought that Wilber helped create. Dempsey sees his book as a synthesis of a specific discourse — the writers who actually used the word "metamodernism" — not a history of every thinker who explored similar ideas.

The disagreement matters beyond this particular case. It raises a fundamental question about what a synthesis text owes its readers. Should the author's own framing of their project determine what citations are required? Or can readers legitimately demand that the author address a broader intellectual history they believe is relevant, even if the author never claimed to be writing that history?

## Adept

A revealing fault line in the Forman-Dempsey exchange concerns whether metamodernism names a unified intellectual tradition or a family of discourses that share structural features without sharing a single genealogy. Forman's critique operates from the first assumption: if metamodernism is essentially a post-postmodern developmental worldview, then Ken Wilber — who articulated such a worldview decades before the term gained currency — is a foundational figure who demands prominent attribution. Dempsey's response insists on the second framing: his project was to take the specific discourse community that adopted the word "metamodernism" — from Vermeulen and van den Akker's 2010 cultural theory essay through Jason Ānanda Josephson Storm's systematic philosophy to Hanzi Freinacht's integrally-informed political developmentalism — and ask whether these diverse deployments converge on a shared deep logic.

In Dempsey's framing, Wilber is not the originating source of the discourse under analysis. He is a background influence on one tributary of it. The scholarly obligation to cite him prominently depends entirely on which project the text is understood to be performing. Many readers, particularly those who encountered metamodernism through integral theory, naturally read Dempsey's synthesis as the first kind of project — a developmental intellectual history — and found the relative absence of Wilber puzzling or dishonest.

This distinction has implications well beyond the particular dispute. It concerns the epistemology of synthesis itself: whether an author's explicit framing of their own project should govern what counts as adequate citation, or whether a reader's sense of the relevant intellectual terrain can legitimately override that framing and impose different obligations. The answer determines not just who gets credited, but what kind of intellectual work a synthesis text is understood to be doing.

## Magus

The Forman-Dempsey exchange crystallizes around a question that is methodological before it is personal: does the term "metamodernism" designate a single intellectual tradition with identifiable precursors and a recoverable genealogy, or does it name a convergence zone where structurally analogous but genealogically independent discourses happen to meet? Forman's position presupposes the former. If metamodernism is fundamentally a post-postmodern developmental worldview, then Ken Wilber's integral framework — articulated systematically from the mid-1990s onward — constitutes prior art of the most consequential kind, and any synthesis that fails to foreground it is either negligent or strategically evasive. Dempsey's counter presupposes the latter. His project takes as its object a specific discourse community defined by explicit use of the term: Vermeulen and van den Akker's periodizing cultural logic, Storm's metamodern systematic philosophy, Freinacht's developmental-political synthesis. The analytical question is whether these heterogeneous deployments share a deep structural grammar. Within that frame, Wilber is not a source but at most a distal influence on one strand.

What makes this more than a citation dispute is its implication for the epistemology of synthetic scholarship. Forman effectively argues that the relevant unit of analysis is the conceptual territory — developmental post-postmodernism — and that anyone working that territory inherits obligations to its major cartographers regardless of terminological boundaries. Dempsey argues that the relevant unit is the discourse itself, bounded by its own self-naming practices, and that importing figures from adjacent traditions conflates the object of study with its intellectual neighborhood.

The deeper tension concerns interpretive authority: whether an author's explicit framing of their project's scope constitutes a binding constraint on what counts as adequate scholarly engagement, or whether readers and critics may legitimately reframe the project according to their own understanding of the field's topology and demand citations accordingly.
