# Game B's Core Diagnosis: Societies That Punish Honesty and Good Faith

A civilization that eats its own virtues

---

> The founding insight of Game B is that modern civilization has built incentive structures where honesty and good faith are punished — making them sucker strategies. Game B is the attempt to design a social operating system where prosocial behavior is structurally rewarded rather than exploited.

One of the foundational insights that launched the entire Game B project came from a single conversation between Jim Rutt and Jordan Greenhall around 2008. The question they were wrestling with was deceptively simple: what kind of society is one in which honesty and good faith are sucker strategies? The answer they arrived at was devastating — it's our society. This realization — that the basic prosocial behaviors that make human cooperation possible have been systematically punished by the incentive structures of late-stage Game A — became the generative seed of everything that followed. If you build a civilization where being honest and acting in good faith gets you outcompeted, exploited, or left behind, you have built something deeply pathological at the level of its operating system. The meta-crisis, the collapse of sensemaking, the rise of bad-faith information propagation — all of these downstream phenomena are symptoms of this root condition. Game B, then, is not primarily a utopian vision — it is a corrective response to a specific, diagnosable failure mode. The goal is to build a social operating system in which honesty and good faith are not sucker strategies but are instead load-bearing pillars — where the incentive structures reward rather than punish the behaviors that make genuine human flourishing possible. That single diagnostic insight — that we live in a society that punishes its own virtues — remains the moral and analytical core of the entire Game B project.

## Apprentice

Around 2008, Jim Rutt and Jordan Greenhall asked a simple but piercing question: what kind of society makes honesty and good faith into losing strategies? Their answer was unsettling — it's the one we already live in. If you play fair, act transparently, and trust others in many of today's competitive environments, you tend to get outmaneuvered by those willing to cut corners, manipulate, and deceive.

This isn't just a complaint about bad actors. The deeper point is that the rules of the game itself — the economic incentives, political structures, and social norms of modern civilization — systematically punish the very behaviors that make human cooperation work. When being a good person becomes a competitive disadvantage, something is broken at the most fundamental level.

This single realization became the seed of the Game B project. Game B isn't a utopian fantasy — it's a practical response to a specific diagnosis. The goal is to build new ways of organizing society where honesty, trust, and cooperation are rewarded by the system rather than exploited by it. Many of the crises we face today — misinformation, institutional collapse, eroding trust — are downstream symptoms of this root problem.

## Adept

The generative origin of Game B traces to a conversation between Jim Rutt and Jordan Greenhall circa 2008, centered on a deceptively simple diagnostic question: what kind of civilization structurally converts honesty and good faith into sucker strategies? The conclusion — that late-stage Game A does precisely this — became the analytical and moral foundation of the entire project. The insight is not merely that bad actors exist, but that the prevailing incentive architectures of modern civilization systematically select against prosocial behavior. Cooperation, transparency, and trust become competitive liabilities in environments where extraction, manipulation, and strategic opacity are rewarded.

This framing recontextualizes the meta-crisis as symptomatic rather than primary. The collapse of sensemaking, the proliferation of bad-faith information propagation, institutional decay, and the erosion of social trust are all downstream expressions of a root failure mode: a civilizational operating system that punishes its own load-bearing virtues. The pathology is structural, not incidental — embedded in the selection dynamics of markets, governance, media, and social coordination at scale.

Game B, understood through this lens, is not a utopian aspiration but a corrective engineering challenge. The objective is to design social operating systems in which the incentive gradients align with rather than oppose the prosocial behaviors necessary for genuine human flourishing. Honesty and good faith must become structurally advantageous — not through moralistic enforcement, but through the architecture of the coordination mechanisms themselves. This diagnostic specificity is what distinguishes Game B from generic calls for systemic change.

## Magus

The foundational diagnostic of Game B, crystallized in a 2008 exchange between Jim Rutt and Jordan Greenhall, operates at the level of civilizational selection dynamics: the incentive topography of late-stage Game A has inverted the fitness landscape such that honesty and good faith occupy local minima — they are, in game-theoretic terms, dominated strategies within the prevailing coordination regime. This is not a moral lament but a structural observation about how multi-polar traps, Molochian race-to-the-bottom dynamics, and extractive Nash equilibria have rendered prosociality systematically exploitable. The civilization punishes the very behavioral substrates upon which its coherence depends.

The explanatory power of this single insight is considerable. The meta-crisis — encompassing sensemaking collapse, epistemic corruption, institutional hollowing, and the weaponization of information — resolves into a set of predictable downstream pathologies once the root failure mode is identified. When the selection environment penalizes cooperative transparency and rewards adversarial opacity, every information commons degrades, every trust network frays, and every institution optimizes toward its own legitimacy rather than its stated function. The pathology is autoimmune: the system's optimization pressures attack its own integrative capacities.

Game B thus positions itself not as aspirational utopianism but as a precise engineering response to a diagnosed civilizational defect. The design objective is the construction of coordination architectures in which prosocial behavior — honesty, good faith, genuine reciprocity — constitutes an attractor state rather than a vulnerability. This requires not moral exhortation but incentive redesign at the level of the social operating system itself, such that the fitness landscape is restructured to make virtue load-bearing. The diagnostic remains the moral and analytical nucleus from which all subsequent Game B theorizing radiates.
