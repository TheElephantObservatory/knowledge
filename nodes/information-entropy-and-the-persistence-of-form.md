# Information, Entropy, and the Persistence of Form

A figure carved from the noise

---

> Information and entropy are the same thing measured from different angles. The more improbable a message, the more it tells you — and preserving that distinctiveness over time is what it means to exist.

Information and entropy are not merely analogous — they are structurally unified. The key insight is that improbability and information are deeply linked. Claude Shannon formalized this connection: the more improbable an outcome, the more informative it is. If you roll a die with six sides versus one with six hundred, the outcome of the six-hundred-sided die yields more bits of information, because the range of alternative outcomes was greater. Consider a missing coffee mug: being told it is not in the bathroom sink conveys almost nothing, because there are so many places it could be. But being told exactly where it is — that narrows the vast range of possible states to a single fact — and that is enormously informative. The information content of a message is therefore a function of how much uncertainty it resolves. This connects directly to Boltzmann's thermodynamic entropy: both measure the number of possible states a system could occupy. A system in a highly ordered, constrained configuration has low entropy and high informational richness. A disordered system has high entropy and low informational distinctiveness. From this unified perspective, maintaining informational integrity over time — what the Santa Fe Institute's David Krakauer calls an 'informational individual' — means preserving a figure-ground distinction between a system and its environment. Environments influence systems, systems influence environments, and each influences its own future states. The fidelity with which an entity preserves its informational distinction over time, against an entropic and fluctuating environment, is precisely what defines it as an enduring entity. Existence, at its most fundamental, is an informational achievement.

## Apprentice

When you learn where your missing coffee mug is, that single fact cuts through an enormous number of possibilities. It could have been anywhere — and now you know exactly where. That collapse from many possibilities to one is precisely what information is. Claude Shannon, who founded information theory, showed that the more unlikely an outcome, the more informative it is. A coin flip tells you less than a roll of a thousand-sided die, simply because the die had more possible outcomes to eliminate.

This turns out to be the same thing physicists mean by entropy. A tidy, ordered system — one that could only be arranged in a few ways — has low entropy and is informationally rich. A chaotic, disordered system has high entropy and tells you very little that is distinctive about it. Order and information are two names for the same underlying fact.

This leads to a striking idea: to exist as a persistent, recognisable thing — a cell, a mind, an identity — is to maintain informational distinctiveness against a world that is constantly pushing toward disorder. Researcher David Krakauer calls such a thing an 'informational individual.' Existence, on this view, is not just a physical achievement. It is an informational one.

## Adept

Claude Shannon's foundational insight was that the information content of a message is a function of its improbability: I(x) = -log₂P(x). A highly probable event, when it occurs, resolves little uncertainty and therefore carries little information. A rare event resolves a great deal. This is not merely a mathematical convenience — it reflects something deep about the relationship between possibility spaces and meaning. The more states a system could occupy, the more informative it is to learn which state it actually occupies.

This framework converges with Boltzmann's thermodynamic entropy, which also measures the number of microstates compatible with a given macrostate. High entropy corresponds to a large possibility space — many equivalent configurations — and therefore low informational distinctiveness. Low entropy corresponds to a constrained, improbable configuration, and therefore high information content. Shannon entropy and Boltzmann entropy are not merely analogous; they are structurally isomorphic, unified by the same underlying logic of possibility and constraint.

The implications extend to questions of identity and persistence. David Krakauer at the Santa Fe Institute has developed the concept of the 'informational individual': an entity that maintains a stable figure-ground distinction from its environment across time. Such an entity is not merely physically bounded but informationally coherent — its internal states are not simply reflections of environmental fluctuation but preserve a distinctive causal signature. Entropy, on this reading, is the adversary of individuation. To persist as a recognisable entity is to continuously resist the dissolution of informational structure into background noise.

## Magus

Shannon's entropy, H = -Σ p(x) log p(x), and Boltzmann's thermodynamic entropy, S = k log W, converge on the same formal object: a measure of the cardinality of a system's accessible state space. The isomorphism, made explicit by Jaynes through the maximum entropy principle, is not incidental. Both quantities index the degree to which a system's actual configuration is constrained relative to its phase space — the extent to which realised order departs from equiprobable disorder. Improbability and information are not merely correlated; they are definitionally co-extensive. To receive a message is to undergo a reduction in uncertainty, and the magnitude of that reduction is precisely the message's informational content.

This unification carries ontological weight. A system in a low-entropy, high-constraint configuration occupies a small region of its phase space — it is, in a precise sense, informationally dense. As entropy increases, the system's macrostate becomes compatible with an expanding set of microstates, and its distinctiveness — its departure from the background distribution — diminishes. The thermodynamic arrow of time is simultaneously an arrow of informational degradation. Maintaining improbable order against entropic diffusion is therefore not merely an energetic achievement but an informational one.

Krakauer's construct of the 'informational individual' formalises this intuition within a complex systems framework. An informational individual is defined by the preservation of a non-trivial mutual information between its past and future states — a causal autonomy that resists reduction to environmental forcing. The figure-ground distinction between system and environment is not a given but an achievement, sustained against continuous perturbation. Individuation, persistence, and identity are thus reframed as problems in information theory: existence is the ongoing maintenance of a low-entropy, high-distinctiveness configuration in a universe whose default trajectory runs in the opposite direction.
