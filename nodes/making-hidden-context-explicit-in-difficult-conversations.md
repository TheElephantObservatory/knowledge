# Making Hidden Context Explicit in Difficult Conversations

Speaking the same words in different worlds

---

> Most conversations fail not because people disagree on facts, but because they're operating in entirely different invisible realities. Learning to surface those hidden assumptions is the skill that makes genuine dialogue possible.

Conversation is a game — and most of us are playing it with the rules we absorbed as children, never having been taught that there are actually skills to develop, that there are better and worse ways to show up, that curiosity and impact and genuine understanding can be cultivated rather than just expressed instinctively. One of the most useful frames for understanding why conversations across divides break down is the distinction between content, context, and concern.

Content is the level at which we're explicitly speaking — the words, the arguments, the data. But underneath content is context: our beliefs about what is actually true, what we think we're in together, what reality is. Context is almost always invisible and almost always different between people. The classic example: two people go out for coffee. One thinks they're hanging out; the other thinks it's a date. They're having the same conversation at the content level, but they're in entirely different situations at the context level — different expectations, different emotional stakes, different interpretations of every gesture. The same dynamic plays out in polarized conversations about vaccination, politics, or anything else charged. If I think we're having a theoretical discussion to explore perspectives, and you think I'm trying to convince you to change your behavior, we will have radically different reactions to the exact same words. The conversation will feel threatening to you and baffling to me, and neither of us will understand why.

This is why simply exchanging more content — more facts, more arguments, more evidence — often makes polarized conversations worse rather than better. The disagreement isn't primarily at the content level. It's at the context level: what do we each think is true, what do we think this conversation is for, what are we each trying to get out of it? Making those things explicit — naming them, asking about them, sharing your own — is often the move that allows a real conversation to begin.

## Apprentice

Conversation is a skill, not just an instinct — and most people never get taught how to do it well. One of the most useful tools for understanding why difficult conversations go wrong is a simple distinction: the difference between what is being said (content) and the invisible background assumptions both people are carrying (context).

Context includes things like: what each person thinks is actually true, what they think the conversation is for, and what they expect to happen. It's almost always invisible, and almost always different between the two people talking. A classic example: two people meet for coffee. One thinks they're just hanging out; the other thinks it's a date. The words exchanged might be identical, but they're in completely different situations — different hopes, different stakes, different meanings attached to every small gesture.

The same thing happens in charged conversations about politics, health, or values. If one person thinks they're exploring ideas together and the other thinks they're being pressured to change, the same sentence will land completely differently. Adding more facts or arguments rarely helps — because the real gap isn't about information, it's about those hidden assumptions. The move that actually opens things up is making the invisible visible: naming what you think the conversation is, asking what the other person thinks it is, and building a shared reality before trying to exchange content.

## Adept

A productive framework for diagnosing conversational breakdown distinguishes between three layers: content, context, and concern. Content is the explicit, propositional surface of an exchange — the claims made, the evidence offered, the arguments advanced. Context is the tacit background: each participant's operative beliefs about what is true, what the conversation is for, and what relational or epistemic situation they take themselves to be in. Concern refers to the underlying interests and stakes each person brings, often unarticulated.

The insight is that most breakdowns in polarized or high-stakes conversations occur not at the content level but at the context level. Two interlocutors can be exchanging identical sentences while inhabiting entirely different conversational situations — one treating the exchange as collaborative inquiry, the other as a persuasion attempt or even an attack. Because context is almost never made explicit, each party interprets the other's content through a frame the other doesn't know is operating. This produces the characteristic phenomenology of such conversations: one person feels threatened or manipulated; the other feels baffled by a reaction that seems disproportionate.

The practical implication is counterintuitive: escalating the quantity or quality of content — more data, sharper arguments, better evidence — tends to deepen rather than resolve the impasse, because it leaves the contextual mismatch untouched. The intervention that actually shifts things is meta-communicative: surfacing and negotiating the context itself. This means naming one's own assumptions about what the conversation is for, genuinely inquiring into the other's, and establishing enough shared context that the content exchanged can be interpreted through a common frame.

## Magus

The tripartite distinction between content, context, and concern offers a rigorous diagnostic grammar for the pathologies of cross-divide discourse. Content operates at the level of explicit propositional exchange — the locutionary surface of speech acts. Context, by contrast, constitutes the tacit horizon of presuppositions within which utterances acquire their illocutionary force: each interlocutor's operative ontology, their model of the conversational frame, their assumptions about what speech situation they jointly inhabit. Concern names the motivational and relational substratum — the interests, vulnerabilities, and identity investments that give the exchange its affective charge.

The theoretical weight of this framework lies in its account of why information-deficit models of polarization systematically fail. Such models treat disagreement as a content-level phenomenon, amenable in principle to correction through better evidence or more rigorous argument. But where the breakdown is contextual — where participants are not merely asserting different propositions but inhabiting incommensurable conversational realities — additional content is not merely insufficient but actively counterproductive. It is processed through divergent frames, confirming each party's prior model of what the other is doing. The phenomenological asymmetry is characteristic: one participant experiences threat or manipulation; the other experiences inexplicable resistance. Neither has access to the frame-level divergence generating these reactions because context, by its nature, operates below the threshold of explicit attention.

The corrective move is therefore necessarily meta-communicative — a shift from object-level exchange to frame negotiation. This involves what might be called context disclosure: the explicit articulation of one's own operative assumptions about the conversational situation, paired with genuine inquiry into the other's. This is not merely a rhetorical technique but an epistemically prior condition: shared context is the precondition for content to be interpretable at all. Conversation, on this view, is not primarily an information-transfer protocol but a collaborative construction of a shared situation within which information transfer becomes possible.
