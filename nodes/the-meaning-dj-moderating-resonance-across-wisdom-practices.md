# The Meaning DJ: Moderating Resonance Across Wisdom Practices

In love with growth enough to weed

---

> Layman Pascal and John Vervaeke propose a new role for wisdom communities: a 'meaning DJ' whose job is not to teach or lead but to sense when different practices are enriching each other and when they're falling out of sync, adjusting the tempo accordingly.

Layman Pascal introduces a subtle but important functional role that does not appear explicitly in historical religious institutions but would be essential to any genuinely adaptive wisdom ecology: what he and Vervaeke together develop into the figure of the 'meta-artist moderator' or 'meaning DJ.' This is not a priest, not a theologian, not an administrator. It is someone whose primary competence is sensing resonance and dissonance across different registers of practice simultaneously — between the dialogical and the imaginal, between the theoretical and the aesthetic, between the slow tempo of contemplative practice and the fast tempo of cultural production. Their job is not to impose doctrine but to circulate between practices and report back: these two streams are pollinating each other beautifully, or these two are getting out of sync and need to slow down and find common ground. Vervaeke adds that the crucial qualification for this role is not authority but sensibility — specifically, an amateur's love of the process itself, a genuine aesthetic pleasure in the beauty of resonance and the discomfort of dissonance, pursued for its own sake rather than for institutional power. Pascal frames this as a two-directional regulatory function: the moderator must be able to slow things down when fragmentation threatens, but equally must be able to push things forward when conformity calcifies. Like a good gardener, they have to be in love with growth in order to weed well. This role, Pascal suggests, could also function across institutional boundaries — working with existing traditions like Sufi Islam or local Hindu communities, offering feedback not as an outside authority but as a trusted advisor who can see the system from outside while respecting its internal logic.

## Apprentice

Most religious and spiritual communities have familiar roles: the priest who leads rituals, the theologian who interprets texts, the administrator who keeps things running. But Layman Pascal, in conversation with John Vervaeke, identifies a role that none of these covers — someone whose job is to pay attention to how different practices within a community are relating to each other. Are the people doing contemplative work and the people doing creative projects feeding each other's insights, or are they drifting apart? Is the community getting so diverse it's fragmenting, or so unified it's becoming rigid?

Pascal calls this figure a 'meta-artist moderator' or 'meaning DJ.' The name captures something important: like a DJ, this person isn't composing the music — they're listening to what's already playing and sensing what needs to come next. Their authority doesn't come from rank or doctrine but from a genuine love of the process, what Vervaeke describes as an amateur's sensibility, someone who finds real pleasure in watching different streams of meaning come alive together.

Crucially, Pascal sees this role as working in two directions. Sometimes the moderator needs to slow things down and help fragmented efforts find common ground. Other times they need to push past comfortable consensus. And this function isn't limited to a single community — it could operate across traditions, offering respectful outside perspective to existing religious and spiritual groups.

## Adept

Layman Pascal and John Vervaeke collaboratively develop the concept of a 'meta-artist moderator' or 'meaning DJ' — a functional role with no clear precedent in traditional religious institutional structures. This figure operates across multiple registers of practice simultaneously: dialogical and imaginal, theoretical and aesthetic, contemplative and culturally productive. The moderator's core competence is not doctrinal authority but a refined capacity for sensing resonance and dissonance between these registers, and intervening accordingly. Pascal frames this as a bidirectional regulatory function: decelerating when fragmentation threatens coherence, accelerating when conformity suppresses emergence.

Vervaeke's contribution sharpens the qualification criteria. The person suited to this role must possess what he characterizes as sensibility rather than authority — specifically, the disposition of the amateur in its etymological sense, one who loves the process for its own sake. This aesthetic orientation toward resonance and dissonance, pursued without instrumental motivation, is what prevents the role from collapsing into yet another form of institutional gatekeeping. The moderator's legitimacy derives from demonstrated attunement, not positional power.

Pascal extends the concept beyond any single community or movement. The meaning DJ could function across institutional and traditional boundaries — engaging with Sufi orders, Hindu communities, or other established traditions not as an external authority imposing frameworks but as a trusted interlocutor who can perceive systemic dynamics from outside while honoring internal logics. The analogy Pascal offers is the gardener: one must genuinely love growth in order to weed well. Without that love, discernment becomes mere control.

## Magus

Pascal and Vervaeke's jointly articulated figure of the 'meta-artist moderator' addresses a structural lacuna in the institutional ecology of meaning-making: the absence of a role whose primary function is second-order attunement across heterogeneous registers of practice. This is not the hierophant, the exegete, or the administrator — it is a cybernetic function operating on the relational dynamics between practices rather than within any single practice. The moderator senses when dialogical inquiry and imaginal engagement are cross-pollinating productively, and when theoretical elaboration and aesthetic expression have fallen into phase-misalignment. Pascal's formulation is explicitly bidirectional and regulatory: the role demands both the capacity to introduce friction against premature convergence and the capacity to slow centrifugal fragmentation before it dissolves shared coherence.

Vervaeke's refinement of the qualifying disposition is philosophically precise. The moderator must embody sensibility rather than authority — an orientation he grounds in the amateur's eros, the love of the process as intrinsically rewarding. This is not mere preference but a structural requirement: instrumental motivation toward the role would inevitably reproduce the very doctrinal gatekeeping the function is designed to circumvent. Legitimacy here is performative and emergent, earned through demonstrated attunement rather than conferred by institutional position.

Pascal's most ambitious extension places this function at the boundary between wisdom ecologies and existing religious traditions. The meaning DJ operates as a trans-institutional interlocutor — engaging Sufi, Hindu, or other communities not through epistemic colonization but through what might be termed respectful exo-perception: the capacity to render visible systemic dynamics that are occluded from within, while maintaining genuine deference to the tradition's internal coherence. Pascal's gardening metaphor encodes a deep point — discernment without love degenerates into control, and pruning without investment in flourishing is merely destruction.
