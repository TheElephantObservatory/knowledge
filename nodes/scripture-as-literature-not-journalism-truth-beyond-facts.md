# Scripture as Literature, Not Journalism: Truth Beyond Facts

Madame Bovary never existed and yet she is real

---

> Scripture operates like great literature, not journalism — it doesn't aim at factual correspondence but at giving access to higher-dimensional patterns of reality that analytical frameworks cannot capture. Biblical literalism mistakes the category entirely.

There is a profound difference between the kind of truth that journalism attempts and the kind of truth that great literature achieves — and scripture belongs to the latter category, not the former. Journalism aims at a thin slice of factual correspondence: what happened, when, to whom. But anyone who has been the subject of journalism knows how radically it fails even at that modest goal.

Great literature does something categorically different. Madame Bovary was not a real person you could have touched with your finger, and yet there is something vastly more true about Madame Bovary than there is about any piece of journalism ever written. This is because the universe is extraordinarily high-dimensional — far beyond what our analytical frameworks can capture — and great literature, like great poetry and great music, provides access to those higher dimensions of reality that the left-brain analytical mode cannot reach.

Scripture, on this account, is doing what great literature does, but at a scale and across a range of cultural contexts that no ordinary literary work achieves. The stories of Genesis are not good journalism about ancient cosmology. They are not meant to compete with the standard model of cosmology. They are attempts — remarkably successful ones, given their reach across cultures and centuries — to give human beings access to the deeper patterns of reality in a form that can actually order a life. The test of scripture is not whether it corresponds to journalism, but whether it facilitates genuine relationship with the high-dimensional reality in which we actually live. Biblical literalism makes the mistake of treating scripture as if it were trying to be journalism and succeeding — when in fact it is doing something far more ambitious and far more important.

## Apprentice

There's a crucial difference between the truth a news report tries to deliver and the truth a great novel achieves. Journalism aims to tell you what happened, when, and to whom — a narrow slice of factual accuracy. Great literature does something else entirely. Madame Bovary never existed, yet she reveals something about human experience that no newspaper article ever could. The argument here is that reality is far richer and more complex than any set of facts can convey, and literature, poetry, and music give us access to dimensions of that reality we can't reach through analysis alone.

Scripture, on this view, belongs in the category of great literature — not journalism. The stories in Genesis aren't trying to be accurate reports about how the cosmos was physically assembled. They're trying to give people a way to connect with the deep patterns of existence, in a form powerful enough to actually shape how someone lives. And remarkably, they've done this across vastly different cultures and centuries.

Biblical literalism, then, makes a category error. It treats scripture as though it were attempting journalism and getting it right, when in fact scripture is doing something far more ambitious — offering access to the full richness of reality in a way that bare facts never could.

## Adept

This insight draws a sharp categorical distinction between journalistic truth and literary truth, then locates scripture firmly within the latter. Journalism pursues factual correspondence — thin, propositional accuracy about events. Yet even by its own standards, journalism routinely fails; anyone who has been its subject knows how much it distorts. Great literature operates on an entirely different axis. Flaubert's Madame Bovary never existed as a physical person, yet she discloses something about human reality that surpasses any factual report. The claim is that reality is extraordinarily high-dimensional — far exceeding what left-brain analytical frameworks can model — and that literature, poetry, and music function as modes of access to those dimensions that propositional language cannot reach.

Scripture, on this account, is performing the work of great literature but at civilizational scale and across an extraordinary range of cultural contexts. The Genesis narratives are not competing with the standard model of cosmology. They are structured attempts to render the deeper patterns of reality in forms capable of ordering human life. Their validation criterion is not factual correspondence but functional adequacy: do they facilitate genuine relationship with the high-dimensional reality in which human beings actually exist?

Biblical literalism thus commits a category error of the first order. It collapses the literary-scriptural mode into the journalistic mode, treating texts designed to open access to reality's deeper structure as though they were making empirical claims about surface-level events. This not only misreads scripture — it actively diminishes it, reducing an instrument of profound orientation to a set of propositions competing on terrain where they were never meant to stand.

## Magus

The distinction advanced here is not merely between genres but between epistemic modes with fundamentally different truth-conditions. Journalistic truth operates within a correspondence framework — propositional claims mapped onto discrete events in a low-dimensional factual space. Literary truth, by contrast, functions as what might be called a high-dimensional disclosure: Flaubert's Bovary, ontologically fictive, achieves a density of revelation about human reality that no journalistic account can approximate, precisely because the reality it discloses exceeds the dimensionality that propositional-analytic frameworks can represent. The implicit metaphysics here is striking — reality is taken to be of such extraordinary dimensionality that the left-hemisphere's analytic decomposition necessarily produces radical information loss, and certain cultural technologies (literature, poetry, music, ritual) serve as cognitive instruments for interfacing with what that decomposition discards.

Scripture is then positioned not as a primitive attempt at empirical description that modernity has superseded, but as the most ambitious and culturally durable instance of the literary-disclosive mode. The Genesis cosmogony does not occupy the same epistemic space as the standard model; it addresses orthogonal dimensions of the same reality. Its adequacy criterion is not correspondence but something closer to participatory adequacy — whether it facilitates genuine orientation within, and relationship to, the high-dimensional structure of existence across diverse cultural contexts and historical epochs. That it has demonstrably done so for millennia constitutes its strongest validation.

Biblical literalism, from this vantage, enacts a catastrophic category collapse. By insisting that scriptural authority depends on journalistic-empirical accuracy, it unwittingly subordinates a higher-order epistemic instrument to a lower-order one — demanding that a technology designed for multidimensional disclosure justify itself on the impoverished terms of propositional correspondence. The irony is that literalism, in attempting to defend scripture's truth, systematically destroys the very mode of truth-making that gives scripture its power.
