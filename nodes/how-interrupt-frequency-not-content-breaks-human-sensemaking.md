# How Interrupt Frequency, Not Content, Breaks Human Sensemaking

Set the slider to ten.

---

> What destroys human sensemaking is not bad content but the sheer number of attention interrupts per day. Once digital notifications push that count orders of magnitude beyond what our evolved neurology can handle, cognition doesn't just degrade — it breaks. The fix isn't better content; it's a radical reduction in interrupt frequency.

There is a hypothesis — or more precisely a conjecture — that deserves to be turned into a serious research program: what is destroying individual and collective sensemaking is not the content of our information environment, not the payload, but the sheer number of interrupts per day that we are receiving. Human beings evolved on the East African savannah processing perhaps tens to a low hundred attention-demanding signals per day. Agriculture didn't change that dramatically. But the modern digital environment — with its notifications, headlines, outrage triggers, dopamine hits, oxytocin manipulations, and algorithmic provocations — has pushed that number orders of magnitude higher. And once you cross some critical threshold, which likely varies by individual neurology, something goes seriously wrong in information processing. You don't just get distracted — you become functionally dysfunctional. The attention deficit disorder epidemic that exploded after Wired Magazine declared the attention economy in the mid-1990s is not a disease — it's an adaptive strategy to a world where someone is trying to interrupt and program you everywhere you look. Digital media compounds this because it is structurally staccato. A CD sounds different from an album not just in quality but in rhythm — it samples, it cuts, it quantizes. Every platform built on the digital medium seems to maximize the number of hits rather than any sense of continuity. The long read — what used to just be called an article — is now a marked category because sustained attention has become exotic. The solution is not better content. It is a radical reduction in interrupt frequency, which is why the most important tool someone could build right now is a personal information agent with a simple slider: how many inbounds do I want today? Set it to ten. Let the software do the curation. That is worth more than any content moderation system ever built.

## Apprentice

Human beings evolved in environments where only a handful of things demanded their attention each day — a rustling in the grass, a change in weather, a social signal from a neighbor. For most of history, that number stayed remarkably low. But the modern digital world has exploded it. Notifications, headlines, algorithmic provocations, and outrage triggers now hit us hundreds or thousands of times daily, each one demanding a micro-response from a brain never built for that load.

The key insight is that the problem isn't what those interruptions say — it's how many there are. Cross some threshold, and you don't just feel distracted; your ability to think clearly, make decisions, and understand the world starts to fall apart. The explosion of attention difficulties in recent decades may not be a disease at all, but a rational adaptation to an environment that is constantly trying to hijack your focus.

This reframing suggests a surprisingly simple direction for solutions. Instead of trying to improve the quality of information or moderate harmful content, the most powerful intervention would be to dramatically reduce the number of inbound signals a person receives each day. Imagine a personal tool with a single slider: how many interruptions do you want today? Set it to ten and let software handle the rest. That alone could do more for human thinking than any content policy ever written.

## Adept

This conjecture reframes the crisis of individual and collective sensemaking as fundamentally a problem of interrupt frequency rather than information quality. Human cognitive architecture evolved under conditions where attention-demanding signals numbered in the tens to low hundreds per day — a regime that agriculture barely altered. The digital attention economy, declared as such by Wired Magazine in the mid-1990s, has pushed daily interrupt counts up by orders of magnitude through notifications, dopamine-loop design, oxytocin-exploiting social signals, and algorithmic provocation engines. The hypothesis is that once a critical threshold is crossed — variable by individual neurology — information processing doesn't merely degrade; it becomes functionally dysfunctional.

The structural properties of digital media compound the problem. Digital encoding is inherently staccato: it samples, quantizes, and cuts. Platforms built on this substrate tend to maximize discrete hits rather than sustain continuity of attention. The emergence of "the long read" as a marked category is itself diagnostic — sustained attention has become so rare it requires its own label. Meanwhile, the attention deficit disorder epidemic maps suspiciously well onto the timeline of digital media proliferation, suggesting it may be less a clinical pathology than an adaptive response to an environment engineered to interrupt and reprogram.

The intervention that follows from this analysis is not content moderation, media literacy, or algorithmic transparency — it is radical interrupt reduction. The most consequential tool imaginable would be a personal information agent governed by a single parameter: how many inbound signals does the user want per day? Set the number low, delegate curation to software, and restore the cognitive conditions under which human sensemaking actually functions. This is worth more than any content policy architecture yet devised.

## Magus

The conjecture advanced here is that the collapse of sensemaking — individual and collective — is best modeled not as a content-quality problem but as a signal-interrupt frequency problem operating against fixed evolutionary constraints. Homo sapiens cognitive architecture was calibrated on the East African savannah for an interrupt regime of perhaps tens to a low hundred attention-demanding events per day. The Neolithic transition barely perturbed this parameter. The digital attention economy, however, has driven it up by orders of magnitude, exploiting dopaminergic reward loops, oxytocinergic social bonding mechanisms, and amygdala-mediated threat detection through algorithmically optimized provocation. The hypothesis posits a phase transition: beyond some individually variable neurological threshold, information processing does not degrade linearly but undergoes qualitative breakdown — a shift from distraction to functional dysregulation.

Digital media's material substrate reinforces this dynamic structurally. Digitization is inherently discontinuous — it samples, quantizes, and reconstitutes in discrete packets. Platforms built atop this medium converge on architectures that maximize discrete engagement events rather than sustaining attentional continuity. The cultural emergence of "the long read" as a marked genre is a revealing symptom: what was once simply writing now requires taxonomic distinction because sustained cognition has become the exception. The post-1995 explosion of attention deficit diagnoses, read through this lens, appears less as epidemiological discovery than as mass adaptive recalibration to an environment saturated with programmatic interrupts.

The prescriptive implication is stark and specific. Neither content moderation, epistemic hygiene, nor algorithmic transparency addresses the operative variable. What is needed is an engineered reduction in interrupt frequency — a personal information agent parameterized by a single scalar: desired daily inbound count. Set to ten, with machine curation handling triage, such a tool would restore the cognitive preconditions for coherent thought. This intervention targets the actual causal mechanism and is therefore worth more than the entire content-governance apparatus currently under construction.
