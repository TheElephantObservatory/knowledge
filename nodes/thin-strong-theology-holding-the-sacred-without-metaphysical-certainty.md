# Thin Strong Theology: Holding the Sacred Without Metaphysical Certainty

The seam between myth and cosmos, still open

---

> Theology is the discipline most needed for our moment and most trapped in outdated metaphysics. John Schellenberg's distinction between thick and thin models of the sacred suggests a path: a flexible, non-theistic theology that holds ultimate questions open rather than settled.

One of the structural problems facing any attempt to regenerate a living theology adequate to our moment is that the discipline we most need — theology — is also the discipline most bound to what John Vervaeke calls the Two Worlds mythology and to a substantialist metaphysics in traditional theism. The philosopher John Schellenberg offers a useful framework with his distinction between thick strong and thin strong models of the sacred. A thick strong model carries enormous metaphysical freight that is increasingly untenable. Schellenberg argues for a thin strong orientation: a minimal, flexible, creative metaphysics that is nevertheless genuinely oriented toward the sacred as ultimately real, ultimately transformative, and ultimately normative. What this points toward is a post-theistic, or more precisely non-theistic, theology — not pantheism, but a thin strong orientation that can hold theism and non-theism as open questions rather than settled answers. The polydox theology being developed by thinkers like Catherine Keller, Sharon Betcher, and Roland Faber gestures in this direction: a theology that walks the seam between the mythopoetic and the scientific, and that begins to enact a thought-space adequate to the complexity of our moment. The open question is whether theology as an institution can undergo the active self-transformation required to do this — and the answer depends heavily on the physical, mental, emotional, and communal conditions of the people doing the work.

## Apprentice

There's a paradox at the heart of trying to build a living spiritual framework for today: the field best equipped to do this work — theology — is also the one most weighed down by old assumptions about reality. Traditional theology tends to assume a very specific picture of God and the universe, one that increasingly clashes with what we know from science and philosophy.

Philosopher John Schellenberg offers a helpful way forward. He distinguishes between "thick" approaches to the sacred — loaded with heavy, specific claims about what ultimate reality must be — and "thin" approaches that stay genuinely oriented toward something sacred and transformative while remaining flexible and open. The idea isn't to abandon depth or seriousness, but to stop pretending we've already figured out the biggest questions.

This points toward a theology that doesn't require you to be a traditional theist or an atheist — one that can hold both possibilities as live questions. Thinkers like Catherine Keller, Sharon Betcher, and Roland Faber are already working in this direction, building approaches that walk the line between poetic imagination and scientific rigor. But whether theology as an established institution can actually transform itself this way is an open question — one that depends not just on ideas, but on the real human conditions of the people doing the thinking.

## Adept

A central structural problem confronts any attempt to develop a theology adequate to contemporary conditions: theology itself remains deeply bound to what John Vervaeke identifies as the Two Worlds mythology and to the substantialist metaphysics of traditional theism. The very discipline most needed for the work of meaning-making and sacred orientation is the one most entangled with frameworks that have become increasingly untenable under philosophical and scientific scrutiny.

John Schellenberg's distinction between thick strong and thin strong models of the sacred provides a crucial lever here. A thick strong model — traditional theism with its full metaphysical architecture — carries commitments that are difficult to sustain. Schellenberg advocates instead for a thin strong orientation: minimal and flexible in its metaphysical claims, yet genuinely committed to the sacred as ultimately real, ultimately transformative, and ultimately normative. This is not pantheism or vague spirituality; it is a disciplined openness that holds theism and non-theism as live questions rather than foregone conclusions.

The polydox theology emerging from thinkers like Catherine Keller, Sharon Betcher, and Roland Faber gestures toward exactly this kind of thought-space — one that walks the seam between the mythopoetic and the scientific, refusing to collapse into either register. The deeper question is whether theology as an institutional discipline can undergo the radical self-transformation this demands. That capacity for transformation is not merely intellectual; it depends heavily on the physical, emotional, mental, and communal conditions of the people engaged in the work — conditions that institutional theology has rarely prioritized.

## Magus

The recursive difficulty facing any project of theological regeneration is that theology — the discipline most structurally positioned to address questions of ultimate orientation — remains the discipline most deeply captive to what Vervaeke diagnoses as the Two Worlds mythology and to the substantialist metaphysics underwriting classical theism. The organ of renewal is itself the site of the deepest entrenchment.

Schellenberg's distinction between thick strong and thin strong models of the sacred offers precise analytical traction on this impasse. The thick strong model — theism as metaphysically maximalist commitment — carries epistemic and ontological freight that collapses under its own weight when subjected to serious philosophical pressure. Schellenberg's thin strong alternative preserves genuine sacred orientation — the sacred as ultimately real, ultimately transformative, ultimately normative — while radically minimizing metaphysical commitment, enabling a creative and revisable engagement with ultimacy. This is not the move toward pantheism or apophatic retreat but toward a post-theistic (more precisely, non-theistic) theological posture that suspends the theism/non-theism binary as an open question rather than a resolved foundation. The polydox theology of Keller, Betcher, and Faber enacts something adjacent to this: a theology operating at the seam of mythopoetic and scientific registers, refusing premature closure in either direction.

The genuinely open question is institutional and somatic, not merely conceptual. Whether theology can undergo the active self-transformation required — moving from settled doctrinal architecture to a living, thin strong orientation — depends on conditions that are irreducibly embodied: the physical, emotional, mental, and communal ecology of the practitioners themselves. The transformation of theology presupposes the transformation of theologians, and that is a project with demands no purely intellectual framework can satisfy.
