# Meaning as the Physics of Persistence

Shannon's information theory is fundamentally incomplete because it deals only with the accurate transmission of information — its quantity and fidelity — while remaining entirely silent on whether that information actually matters to anything. Complexity theorists Artemy Kolchinsky and David Wolpert have filled this gap by distinguishing between syntactic information — Shannon's statistical correlations between systems — and semantic information: the syntactic information a physical system has about its environment which is causally necessary for the system to maintain its own existence. Information becomes meaningful to an entity to the degree that it helps that entity resist entropic dissolution. All physical systems face entropy; to persist, a system must extract free energy from its environment, and to do that effectively, it needs accurate and relevant information about that environment. Kolchinsky and Wolpert even identify a connection between semantic efficiency — attending to the right information — and thermodynamic efficiency. This framework is fully generalizable: it applies not just to living organisms but to any physical system whatsoever. Crucially, this yields a profound philosophical insight: if meaning is defined as information causally necessary for an entity's continued existence, then the very fact that anything exists at all entails that meaning exists for it. To be is to have meaning. The persistence of any entity through time is itself evidence that meaningful information has been successfully processed. This generates a natural evolutionary logic: entities more effective at attending to meaningful information will be more viable and evolutionarily successful, creating a feedback cycle in which complexity demands more energy and information-processing capacity, which drives further complexification. What emerges is a picture of cosmic evolution as a single great learning process, in which the accumulation of meaningful knowledge is not incidental but is the very driving logic of how things persist, diversify, and grow in complexity across all levels of emergence.

## Apprentice

Most theories of information only ask: did the message get through clearly? But they never ask: did it matter? Complexity theorists Artemy Kolchinsky and David Wolpert noticed this gap and proposed something remarkable. Information becomes genuinely meaningful to something when it helps that thing survive. Every physical system — a cell, an animal, even a simple chemical structure — faces the constant threat of falling apart. To keep existing, a system needs to pull useful energy from its surroundings. And to do that well, it needs accurate, relevant information about those surroundings. That is what meaning is, in the most fundamental sense: information that helps something persist. This leads to a striking philosophical conclusion. If meaning is defined as information necessary for continued existence, then anything that exists at all already has meaning — because its very persistence proves that some useful information has been successfully used. To exist is to have meaning. Furthermore, systems that are better at attending to the right information tend to survive longer and grow more complex. This creates a kind of cosmic learning process: the universe, over time, accumulates entities that are increasingly good at knowing what matters. Complexity and meaning grow together.

## Adept

Shannon's information theory is a masterpiece of abstraction precisely because it brackets meaning entirely — treating information as a purely statistical quantity divorced from any semantic content. This was scientifically productive but philosophically incomplete. Kolchinsky and Wolpert address this lacuna by introducing a rigorous distinction between syntactic information — Shannon's measure of statistical correlation between two systems — and semantic information, defined as the syntactic information a physical system possesses about its environment that is causally necessary for the system to maintain its own far-from-equilibrium state. The grounding move here is thermodynamic. All physical systems are subject to entropic degradation; persistence requires the continuous extraction of free energy from the environment. Effective free-energy extraction depends on possessing accurate, relevant models of that environment. Information is meaningful to a system precisely to the degree it enables this thermodynamic work. Kolchinsky and Wolpert further identify a correspondence between semantic efficiency — attending to causally relevant environmental variables — and thermodynamic efficiency, giving the framework empirical teeth. The philosophical yield is considerable. If meaning is constitutively tied to causal necessity for existence, then the bare fact of any system's persistence through time is sufficient to establish that meaningful information processing has occurred. Existence entails meaning; there is no ontological free lunch. This generates a natural selectional logic: systems more adept at extracting and processing semantically relevant information will be thermodynamically more viable, driving an evolutionary feedback between complexity, energy demand, and information-processing capacity. The result is a framework in which cosmic complexification is not merely contingent but reflects the deep logic of what it takes for anything to endure.

## Magus

The Kolchinsky-Wolpert framework intervenes at the precise point where Shannon's formalism, by design, goes silent: the question of relevance. Shannon's entropy measure, H, quantifies the reduction of uncertainty across a channel without reference to any receiver's interests or survival conditions — a deliberate abstraction that enabled communication engineering but foreclosed questions of meaning. Kolchinsky and Wolpert reopen these questions by anchoring semantic information to a system's causal-physical situation. Semantic information is defined as that subset of syntactic mutual information between a system S and its environment E which is causally necessary for S to maintain its characteristic non-equilibrium steady state — its viability. The grounding is explicitly thermodynamic and draws on the Jarzynski equality and related non-equilibrium statistical mechanics: persistence requires free-energy extraction; free-energy extraction is optimized by accurate environmental modeling; therefore, the informational states that track causally relevant environmental variables are, in a precise sense, the meaningful ones. The framework recovers a connection between semantic efficiency and thermodynamic efficiency, suggesting that the two are not merely analogous but structurally coupled. Philosophically, the move is consequential. By defining meaning as causal necessity for continued existence rather than as intentionality or representation, Kolchinsky and Wolpert naturalize semantics without eliminativism. The implication — that existence is jointly sufficient and necessary for the presence of meaning — dissolves the traditional hard problem of how meaning enters a purely physical world: it was never absent. Every persisting system is, by definition, a locus of semantic processing. This yields an evolutionary epistemology of considerable scope: selectional pressures favor systems with higher semantic efficiency, coupling complexification to the accumulation of world-knowledge in a manner that renders the arrow of cosmic evolution and the arrow of increasing meaningful information co-extensive. The universe learns because only learning systems endure.
