# Jim Rutt's Case Against Both Political Parties

A plague on both your houses, carefully itemized.

---

> Jim Rutt constructs a disciplined double-hater framework, cataloguing specific failures of both Democrats and Republicans in parallel — not as false equivalence, but as a precondition for honest political judgment that refuses to let one side's failures excuse the other's.

Jim Rutt offers a structured double-hater framework — a deliberate refusal to collapse into either partisan identity — and walks through his specific grievances with both parties in parallel. Against the Democrats: oikophobia and the indoctrination of young people against Enlightenment values, rising antisemitism traceable to campus culture, unrealistic climate timelines disconnected from engineering reality, excessive government spending, and what he sees as a kind of medievalist sympathy in the party's tolerance for Hamas. Against the Republicans: the creeping imposition of Christian nationalism and the erosion of church-state separation that Jefferson and Madison built into the Virginia Statute of Religious Freedom before the Constitution itself; cultural control of individual behavior through abortion restrictions, drug wars, and end-of-life policy; anti-free-speech tendencies of a different flavor than the left's — book banning, mandated medical speech; climate denialism that is, in his view, straightforwardly dishonest among educated Republican figures who know better; and fiscal irresponsibility that is actually worse than the Democrats' because it combines increased spending with tax cuts, making Republicans the primary drivers of deficit expansion since 2000. The framework is not bothsidesism — Rutt is explicit that he finds the double-hatred genuinely difficult to navigate and that it produces real uncertainty about how to vote. The value of the framework is that it refuses the tribal logic that one team's failures excuse the other's, and insists that an honest accounting of both is the precondition for any serious political judgment.

## Apprentice

Jim Rutt describes himself as a "double hater" — someone who has serious, specific problems with both major American political parties and refuses to pick a team. Against the Democrats, he points to a hostility toward traditional Western values, rising antisemitism rooted in campus culture, climate goals that ignore engineering reality, excessive spending, and a troubling tolerance for extremism. Against the Republicans, he identifies the push to blend Christianity with government, restrictions on personal freedom around abortion and drugs, their own brand of anti-free-speech behavior like book banning, dishonest climate denial, and fiscal irresponsibility that actually outpaces the Democrats' through the combination of tax cuts and spending increases.

This isn't a shrug of "both sides are bad" and a retreat into apathy. Rutt is clear that holding both sets of grievances at once is genuinely uncomfortable and creates real uncertainty about how to vote. The point is that honest people should be able to name what's broken on both sides without tribal loyalty forcing them to excuse their own team's failures.

The framework's value lies in its insistence that serious political thinking begins only after you've done a full accounting of both parties' problems — not as a performance of neutrality, but as a discipline of intellectual honesty.

## Adept

Jim Rutt articulates what he calls a double-hater framework: a structured, parallel indictment of both American parties that resists the gravitational pull of partisan identity. His Democratic grievances include oikophobia — a culturally self-negating hostility toward Enlightenment values — rising antisemitism traceable to academic culture, climate policy timelines disconnected from engineering feasibility, fiscal excess, and what he characterizes as medievalist sympathy in the party's posture toward Hamas. His Republican grievances are equally specific: Christian nationalism eroding the church-state separation codified in the Virginia Statute of Religious Freedom before the Constitution existed; state control of individual behavior through abortion restrictions, drug prohibition, and end-of-life policy; anti-free-speech tendencies distinct from but parallel to the left's, including book banning and compelled medical speech; climate denialism he regards as knowingly dishonest among educated party figures; and fiscal irresponsibility that surpasses the Democrats', since Republican governance since 2000 has combined spending increases with tax cuts to become the primary engine of deficit expansion.

Crucially, Rutt distinguishes this framework from bothsidesism. He does not claim the failures are equivalent or symmetrical, nor does he use one party's problems to minimize the other's. Instead, he insists that the double hatred produces genuine decisional difficulty — real uncertainty about how to vote that cannot be resolved by tribal affiliation.

The framework's intellectual contribution is procedural rather than substantive: it argues that honest political judgment requires a complete parallel accounting of both parties' failures as a precondition, not a conclusion. The refusal to let partisan logic convert one side's pathologies into the other side's vindication is itself the discipline being proposed.

## Magus

Jim Rutt's double-hater framework operates as a deliberate epistemic discipline against what might be called partisan modus tollens — the inferential habit whereby one party's failures are treated as sufficient warrant for the other's legitimacy. His indictment of the Democrats identifies oikophobia as a cultural-civilizational posture that has metastasized through educational institutions, corroding Enlightenment commitments from within; he traces rising antisemitism to the same campus epistemology, flags climate policy timelines as engineering-illiterate despite their moral urgency, and characterizes the party's tolerance for Hamas as a species of medievalist sympathy incompatible with liberal universalism. His Republican indictment is no less granular: Christian nationalism as a direct violation of the Jeffersonian-Madisonian settlement codified in the Virginia Statute of Religious Freedom — a pre-Constitutional commitment to disestablishment; behavioral regulation through abortion restriction, drug prohibition, and end-of-life control as exercises of cultural rather than constitutional authority; anti-free-speech tendencies orthogonal to the left's but equally corrosive, manifesting as book banning and compelled medical speech; climate denialism he regards as epistemically dishonest rather than merely mistaken among educated Republican elites; and a fiscal record since 2000 that combines spending expansion with revenue reduction, making Republicans the structurally dominant contributors to deficit growth.

The framework's sophistication lies in its refusal of the symmetry thesis that typically accompanies centrist positioning. Rutt does not claim equivalence; he claims that the asymmetries run in different directions on different issues, producing a genuinely irreducible decisional problem rather than a comfortable middle ground.

What emerges is less a political position than a meta-political commitment: the insistence that tribal epistemology — in which one's own coalition's failures are perpetually discounted against the other's — is the primary obstacle to serious judgment, and that only a complete parallel ledger, honestly maintained, can serve as the foundation for political reasoning worthy of the name.
