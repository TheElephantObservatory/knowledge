# A Triadic Framework for Organizing the Fragmented Field of Psychology

When everything is called the same name

---

> Psychology is incoherent because its subdisciplines presuppose fundamentally different ontologies of mind. Peirce's categories of Firstness, Secondness, and Thirdness provide a principled metaphysical foundation for organizing the field into domains of Transcendence, Ensoulment, and Intelligence — making their relationships visible rather than accidental.

When you look at the field of psychology as a philosopher, you want to tear your hair out — it is simply a mess. You have people doing archetypal dream analysis sitting in the same department as people figuring out how the eyeball relates to the visual cortex, alongside comparative psychologists studying monkey mating behavior and researchers inducing altered states with DMT. These are all called psychology, and yet each of them presupposes a fundamentally different implicit ontology about what the mind is and what counts as evidence about it. This incoherence does not exist in physics or biology, where what you are studying and what you are doing is relatively clear.

The resolution begins with a foundational metaphysics. Charles Sanders Peirce — probably one of the most important philosophers ever to live, the founder of American pragmatism and the first non-dual evolutionary cosmology in genuine conversation with post-Darwinian science — divided the universe categorically into what he called Firstness, Secondness, and Thirdness. These are not arbitrary divisions; they are mathematical, syntactic, logical, grammatical categories built into the very structure of language and the nature of semiotics. They map onto what integral theory later called I, We, and It.

From this foundation, you can organize the entire field of psychology principally. The psychologies primarily concerned with first-person phenomenological experience belong in the domain of Transcendence. The psychologies concerned with interpersonal relationship, the internalization of linguistic schemes, the imaginal landscape of personality — psychodynamics, analytical psychology, archetypal psychology — belong in the domain of Ensoulment. And the psychologies concerned with objective truth, instrumental capacity, and the organism's ability to control and predict its environment — cognitive science, cognitive neuroscience, developmental psychology — belong in the domain of Intelligence or Development. This is not a reduction; it is a principled map that makes the relationships between these fields visible and allows them to correct each other rather than talk past each other.

## Apprentice

Psychology is a strangely disorganized field. Dream analysts, neuroscientists, primate researchers, and psychedelic investigators all share the same department, yet they disagree — often without realizing it — about what the mind actually is and what counts as real evidence about it. Physics and biology don't have this problem. Psychology does because it never settled its foundational questions.

The philosopher Charles Sanders Peirce, founder of American pragmatism, offered a way through. He identified three fundamental categories woven into the structure of reality itself: raw immediate experience, brute interaction with the world, and the patterns of meaning that connect things together. These aren't arbitrary labels — they reflect deep features of logic, language, and how signs work.

Applied to psychology, these categories sort the chaos into three coherent domains. Psychologies focused on direct inner experience — meditation, altered states, contemplative practice — belong to a domain of Transcendence. Psychologies focused on personality, imagination, relationship, and the stories we internalize — like depth psychology and archetypal work — belong to a domain of Ensoulment. And psychologies focused on measurable capacities, brain function, and how organisms develop and adapt belong to a domain of Intelligence. This isn't about ranking them. It's about finally seeing how they relate to each other, so they can have a real conversation instead of talking past one another.

## Adept

The field of psychology suffers from a unique structural incoherence: its subdisciplines operate from fundamentally incompatible implicit ontologies about what mind is and what constitutes valid evidence about it. Archetypal dream analysis, computational neuroscience, comparative ethology, and psychedelic phenomenology coexist under one disciplinary umbrella without any shared metaphysical ground. This is not mere methodological pluralism — it is an unacknowledged crisis of foundations that other sciences resolved long ago.

Charles Sanders Peirce's triadic categories offer a principled resolution. Firstness (qualitative immediacy), Secondness (dyadic brute interaction), and Thirdness (triadic mediation and law) are not arbitrary philosophical impositions but structural features of semiotics, logic, and mathematics. They correspond closely to what integral theory later mapped as I, We, and It — the irreducible perspectival domains of any coherent account of reality.

Applied as an organizing framework, these categories yield three principled domains of psychology. Transcendence encompasses first-person phenomenological psychologies — contemplative, transpersonal, and altered-state research — grounded in Firstness. Ensoulment encompasses the psychologies of interpersonal meaning-making, internalized linguistic schemes, and the imaginal landscape — psychodynamic, analytical, and archetypal traditions — grounded in Secondness. Intelligence or Development encompasses the objective, third-person psychologies — cognitive science, neuroscience, developmental psychology — grounded in Thirdness. The power of this map is not reductive but relational: it makes visible why these fields diverge, where they legitimately differ, and how they might correct and enrich each other rather than perpetually talking past one another.

## Magus

Psychology's disciplinary incoherence is not a sociological accident but a symptom of unresolved metaphysical foundations. Subdisciplines presupposing radically divergent ontologies of mind — phenomenological, relational-hermeneutic, and naturalistic-mechanistic — cohabit institutionally without any principled account of their mutual relations. The result is not productive pluralism but systematic equivocation: the same term, "psychology," names inquiries whose objects, methods, and evidential standards are incommensurable absent a metatheoretical framework capable of grounding their differences categorically rather than merely taxonomically.

Peirce's phaneroscopic categories furnish precisely this ground. Firstness, Secondness, and Thirdness — the monadic quality of immediate feeling, the dyadic facticity of brute encounter, and the triadic generality of law-governed mediation — are not empirical generalizations but formal-structural features of semiosis itself, derivable from the mathematics of relational logic. Their isomorphism with the perspectival domains later articulated in integral metatheory (first-person, second-person, third-person) is not coincidental; both frameworks converge on the irreducibility of phenomenal immediacy, intersubjective meaning-constitution, and objective regularity as distinct but interpenetrating dimensions of any adequate ontology.

The resulting organization is tripartite and non-reductive. Transcendence names the domain of Firstness-grounded psychologies: contemplative phenomenology, transpersonal inquiry, and the investigation of non-ordinary states qua qualitative immediacy. Ensoulment names the Secondness-grounded domain: psychodynamics, analytical and archetypal psychology, and the study of personality as internalized dialogical structure within an imaginal-linguistic field. Intelligence or Development names the Thirdness-grounded domain: cognitive science, neuroscience, and developmental psychology as investigations of lawful regularity and adaptive capacity. What this triadic architecture enables is not merely classification but genuine dialectical correction — each domain's pathologies become visible from the standpoint of the others, and the field's fragmentation yields to a coherent, if irreducibly complex, unity.
