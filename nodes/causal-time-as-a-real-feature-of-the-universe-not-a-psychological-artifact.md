# Causal Time as a Real Feature of the Universe, Not a Psychological Artifact

DNA remembers every second it took to arrive.

---

> Jim Rutt argues that causal time — the real, irreversible sequence in which things come into existence — is not a psychological illusion but an objective feature of reality, and that emergence itself proves the block universe wrong because complex things like DNA are literally records of elapsed causal history.

Jim Rutt proposes that causal time — the idea that A happened before B, which happened before C, and that this sequence is real and not illusory — may need to be added as a fourth axiomatic commitment to his minimum viable metaphysics. This is a direct challenge to the 'block universe' view that has dominated physics for decades, in which past, present, and future all exist simultaneously and the experience of temporal flow is a psychological artifact. Rutt argues that the block universe is undermined by emergence itself: you cannot get molybdenum before you have stellar nucleosynthesis, and you cannot get life before you have molybdenum. The causal sequence is not optional — it is constitutive of what these things are. He cites the work of Sara Walker and Lee Cronin on Assembly Theory as particularly compelling: their framework allows you to look at any molecule and calculate approximately how far from the origin of the universe it must be, because complex molecules like DNA imply billions of years of prior causal history. A molecule of that complexity cannot exist in a block universe where all times are equally present — it is, in a deep sense, a record of elapsed causal time. This argument reframes temporal sequence not as a subjective experience to be explained away but as an objective feature of reality that any adequate metaphysics must accommodate. The arrow of time is not an illusion; it is the precondition for anything interesting existing at all.

## Apprentice

Most of modern physics treats time as something of an illusion. In the so-called "block universe" view, past, present, and future all exist at once, like frames of a film laid out on a table. The feeling that time flows is just a trick of human psychology. Jim Rutt pushes back hard against this idea, arguing that causal sequence — the fact that A must happen before B, which must happen before C — is a real and irreducible feature of the world.

His argument is elegantly simple: you can't have life before you have the right chemical elements, and you can't have those elements before stars forge them. This ordering isn't optional or merely apparent. It's built into what these things are. A molecule like DNA doesn't just happen to exist after billions of years of cosmic history — it couldn't exist without that history. It is, in a very real sense, a physical record of all the causal steps that had to come before it.

Rutt draws on the work of scientists Sara Walker and Lee Cronin, whose Assembly Theory provides a way to measure how much causal history a molecule encodes. Their framework suggests that the complexity of a molecule tells you roughly how far from the beginning of the universe it must be. This reframes the arrow of time not as something to explain away, but as the very precondition for a universe that contains anything interesting at all.

## Adept

Jim Rutt has proposed elevating causal time to the status of a foundational axiom within his minimum viable metaphysics — a framework that seeks the smallest set of commitments necessary to do serious ontological work. This move directly challenges the block universe interpretation that has dominated theoretical physics since Minkowski and Einstein, in which all moments exist tenselessly and the experience of temporal flow is epiphenomenal. Rutt's counter-argument is grounded in emergence: the causal sequence that produces stellar nucleosynthesis before heavy elements, and heavy elements before biological complexity, is not merely descriptive but constitutive. These entities are defined by their causal ancestry.

The argument gains particular force through reference to Assembly Theory, developed by Sara Walker and Lee Cronin. Their framework quantifies the minimum number of joining operations required to construct a given molecule, effectively encoding how much causal history that molecule presupposes. A molecule with high assembly index — DNA being a paradigmatic example — implies billions of years of prior physical and chemical processes. It cannot be adequately described in a framework where all times are ontologically co-present, because its very identity is inseparable from the sequential process that produced it.

This perspective reframes the arrow of time from an explanatory problem to an explanatory resource. Rather than treating temporal asymmetry as a puzzle to be dissolved by fundamental physics, Rutt treats it as an objective structural feature of reality — one without which emergence, complexity, and ultimately anything worth explaining would be impossible. The arrow of time is not a residual illusion awaiting reduction; it is the precondition for ontological novelty.

## Magus

Jim Rutt's proposal to install causal time as a fourth axiomatic commitment within his minimum viable metaphysics represents a pointed intervention against the tenseless ontology of the block universe — the Parmenidean inheritance that has shaped relativistic physics since Minkowski's geometrization of spacetime. The block universe renders temporal passage epiphenomenal: all events are equally real across the four-dimensional manifold, and the distinction between past, present, and future is a perspectival artifact. Rutt's objection is not phenomenological but structural. Emergence, he argues, is irreducibly sequential. Stellar nucleosynthesis must precede the existence of molybdenum; molybdenum must precede the biochemistry it enables. This ordering is not supervenient on a deeper timeless reality — it is constitutive of the entities in question.

The argument finds its sharpest empirical anchor in Walker and Cronin's Assembly Theory, which assigns to any molecular species an assembly index representing the minimum number of recursive joining operations required for its construction from elementary building blocks. High-index molecules are not merely complex; they are temporally deep. A molecule like DNA encodes in its very structure an implicit record of the causal depth of the universe at the point of its emergence. Assembly Theory thus provides a metric that renders causal history physically measurable, collapsing the supposed gap between objective physics and the experienced arrow of time.

What Rutt achieves with this move is a reversal of the standard explanatory burden. The block universe asks: why does time seem to flow? Rutt's framework asks instead: how could anything with ontological novelty exist without irreversible causal sequence? Temporal asymmetry ceases to be a puzzle requiring dissolution and becomes a necessary condition for the existence of structured complexity — the sine qua non of an interesting universe.
