# Why Logical Minds Cannot Grow: Fodor's Challenge and the Organismic Response

The seed was never a smaller tree.

---

> Fodor argued that genuinely new cognitive capacities cannot emerge from weaker ones through any logical process, making qualitative development impossible. The resolution lies not in better logic but in replacing the computational ontology altogether with an organismic framework where transformation is what living systems inherently do.

Fodor's specificity of learning argument is one of the most powerful philosophical challenges to qualitative development. If the mind is essentially a logical structure, then changing its competence means moving from a weaker logic to a stronger one. But there is no logical way to manipulate a weaker logic and arrive at a stronger one — the stronger system is simply not reachable from within the weaker one. So if qualitative development means acquiring genuinely new cognitive functions, it is computationally impossible. Fodor concluded that either qualitative development is an illusion, or everything is already present innately. The response came from dynamical systems theory and neural networks: when you model the mind as a dynamically self-organizing system rather than a logical program, the distinction between function and development collapses. A self-organizing system functions by developing and develops by functioning. This is actually what Piaget always meant, though it took neural network models of the 1990s to make it formally tractable. Fodor's argument was not really about psychology — it was about ontology. The deeper problem with developmental psychology, and with modern science broadly, is a flatland ontology of disconnected entities governed by mechanical or logical rules, in which transformation becomes literally inconceivable. Consider a seed becoming a tree, or a caterpillar becoming a butterfly — these are the same questions that make human psychological transformation seem paradoxical. What the organismic paradigm offers instead is a framework where relationship is primary, temporal process is real, and the self is inextricably tied through metabolic relationship and co-evolution with its environment. In such a framework, transformation is not a mystery — it is what living systems do. The computational paradigm can be transcended and included within this broader organismic view, but not the reverse. And once you get the ontology right, normative questions about what kinds of transformations are good can be asked properly rather than smuggled in as unexamined cultural assumptions.

## Apprentice

The philosopher Jerry Fodor posed a devastating challenge to the idea that people can undergo real psychological growth. His argument goes like this: if the mind works like a logic system, then developing a new ability means upgrading from a weaker logic to a stronger one. But you cannot get to a stronger logic using only the tools of the weaker one — it is like trying to build a ladder to the moon using only the rungs you already have. So either development is fake, or everything was already built in from birth.

The breakthrough came from thinking about the mind not as a computer program but as a living, self-organizing system — more like a growing organism than a calculating machine. In a self-organizing system, there is no hard line between how it works and how it changes. It grows by working and works by growing. This is actually what the developmental psychologist Piaget was getting at all along, though it took new mathematical tools to make the idea rigorous.

The deeper lesson is that Fodor's puzzle was never really about psychology alone. It was about how we picture reality. If you imagine the world as a collection of separate parts following fixed rules, then genuine transformation — a seed becoming a tree, a caterpillar becoming a butterfly, a person becoming wiser — looks impossible. An organismic worldview, where relationships and processes come first, makes transformation not mysterious but natural.

## Adept

Fodor's specificity of learning argument remains one of the sharpest philosophical objections to qualitative cognitive development. If mental competence is modeled as a formal logical system, then developmental change requires transitioning from a weaker logic to a stronger one. But no sequence of operations within the weaker system can generate the stronger system — the target is computationally unreachable from the starting point. Fodor's conclusion was stark: either qualitative development is illusory, or all cognitive structures are innately specified.

The response from dynamical systems theory and connectionist modeling reframed the problem entirely. When cognition is modeled as a self-organizing dynamical process rather than a symbolic-logical program, the rigid distinction between function and development dissolves. A self-organizing system functions by developing and develops by functioning — precisely the insight Piaget articulated philosophically but could not formalize. Neural network models of the 1990s demonstrated that genuinely novel computational properties can emerge through the dynamics of learning without requiring pre-specification in a stronger formal language.

The critical recognition, however, is that Fodor's argument was fundamentally ontological, not merely psychological. It exposed the deeper commitments of a mechanistic worldview in which reality consists of discrete entities governed by fixed rules — a "flatland ontology" where genuine transformation is literally inconceivable. The organismic alternative posits relationship as primary, temporal process as irreducible, and organism-environment co-constitution as foundational. Within this framework, transformation — from seed to tree, caterpillar to butterfly, or novice to expert — is not paradoxical but constitutive of what living systems are. The computational paradigm can be transcended and included within this broader view, and once the ontological ground is corrected, normative developmental questions can be posed explicitly rather than smuggled in as unexamined assumptions.

## Magus

Fodor's specificity of learning argument functions as a reductio against qualitative development within any framework committed to characterizing mental competence as a formal logical system. The argument's force is straightforward and devastating: transition from a weaker logic L₁ to a stronger logic L₂ cannot be achieved through any procedure expressible in L₁, since L₂ by definition contains theorems not derivable within L₁. Development thus becomes either epiphenomenal redescription of quantitative change or evidence of nativist pre-specification. What makes the argument so instructive is precisely what it reveals about its own presuppositions.

The dynamical systems and connectionist response — anticipated philosophically by Piaget's genetic epistemology but requiring the formal apparatus of attractor dynamics and distributed representation to become tractable — dissolves the problem by rejecting its framing. In a self-organizing system, competence is not a static logical structure but a dynamical regime; developmental transition corresponds to phase-space reorganization rather than logical derivation. Function and development become co-constitutive: the system functions by developing and develops by functioning. This is not a workaround but a category shift — from the syntactic manipulation of discrete symbols to the continuous self-organization of coupled processes.

The deepest implication, however, is ontological. Fodor's argument is irrefutable within a substance ontology of disconnected entities governed by extrinsic rules — the same ontology that renders metamorphosis, morphogenesis, and genuine psychological transformation equally inconceivable. The organismic alternative, rooted in process-relational metaphysics, treats relationship as constitutive, temporality as irreducible, and organism-environment co-evolution as the primary unit of analysis. Transformation becomes not anomalous but paradigmatic of living organization. Crucially, the computational paradigm is asymmetrically related to this broader framework: it can be transcended and included within the organismic view, but not vice versa. And with the ontological correction comes an epistemic one — normative developmental questions, previously occluded by mechanistic commitments, become available for explicit inquiry.
