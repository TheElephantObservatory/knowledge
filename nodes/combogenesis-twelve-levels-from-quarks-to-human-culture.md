# Combogenesis: Twelve Levels From Quarks to Human Culture

The universe keeps making things that make things.

---

> Tyler Volk's combogenesis proposes that the universe builds complexity through roughly 12 discrete levels — from quarks to culture — via a single repeating logic: things in relations produce new things with new relations, and this pattern is structural, not metaphorical.

Tyler Volk's concept of 'combogenesis' describes the process by which things in relations build new things that are larger and have new relations — his attempt to count the steps from the standard model of particle physics all the way up to human culture, arriving at roughly 12 defensible levels: from quarks to atoms, molecules, prokaryotic cells, eukaryotic cells, multicellular organisms, animals with mentality, and eventually human cultural systems. The key insight is that at each transition, the relations are not merely physical or biological — they are maximally general. A mind having an idea it wants to develop tomorrow is a relation with the universe in the same structural sense as a wasp fertilizing a fig tree. What makes combogenesis powerful as a framework is that it refuses to privilege any one scale. The same logic of 'things in relations producing new things with new relations' operates whether you are describing the origin of the carbon cycle, the emergence of multicellular life, or the formation of cultural agreement systems. This is not a metaphor — Volk argues it is a structural feature of how complexity actually accumulates across cosmic history.

## Apprentice

Tyler Volk noticed something striking: the universe seems to build complexity the same way at every scale. Quarks combine to make protons. Atoms combine to make molecules. Simple cells combine to make complex cells. At each step, things enter into relationships and produce something new — something bigger, with its own new set of relationships. He calls this process combogenesis, and he counts roughly twelve of these major transitions from the smallest particles of physics all the way up to human culture.

What makes this idea genuinely surprising is that Volk insists the pattern isn't just a loose analogy. A wasp pollinating a fig tree and a person planning tomorrow's work are, in his framework, the same kind of event — things in relation generating something new. The logic doesn't change just because one example is biological and the other is mental. The universe doesn't play favorites with scale.

This means that the emergence of cultural systems — languages, institutions, shared agreements — follows the same deep structural pattern as the emergence of atoms from subatomic particles. Combogenesis offers a single lens for understanding how complexity accumulates across all of cosmic history, without reducing any level to the one below it.

## Adept

Tyler Volk's combogenesis is an attempt to formalize the recursive structure of emergent complexity across cosmic history. The core claim is deceptively simple: things enter into relations and produce new things at a higher level, which then enter into their own relations. Volk traces this logic through approximately twelve defensible transitions — from quarks to hadrons, atoms to molecules, prokaryotes to eukaryotes, single cells to multicellular organisms, organisms with neural mentality to human cultural systems. Each level is not merely an aggregation of the prior one but constitutes a genuinely new domain of relational possibility.

What distinguishes combogenesis from other complexity frameworks — major evolutionary transitions theory, for instance — is its insistence on maximal generality. Volk refuses to privilege biological or physical scales. The relation between a wasp and a fig tree is structurally equivalent, in his framework, to the relation between a mind and a future intention. Both are instances of the same combinatorial logic producing novel systemic wholes. This is presented not as metaphor but as ontological claim: the universe accumulates complexity through a single iterated mechanism.

This has significant implications for how we understand culture. If human agreement systems, institutions, and symbolic orders are the twelfth level of combogenesis, they are not epiphenomenal or merely emergent-in-the-weak-sense. They are the latest expression of the same structural process that generated atoms and cells. Volk's framework thus offers a non-reductive materialism that takes every scale of organization seriously on its own terms while binding them into a unified developmental logic.

## Magus

Tyler Volk's combogenesis constitutes a sustained argument that complexity accumulation across cosmic history is governed by a single iterated operation: entities in relation generate higher-order entities possessing novel relational capacities. Volk identifies approximately twelve such transitions — from quarks through hadrons, atoms, molecules, prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells, multicellular organisms, animals with neural mentality, and finally human cultural systems — each representing not mere aggregation but the emergence of a qualitatively distinct relational domain. The framework's ambition is to subsume the major evolutionary transitions literature, thermodynamic accounts of self-organization, and hierarchical complexity theory under one parsimonious generative logic.

The decisive philosophical move is Volk's refusal to grant ontological privilege to any particular scale. Relations are maximally general: a mutualistic symbiosis between hymenopteran pollinator and angiosperm host is structurally isomorphic, within the framework, to a cognitive agent's anticipatory relation to a future state. This is not analogical reasoning but a claim about the deep grammar of combinatorial emergence. Combogenesis thereby positions itself against both eliminative reductionism and strong emergentism — each level is fully real and causally efficacious, yet each is generated by the same trans-scalar operation.

The implications for understanding human cultural formations are particularly consequential. If symbolic systems, normative institutions, and collective epistemic structures represent the current frontier of combogenesis, then culture is not a supervening gloss on biological substrates but the latest instantiation of the universe's fundamental complexification logic. Volk's framework demands that we treat the carbon cycle and the social contract as products of the same structural process — differing in substrate and timescale but identical in generative form. This positions combogenesis as a rare attempt at genuine ontological unification without explanatory collapse.
