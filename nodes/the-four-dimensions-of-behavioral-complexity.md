# The Four Dimensions of Behavioral Complexity

From gravity's pull to the spoken word

---

> Not all behavior is the same kind of thing. A dead cat falling, a sleeping cat breathing, and a waking cat landing on its feet are three fundamentally different phenomena — and psychology has confused them for over a century.

In the UTOK framework, behavior is not a single unified phenomenon but a nested hierarchy of qualitatively distinct types, each corresponding to a different dimension of complexity in nature. To illustrate this, consider three cats dropped from a tree. The first is dead: it falls and bounces purely as a material object, governed by physics. The second is anesthetized: outwardly it also obeys gravity, but internally its metabolism, organ coordination, and living processes continue — this is living behavior, the functional self-organization of biological systems. The third is alive and awake: it rotates mid-air, lands on its feet, and runs — this is animal mental behavior, the whole-organism navigation of an environment. And we can imagine a fourth, impossible cat that lands and says, 'Why did you drop me?' — that would be person behavior, the domain of culture, language, and self-reflective agency. What this framework accomplishes is a precise answer to a question that has haunted psychology since Skinner: what exactly counts as behavior? The answer is that behavior is entity-field change across dimensions of complexity — material, living, mental, and cultural — and that psychology specifically is the science of mental behavior, not behavior in general. This seemingly simple clarification dissolves enormous amounts of conceptual confusion that has plagued the discipline for over a century.

## Apprentice

Imagine dropping three cats from a tree. The first is dead: it falls like a rock, obeying gravity. The second is asleep under anesthesia: it also falls, but inside, its heart beats, its cells do their work — it's alive, even if it can't act. The third is awake: it twists in midair, lands on its feet, and bolts. These aren't just different degrees of the same thing. They are genuinely different kinds of behavior.

This is the core of a framework developed by psychologist Gregg Henriques. He argues that 'behavior' isn't one unified thing but a nested set of layers: matter moving through physics, living systems self-organizing, animals navigating their environments, and people using language and culture to reflect on their own existence. Each layer is real and distinct.

The payoff is a long-overdue answer to a question psychology has never cleanly resolved: what exactly is psychology the science of? The answer this framework gives is precise — psychology is the science of mental behavior, meaning the whole-organism navigation of an environment by animals. Not all behavior, not just inner experience, not just observable action. That single clarification untangles decades of disciplinary confusion.

## Adept

Within the Unified Theory of Knowledge (UTOK) framework developed by Gregg Henriques, behavior is reconceptualized not as a single natural kind but as a family of qualitatively distinct phenomena organized across what Henriques calls the 'Tree of Knowledge' — the nested dimensional hierarchy of matter, life, mind, and culture. The three-cats thought experiment makes this concrete: a dead cat falling instantiates material behavior (physics); an anesthetized cat instantiates living behavior (biological self-organization); a conscious cat righting itself mid-fall instantiates animal mental behavior (whole-organism environmental navigation); and a hypothetical speaking cat would instantiate person behavior (language-mediated, culturally embedded agency).

The theoretical move here is to define behavior formally as 'entity-field change' — the relationship between a system and the field it operates within — and to recognize that this relationship takes on categorically different characters at each level of complexity. Psychology, on this account, is not the science of behavior in general but specifically the science of mental behavior: the domain where nervous systems generate adaptive, environment-directed action.

This has significant disciplinary consequences. The long-running conflict between behaviorism, cognitive science, and phenomenology can be partially attributed to a failure to distinguish these levels — each tradition was, in effect, studying a real phenomenon while mistaking it for the whole. UTOK's taxonomy doesn't merely organize existing knowledge; it dissolves pseudo-problems that arose from category confusion, offering psychology a principled boundary that neither Skinner's radical behaviorism nor its cognitivist successors managed to establish.

## Magus

The UTOK framework's treatment of behavior represents a substantive contribution to the meta-theoretical foundations of psychology, one that engages directly with the demarcation problem that has destabilized the discipline since its inception. Henriques operationalizes behavior through the concept of 'entity-field change,' a relational ontology in which the relevant unit of analysis is not the organism in isolation but the organism-environment system — a move consonant with Gibsonian ecological psychology and Dewey's transactionalism, but embedded within a broader dimensional ontology derived from the Tree of Knowledge system.

The four-level taxonomy — material, living, mental, and person behavior — is not merely classificatory but ontologically graded. Each level exhibits emergent properties irreducible to the one below: living behavior introduces teleonomic self-organization absent from purely physical systems; mental behavior introduces nervous-system-mediated, whole-organism environmental navigation irreducible to metabolic function; person behavior introduces the semiotic and normative dimensions of language and culture, constituting a fourth dimension of complexity. The thought experiment of the three (and four) cats functions as a phenomenological reduction, isolating each behavioral register by controlling for the others.

The disciplinary implication is that psychology's chronic identity crisis — its oscillation between eliminative behaviorism, mentalistic cognitivism, and biological reductionism — is a predictable consequence of failing to specify which level of behavioral complexity constitutes its proper object domain. UTOK's answer is unambiguous: psychology is the science of mental behavior, the third register in the hierarchy. This demarcation is neither arbitrary nor merely pragmatic; it tracks a real discontinuity in nature, one that aligns with the emergence of nervous systems as a novel mode of organism-environment coupling. The framework thus offers not a paradigm shift within psychology but a meta-theoretical foundation from which its paradigm wars become, in retrospect, intelligible.
