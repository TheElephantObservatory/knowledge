# Embodiment as Existential Consent to One's Actual Life

Half in the spirit world, half arriving

---

> Embodiment is not a technique but an existential commitment — the ongoing act of agreeing to be fully here, as this person, in this body, with this life. Many spiritual orientations paradoxically arise from a refusal of that very descent into incarnate existence.

There is a deep ambivalence at the heart of embodiment that goes beyond physical sensation or somatic practice. Embodiment, as Geoff Fitch describes it, is fundamentally the act of agreeing to be here — not just agreeing to existence in the abstract, but agreeing to existence as this particular person, in this particular body, in this particular historical moment, with this specific karma, these relationships, these cultural pressures, and these civilizational challenges. Many people, especially those drawn to spiritual practice, carry an ambivalent relationship to being here at all. And paradoxically, some spiritual orientations can themselves emerge from a trauma of not being fully embodied — arriving half in the spirit world and half in the human world. The gift of that position is real: it brings a sensitivity to what lies beyond ordinary experience. But the work is to become fully incarnate, to complete the path of descent into life. This reframes embodiment not as a technique or a practice modality but as an existential commitment — a kind of ongoing consent to the full weight and texture of one's actual life. The body is not merely a vehicle for awareness; it is the only medium through which we know anything about this world at all.

## Apprentice

Most people think of embodiment as something like body awareness — feeling your feet on the ground, noticing your breath. But Geoff Fitch points to something much deeper: embodiment is the act of agreeing to be here. Not just existing in general, but consenting to be this specific person, with this body, this history, these relationships, and all the difficulty that comes with them.

Many people, especially those drawn to spiritual life, carry a quiet ambivalence about being here at all. Some spiritual sensitivities may actually grow from that very reluctance — from living half in the world and half somewhere else. That in-between position brings real gifts, like a feeling for what lies beyond ordinary experience. But it can also mean someone has never fully arrived in their own life.

The invitation, then, is not to add a new practice but to make a deeper commitment: to complete the descent into being fully human. The body isn't just a tool for awareness. It's the only way any of us actually know anything about this world.

## Adept

Geoff Fitch reframes embodiment not as a somatic modality or mindfulness technique but as an existential act — the ongoing agreement to be fully incarnate as this particular person, in this particular body, carrying this specific karma, these relationships, and these civilizational pressures. This framing shifts the conversation from sensation and interoception to something more fundamental: the question of whether one has actually consented to being here at all.

This perspective identifies a deep ambivalence that runs through many spiritual communities. Some orientations toward the transcendent may themselves be expressions of incomplete incarnation — a kind of developmental position in which the person remains partially in the spirit world, never having fully descended into human life. The gifts of that liminal position are real: heightened sensitivity, access to non-ordinary states, a felt sense of dimensions beyond the material. But the shadow is a subtle refusal of the density, limitation, and particularity that define embodied existence.

The work, then, is not ascent but descent — completing the arc of incarnation by agreeing to the full weight and texture of one's actual life. The body is repositioned not as a vehicle for consciousness but as the irreducible medium through which anything about this world can be known. Embodiment becomes less a practice one does and more a commitment one makes, continuously, to the radical specificity of being alive.

## Magus

Geoff Fitch articulates a phenomenology of incarnation that disrupts the standard framing of embodiment as somatic technique or interoceptive refinement. In his account, embodiment names an existential act of consent — the agreement to be here as this particular being, bearing this specific karmic inheritance, these relational entanglements, these cultural and civilizational pressures. The emphasis falls not on the body-as-felt but on the body-as-commitment: a continuous ratification of one's descent into the density and limitation of a singular human life.

This reframing carries significant implications for understanding spiritual vocation. Fitch suggests that certain orientations toward transcendence may themselves be symptomatic of incomplete incarnation — a developmental position in which the person remains partially unborn, suspended between the spirit world and the human world. The phenomenological gifts of this liminality are genuine: permeability to non-ordinary dimensions, sensitivity to subtle fields, a native fluency with what exceeds the empirical. But these gifts coexist with, and may compensate for, a fundamental ambivalence about the act of being here — an ambivalence that spiritual practice can reinforce rather than resolve when it privileges ascent over descent.

The corrective movement is not toward more body awareness but toward what might be called ontological consent: the full assumption of incarnate particularity as the ground of all knowing. The body is not a vehicle for awareness but the sole epistemic medium through which world-contact occurs. Embodiment, so understood, is neither practice nor state but an ongoing existential wager — the choice to inhabit the irreducible thickness of one's actual situation rather than hovering above it in the name of liberation.
