# Choosing a Metaphysics Without Proof: Jim Rutt's Pragmatic Ethics

Strong convictions, loosely grounded.

---

> Jim Rutt proposes a 'minimum viable metaphysics' — a set of foundational beliefs chosen not because they can be proven true, but because they work. Strong values held with radical honesty about their groundlessness offer a coherent path between dogma and nihilism.

Jim Rutt articulates what he calls a 'minimum viable metaphysics' — a set of commitments chosen not because they can be proven, but because they are useful for living. He is explicit that he cannot prove any of his metaphysical claims. The universe could be five seconds old, created with memories intact, fossils in the ground, and planes in the air — the so-called Boltzmann brain hypothesis — and he cannot disprove it. Given this radical epistemic humility, the question becomes: on what basis do we choose a metaphysics at all? His answer is pragmatic utility. You choose the commitments that allow you to navigate life effectively. This is not relativism — he holds that some outcomes are genuinely better than others, evaluated against values he holds firmly: human flourishing, ecological richness, and preserving humanity's path through the civilizational bottleneck. But he is honest that those values themselves cannot be proven to be correct. They are the ones he happens to hold and believes to be right. The architecture here is important: strong values, held with epistemic humility about their ultimate grounding, evaluated pragmatically for their consequences. This is a coherent alternative to both dogmatic religion and nihilistic relativism — a naturalist ethics that acknowledges its own contingency without collapsing into 'anything goes.'

## Apprentice

Jim Rutt openly admits he can't prove any of his deepest beliefs about reality. The universe might be five seconds old, complete with fake memories and fossils already in the ground, and there's no way to definitively rule that out. Rather than letting this uncertainty paralyze him, he asks a different question: if we can't prove our starting assumptions, which ones should we pick?

His answer is surprisingly practical — choose the beliefs that help you live well. He calls this a 'minimum viable metaphysics,' borrowing startup language to describe the leanest possible set of commitments you need to navigate life effectively. This isn't the same as saying all beliefs are equally valid. Rutt holds firm values — human flourishing, ecological health, guiding civilization through its current dangers — and judges his metaphysical choices by how well they serve those goals.

What makes this approach distinctive is its honesty. Rutt doesn't pretend his values are written into the fabric of the universe. He acknowledges they're the ones he holds and believes to be right, without claiming cosmic proof. The result is a way of living with deep conviction and deep humility at the same time — avoiding both the rigidity of dogma and the paralysis of believing nothing matters.

## Adept

Jim Rutt's concept of a 'minimum viable metaphysics' represents a deliberate philosophical architecture: a pragmatist foundation for ethics and worldview that foregrounds epistemic humility without surrendering normative commitment. Rutt is explicit that none of his metaphysical premises — realism, materialism, the reliability of memory and evidence — can be proven. He invokes the Boltzmann brain hypothesis not as a serious cosmological proposal but as a limit case demonstrating the impossibility of foundational certainty. The universe could have sprung into existence moments ago, fully furnished with apparent history, and no empirical test could distinguish this from genuine deep time.

Given this irreducible uncertainty, Rutt's move is pragmatic rather than foundational. Metaphysical commitments are selected on the basis of their utility for navigating life and advancing specific values: human flourishing, ecological richness, and safe passage through what he frames as a civilizational bottleneck. This is recognizably Jamesian pragmatism applied to existential and ethical questions — truth as operational adequacy rather than correspondence.

Critically, this framework resists collapse into relativism. Rutt maintains that some outcomes are genuinely better than others, evaluated against his stated values. But he is transparent that those values themselves lack ultimate grounding — they are contingent commitments held with conviction. The resulting structure is a naturalist ethics that acknowledges its own contingency while maintaining evaluative force. It positions itself as a coherent third path between dogmatic metaphysical realism and the nihilistic conclusion that without proof, no commitment is warranted.

## Magus

Jim Rutt's 'minimum viable metaphysics' performs a precise philosophical operation: it decouples normative commitment from epistemic foundationalism while preserving the action-guiding force of both. The gesture toward the Boltzmann brain scenario functions not as cosmological speculation but as a reductio against any claim to metaphysical certainty — an acknowledgment that the underdetermination problem extends all the way down. No observation can distinguish a universe with genuine causal history from one instantiated moments ago with all evidential traces intact. Rutt treats this not as a crisis but as a boundary condition for honest philosophy.

The pragmatist turn that follows is carefully calibrated. Metaphysical commitments — naturalism, temporal realism, the causal closure of the physical — are adopted not as truth-claims but as instrumental selections optimized against explicitly stated values: human flourishing, ecological complexity, civilizational persistence. This echoes James's will to believe but with a distinctly consequentialist evaluation criterion. The 'minimum viable' framing signals parsimony: carry only the ontological commitments that do real work in guiding action and evaluating outcomes. Anything beyond that is metaphysical excess.

What distinguishes this architecture from garden-variety pragmatism is its recursive honesty. Rutt does not exempt his values from the same epistemic modesty he applies to his metaphysics. Flourishing, ecological richness, civilizational survival — these are held as genuine commitments, not derived from first principles. The contingency is acknowledged without being treated as delegitimizing. This produces a stable configuration: strong normative force without foundationalist pretense, conviction without dogma, and a naturalist ethics that can account for its own genealogy without self-undermining. It is, in effect, a post-foundationalist virtue ethics operating on pragmatist rails.
