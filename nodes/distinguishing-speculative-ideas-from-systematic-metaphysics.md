# Distinguishing Speculative Ideas from Systematic Metaphysics

Carving nature at its joints

---

> The word 'metaphysics' means two opposite things — the deepest possible inquiry into reality, and empty speculation about nothing. Resolving that ambiguity unlocks a rigorous, science-compatible discipline for mapping the fundamental structure of what exists.

Metaphysics suffers from a fatal ambiguity that must be resolved before it can be useful. On one side, it means the most fundamental and important inquiry possible — the study of the basic structure of reality, what exists, how things relate, how we know. On the other side, it has become a term of dismissal, meaning speculation untethered from experience — angels on pinheads, supernatural claims, things beyond any possible empirical contact. This split is not merely semantic; it reflects a genuine fork in how one can orient toward foundational questions. The resolution proposed here is to introduce two qualifiers: 'pure' metaphysics, which refers to claims genuinely outside of possible experience and therefore unverifiable, and 'descriptive systematic' metaphysics, which refers to the careful work of defining concepts, specifying what they refer to in the world, and building coherent inter-relations among them. Descriptive systematic metaphysics is not speculation — it is the disciplined act of what Plato called carving nature at its joints. It asks: what are our fundamental categories, how do they relate, and how do they connect to what we can actually observe and investigate? This distinction matters enormously because it rescues metaphysics from both its inflated mystical reputation and its deflated dismissal by scientists. The tree of knowledge system is offered as exactly this kind of descriptive metaphysical system — a cosmology that specifies an ontology of energy, matter, life, mind, and culture, mapped onto the epistemological processes that gave rise to the physical, biological, psychological, and social sciences. Metaphysics, properly understood, is not the enemy of science. It is the framework within which science becomes intelligible.

## Apprentice

The word 'metaphysics' has a serious identity problem. To some people, it means the most important kind of thinking possible — asking what reality is made of, what exists, how things connect. To others, it's a term of mockery, meaning fuzzy speculation about angels, spirits, and things nobody could ever check. These two meanings pull in completely opposite directions, and the confusion causes real damage.

The fix is to split the word into two distinct ideas. 'Pure' metaphysics covers claims that are genuinely beyond any possible experience — things that can never be tested or observed in any way. 'Descriptive systematic' metaphysics is something entirely different: the careful, disciplined work of defining your basic categories, being precise about what they refer to in the real world, and showing how they fit together coherently. This is what Plato meant by carving nature at its joints.

This second kind of metaphysics isn't the enemy of science — it's the map that makes science legible. Every scientific field rests on assumptions about what kinds of things exist and how they relate. Making those assumptions explicit and coherent is not speculation; it's intellectual housekeeping of the highest order. A system that maps energy, matter, life, mind, and culture onto the sciences that study them is doing exactly this kind of work.

## Adept

Metaphysics occupies a peculiar position in contemporary intellectual life: simultaneously indispensable and disreputable. The term carries two irreconcilable connotations — on one reading, it names the foundational inquiry into ontology, the structure of reality, and the conditions of intelligibility; on another, it signals untethered speculation, claims immune to empirical contact, the paradigm case of meaningless discourse. Logical positivism weaponized the second reading, but even its critics rarely rehabilitated the first with sufficient precision.

The proposed resolution introduces a principled distinction. 'Pure' metaphysics designates claims that fall genuinely outside possible experience — neither verifiable nor falsifiable by any conceivable empirical procedure. 'Descriptive systematic' metaphysics, by contrast, designates the disciplined construction of conceptual frameworks: specifying ontological categories, clarifying their referents, and articulating their inter-relations in ways that remain answerable to observation and scientific practice. This is Strawson's project of descriptive metaphysics extended and made explicit — not the revision of common-sense ontology, but its systematic clarification and grounding.

The practical stakes are high. Every scientific discipline presupposes an ontology — physics assumes fields and particles, biology assumes organisms and functions, psychology assumes mental states, sociology assumes norms and institutions. Descriptive systematic metaphysics makes these presuppositions explicit, tests their coherence, and maps their relations. A cosmological system that organizes energy, matter, life, mind, and culture into a unified ontological hierarchy — correlated with the epistemological traditions of the natural, biological, psychological, and social sciences — exemplifies this approach. Metaphysics so construed is not prior to science in a foundationalist sense; it is continuous with it.

## Magus

The semantic bifurcation of 'metaphysics' is not merely a lexical inconvenience but a symptom of unresolved tension in post-Kantian philosophy. The term inherits two incompatible genealogies: the Aristotelian project of first philosophy, concerned with being qua being, substance, causation, and the categories of existence; and the Enlightenment critique, sharpened by Hume and systematized by the Vienna Circle, which recast metaphysical discourse as cognitively vacuous — pseudo-propositions failing the verification criterion. The result is a term that simultaneously names the most rigorous foundational inquiry and serves as a term of epistemic opprobrium.

The disambiguation proposed here draws a principled boundary between 'pure' metaphysics — claims structurally insulated from empirical contact, neither confirmable nor disconfirmable by any possible observation — and 'descriptive systematic' metaphysics, which undertakes the Platonic task of diaeresis: carving the joints of nature through disciplined conceptual analysis, ontological specification, and the construction of coherent categorial frameworks answerable to scientific practice. This distinction has precedent in Strawson's revisionary/descriptive divide, but extends it by explicitly linking descriptive metaphysics to the epistemological architecture of the sciences rather than to the analysis of natural language.

The philosophical payoff is substantial. Quine's naturalized ontology and Sellars's synoptic vision both gesture toward a metaphysics continuous with science rather than prior to it; the present framework operationalizes that continuity. By mapping an ontological hierarchy — energy, matter, life, mind, culture — onto the disciplinary matrix of the physical, biological, psychological, and social sciences, descriptive systematic metaphysics functions as a metatheoretical integrator: not legislating to the sciences, but rendering their presuppositions explicit, their inter-relations tractable, and their collective output intelligible as a unified account of what there is.
