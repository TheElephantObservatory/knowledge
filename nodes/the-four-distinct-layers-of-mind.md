# The Four Distinct Layers of Mind

A tower of Babel in the skull

---

> The word 'mind' secretly refers to four different things, which is why philosophers and scientists have argued about it for centuries without resolution. A precise tripartite framework finally gives each meaning its proper address.

One of the deepest sources of confusion in psychology and philosophy of mind is that the word 'mind' is actually being used to point at four genuinely different phenomena simultaneously, and most debates about the nature of mind are really just people talking past each other because they are anchored to different referents. The first referent is mind as behavior — not simply the behaviorist attempt to eliminate mind, but a redefinition of mind as what the body does, as Skinner articulated: 'the mind is what the body does, it is what the person does.' The second referent is mind as neuro-information processing — the cognitive revolution's insight that the nervous system is an input-output computational device. The third referent is mind as subjective conscious experience — the felt interiority of sadness, perception, and awareness that people mean in everyday life when they ask 'what's going on in your mind?' The fourth referent, inherited from Descartes and the Enlightenment, is mind as higher-order self-conscious reasoning — the reflexive 'I think therefore I am' function that seems categorically different from the rest of the physical world. Gregg Henriques calls this the BM3 problem — the behavior-mind-mind-mind problem — and the failure to disentangle these four referents is responsible for centuries of philosophical and scientific confusion. The UTOK system resolves this through Mind One, Mind Two, and Mind Three. Mind One covers the neuro-cognitive and behavioral referents: Mind 1A refers to information instantiated and processed inside the nervous system, and Mind 1B refers to the overt functional activities behaviorists emphasized. Out of Mind One emerges Mind Two — subjective conscious experience — which is categorically distinct because it is only accessible from the inside and irreducibly first-personal. Mind Three is the domain of self-conscious reflection, private and public language, and justification; it is intersubjective, flowing through the skin, shared between persons and culture rather than locked inside the individual. This tripartite map assigns each tradition its proper domain, giving us for the first time a vocabulary precise enough to actually define what we mean when we say 'mind.'

## Apprentice

When people argue about whether the mind is just the brain, or whether computers can think, or what makes human consciousness special, they are often not disagreeing about facts — they are using the same word to mean completely different things. 'Mind' can mean behavior, brain processing, inner experience, or self-aware reasoning, and most debates quietly assume one meaning while the opponent assumes another.

Gregg Henriques identified this as the BM3 problem — behavior plus three distinct senses of mind tangled into one word. His solution organizes these into three categories. Mind One covers brain processing and outward behavior. Mind Two is the felt, private experience of being conscious — the actual sensation of seeing red or feeling sad, which only you can access from the inside. Mind Three is the layer of self-reflection, language, and reasoning that humans share with each other through culture.

This map does not declare a winner in old debates. Instead, it shows that behaviorists, neuroscientists, philosophers of consciousness, and rationalists were each studying something real — just different things. With clearer labels, conversations that have been circular for centuries can finally move forward.

## Adept

A persistent source of theoretical deadlock in philosophy of mind and psychology is that the term 'mind' functions as a homonym, simultaneously indexing at least four distinct phenomena: behavioral dispositions, neuro-computational processing, phenomenal consciousness, and higher-order self-reflective cognition. Skinner's claim that 'the mind is what the body does' is not simply eliminativism — it is a coherent redefinition anchored to one legitimate referent. The cognitive revolution's input-output model, the hard problem of consciousness, and the Cartesian cogito each stake out a different referent. Gregg Henriques formalizes this as the BM3 problem: behavior plus three non-identical senses of mind, routinely conflated.

Henriques's Unified Theory of Knowledge (UTOK) resolves the conflation through a tripartite architecture. Mind One encompasses both neuro-informational processing (Mind 1A — representations instantiated within the nervous system) and behavioral output (Mind 1B — the functional activities foregrounded by behaviorism). Mind Two designates phenomenal consciousness: the irreducibly first-personal, interoceptively accessible domain of qualia and felt experience that constitutes the hard problem's explanandum. Mind Three covers the intersubjective stratum of self-conscious reflection, propositional language, and normative justification — a domain that is not sealed inside the individual skull but flows through symbolic exchange and cultural scaffolding.

The framework's value is not merely taxonomic. By assigning each major tradition its proper domain, it dissolves apparent contradictions: behaviorism and cognitive neuroscience both describe Mind One; phenomenology and consciousness studies address Mind Two; hermeneutics, social epistemology, and much of philosophy address Mind Three. Debates that appeared to be about the same thing can now be recognized as operating at different levels of analysis.

## Magus

The philosophy of mind has long suffered from what Gregg Henriques designates the BM3 problem: the unremarked polysemy of 'mind' as it simultaneously indexes behavioral dispositions, sub-personal neuro-computational states, phenomenal consciousness, and higher-order reflexive cognition. This is not merely a terminological inconvenience. Because each major research tradition — radical behaviorism, classical cognitivism, phenomenology, and Cartesian rationalism — has implicitly legislated one of these referents as the canonical meaning, cross-paradigm discourse has been structurally isomorphic to equivocation. Skinner's deflationary redefinition, Fodor's language of thought, Nagel's what-it-is-like formulation, and Descartes' cogito are not competing answers to a single question; they are answers to four different questions dressed in identical vocabulary.

Henriques's Unified Theory of Knowledge addresses this through a principled tripartite ontology. Mind One is parsed into 1A — the neuro-informational substrate, comprising representations and transformations instantiated within nervous tissue — and 1B — the overt functional behavioral repertoire that third-person methodologies can operationalize. From the complexity of Mind One there emerges, under conditions not yet fully specified, Mind Two: phenomenal consciousness, characterized by intrinsic first-person accessibility, qualitative character, and the explanatory gap that resists reduction to functional or computational description. Mind Three is categorically distinct in a different direction: rather than being more interior than Mind One, it is more exterior — constituted through intersubjective symbolic exchange, normative justification practices, and the cultural-linguistic scaffolding that Vygotskian and Wittgensteinian traditions have separately theorized.

The architecture's explanatory leverage lies in its capacity to reframe rather than adjudicate. The hard problem is real but local to the Mind One/Mind Two interface. The social construction of selfhood is real but operative at the Mind Two/Mind Three boundary. Eliminativist and phenomenological programs are not incommensurable — they are simply non-overlapping in their domains of application. What emerges is a metatheoretical grammar precise enough to make previously undecidable disputes either tractable or recognizably dissolved.
