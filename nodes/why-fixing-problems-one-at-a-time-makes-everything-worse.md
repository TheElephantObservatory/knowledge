# Why Fixing Problems One at a Time Makes Everything Worse

The committee never reaches the nursing mother.

---

> Most crisis responses break problems into separate categories and tackle them one by one, but the metacrisis is an entangled living system. The real work is not matching solutions to parsed-out problems but meeting the whole — tending to the intimate relational core from which all the categories arise.

There is a critical difference between matching and meeting — and almost all of our institutional responses to crisis are stuck in matching. To match is to parse out a committee for each problem in a metacrisis, schedule solutions for each institution, and hope that fixing them sequentially adds up to something. But each context is actually requiring that all the others be met at once. Fix the economy and you trash the ecology. Fix the education system and you destabilize the economy. The contradictions are not incidental — they are structural, because these are not separate problems. They are expressions of the same entangled living system. To meet is to ask: what are the gestures, what is the work, that ties all of this together and expresses through all of them simultaneously? That requires a different kind of perception entirely. Consider the image of a woman nursing her baby. Everything in the metacrisis is right there — health, economics, ecology, justice, intergenerational transmission, the relationship between body and world. There is no committee for supporting nursing mothers. We presuppose that if we fix all the parsed-out categorized things, the nursing mothers will be okay. But we have it backwards. Tend to the nursing mothers — tend to the intimate, living, relational core — and the other categories will move differently. This is what biology is already doing. Every organism in evolution is nourishing possibilities it does not yet know will be needed. The model is everywhere. The question of how to do this is a mystery only because we have trained ourselves not to see it.

## Apprentice

When we face big, interconnected problems — economic instability, ecological collapse, failing education, health crises — the usual approach is to assign a committee to each one and work through them like a checklist. This is what can be called "matching": pairing each problem with its own dedicated fix. But it rarely works, because solving one problem often makes another worse. Fix the economy and you damage the environment. Reform education and you destabilize the workforce. The problems aren't really separate — they're tangled together at the root.

The alternative is "meeting" — asking what kind of action or attention could move through all these crises at once, because they share a common source. Think of a mother nursing her baby. In that single intimate act, health, economics, ecology, justice, and the transmission of life between generations are all present simultaneously. There is no committee for nursing mothers, yet that living relationship touches everything.

The insight is that we have it backwards. We assume that if we fix all the separate institutional categories, the intimate human core will be fine. But if we tend to that core — the relational, living center of things — the categories begin to shift on their own. Biology already works this way: every organism nourishes possibilities it doesn't yet know it will need. The model is already everywhere. We just have to learn to see it again.

## Adept

A sharp distinction emerges between two modes of institutional response to systemic crisis: matching and meeting. Matching is the dominant paradigm — decomposing the metacrisis into discrete problem domains, assigning specialized committees, and sequencing interventions as though the domains are independent. But the structural reality of entangled systems means that interventions in one domain routinely generate contradictions in others. Economic repair degrades ecology; educational reform destabilizes labor markets. These contradictions are not failures of execution — they are artifacts of the parsing itself. The problems are not separate; they are co-expressions of a single entangled living system.

To meet the metacrisis is to ask a fundamentally different question: what gestures, what forms of attention, express through all domains simultaneously? The image of a woman nursing her baby serves as a powerful heuristic. Health, economics, ecology, justice, intergenerational transmission, and the body-world relationship all converge in that single relational act. No institutional category owns it, yet it is the living substrate from which all categories draw their meaning. The conventional assumption — that tending to parsed-out categories will eventually serve the relational core — is precisely inverted. Tending to the relational core is what reorganizes the categories.

This inversion finds its deepest precedent in biological organization itself. Every organism nourishes capacities whose future relevance is unknown — a form of pre-adaptive generosity that sustains evolutionary possibility. The model for meeting rather than matching is not hidden; it is ubiquitous. The inability to perceive it is not a mystery of nature but a consequence of trained perception, a disciplinary blindness that mistakes its own parsing for the structure of reality.

## Magus

The distinction between matching and meeting constitutes a critique not merely of policy methodology but of the epistemic architecture underlying institutional responses to polycrisis. Matching — the decomposition of entangled systemic failure into discretely addressable problem-domains with sequenced, committee-mediated interventions — operates on an implicit ontology of separability. Yet the contradictions generated by domain-specific interventions (economic repair degrading ecology, educational reform destabilizing economic structure) are not contingent failures but necessary expressions of the fact that these domains are co-constitutive aspects of a single living system. The parsing is the pathology.

Meeting demands a radically different perceptual and practical orientation: the capacity to identify gestures that express through multiple domains simultaneously, because they operate at the level of the entangled whole rather than its institutional projections. The figure of the nursing mother functions here not as metaphor but as ontological exemplar — a concrete locus where health, economics, ecology, justice, intergenerational transmission, and body-world relationality are indissolubly present. No institutional category encompasses this act, yet every category presupposes it. The conventional assumption that categorical repair will eventually serve the relational substrate inverts the actual order of dependence. The relational substrate is generative; the categories are derivative.

This insight finds its deepest structural homology in biological pre-adaptation: every organism nourishes developmental possibilities whose selective relevance remains undetermined. Life itself operates through a logic of meeting — sustaining systemic coherence across domains whose future demands cannot be parsed in advance. That this model is ubiquitous yet invisible to institutional perception is not an epistemic mystery but a diagnostic: the trained incapacity to perceive relational wholes is itself a central expression of the metacrisis it fails to address.
