# Waking Up vs. Growing Up: Two Distinct Axes of Human Development

You cannot see the grammar you speak in.

---

> Mystical awakening and psychological development are two entirely different axes of human growth. One has been practiced for millennia, the other discovered barely a century ago — and because developmental stages are invisible from the inside, growing up remains the most powerful yet most neglected dimension of self-knowledge.

The distinction between waking up and growing up is one of the most consequential and least understood ideas in the entire history of human self-knowledge. Waking up — the direct mystical experience of unity with reality, the dissolution of the separate self into oneness with God or the cosmos — has been known and practiced for at least 50,000 years, possibly far longer. Every major world religion has a mystical sub-school devoted to it: Christian mystics like Meister Eckhart, Sufi Islam, Kabbalistic Judaism, Advaita Vedanta, Zen Buddhism. William James documented it rigorously in 'The Varieties of Religious Experience.' It is a first-person experience — immediate, unmistakable, and known to the person having it. Growing up, by contrast — the movement through developmental stages from egocentric to ethnocentric to worldcentric to kosmocentric — was only discovered approximately a hundred years ago, around the same time Freud published his first work. And it was only discovered then because it cannot be seen by looking within. These stages operate like grammar: you follow the rules without being able to introspect them. You have no direct access to what stage of development you are at. This is why developmentalism remains the single most underrepresented factor in philosophy, psychology, and sociology — despite being arguably the most explanatorily powerful. A person can have profound waking up experiences and still be at a Mythic or ethnocentric stage of growing up. Conversely, someone can be at an integral stage of growing up and have had no significant waking up experiences at all. Both axes matter. Both are required for genuine wholeness. And the fact that growing up has only been a known territory for a century means we are, collectively, still very early in learning how to navigate it.

## Apprentice

There are two fundamentally different ways a human being can mature, and confusing them has caused enormous trouble. The first — waking up — is the direct experience of feeling at one with everything, the dissolving of your sense of being a separate self. Mystics across every tradition have described it for thousands of years. It's immediate and unmistakable to the person having it.

The second — growing up — is the gradual expansion of your perspective, from caring only about yourself, to caring about your group, to caring about all people, to something even wider. This kind of development was only identified about a hundred years ago, and for a striking reason: you can't see it by looking inward. Your current stage of development is like the grammar of your native language — you use it constantly without being aware of its rules.

This means someone can have a life-changing mystical experience and still hold a narrow, tribal worldview. And someone with a remarkably broad, inclusive perspective may never have had a mystical experience at all. Both dimensions matter for genuine human wholeness, and the fact that one of them has been invisible for almost all of human history helps explain why so many wise-seeming people still cause harm.

## Adept

The waking up / growing up distinction represents one of the most consequential differentiations in the study of human consciousness. Waking up — the phenomenological territory of mystical experience, nondual realization, and unitive states — has been mapped by contemplative traditions for millennia, from Meister Eckhart to Advaita Vedanta to Zen. William James gave it rigorous empirical attention in 'The Varieties of Religious Experience.' These are first-person experiences: immediate, self-authenticating, accessible through introspection and practice.

Growing up — the movement through developmental stages from egocentric to ethnocentric to worldcentric to kosmocentric — belongs to an entirely different epistemological category. Discovered only about a century ago, roughly contemporaneous with Freud's early work, developmental stages cannot be detected through introspection. They function like deep grammatical structures: they shape cognition and identity without appearing as objects in awareness. This is precisely why developmentalism remains, as Ken Wilber and others have argued, the single most underrepresented explanatory framework in philosophy, psychology, and sociology — despite its extraordinary power.

The critical implication is that these two axes are independent. A person can achieve profound states of nondual awakening while remaining embedded in Mythic-level cognition — ethnocentric, literalist, pre-rational in their interpretive frameworks. Conversely, someone operating from an integral or post-conventional stage of development may have no significant state experience whatsoever. Genuine wholeness requires both. And because growing up has been a known territory for barely a century, humanity is still in the earliest stages of learning how to integrate these two dimensions of transformation.

## Magus

The waking up / growing up distinction isolates what may be the most consequential orthogonality in the cartography of human interiority. The phenomenological territory of waking up — nondual realization, unio mystica, satori, fana — constitutes a domain of first-person acquaintance knowledge documented across every major contemplative lineage for at least fifty millennia. Its epistemology is radically direct: the subject has privileged access to the state and its self-authenticating character, as James catalogued in 'The Varieties of Religious Experience.' The mystical sub-schools — Eckhart's Rhineland tradition, Ibn Arabi's Sufi metaphysics, Shankara's Advaita, Dōgen's Zen — all converge on structurally isomorphic descriptions of this territory.

Growing up, by contrast, names a domain that is constitutively invisible to first-person inspection. The developmental stages identified by Baldwin, Piaget, Kohlberg, Loevinger, Kegan, Cook-Greuter, and synthesized in Wilber's integral framework operate as transcendental structures in something close to the Kantian sense: they condition the very cognition that would need to apprehend them. Like syntactic rules internalized below the threshold of reflective awareness, one's current stage of development cannot be introspected — it can only be reconstructed through second- and third-person methodologies. This epistemic asymmetry explains why developmentalism was discovered only circa 1900 and remains the most underrepresented variable in the human sciences despite its unrivaled explanatory reach.

The independence of these two axes generates the decisive insight: state attainment does not entail stage development, nor vice versa. Mythic-stage cognition can host genuine nondual realization; integral-stage cognition can proceed without any significant state experience. Both vectors are necessary conditions for comprehensive human maturation — and the radical recency of developmental discovery means the integration project is, historically speaking, barely underway.
