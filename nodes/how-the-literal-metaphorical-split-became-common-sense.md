# How the Literal-Metaphorical Split Became Common Sense

Before the bread was only bread

---

> Ivan Illich argues that the split between the literal and the metaphorical — which we treat as a basic feature of reality — is not a timeless truth but a historical event traceable to a twelfth-century rupture in the Western Church, with consequences still unfolding today.

One of the deepest things Ivan Illich does is locate the roots of modernity not in the Enlightenment or the Scientific Revolution, but in a rupture that occurred within the Western Church around the twelfth century. His question is deceptively simple: how did Christianity go a thousand years without tearing itself apart over what is actually happening in the Eucharist? The controversy over transubstantiation — whether the bread and wine are materially transformed or symbolically represented — becomes a recurring conflagration from the twelfth century onward, through the Reformation and beyond. But for the first millennium, it simply wasn't the kind of question that generated that kind of conflict. Illich's argument is that this tells us something profound: on the far side of that rupture, what seems like common sense to us about the distinction between the literal and the metaphorical simply did not make sense to people. There was not yet a clean separation between what a thing is and what it means. The symbol and the reality were not yet in different ontological categories. What this points to is that the split between the literal and the metaphorical — which we take as a basic feature of how reality works — is not a discovery or an achievement. It is a historical event, with a traceable origin, and consequences that have been playing out ever since. The controversies of modernity, including the ones we are living through now, are in many ways a playing out of that fundamental rupture.

## Apprentice

For a thousand years, Christians shared the Eucharist without tearing each other apart over what exactly was happening to the bread and wine. Then, starting around the twelfth century, the question of whether the elements were literally or symbolically transformed became an explosive controversy that fueled conflicts for centuries. Ivan Illich asks: why did this question suddenly become so divisive?

His answer is striking. Before that rupture, people did not experience a clean divide between what something literally is and what it symbolically means. A symbol and the reality it pointed to were not yet sorted into separate boxes. The bread could be Christ's body without anyone needing to decide whether that was a physical fact or a poetic metaphor, because that distinction had not yet hardened into the way people understood the world.

This means that the separation between literal and metaphorical — something most of us take for granted as just how thinking works — is not a permanent feature of human understanding. It appeared at a specific moment in history, inside a specific culture, and it reshaped everything that followed. Illich locates the roots of modernity not in the Enlightenment or the rise of science, but in this earlier, deeper shift in how people related to meaning itself.

## Adept

Ivan Illich's genealogy of modernity bypasses the usual suspects — the Enlightenment, the Scientific Revolution, the Reformation — and traces the decisive rupture to twelfth-century Latin Christendom. His diagnostic question is precise: how did the Western Church sustain a millennium of eucharistic practice without generating the kind of ontological controversy that became endemic from the twelfth century onward? The transubstantiation debates, which would eventually help fracture Western Christianity, were not suppressed or deferred during the first millennium. They were, in a meaningful sense, unthinkable — not because people lacked sophistication, but because the conceptual architecture that would make them possible had not yet been constructed.

What Illich identifies is the emergence of a new epistemic regime in which the literal and the metaphorical become distinct ontological categories. Prior to this shift, symbol and reality were not experienced as occupying separate registers requiring mediation or reconciliation. The sacramental could be real without being reducible to material causation, and meaning could inhere in things without being projected onto them by an interpreting subject. The rupture Illich describes is the moment when this participatory ontology gave way to one organized around the literal-metaphorical binary.

The implications are far-reaching. If the distinction between the literal and the metaphorical is not a discovery about how language and reality relate but a historical event with a traceable origin, then the controversies that flow from it — including modern debates about representation, meaning, and the status of the symbolic — are not permanent features of intellectual life. They are consequences of a specific civilizational choice, still playing out in ways that remain largely unexamined.

## Magus

Illich's intervention into the genealogy of modernity is radical in the precise sense: it relocates the root. Where standard intellectual histories identify the decisive breaks in early modern epistemology — Cartesian dualism, mechanistic natural philosophy, Protestant hermeneutics — Illich insists that these are secondary elaborations of a more fundamental rupture occurring within twelfth-century Latin ecclesiology. His hermeneutic key is the eucharistic controversy. The question is not why transubstantiation became contested, but why it was not contested for a millennium. The absence of controversy is the datum that requires explanation, and Illich reads it as evidence of a pre-schismatic ontology in which the symbolic and the real had not yet undergone the fission that would make their relationship a problem.

This is not a claim about pre-modern naïveté or undifferentiated thinking. It is a claim about a different organization of the relationship between sign and referent — one in which participation, rather than representation, structured the encounter between meaning and materiality. The sacramental real was neither literalism nor metaphor because the binary itself had not yet crystallized as an operative category. Illich's argument converges here with certain currents in medieval scholarship — the work of Henri de Lubac on the fourfold sense of Scripture, Chenu on twelfth-century naturalism — but presses further by treating the emergence of the literal-metaphorical distinction as the generative event of Western modernity as such.

The consequences ramify across every domain modernity touches. The institutionalization of meaning, the bureaucratization of care, the reduction of knowledge to information — all presuppose the ontological segregation of what a thing is from what it means. Illich's provocation is that this segregation is not a precondition of rigorous thought but a wound in the Western episteme, one whose sequelae constitute the very texture of modern life.
