# How Small Communities Can Resist Domination Through Binding Collective Accords

You chose the vow. Now you keep it.

---

> The only proven defense against competitive races to the bottom is the rebuilding of enforceable cultural norms — accords — in small communities that scale upward through nested layers, each governing fewer concerns, voluntary to enter but non-negotiable once joined.

The only known counterforce to the multipolar trap — to the collective action problem of competitive defection — is what Game B calls the accords: strong, enforced, life-or-death cultural norms built from the bottom up. Jim Rutt draws on Christopher Boehm's 'Hierarchy in the Forest' to make the point that for roughly 95% of human history, forager societies successfully resisted domination by charismatic sociopaths — not through formal institutions, but through ridicule, subversion, exile, and the credible threat of lethal enforcement if the exile returned. The family of the exiled person was often required to carry out the killing, precisely to prevent inter-clan feuds. The accord was total: if you are unwilling to enforce it, you too are expelled. This is what Rutt means by strong sauce — a term coined by Jordan Hall — and he contrasts it sharply with the weak, individualism-saturated culture of the contemporary West, where no one is willing to say what they think or enforce any shared standard of right and wrong. The Game B hypothesis is that new communities must rebuild this kind of accord from the ground up, in small Dunbar-scale groups of roughly 150 people, with accords that are genuinely non-negotiable. These communities then form intermediate membranes — cooperative structures at a larger scale — where some accords are local to that membrane and others are mandatorily inheritable down through all constituent communities. The architecture scales upward through bioregional and larger levels, with each higher level governing a narrower and narrower set of concerns. The key insight is that this must be voluntary — people choose to enter the accord — but once entered, it is not negotiable. The trade works: like a marriage vow, you surrender some freedom and gain something deeper in return. The danger of the dictatorship trap is avoided not by weakening the accords but by building them horizontally across a plurality of communities that may differ substantially from one another, unified only at the highest level by the overarching goal of raising human well-being within safe planetary limits.

## Apprentice

For most of human history, small societies kept would-be tyrants in check — not with courts or constitutions, but with shared rules everyone was willing to enforce, up to and including exile or death. If someone refused to help enforce the rules, they were cast out too. The system worked because the groups were small enough that everyone knew everyone, and the stakes were clear.

Jim Rutt, drawing on anthropologist Christopher Boehm's research, argues that modern culture has lost this capacity entirely. In today's individualistic West, people are reluctant to hold each other to any shared standard of right and wrong. This weakness leaves societies vulnerable to destructive competition — races to the bottom where everyone defects because no one enforces cooperation.

The proposed solution is to rebuild these strong cultural agreements from scratch in small groups of about 150 people. Joining is voluntary, like a marriage vow, but once you're in, the rules are real. These small communities then link together into larger cooperative structures, each higher level governing fewer and more universal concerns. Dictatorship is avoided not by making the rules weak but by ensuring many different communities exist side by side, each with their own local norms, united only by a shared commitment to human flourishing within ecological limits.

## Magus

The Game B accords framework, as elaborated by Jim Rutt and informed by Jordan Hall's conceptual vocabulary, constitutes a structural response to the multipolar trap — the game-theoretic condition in which competitive dynamics compel universal defection even when cooperation is Pareto-superior. Rutt grounds the framework historically in Christopher Boehm's 'Hierarchy in the Forest,' which documents how acephalous forager societies maintained reverse dominance hierarchies for the vast majority of Homo sapiens' existence. The enforcement gradient — ridicule, subversion, exile, and ultimately lethal sanction — was not incidental but architecturally essential. The requirement that the exile's own kin carry out the killing was a precise mechanism for preventing the escalation of inter-lineage conflict, transforming enforcement from a bilateral act into a communal one. The accord's totality was recursive: refusal to enforce constituted defection from the accord itself, triggering the same escalatory sequence.

Rutt's diagnosis of modernity centers on the collapse of this enforcement capacity under liberal individualism's normative hegemony. The result is a culture structurally incapable of resisting coordination failures — what he characterizes, using Hall's term, as the absence of "strong sauce." The reconstruction must begin at Dunbar scale (~150), where the social graph is dense enough to support the reputational and relational infrastructure that enforcement requires. Entry is elective; persistence is obligatory. The phenomenological analog is the marriage vow: a voluntary surrender of optionality that enables access to goods unavailable under conditions of perpetual exit.

The scaling architecture proceeds through nested membranes with differentiated accord inheritance — some norms local, others mandatorily propagated downward. Each successive level of organization governs a strictly narrower domain. The dictatorship trap is structurally foreclosed not by attenuating enforcement but by maintaining horizontal pluralism across communities with divergent local accords, converging only at the highest level on the meta-accord: the optimization of human well-being within biophysical planetary boundaries.
