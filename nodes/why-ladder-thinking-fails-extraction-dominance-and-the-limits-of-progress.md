# Why Ladder Thinking Fails: Extraction, Dominance, and the Limits of Progress

You cannot get the grapes out of the wine.

---

> The belief that wisdom, growth, and justice move upward through stages toward purity is itself the extractive logic driving ecological and social exploitation. Real wisdom means staying in messy not-knowing rather than leaping to the next clean platform.

There is a pervasive cultural illusion that wisdom is a purification process — that evolution, personal growth, and social progress are all movements up a ladder toward some refined, enlightened state. This illusion is everywhere: in developmental stage models, in spiritual hierarchies, in the assumption that education lifts you toward a better version of yourself, in the idea that social movements are marching toward a cleaner, more correct way of being human. But this ladder-and-stages framework is itself part of the matrix we are trying to escape — and it is the same matrix that drives exploitation of nature, of women, and of labor in distant supply chains. The predatory dynamic exposed by the MeToo movement is the same dynamic that drives ecological destruction: a society that celebrates conquest, accumulation, and dominance as the markers of success cannot be reformed by punishing a few high-profile offenders or by designing incentive systems that nudge people toward correct conduct. The culture says 'good on you — you got the girl, you got the money, you beat the system' — and a handful of criminal accusations, hashtag movements, or policy proposals cannot undo that deep grammar of success, because they reproduce the ladder logic. They assume there is a clean platform to jump to. The wisdom we actually need is, as Nora Bateson puts it, 'kinkier than you thought' — not about purification but about sensitivity to systemic complexity, which is rough, weird, confusing, and not politically correct in any direction. You cannot get the grapes out of the wine, the salt out of the broth, the person out of the context: the matrix produces the behavior, and you cannot address climate justice or social justice without confronting the underlying epistemology of extraction and conquest. The deeper work is to sit with genuine not-knowing — to say 'I have no idea how to be in relationship without triggering deep ancestral presuppositions about dominance and desire, and I'm not willing to pretend I do, but I am willing to stay in the question.' That willingness to remain in uncertainty without collapsing into a new solid platform is itself the more honest and more generative response. And the change is not coming from the top — not from change-makers, institutions, or sanctioned media — but from the intimate level: from how we are as parents, lovers, friends, and hosts to the microbiome organisms living inside us. That is where familiarity with complexity becomes a genuine game-changer.

## Apprentice

There is a deep assumption running through modern culture that getting wiser means climbing a ladder — that personal growth, social progress, and even spiritual development are all about moving up toward some cleaner, more enlightened state. This shows up everywhere: in education, in self-help, in the idea that social movements are marching us toward a better version of humanity. But this assumption may be the very thing keeping us stuck.

The same logic that says "climb higher, get purer" also says "conquer more, accumulate more." The predatory dynamics exposed by movements like MeToo and the destruction of ecosystems share a common root: a culture that celebrates dominance and extraction as signs of success. Punishing a few bad actors or designing better policies doesn't touch that root, because those responses still assume there's a clean platform to jump to — a correct place to stand.

The deeper move, as thinker Nora Bateson suggests, is far messier than that. It means sitting with genuine confusion and not-knowing — admitting we don't know how to live without reproducing patterns of dominance, but being willing to stay in the question rather than grabbing for answers. And the real shifts happen not through institutions or movements but at the intimate level: in how we parent, love, relate, and even coexist with the organisms living inside our own bodies.

## Adept

The developmental stage model — the assumption that wisdom, morality, and social progress ascend a hierarchy toward purification — is not merely incomplete but actively complicit in the epistemology of extraction it claims to transcend. This insight, drawing on Nora Bateson's work on transcontextual thinking, argues that ladder logic is the deep grammar connecting seemingly disparate crises: the predatory dynamics of sexual exploitation, ecological destruction, and labor extraction in global supply chains all emerge from a culture that codes conquest and accumulation as markers of evolutionary success. Reform efforts that operate within this grammar — criminal prosecutions, hashtag campaigns, incentive redesigns — reproduce the very structure they oppose by assuming a clean normative platform exists to which we can ascend.

The critical move is recognizing that systemic complexity cannot be addressed through purification. You cannot extract the grapes from the wine or the person from the context. The matrix produces the behavior, and confronting climate justice or social justice requires confronting the underlying epistemology, not just its symptoms. Bateson's formulation that wisdom is "kinkier than you thought" points toward a sensitivity to entanglement that is rough, paradoxical, and uncomfortable in every political direction — not a new stage to achieve but a different relationship to not-knowing.

This reframes where meaningful change originates. It is not institutional, top-down, or policy-driven at its root. The generative edge lies at the intimate scale: in parenting, in erotic and relational life, in friendship, even in our relationship to the microbiome. Familiarity with complexity at this level — the willingness to remain in uncertainty without collapsing into a new solid position — constitutes the epistemological shift that no amount of stage-climbing can deliver.

## Magus

The critique advanced here strikes at the axiomatic structure of progressivist epistemology itself: the assumption that ontogenetic, phylogenetic, and sociopolitical development proceed through hierarchically ordered stages toward refinement is not a neutral descriptive framework but the operative logic of extraction. Drawing substantially on Nora Bateson's transcontextual analysis, the argument holds that developmental teleology — whether Kohlbergian, Wilberian, or embedded in liberal meliorism — instantiates the same deep grammar that codes conquest, accumulation, and dominance as evolutionary fitness. The predatory dynamics surfaced by MeToo and the metabolic rift driving ecological collapse are not analogous problems requiring separate policy interventions; they are isomorphic expressions of a culture whose success metric is extraction, and whose reform mechanisms necessarily reproduce ladder logic by presupposing a normatively clean platform of arrival.

The epistemological pivot demanded is not dialectical sublation but something more radical: an abandonment of the purification imaginary altogether. Bateson's provocation that wisdom is "kinkier than you thought" signals an orientation toward irreducible entanglement — the impossibility of extracting grapes from wine, salt from broth, agent from context. This is not relativism but a rigorous acknowledgment that systemic behavior is produced by systemic conditions, and that addressing justice claims (ecological, sexual, economic) without confronting the epistemology of separation is performative contradiction. The willingness to inhabit genuine aporia — to refuse collapse into new normative solidity — becomes the methodological commitment.

Crucially, the locus of transformation shifts from institutional and discursive scales to the intimate and somatic: parenting, erotic life, hospitality, even interspecies symbiosis with gut microbiota. This is where familiarity with complexity ceases to be theoretical and becomes enacted — where the refusal of ladder logic meets the lived texture of relational uncertainty, and where epistemological change becomes, finally, ontological.
