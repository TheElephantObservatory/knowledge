# Abstraction as Political Act: What AI Builders Leave Unexamined

The blind spots we scale into gods.

---

> Abstraction is the hidden master process through which humans build reality — from education to governance to AI — and the collective failure to examine it consciously means powerful systems inevitably encode our blind spots at civilizational scale.

One of the most underappreciated dimensions of the AI discourse is that it is fundamentally a problem of abstraction — and abstraction is something that most thinkers, including most philosophers, have either compartmentalized or left undertheorized. Piaget's notion of reflective abstraction is expansive but also limiting. Within computer science, abstraction has related but distinct meanings and applications. What's being left out across the board — from the tech developer side, from the high-level meta-theory side, and from the political side — is the failure to make abstraction explicit, reflexive, and socially accountable. The tech entrepreneurs building these systems are not the most politically literate actors, yet what they do is deeply political. The high-level meta-theorists hand-wave intersectional issues because they're inconvenient, which means those higher abstractions simply cannot account for the social dynamics that continue to manifest. The more we are explicit about how we abstract — how we build education systems, governance systems, computer systems, and artificial intelligence — the better we can be at it, and the more we can ensure that critical things aren't left out. Abstraction is not just a technical concept; it is the master process through which humans construct reality at every scale, and failing to examine it consciously is how we end up with powerful systems that encode our blind spots at civilizational scale.

## Apprentice

Every time we build something complex — a school curriculum, a legal system, a piece of software — we decide what matters and what to leave out. That process of simplifying and selecting is called abstraction, and it shapes everything. The argument here is that abstraction isn't just a technical trick used by programmers; it's the fundamental way humans construct their understanding of reality at every level.

The problem is that almost nobody talks about it openly. The tech entrepreneurs building AI systems are making deeply political choices about what to include and exclude, but they rarely think of their work in political terms. Meanwhile, the philosophers and theorists who could examine these choices tend to treat abstraction as a narrow academic topic, or they skip over the messy social realities — like inequality and power — because those realities complicate their elegant frameworks.

The core insight is straightforward but profound: the more conscious and transparent we become about how we abstract — how we decide what counts and what gets ignored — the less likely we are to build systems that silently carry forward our worst blind spots. When abstraction stays invisible, it becomes the mechanism through which bias and exclusion get scaled up to affect entire civilizations.

## Adept

This perspective identifies abstraction as the undertheorized crux of the AI discourse — not abstraction in its narrow computer science sense (information hiding, layered interfaces) nor solely in Piaget's developmental sense (reflective abstraction as cognitive construction), but abstraction as the master process through which humans construct reality across every domain. The claim is that AI's deepest challenges are not primarily about alignment, capability, or regulation in isolation, but about the unexamined abstractions that structure all of these conversations simultaneously.

The critique operates on three fronts. Tech developers perform abstraction constantly — selecting features, defining categories, compressing complexity into tractable models — yet lack the political literacy to recognize these as acts of power. High-level meta-theorists, who might provide the reflexive framework needed, tend to hand-wave intersectional and social dynamics because incorporating them would destabilize their clean theoretical architectures. And political actors, who understand power, rarely engage with the technical and epistemological mechanics of how abstraction actually works in system design.

What emerges is a call for abstraction to become explicit, reflexive, and socially accountable — treated not as a background cognitive operation but as a practice that demands the same scrutiny we apply to governance or institutional design. The stakes are civilizational: when abstraction remains unconscious, the blind spots it encodes don't merely persist — they get amplified through the very systems we build to manage complexity. Making the process of abstraction itself a site of critical inquiry is positioned as a precondition for building AI, education, and governance systems that don't reproduce exclusion at scale.

## Magus

This intervention reframes the AI problematic as fundamentally an abstraction problematic — and then indicts the entire intellectual landscape for its failure to treat abstraction with the seriousness it demands. Piaget's reflective abstraction, while generative in tracing how cognitive structures bootstrap themselves through recursive self-reference, remains confined to developmental epistemology and cannot, on its own terms, account for the political economy of what gets abstracted away. Computer science's formalizations — from Liskov's substitution principle to the layered abstractions of software architecture — operationalize abstraction as information hiding, a framing that is technically powerful but epistemologically impoverished insofar as it naturalizes the selection criteria governing what is hidden from whom.

The tripartite critique is precise: technologists abstract without political reflexivity, meta-theorists abstract without intersectional accountability, and political actors engage power without grasping the epistemic machinery through which abstraction constitutes the very categories of governance. Each failure mode reinforces the others. The developer's naive empiricism, the theorist's convenient universalism, and the politician's instrumental pragmatism converge on a shared deficit — the refusal to make the abstractive act itself an object of sustained critical scrutiny.

What is being articulated is not merely a call for interdisciplinarity but a more radical claim: that abstraction is the generative grammar of human reality-construction at every scale, from perceptual categorization to civilizational infrastructure. To leave it unexamined is not an oversight but a structural condition through which power reproduces itself. The implication for AI is stark — systems built through unconscious abstraction don't merely reflect existing biases; they crystallize and scale the epistemological architecture that produces those biases, rendering them infrastructural and thereby increasingly resistant to contestation.
