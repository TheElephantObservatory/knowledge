# Most Adults Lack the Cognitive Complexity Democracy Requires

The rules were written for minds we haven't built yet.

---

> Most adults never develop the level of abstract reasoning that democratic institutions were designed to require. The gap between what modern governance demands of citizens and what most citizens can cognitively deliver is a civilizational problem hiding in plain sight.

The developmental data on where adults actually sit on scales of moral and cognitive complexity is genuinely alarming, and it has direct implications for the functioning of democratic institutions. Kohlberg's model describes three broad tiers: pre-conventional reasoning, where decisions are driven by punishment avoidance and instrumental self-interest; conventional reasoning, where the group's norms become the organizing framework; and post-conventional reasoning, where abstract principles and social contract thinking come online. The data suggests that a very large fraction of the adult population is operating at the pre-conventional level, the vast majority are somewhere in the conventional range, and very few reach post-conventional thinking. The problem, as Kegan argued in 'In Over Our Heads,' is that modern society is structurally predicated on post-conventional capacities. Concepts like due process, separation of powers, and rule of law require the ability to hold abstract principles in tension with immediate outcomes, to understand why a system that sometimes produces bad results in individual cases is nonetheless worth preserving. If you cannot operate at that level of abstraction, due process looks like bureaucratic obstruction. This is not a fixed genetic ceiling — developmental models are explicit that growth is possible throughout life. But it does mean that the gap between the cognitive demands of our institutions and the cognitive capacities of the population is a civilizational-scale problem that we have largely failed to address.

## Apprentice

Democratic systems rely on ideas like due process, separation of powers, and the rule of law. These concepts require a specific kind of thinking — the ability to value an abstract principle even when it produces a frustrating result in a specific case. If you can't think that way, a fair trial for a guilty person looks like the system is broken, and checks on power look like pointless obstruction.

Developmental psychology, particularly the work of Lawrence Kohlberg and Robert Kegan, maps out stages of moral and cognitive growth. Most people reason based on self-interest or by following the norms of their group. Very few reach the stage where they can hold abstract principles steady against the pull of immediate outcomes. The uncomfortable finding is that modern democratic society was essentially built to be run by people at that highest stage.

This isn't about intelligence or genetic destiny — developmental researchers are clear that growth is possible throughout life. But the gap between what our institutions demand and where most people actually are in their development is enormous, and almost no one is treating it as the serious structural problem it is.

## Adept

Kohlberg's model of moral development describes three tiers: pre-conventional reasoning driven by punishment avoidance and self-interest, conventional reasoning organized around group norms and social conformity, and post-conventional reasoning where abstract principles and social contract logic become operative. The empirical data on adult populations is sobering: a substantial fraction remains at the pre-conventional level, the vast majority cluster in the conventional range, and post-conventional reasoning is rare. Robert Kegan's "In Over Our Heads" crystallized the structural implication — modern liberal democracies are architecturally predicated on post-conventional capacities that most citizens have not developed.

The consequences are not hypothetical. Concepts like due process, judicial independence, and separation of powers require the capacity to subordinate immediate outcomes to systemic principles. Due process means protecting procedural rights even for the obviously guilty. Separation of powers means tolerating institutional friction that slows desired action. For someone operating at a conventional or pre-conventional level, these features register not as design strengths but as system failures — bureaucratic obstruction, elite conspiracy, or simple incompetence.

This framing rejects genetic determinism. Developmental models are explicit that stage progression is possible throughout adulthood given the right conditions. The problem is that virtually no institutional effort is directed at fostering this development at scale. The result is a civilizational mismatch: governance structures that assume capacities the population largely lacks, with no systematic strategy for closing the gap.

## Magus

The developmental literature converges on a disquieting empirical picture. Kohlberg's stage model — pre-conventional (instrumental hedonism, punishment avoidance), conventional (socionomic conformity, interpersonal concordance), and post-conventional (principled moral reasoning, social contract orientation) — when mapped onto adult populations, reveals that post-conventional reasoning remains a statistical rarity. Kegan's constructive-developmental framework, particularly as articulated in "In Over Our Heads," extends this finding into an institutional critique: the epistemological demands of modernity — its legal abstractions, its procedural commitments, its tolerance for principled ambiguity — presuppose a self-authoring or self-transforming mind that most adults have not constructed.

The implications for democratic legitimacy are severe. Constitutional governance is not merely a set of rules but a meta-cognitive commitment: the capacity to hold systemic principles in productive tension with particular outcomes, to grasp that due process is not an obstacle to justice but its precondition, and that separation of powers is a feature rather than a defect. At the conventional level, loyalty to the in-group's interpretation substitutes for principled adjudication. At the pre-conventional level, institutional constraints register purely as impediments to desired ends. In neither case does the architecture of liberal democracy become legible as designed.

Critically, this analysis does not entail a counsel of despair. Developmental trajectories are not fixed; stage transition is empirically documented across the lifespan under facilitative conditions. The civilizational failure lies not in the distribution itself but in the near-total absence of systematic efforts to scaffold vertical development at population scale. The gap between institutional demand and developmental supply constitutes what might be termed a meta-crisis — a structural condition that amplifies every other political dysfunction while remaining largely invisible to the frameworks those dysfunctions generate.
