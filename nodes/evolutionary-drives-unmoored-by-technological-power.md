# Evolutionary Drives Unmoored by Technological Power

Grown-up weapons, adolescent hands

---

> The metacrisis stems from an evolutionary mismatch: drives that were naturally constrained in ancestral environments — toward power, acquisition, narrow optimization — have been unleashed by technology, while the nervous system generating those drives remains unchanged. We wield grown-up tools with adolescent psychology.

There is a deep evolutionary mismatch at the root of the metacrisis. The biosphere has been self-organizing for billions of years according to generative dynamics — processes that produce more life rather than terminating it. Humans emerged from that biosphere, yet in a short span of geological time we have created conditions capable of removing all life from the planet. The answer lies in what evolution optimized for in a context of scarcity. Sugar and salt were rare in the evolved environment, so powerful drives to acquire them developed — drives that become pathological when industrial technology makes them ubiquitous. The same logic applies to power and narrowly defined intelligence. Every organism has a drive toward power in the basic sense of staying alive and propagating. In the evolved environment, that drive was naturally constrained — you could not accumulate weapons capable of radical asymmetries of force, or currency systems allowing radical concentrations of choice-making potential. Technology has removed those natural constraints while the nervous system generating the drive has not changed. Iain McGilchrist frames this in terms of the hemispheres: the left hemisphere isolates a target, ignores everything else, and optimizes acquisition — the predatory, mechanical engineering mind. It needs to be balanced by the right hemisphere's holistic perception of the whole field of value and context. When that balance breaks down, a narrow intelligence runs amok. The tragedy is not that these capacities are bad — in the evolved environment they were essential. The problem is that humans have the additional capacity for reflective abstraction, cultural transmission, and the construction of technological worlds that far outstrip what the nervous system was designed to handle. We have built grown-up technologies with an adolescent psychology — the disproportionality between power and wisdom is the key to the whole problem.

## Apprentice

For billions of years, life on Earth organized itself through processes that generated more life. Humans emerged from that living system, yet in a geological eyeblink we've built the capacity to destroy it entirely. How did a species born from life-generating processes become a threat to all life? The answer involves a mismatch between our ancient biology and our modern world.

Think about sugar and salt. They were scarce in the environments where humans evolved, so we developed powerful cravings for them. Those cravings made perfect sense when sugar was rare — but become dangerous when cheap candy and processed food are everywhere. The same logic applies to power and narrow problem-solving intelligence. Every creature has a drive to survive and grow stronger, but in the ancestral world, natural limits kept that drive in check. No one could stockpile world-ending weapons or concentrate vast decision-making power through abstract currency systems.

Technology removed those natural guardrails, but our nervous systems stayed the same. The deeper tragedy is that humans also developed the ability to think abstractly, pass knowledge across generations, and build technological systems far beyond what our brains were designed to manage. The result is a dangerous gap between our power and our wisdom — like handing the keys to civilization's most powerful machinery to a psychology that never evolved to handle it responsibly.

## Adept

The metacrisis can be understood as a profound evolutionary mismatch. The biosphere self-organized for billions of years through generative dynamics — processes that produced more life, more complexity, more resilience. Humans emerged from these dynamics, yet within a sliver of geological time have constructed conditions capable of terminating the entire experiment. The key mechanism is that evolution optimized acquisition drives under conditions of scarcity. Sugar, salt, power, and narrow instrumental intelligence were all naturally constrained in the ancestral environment — the drive to acquire them was adaptive precisely because limits were built into the ecology. Industrial technology and abstract systems of currency and weaponry have removed those constraints while leaving the underlying neurological architecture untouched.

Iain McGilchrist's hemispheric framework illuminates the cognitive dimension of this mismatch. The left hemisphere isolates targets, suppresses context, and optimizes acquisition — a mode essential for predatory focus and mechanical manipulation. The right hemisphere holds the broader field of value, relationship, and context. In the evolved environment, these modes were balanced by necessity. When technological amplification privileges the left hemisphere's narrow optimization without corresponding holistic constraint, a kind of runaway intelligence emerges — powerful in execution, blind to consequence.

The deepest layer of the problem is that humans possess capacities — reflective abstraction, cultural transmission, technological world-construction — that radically outstrip the parameters the nervous system was calibrated for. The disproportionality between accumulated power and developmental wisdom is not a peripheral feature of the crisis but its structural core. The drives themselves are not pathological; their liberation from ecological constraint is.

## Magus

The generative dynamics of the biosphere — self-organizing processes that have reliably produced increasing complexity and life over billions of years — constitute the deep background against which the metacrisis must be read. Homo sapiens, a product of those dynamics, has in negligible geological time constructed techno-institutional systems capable of comprehensive biospheric termination. The explanatory fulcrum is evolutionary mismatch at the level of drive architecture. Natural selection calibrated acquisition drives — for caloric density, for social dominance, for instrumental problem-solving capacity — against environments where scarcity imposed intrinsic constraint. The pathology emerges not from the drives themselves but from the removal of their ecological boundary conditions by technological amplification: weaponry enabling radical asymmetries of force, currency systems enabling radical concentrations of choice-making potential, information systems enabling radical compression of decision cycles.

McGilchrist's hemispheric analysis provides the neurocognitive correlate. Left-hemispheric processing — decontextualized target isolation, sequential optimization, mechanical manipulation — is the cognitive mode most readily amplified by technology and most naturally aligned with accumulative dynamics. Its adaptive complement, the right hemisphere's apprehension of the relational field, contextual embedding, and value-laden wholeness, lacks equivalent technological affordance. The resulting asymmetry is not merely cognitive but civilizational: an entire techno-economic order structured around the optimization logic of one cognitive mode operating without its evolved counterbalance.

What renders this mismatch uniquely dangerous is the human capacity for reflective abstraction and cumulative cultural transmission — capacities that enable the construction of technological worlds whose parameters exceed the design tolerances of the nervous system that built them. The disproportionality between power and wisdom is not epiphenomenal to the crisis; it is its generative grammar. The ancestral drives are running on hardware unchanged since the Pleistocene, executing in an operating environment they were never selected to navigate.
