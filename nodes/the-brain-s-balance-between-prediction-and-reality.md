# The Brain’s Balance Between Prediction and Reality

The Heavy Burden of Perfect Sight

---

> Mental illness may be a failure of inference — the brain's model of reality weighted too far toward prior belief or toward raw sensation. The hollow mask illusion and schizophrenia together reveal this dial in action.

Psychiatric and neurological disorders can be understood as failures of inference — false modeling of the world. Type one inferential error (inferring something is there when it is not) maps onto hallucinations and delusions; type two (inferring something is not there when it is) maps onto neglect and dissociative syndromes. The hollow mask illusion deepens this picture: most people cannot see an inverted hollow face as hollow because their generative model imposes the simplest prior. Remarkably, many people with schizophrenia resist this illusion — they weight sensory accuracy more heavily relative to prior beliefs. In severe autism, sensory precision is so high that nothing can be ignored, making the world overwhelming and driving repetitive behavior to maximize predictability. The fundamental dial that psychiatric conditions dysregulate is the balance between accuracy and complexity, mediated by the precision — the credence — assigned to sensory evidence relative to prior beliefs.

## Apprentice

The brain does not passively receive the world — it constantly makes predictions and checks them against incoming signals. When this process breaks down, mental illness can follow. Hallucinations may occur when the brain trusts its own predictions too strongly, conjuring things that are not there. Neglect or dissociation may occur when it dismisses real signals as noise.

A striking demonstration comes from the hollow mask illusion. Most people cannot perceive an inverted hollow face as hollow — the brain insists faces must be convex and overrides what the eyes actually report. Many people with schizophrenia, however, see the hollow mask correctly. Their brains weight incoming sensory information more heavily than the stored expectation.

In severe autism, the opposite pressure operates: sensory signals arrive with such force that no prior belief can dampen them. Everything demands attention at once, making ordinary environments overwhelming. Repetitive behaviors then serve a purpose — they make the immediate future maximally predictable, reducing the flood of unpredicted sensation. The common thread across these conditions is a miscalibrated dial between trusting the world as it appears and trusting the world as the mind expects it to be.

## Adept

Predictive processing frameworks propose that the brain is fundamentally a generative model, continuously issuing top-down predictions and updating them against bottom-up prediction errors. Psychiatric and neurological disorders can be reframed as systematic failures of this inferential process. A type one error — inferring signal where there is none — maps onto positive symptoms such as hallucinations and delusions. A type two error — suppressing genuine signal — maps onto neglect syndromes and dissociative states. The pathology, on this account, is not in perception per se but in the calibration of inference.

The hollow mask illusion provides a clean empirical probe. Neurotypical observers cannot sustain the percept of an inverted hollow face; the prior probability of convex faces is so strong that it overrides veridical depth cues. Individuals with schizophrenia frequently resist this illusion, perceiving the hollow correctly. This suggests their generative models assign relatively higher precision — epistemic weight — to sensory evidence than to prior beliefs, consistent with the hyper-salience accounts of psychosis.

Autism spectrum conditions illuminate the other pole of the same axis. Elevated sensory precision means prediction errors are rarely attenuated, producing a perceptual environment of relentless, unfiltered signal. Repetitive and ritualistic behaviors can be understood as active strategies to minimize surprise by engineering a maximally predictable sensory context. The unifying principle is that psychiatric conditions dysregulate the precision weighting that mediates the trade-off between model complexity and sensory accuracy — the fundamental dial of the Bayesian brain.

## Magus

Within the free energy principle and active inference framework, perception and action are jointly understood as approximate Bayesian inference over hierarchical generative models. Psychopathology admits a principled taxonomy under this scheme: disorders resolve into characteristic dysregulations of precision — the estimated inverse variance assigned to prediction errors at different levels of the cortical hierarchy. A hyperprior-dominant regime, in which high precision on top-down predictions suppresses ascending error signals, produces the phenomenology of positive psychotic symptoms: hallucinations arise as percepts unmoored from sensory constraint, delusions as high-confidence posterior beliefs resistant to Bayesian updating. The complementary failure mode — attenuated precision on prior beliefs relative to likelihood — yields neglect, depersonalization, and dissociative syndromes, wherein the generative model fails to impose coherent structure on sensory streams.

The binocular rivalry variant known as the hollow mask illusion operationalizes this axis with unusual clarity. The convexity prior, instantiated at high precision in the ventral visual hierarchy, ordinarily defeats veridical disparity and shading cues, rendering the percept of a hollow face phenomenologically inaccessible to neurotypical observers. The well-replicated finding that individuals meeting criteria for schizophrenia spectrum disorders resist this illusion — perceiving the veridical hollow geometry — is consistent with globally reduced precision on priors, or equivalently, a flatter posterior landscape in which likelihood terms carry disproportionate inferential weight.

Autism spectrum conditions instantiate the reciprocal dysregulation: interoceptive and exteroceptive prediction errors are assigned anomalously high precision, producing a failure of sensory attenuation and the clinical phenomenology of sensory hypersensitivity, attentional inflexibility, and affective dysregulation under environmental novelty. Repetitive and restricted behaviors are interpretable as precision-weighted active inference policies that minimize expected free energy by collapsing the variance of predicted sensory states. The theoretical payoff is a unified, dimensionally continuous account of seemingly disparate nosological categories, grounded in the single computational parameter of hierarchical precision weighting.
