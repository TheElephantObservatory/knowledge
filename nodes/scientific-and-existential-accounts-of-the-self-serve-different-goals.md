# Scientific and Existential Accounts of the Self Serve Different Goals

We do not need a master blueprint.

---

> Scientific and existential accounts of the self serve genuinely different purposes. Mapping them onto each other can be illuminating, but forcing perfect alignment is neither necessary nor desirable — and existential usefulness, not scientific elegance, should be the primary criterion for any inquiry aimed at helping people become themselves.

A scientific account of the self and an existentially useful account of the self are genuinely different projects, and we should not assume that achieving one automatically achieves the other. A scientific account can be perfectly plausible, internally elegant, and empirically well-grounded while being existentially useless — or even existentially counterproductive. And an existentially invigorating model, one that is normatively instructive and prescriptive for the project of becoming oneself, may not map perfectly onto the scientific account. This matters because when we are mapping different accounts of the self over one another — say, a relevance realization model over a Jungian model, or a predictive processing model over a Vedantic model — finding salient correspondences is genuinely useful, but we should not assume that achieving perfect symmetry between them is either necessary or desirable. A topographic map does something different than a demographic map. We do not need a master blueprint. What we do need to be honest about is which goal is primary for a given inquiry. For me, existential usefulness is not a subsidiary goal — it is the principal goal. The reason to undertake this entire project of understanding the self is not merely scientific curiosity but the possibility of re-homing our practices and our phenomenology in a way that is empowering rather than estranging. The scientific account earns its place insofar as it enables, legitimates, and enhances the existential project — not as an end in itself.

## Apprentice

There are many ways to describe who you are. Science might explain the self through brain processes, prediction, and pattern recognition. Spiritual and psychological traditions might describe the self through archetypes, stages of growth, or layers of awareness. These approaches sometimes overlap in striking ways — but they are not trying to do the same thing. A scientific model wants to be accurate. An existential model wants to be useful for actually living your life.

When thinkers find interesting parallels between, say, a cognitive science framework and a depth psychology framework, that can be genuinely valuable. But the assumption that we need to merge them into one perfect master theory is a mistake. A road map and a weather map both describe the same territory, but they serve different purposes. Neither one replaces the other.

The key move here is honesty about what you are actually trying to accomplish. If the goal is to understand the self in a way that helps people navigate their lives with more agency and less alienation, then that existential purpose should lead. Science earns its seat at the table by serving that purpose — by making the existential project more grounded, more credible, and more effective — not by replacing it.

## Adept

This insight draws a sharp distinction between two fundamentally different projects in the study of selfhood: the scientific project, which aims at empirical accuracy and internal coherence, and the existential project, which aims at normative guidance for the lived task of becoming oneself. The claim is not that these projects are unrelated, but that they are genuinely non-identical — and that conflating them leads to subtle but consequential errors. A predictive processing account of self-modeling, for instance, may be empirically robust while offering nothing prescriptive about how to inhabit one's life more fully. Conversely, a Jungian or Vedantic framework may be existentially potent without mapping cleanly onto any neuroscientific model.

The cartographic metaphor is instructive: topographic and demographic maps describe the same territory but serve incommensurable purposes. When thinkers map relevance realization frameworks over depth-psychological or contemplative models, the correspondences they find are real and useful — but the expectation of perfect structural symmetry between accounts reflects a category error. No master blueprint is required, and pursuing one may actually impoverish both projects.

What is required instead is methodological honesty about which goal is primary in a given inquiry. The position articulated here is explicit: existential usefulness is not a pleasant side effect of good science but the principal criterion by which any account of the self earns its place. The scientific account is valued instrumentally — insofar as it enables, legitimates, and deepens the existential project of re-homing human phenomenology and practice in frameworks that empower rather than estrange.

## Magus

The distinction advanced here is between the epistemic and the existential as genuinely orthogonal axes of adequacy in accounts of selfhood. A scientific model — whether framed in terms of predictive processing, relevance realization, or enactivist self-constitution — optimizes for empirical fidelity, parsimony, and explanatory scope. An existentially useful model optimizes for normative traction: its capacity to orient, prescribe, and empower the subject engaged in the recursive project of self-becoming. These optimization targets do not converge by necessity, and assuming they do produces a form of scientistic overreach that is epistemically innocent but existentially corrosive.

The practice of cross-mapping frameworks — laying Vervaeke's relevance realization over Jung's individuation, or Friston's free energy principle over Śaṅkara's adhyāsa — generates genuinely illuminating structural correspondences. But the impulse toward totalizing isomorphism mistakes resonance for identity. The cartographic analogy is precise: different projections of the same territory serve incommensurable navigational purposes, and the demand for a single master projection is not methodological rigor but a failure to recognize the pluralism inherent in legitimate inquiry. What is needed is not synthesis but honest declaration of which adequacy criterion governs a given investigation.

The normative commitment here is unambiguous and deliberately foregrounded: existential usefulness is the architectonic criterion. Scientific accounts are not dismissed but instrumentalized — they earn their place within the larger project to the extent that they render existential frameworks more credible, more precise, and more resistant to self-deception, without colonizing the normative space those frameworks inhabit. The goal is re-homing phenomenology and practice in structures that are empowering rather than alienating — and that goal is not downstream of the science but upstream of it.
