# Hegel's Historical Stages Applied to Western Secularism and the Christian Revival

God migrated into capital while we weren't looking

---

> Hegel's phenomenology maps onto the decades of this century — from New Atheism (Reason) to spirituality (Spirit) to today's Christian conversions (Religion) — but the deeper claim is that capital, not Christianity, is the actual operative religion of our time.

The reading of the current wave of Christian conversions through Hegel's phenomenology of spirit offers a genuinely unusual diagnostic framework. The argument is that Western secular society has been moving through a sequence of historical forms — Consciousness, Self-Consciousness, Reason, Spirit, Religion, Absolute Knowing — and that each decade of this century has been dominated by a different form. The early 2000s were characterized by the dominance of Reason: the Dawkins moment, the New Atheism, the belief that science and rational secularism could simply replace religion. That collapsed. The 2010s moved into Spirit: the proliferation of meditation, psychedelics, inner work, the 'spiritual but not religious' identity that maintained antagonism toward institutional religion while embracing inner experience. Now something is shifting again toward Religion — toward the institutional, the communal, the doctrinal. But the critical question being posed here is whether this religious turn is genuine or larping: are people actually going to church, forming families, raising children within religious institutions — which is how religions actually grow, through birth and retention, not conversion — or is this primarily a virtual symptom, a YouTube-mediated identity performance? The deeper provocation is that the real religion of our time is capital. If Hegel thought God was in the state, the argument is that God has migrated into capital — that capital is what actually moves society universally at the level of unconscious motion. The person who says they are not religious but goes to a job they hate nine to five is enacting their religion. The Christian conversion wave may be a symptom of people sensing this and reaching for an alternative container, but the absolute knowing that Hegel points toward — the form that breaks open through religion rather than being contained by it — has not yet been socially birthed.

## Apprentice

Each decade of this century seems to have been dominated by a different way people relate to meaning. The early 2000s were the era of confident atheism — thinkers like Dawkins insisted science could replace religion entirely. That gave way in the 2010s to a softer spiritual turn: meditation apps, psychedelics, people calling themselves "spiritual but not religious." Now, in the 2020s, something is shifting again — toward actual churches, doctrines, and institutional religion, especially Christianity.

But is this shift real? The critical question is whether these conversions translate into the things that sustain religions over time — families formed within communities, children raised in the faith, lives genuinely restructured — or whether it's mostly an online performance, a kind of identity cosplay mediated by YouTube and podcasts.

The most provocative layer of this argument goes further: the real religion of our time isn't Christianity or atheism — it's capital. The person who drags themselves to a job they hate every day, organizing their entire life around economic demands they didn't choose, is already practicing a religion. They just don't call it that. The wave of Christian conversions may reflect people sensing this unconsciously and reaching for something that feels like an alternative — but the deeper breakthrough, the moment where people see through all these containers rather than just switching between them, hasn't arrived yet.

## Adept

This framework reads the twenty-first century's shifting relationship to meaning through Hegel's Phenomenology of Spirit, mapping its sequence of historical forms onto recent cultural decades. The early 2000s correspond to the dominance of Reason — New Atheism, confident scientific secularism, the assumption that religion was simply an error to be corrected. The 2010s mark a transition into Spirit: inner experience valorized through meditation, psychedelics, and the "spiritual but not religious" identity, which preserved hostility toward institutions while embracing subjective transcendence. The current moment represents a turn toward Religion proper — the institutional, communal, and doctrinal dimensions that Spirit had deliberately bypassed.

The diagnostic tension, however, lies in distinguishing genuine religious formation from performative adoption. Religions grow primarily through birth and retention within functioning communities, not through adult conversion. If the Christian turn is primarily a virtual phenomenon — a YouTube-mediated identity marker rather than a lived restructuring of family formation, community obligation, and institutional commitment — then it remains symptomatic rather than substantive.

The deepest provocation is that capital itself occupies the structural position of religion in the present. If Hegel located the divine in the rational state, the argument here is that God has migrated into capital — the force that organizes life universally at the level of unconscious compulsion. Someone who rejects all religion but submits daily to wage labor they find meaningless is already enacting their faith. The Christian conversion wave may represent an intuition that something occupies this religious position and a desire to contest it, but Hegel's Absolute Knowing — the form that breaks through religion rather than being enclosed by it — remains socially unborn.

## Magus

The diagnostic move here is to take Hegel's phenomenological sequence — Consciousness, Self-Consciousness, Reason, Spirit, Religion, Absolute Knowing — not as an abstract logical progression but as a periodizing schema for twenty-first-century Western culture's relationship to transcendence. New Atheism instantiates Reason's self-certainty: the conviction that Verstand, properly applied, dissolves religious content without remainder. The 2010s' turn to meditation, psychedelics, and inner experience corresponds to Spirit's moment — Geist apprehending itself through subjective interiority while maintaining the negation of institutional Sittlichkeit. The current Christian turn marks the dialectical return to Religion: the recognition that Spirit's formlessness cannot sustain itself and requires the determinate content of doctrine, liturgy, and communal Vorstellung.

The critical intervention concerns the distinction between Religion as lived Gestalt and Religion as aesthetic appropriation. Hegel's Religion involves the community's self-representation through cultus — actual practices of collective life that reproduce themselves intergenerationally. If the conversion wave operates primarily at the level of digital self-narration rather than demographic and institutional reality — if it lacks the mechanism of biological and social reproduction through which religions historically persist — then it functions as a symptom within Spirit rather than a genuine transition to Religion's form.

The most radical thesis is that capital now occupies the position Hegel assigned to the divine within the ethical state. Capital operates as universal, self-moving substance-become-subject — organizing social life at the level of unconscious necessity in precisely the way Geist was supposed to. The worker who disavows all religion yet submits to the discipline of abstract labor performs the cultus of capital daily. The Christian turn may register a pre-conceptual awareness of this displacement, an attempt to install a counter-universal. But Absolute Knowing — the form in which Geist comprehends its own movement through and beyond religious representation — remains the unactualized horizon, the shape of spirit that has not yet found its social body.
