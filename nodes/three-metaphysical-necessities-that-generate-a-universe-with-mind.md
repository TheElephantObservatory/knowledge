# Three Metaphysical Necessities That Generate a Universe With Mind

The floor has exactly three boards.

---

> Only three things are metaphysically necessary to derive a universe with mind: something must happen (process), it must self-differentiate, and the differentiated parts must participate in relation. Bonnitta Roy argues this minimal floor dissolves the false choice between panpsychism and emergentism.

There are only three metaphysical necessities required to derive the existence of a universe with mental phenomena. First, something must happen — if nothing happens, there is no universe. That something happening is simply process. Second, that process must self-differentiate, because if there is only one undivided process, there is nothing around to register that process exists. Self-differentiation is required by the logic of the situation: you are the only thing around, so you must become more than one. Third, the differentiated parts must participate with each other — they must be in relation. Without relational participation, you could have a universe of self-differentiating processes with no perspective, no encounter, no knowing. These three — process, self-differentiation, and relational participation — are the minimal metaphysical floor. Everything else, from contemporary science to the world's contemplative traditions, can be derived from these along different creative pathways. Bonnitta Roy notes that you find structural echoes of this triad everywhere: in Whitehead's process philosophy, in Buddhist Storehouse Consciousness, in Brahman, in developmental stage theory's own logic of differentiation and integration. The Mahamudra practitioner might recognize it as appearances, the interaction of appearances, and the knowing awareness that discerns them. The point is not that these traditions are saying the same thing, but that they are all constrained by the same metaphysical floor — and a rigorous metaphysics should be able to show why that floor has exactly three boards, no more and no fewer. This framework also dissolves a recurring error in contemporary consciousness discourse: the minimal-to-maximal leap, where thinkers grant mind or sentience to progressively simpler systems and then, finding no further recourse, declare that Consciousness or Information must have been fundamental from the start. Bonnitta Roy argues this move is metaphysically problematic — not because panpsychism or idealism are necessarily wrong, but because the leap is driven by the exhaustion of one framework rather than by a positive metaphysical argument. The stack itself is the problem: stratifying reality into layers — matter, life, mind, spirit — and then asking which layer is foundational already presupposes a spatial, hierarchical image of reality that process relational philosophy rejects. Roy's alternative is to begin not by asking what reality is made *of*, but by asking what is metaphysically necessary for this existence, and then to derive a story from those necessities that is consilient with both science and religion without privileging either. The goal is not to resolve the debate between emergentism and panpsychism, but to dissolve the framing that makes that debate feel exhaustive.

## Apprentice

What is the bare minimum the universe needs in order for experience to exist at all? Bonnitta Roy proposes exactly three requirements. First, something must happen — call it process. If nothing happens, there is no universe. Second, that process must split into more than one thing, because a single undivided process has nothing around to notice it. Third, those differentiated parts must interact, must be in relation with each other. Without that relational participation, you could have countless processes that never encounter one another and never give rise to perspective or awareness.

These three — process, self-differentiation, and relational participation — form what Roy calls the metaphysical floor. She points out that echoes of this same threefold structure show up across very different traditions: in Whitehead's philosophy, in Buddhist thought, in Hindu metaphysics, and in developmental psychology. The claim is not that all these traditions are secretly saying the same thing, but that they are all constrained by the same underlying necessities.

This framework also offers a way out of a frustrating loop in modern consciousness debates. Many thinkers grant mind to simpler and simpler things until they run out of road and declare consciousness must be fundamental. Roy argues this leap happens not because the evidence demands it, but because the question was framed badly from the start. Instead of asking what reality is made of, she asks what must be true for anything to exist at all — and builds from there.

## Adept

Bonnitta Roy identifies exactly three metaphysical necessities sufficient to derive a universe capable of mental phenomena. First, process: something must happen, or there is no universe to speak of. Second, self-differentiation: if there is only one undivided process, there is no vantage point from which that process can be registered — the logic of the situation demands that the singular become plural. Third, relational participation: the differentiated processes must stand in relation, must encounter one another, or there is differentiation without perspective, without knowing. These three constitute the minimal metaphysical floor — necessary and jointly sufficient.

Roy observes that structural echoes of this triad appear across disparate intellectual lineages: Whitehead's process philosophy, Buddhist ālaya-vijñāna, Brahman, developmental stage theory's logic of differentiation and integration, and Mahamudra's phenomenology of appearances, their interaction, and discerning awareness. The argument is not one of perennial equivalence but of shared constraint — each tradition is shaped by the same metaphysical necessities, explored along different creative pathways.

Critically, this framework dissolves what Roy calls the minimal-to-maximal leap in contemporary consciousness discourse. When thinkers extend sentience to progressively simpler systems and, finding no natural stopping point, declare consciousness or information fundamental, they are not making a positive metaphysical argument — they are exhausting the resources of a stratified ontology that presupposes spatial, hierarchical layers of reality. Process relational philosophy rejects that image entirely. The question shifts from "what is reality made of?" to "what is metaphysically necessary for this existence?" — yielding a derivation consilient with both scientific and contemplative accounts without privileging either, and dissolving the framing that makes the emergentism-panpsychism debate feel exhaustive.

## Magus

Bonnitta Roy's metaphysical argument is austere in its economy: exactly three necessities — process, self-differentiation, and relational participation — constitute the minimal conditions from which a universe with mental phenomena can be derived. Process is axiomatic: absent happening, there is no ontological situation whatsoever. Self-differentiation follows by internal logical compulsion — a singular, undivided process admits no perspective, no registration; the one must become more-than-one not by external agency but by the reflexive demand of its own situation. Relational participation completes the triad: without mutual encounter among differentiated processes, there is plurality without interiority, difference without discernment. These three are jointly sufficient and individually necessary — the metaphysical floor has exactly three boards.

The structural resonance of this triad across traditions is offered not as perennialist conflation but as evidence of shared metaphysical constraint. Whitehead's creativity-novelty-concrescence, the Buddhist architectonic of ālaya-vijñāna, the self-differentiating Brahman, Mahamudra's phenomenological triad of appearances, their co-arising interaction, and rigpa's discerning awareness — each instantiates the same necessities along divergent creative pathways. Roy's claim is that a rigorous process relational metaphysics should be able to demonstrate why these convergences obtain and why they are structurally isomorphic without being semantically identical.

The polemical edge of the framework targets the minimal-to-maximal leap endemic to contemporary consciousness discourse. Roy diagnoses this move — from micropsychism through panpsychism to fundamental Consciousness or Information — as symptomatic not of genuine metaphysical discovery but of the exhaustion of a stratified ontology that spatializes reality into hierarchical layers and then asks which layer is foundational. This very image is what process relational philosophy refuses. By beginning with metaphysical necessity rather than ontological inventory, Roy's approach dissolves the emergentism-panpsychism antinomy as an artifact of framing, offering instead a derivation consilient with both scientific naturalism and contemplative phenomenology without requiring either to serve as ground.
