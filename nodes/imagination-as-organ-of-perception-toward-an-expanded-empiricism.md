# Imagination as Organ of Perception: Toward an Expanded Empiricism

The eye the sun made, to see the sun.

---

> Imagination is not a fiction-making faculty but an unfinished organ of perception. If the senses themselves evolved out of a deeper perceptual capacity, then contemplative and imaginal inquiry count as frontier empiricism — explorations of real domains not yet mapped by ordinary sense experience.

The imagination, properly understood, is not a faculty for generating fictions — it is an organ of perception whose development is not yet complete. The traditional naturalist picture assumes a fixed cognitive apparatus: five senses plus logical and mathematical abstraction, from which we construct our model of nature. But this picture prejudges the limits of empiricism before the investigation has been conducted. If the imagination is understood instead as the living process — the natura naturans rather than the natura naturata — out of which the physiological sense organs themselves emerged over evolutionary time, then the senses we have are not arbitrary survival dashboards but microcosmically resonant with what is macrocosmically real. Goethe's formulation captures this: the eyes are sunlike because light, in some sense, called them forth. The relationship between perceiver and perceived is not one of contingent fit but of deep attunement. This has consequences for what science can be. If our capacity to experience is not closed and finished, then phenomenology of religion, contemplative inquiry, and imaginal perception are not pre-scientific curiosities to be explained away — they are frontier empiricism, explorations of domains that are inner or imaginal but still objective in the sense that peers can review one another's insights and cultivate genuine intersubjective purchase on something real. The distinction between the imaginal and the imaginary is therefore not merely terminological: the imaginal has ontological heft, it is neither mere concept nor mere percept, it occupies the in-between space that Kant was already pointing toward, and it is the space in which ritual, contemplative practice, and scientific imagination all operate when they are working at their best.

## Apprentice

We usually think of imagination as the part of the mind that makes things up — fantasies, daydreams, inventions. But there is an older and more radical idea: imagination is actually a way of perceiving reality, one that hasn't finished developing yet. Our five senses didn't appear from nowhere. They evolved over vast stretches of time, shaped by the very world they now detect. Goethe put it memorably: the eye is "sunlike" because light itself, in some deep sense, called the eye into being. Perceiver and perceived are not strangers — they are intimately attuned.

If that attunement is still unfolding, then experiences explored through contemplation, ritual, and disciplined inner practice are not just subjective feelings to be explained away. They may be early encounters with dimensions of reality that our current senses don't yet fully register — a kind of frontier science conducted with capacities we are still growing into.

The key distinction is between the "imaginary" (pure fiction) and the "imaginal" (something experienced inwardly but genuinely real, in the way that mathematical truths are real even though you can't touch them). When people share and cross-check their contemplative findings, they can build real, reviewable knowledge — not private fantasy, but a new form of empiricism.

## Adept

The standard naturalist framework treats the human cognitive apparatus as essentially fixed: five senses plus formal reasoning, from which all legitimate knowledge must be derived. This perspective challenges that assumption at its root. If imagination is understood not as a fiction-generating faculty but as the living formative process — natura naturans rather than natura naturata — out of which the physiological senses themselves crystallized over evolutionary time, then our perceptual capacities are not closed. They are developmental, still unfolding, and deeply attuned to the structures of the world that called them forth. Goethe's principle that the eye is sunlike because light shaped it captures this participatory ontology: the relationship between perceiver and perceived is one of co-constitution, not contingent fit.

This reframing has direct consequences for the scope of empiricism. Contemplative inquiry, phenomenology of religion, and imaginal perception cease to be pre-scientific curiosities awaiting reductive explanation. They become frontier empiricism — disciplined explorations of domains that are inner or imaginal yet still objective in the sense that intersubjective review and cultivation of shared discernment are possible. The imaginal, as distinct from the imaginary, carries ontological weight: it is neither mere concept nor mere sensory datum but occupies the in-between space that Kant's transcendental aesthetic was already gesturing toward.

Ritual, contemplative practice, and scientific imagination all operate in this intermediate zone when they are working at their best. Recognizing this does not dissolve the rigor of science — it extends it, by refusing to prejudge the limits of experience before the investigation has been conducted.

## Magus

The conventional empiricist settlement presupposes a closed perceptual apparatus — five sense modalities plus formal abstraction — and then legislates the boundaries of possible knowledge accordingly. But this settlement is self-undermining: it forecloses the question of perceptual development before conducting the inquiry. The move proposed here is to read imagination as natura naturans, the living formative activity out of which the determinate sense organs precipitated across phylogenetic time. On this reading, the senses are not arbitrary survival dashboards but microcosmic crystallizations of macrocosmic processes — Goethe's sonnenhaft principle rendered evolutionary. The perceiver-perceived relation is thereby reconceived as participatory co-constitution rather than Kantian imposition or naïve realist mirroring.

The epistemological consequences are substantial. If perceptual capacity is developmental and not terminal, then the phenomenology of contemplative states, imaginal perception, and ritual cognition are not residual enchantments awaiting deflationary explanation but genuine empirical frontiers. The imaginal — distinguished rigorously from the imaginary — possesses ontological heft: it designates a domain that is neither purely eidetic nor purely sensory but occupies the metaxic space between. This is the space Kant's schematism gestured toward without fully inhabiting, the space Corbin excavated in his mundus imaginalis, and the space in which Goethean science, Husserlian Wesensschau, and contemplative epistemologies converge.

Critically, objectivity in this domain is not abandoned but reconstituted. Intersubjective review remains operative: practitioners cultivate discernment, compare findings, and achieve genuine purchase on structures that resist private fantasy precisely because they exhibit lawfulness, recurrence, and responsiveness to disciplined attention. What shifts is not the commitment to rigor but the prejudgment that rigor requires restriction to the already-sedimented senses.
