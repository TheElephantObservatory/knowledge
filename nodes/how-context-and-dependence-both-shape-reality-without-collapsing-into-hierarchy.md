# How Context and Dependence Both Shape Reality Without Collapsing Into Hierarchy

The tracks are parallel. The tracks converge. Both are true.

---

> Can things have genuinely different properties in different contexts without any context being more fundamental? Cahoone argues that contextualism alone is incomplete — asymmetric dependence between orders of nature establishes a hierarchy of constitution, not of reality.

A central tension in any pluralistic metaphysics is whether contextualism — the view that something can have genuinely different and even apparently contradictory properties in different contexts — is sufficient, or whether it must be supplemented by some notion of asymmetric dependence. Cahoone uses the train tracks as a precise test case. Buchler's objective relativism would say the tracks are both parallel and convergent: parallel in relation to the ground and the train, convergent in relation to the binocular vision of a human observer. Both are real properties of the tracks. No contradiction arises as long as you keep the contexts separate. Cahoone accepts this but argues it is incomplete. Once you take emergence and hierarchical systems seriously, you recognize that the parallelism is prior — not more real, but ontologically prior — because the tracks are material objects structured in a stable way, and the convergence is an emergent property that arises only in the biological order, requiring a living organism with visual perception. The convergence depends on the parallelism; the parallelism does not depend on the convergence. This is the key move: asymmetric dependence does not collapse into a hierarchy of reality, but it does establish a hierarchy of constitution. Everything mental depends on something biological, which depends on something material. The natural sciences therefore have a kind of priority — but it is not epistemic priority, not greater certainty or truth-value. It is simply that physics applies to more of the universe, and the higher orders are constituted by the lower. Neither is more real. But they are not symmetrically related.

## Apprentice

Imagine looking down a set of train tracks. They're parallel — you can measure it. But they also appear to converge in the distance. A pluralist philosopher like Buchler would say both properties are real: the tracks are parallel relative to the ground, and convergent relative to human vision. No contradiction, just different contexts.

Cahoone accepts this but pushes further. He points out that these two contexts aren't on equal footing. The parallelism is a physical property of steel and wood laid in the ground. The convergence only exists because a living creature with eyes is looking at them. You need the physical tracks before you can have the visual experience of convergence — but you don't need anyone looking at them for them to be parallel. One depends on the other, not the reverse.

This asymmetry doesn't mean the convergence is fake or less real. It means there's a natural ordering: material things come first in the chain of what makes what possible. Biology depends on physics, mind depends on biology. Physics isn't truer or more certain — it just applies more broadly, and everything else is built from it. The hierarchy is about constitution, not about which level of reality matters more.

## Adept

Buchler's ordinal naturalism holds that any entity — or "natural complex" — possesses whatever properties it exhibits in its various relational contexts, and that no context is metaphysically privileged. Applied to the classic train-tracks case, the tracks are genuinely parallel (in relation to the ground) and genuinely convergent (in relation to binocular vision). Contextualism dissolves the apparent contradiction by distributing the properties across distinct orders of relation. So far, so good.

Cahoone's intervention is to argue that this symmetric pluralism, while correct as far as it goes, is incomplete once emergence enters the picture. The parallelism is a property of material objects in stable physical configuration. The convergence is an emergent property arising only within the biological order — it requires a perceiving organism with a specific visual apparatus. The convergence ontologically depends on the parallelism; the reverse does not hold. This asymmetric dependence is not a claim about degrees of reality. Both properties remain equally real. But they are not symmetrically constituted.

The broader philosophical payoff is a layered ontology that preserves pluralism while acknowledging hierarchical constitution. Physics applies to more of the universe than biology, and biology to more than psychology — not because physics is epistemically superior, but because higher-order complexes are constituted by lower-order ones. The natural sciences enjoy a priority of scope and constitution, not of truth-value or explanatory privilege. Cahoone thus threads a needle between reductive physicalism, which collapses the hierarchy into identity, and flat-footed relativism, which refuses to recognize any ordering among contexts at all.

## Magus

The crux of Cahoone's argument is that Buchler's objective relativism — in which every natural complex is equally real in each of its ordinal locations — provides a necessary but insufficient framework for a naturalistic metaphysics. Buchler's contextualism correctly dissolves pseudo-contradictions by distributing apparently incompatible properties across distinct relational contexts: the train tracks are parallel qua material configuration and convergent qua object of binocular perception. No subreption of contexts, no contradiction. Yet this analysis treats the two ordinal locations as symmetrically related, and it is precisely this symmetry that Cahoone contests.

The asymmetry is constitutive, not evaluative. Convergence is an emergent property instantiated only within the biological order; it presupposes the material order in which parallelism obtains, whereas parallelism presupposes no perceiver. This is asymmetric ontological dependence without asymmetric ontological dignity. Cahoone is careful to distinguish this from both classical foundationalism and from eliminative physicalism: the claim is not that the material order is more real, nor that higher-order properties reduce to lower-order descriptions. It is that the orders of nature — material, biological, psychological, cultural — stand in relations of constitutive dependence that are irreversible. Each higher order is a genuine emergent domain with its own integrity, but each requires the lower as a condition of its possibility.

The implications for the philosophy of science are precise. Physics enjoys priority of scope — it applies to every natural complex — but this confers no epistemic privilege, no greater truth-value, no explanatory hegemony. The priority is ontological-constitutive, not epistemological. Cahoone thereby occupies a position that is neither Buchler's flat pluralism nor the stratified realism of, say, Bhaskar, but a pluralism disciplined by the directionality of emergence.
