# Why Psychology Cannot Choose Between Subjectivity and Objectivity

The beetle in the box casts no shadow.

---

> Psychology's split between private inner experience and objective outer observation is a false dichotomy. Both poles collapse under scrutiny; what precedes them is intersubjectivity — the shared, relational space where meaning is born before it becomes either 'inner' or 'outer.'

Psychology — and social science more broadly — is haunted by a false dichotomy inherited from Descartes: the split between an inner subjective world and an outer objective world. This dichotomy generates an endless oscillation. On one side, the scientific impulse demands objective observation of publicly available behavior. On the other, critics insist we must introspect, must get inside the lived experience of persons. Neither pole can be sustained.

Subjectivity fails because if experience were truly private — hidden in what Wittgenstein calls the 'beetle in the box' — no word we used to name it could ever acquire shared meaning. The argument begins with a deceptively simple thought experiment: imagine that every person has a box, and inside that box is an object they call a 'beetle.' No one can ever look into anyone else's box. Now ask: what does the word 'beetle' mean? The answer is: nothing stable, nothing shared. Without any public criterion for identifying what counts as a beetle, the word cannot function as a meaningful term in a shared language. If my experience of anger is sealed inside me and yours is sealed inside you, the word 'anger' could never refer to the same thing for both of us. The very fact that psychological language works — that when I say 'fear' you know what I mean — proves that experience cannot be a priori private.

Objectivity fails for the opposite but equally fatal reason: we never simply record what is there. When we watch a baby reach for a bottle, we do not see a hand moving through coordinates in space. We see agency, intention, desire. The 'subjective' is already saturated into the 'objective' before we even begin to describe it. Every psychological term — authoritative parenting, social competence, moral development, wisdom — carries normative, evaluative, even moral content that cannot be stripped away without destroying the phenomenon itself.

The solution is not a compromise between the two poles but a third term that precedes both: intersubjectivity. Psychological knowledge originates neither in private introspection nor in detached observation, but in the shared, relational, semiotic space between people. The meaning of the word 'hurt' does not arise because a child looks inward and labels a sensation. It arises because a caregiver sees the child's expression, names it — 'that must have hurt' — and over thousands of such exchanges the child internalizes that socially constructed meaning and applies it reflexively to their own experience. The inner and the outer are not two things with a mysterious gap between them — they are two faces of a single process. Children do not first have private experiences and then learn to express them; they have experiences that are already partly constituted by the social interactions through which those experiences get named. Psychological self-knowledge is therefore not a matter of looking inward at pre-existing contents — it is a matter of applying socially constructed meanings to one's own ongoing experience in an act of reflection that is itself social in origin.

This reframes what science itself is: not a correspondence between theory and independently existing data, but a progressive corroboration of intersubjectively mediated descriptions, constrained from multiple directions, always value-laden, always open to revision, and more rigorous — not less — for acknowledging that openly.

## Apprentice

Psychology has long been caught between two impossible positions. One side says we must study only what we can observe from the outside — measurable behavior, visible actions. The other side insists that what really matters is inner experience — what it feels like to be afraid, or hurt, or in love. The trouble is that neither position actually works on its own.

Pure inner experience fails because if feelings were truly locked inside each person, we could never share a language about them. The philosopher Wittgenstein made this vivid: imagine everyone has a sealed box with something they call a 'beetle' inside, but no one can look in anyone else's box. The word 'beetle' would become meaningless. Yet psychological words do work — when someone says 'I'm afraid,' we understand. That understanding proves experience was never purely private to begin with.

Pure objectivity fails too, because we never just record neutral facts. When we watch a child reach for a toy, we already see wanting, trying, choosing — not just a hand moving through space. The insight here is that meaning doesn't start inside individuals or outside them. It starts between them — in the exchanges where a caregiver says 'that must have hurt' and a child gradually learns what 'hurt' means. The inner world and the outer world are two sides of one shared process, not two separate realms needing a bridge.

## Adept

The Cartesian split between subjective interiority and objective exteriority has structured psychology's self-understanding for centuries, generating an unresolvable oscillation between behaviorist observation and phenomenological introspection. This analysis argues that both poles are internally incoherent and that the way forward lies not in synthesis but in recognizing a more fundamental category: intersubjectivity.

Subjectivity collapses via Wittgenstein's private language argument. If mental states were epistemically sealed — each person's experience a 'beetle in a box' accessible only to its owner — then psychological predicates could never acquire shared meaning. The very functionality of emotion terms in ordinary language demonstrates that experience is not a priori private. Objectivity collapses from the opposite direction: psychological observation is always already saturated with normative and intentional content. Concepts like agency, moral development, or social competence cannot be reduced to value-free behavioral descriptions without destroying the phenomena they pick out. The data of psychology are never raw; they arrive pre-interpreted through frameworks laden with evaluative commitments.

The resolution is that psychological meaning originates in the relational, semiotic exchanges between persons — paradigmatically, in the caregiver-child dyad where expressions are named, feelings are co-constructed, and self-knowledge emerges as the internalization of socially mediated categories. Children do not first have private experiences and then learn to express them; their experiences are partly constituted by the interactive processes through which those experiences are articulated. This reframes scientific rigor itself: not as correspondence between theory and mind-independent data, but as progressive corroboration of intersubjectively mediated descriptions, constrained from multiple directions, openly value-laden, and more epistemically honest for acknowledging that fact.

## Magus

The Cartesian bifurcation of mind into res cogitans and res extensa bequeaths to psychology a structurally irresolvable antinomy: subjectivism, which privileges first-person epistemic access, versus objectivism, which demands third-person observational warrant. This analysis demonstrates that both poles are self-undermining and that their apparent exhaustiveness occludes the genuinely foundational category — intersubjectivity as the constitutive ground of psychological meaning.

The subjectivist pole is dismantled through rigorous application of Wittgenstein's private language argument. If phenomenal states were epistemically quarantined — each subject's experience the 'beetle in the box' that no criterion external to the subject could identify — then psychological predicates would be semantically vacuous, incapable of sustaining the normativity required for linguistic meaning. That emotion terms function communicatively is not an empirical curiosity but a transcendental constraint: experience must be, in principle, publicly articulable. The objectivist pole fails symmetrically. Psychological observation is irreducibly theory-laden and normatively saturated; intentional descriptions — agency, desire, competence, wisdom — carry evaluative content that is not eliminable residue but constitutive of the phenomena. Strip the normativity and the explanandum dissolves.

What precedes both poles is the semiotic, relational matrix of intersubjective exchange. Psychological self-knowledge is not introspective discovery of pre-given mental contents but the reflexive application of socially constituted categories internalized through dyadic interaction — the caregiver's 'that must have hurt' iterating across thousands of exchanges until the child can deploy the concept autoreflexively. Experience is always already partly constituted by the interpretive practices through which it is articulated. Science, on this account, is not correspondence with mind-independent data but progressive corroboration across intersubjectively mediated descriptions — constrained, value-laden, fallibilist, and epistemically stronger precisely for its refusal of the neutrality myth.
