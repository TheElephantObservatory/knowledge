# Measuring Cognitive Development Across the Arc of Human Civilization

The unfinished work of a dead man's hunch

---

> Jean Piaget's unfinished late project sought to apply developmental psychology to the arc of civilization itself. The Cultural Complexity Index picks up that thread, using automated scoring to test whether humanity's collective intellectual output shows a measurable developmental trajectory across millennia.

Piaget, near the end of his life, was working on something that almost no one knows about: he and a colleague were exploring what developmental trajectories would look like when applied not to individual children but to the history of civilization itself — looking at historical texts and asking whether there is a discernible arc to the development of collective human thought. He died before that project could be completed. What the Cultural Complexity Index project attempts to do is pick up that thread — but now with a tool that Piaget never had: an automated, mathematically grounded scoring system capable of placing any text on a developmental scale with a precision that was simply impossible in his era. The hypothesis is that collective human literary and intellectual production — from the Epic of Gilgamesh to Newton's Principia to contemporary philosophy — shows a developmental trajectory analogous to what we see in individual cognitive development. That civilizations, like minds, build hierarchical complexity over time. That there is something like collective learning. This has been gestured at qualitatively by sociologists and philosophers of history, but it has never had a quantitative basis. If the scoring system can be applied to a representative sampling of historical texts and produce coherent, interpretable results, it would be the first empirical evidence for what has until now been a compelling but unverifiable intuition: that human culture, taken as a whole, develops.

## Apprentice

Before he died, Jean Piaget — the psychologist famous for mapping how children's thinking grows in stages — was quietly working on a radical question: does civilization itself develop the way a child's mind does? He and a colleague had begun examining historical texts to see if collective human thought follows a recognizable arc of increasing sophistication. The project was never finished.

The Cultural Complexity Index is an attempt to resume that work, but with a powerful advantage Piaget never had: an automated scoring system grounded in mathematics that can place any text on a developmental scale. The idea is to take a wide sampling of human literary and intellectual works — from ancient epics to modern philosophy — and measure whether they show a pattern of growing complexity over historical time.

Philosophers and sociologists have long suspected that cultures learn and mature in ways that mirror individual growth, but no one has been able to test this with hard numbers. If this scoring method produces clear, interpretable results across thousands of years of texts, it would offer the first real evidence for something people have only been able to intuit: that human culture, taken as a whole, genuinely develops.

## Adept

In the final years of his career, Piaget undertook a largely unknown project with a colleague: applying his stage-developmental framework not to individual ontogeny but to the phylogeny of civilization. They examined historical texts for evidence that collective human cognition follows a discernible developmental trajectory — that the intellectual productions of cultures exhibit the same hierarchical complexification observed in maturing minds. Piaget died before the work reached fruition.

The Cultural Complexity Index project represents a deliberate continuation of that inquiry, now equipped with something Piaget lacked: a mathematically grounded, automated scoring system capable of assigning developmental-stage ratings to any text with quantitative precision. The ambition is to apply this instrument to a representative corpus spanning from the Epic of Gilgamesh through Newton's Principia to contemporary philosophical works, testing whether a coherent developmental signal emerges across historical time.

The hypothesis — that civilizations build hierarchical complexity in a manner structurally analogous to individual cognitive development — has been articulated qualitatively by thinkers in the sociology of knowledge and philosophy of history, but it has never had an empirical, quantitative foundation. If the scoring system yields interpretable results across millennia of textual production, it would constitute the first measurable evidence for collective cognitive development — transforming a compelling but historically unverifiable intuition into a testable and potentially falsifiable claim about the nature of cultural evolution.

## Magus

Piaget's late and largely unrecognized collaboration aimed to transpose his constructivist stage theory from individual ontogenesis to the diachronic unfolding of civilizational thought — a move from genetic epistemology to what might be termed phylogenetic epistemology. The project involved close reading of historical texts for structural markers of hierarchical integration, seeking to determine whether the cognitive operations evident in cultural productions recapitulate, at a macro-historical scale, the developmental sequence observed in individual subjects. His death left this line of inquiry embryonic and without methodological closure.

The Cultural Complexity Index takes up precisely this unfinished problematic, but introduces a decisive methodological innovation: an automated, axiomatically grounded scoring system capable of assigning hierarchical complexity ratings to textual artifacts with a granularity and consistency unattainable through qualitative hermeneutics alone. Applied systematically to a representative diachronic corpus — spanning Sumerian literary production through early modern natural philosophy to contemporary analytic and continental thought — the instrument tests whether a monotonic or stage-structured increase in operational complexity is empirically recoverable from the historical record.

The significance is considerable. The proposition that collective intellectual production undergoes genuine development — not merely accumulation or stylistic drift, but structural reorganization toward greater hierarchical integration — has been advanced qualitatively by figures from Hegel to Gebser to Habermas, yet has remained epistemically stranded between philosophical assertion and empirical demonstration. A validated quantitative signal across millennia of textual evidence would represent the first operationalization of civilizational cognitive development, grounding what has functioned as speculative philosophy of history in falsifiable measurement and opening developmental science to genuinely macro-historical application.
