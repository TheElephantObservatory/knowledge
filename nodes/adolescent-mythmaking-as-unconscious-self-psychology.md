# Adolescent Mythmaking as Unconscious Self-Psychology

The child who mapped her own exile in stars

---

> Bruce Alderman's adolescent mythology — a goddess who exiles her own unrecognized child — turns out to be a precise symbolic map of psychospiritual suppression. The imaginal world-building of unusual children is not escapism but depth psychology conducted before the vocabulary exists.

Bruce Alderman describes the construction of an elaborate personal mythology in early adolescence — a cosmogony featuring a goddess named Rhea who radiates light into boundless openness, becomes disturbed by the infinity she cannot reach, encloses herself in a shell that becomes the crust of a planet, and then fails to recognize her own firstborn moving within her. She summons a Tamer to exile what she cannot recognize, and the exiled child becomes the moon — the realm of the tormented dead. What is striking is not the mythology itself but Bruce's retrospective reading of it: looking back with different eyes, he can feel the psychospiritual import of what his adolescent imagination was working through. The goddess who cannot recognize her own child, who mistakes her own creative depth for a threat and calls in force to suppress it — this is a precise symbolic map of a psyche under pressure, of creative and mystical interiority being exiled by the parts of the self that need to manage danger. The mythology was not escapism; it was depth psychology conducted through story before the vocabulary of depth psychology was available. This points toward something broader about the function of world-building, language-invention, and mythmaking in developmentally unusual children: the imaginal is not a retreat from reality but a more honest engagement with interior reality than the social world permits.

## Apprentice

As a young teenager, Bruce Alderman built an elaborate personal mythology: a goddess named Rhea who radiates light into infinite openness, becomes frightened by what she cannot reach, seals herself inside a shell, and then fails to recognize her own firstborn child moving within her. She calls in a force to exile the child, who becomes the moon — a realm of torment. It's a strange, vivid story, the kind of thing a creative adolescent might construct and later dismiss as fantasy.

What makes this remarkable is Alderman's retrospective reading. Looking back as an adult, he recognizes the story as a precise map of something happening inside him: a psyche under pressure, where the deepest creative and spiritual parts of the self are being treated as threats and pushed away. The goddess who cannot recognize her own child is a portrait of a mind that suppresses its own depth because that depth feels dangerous.

This points to something important about imaginative children who build worlds, invent languages, and write mythologies. These activities are often treated as escapism or mere play. But in many cases, the imaginal work is doing something the child has no other way to do — it is engaging honestly with interior realities that the social world has no room for. The mythology was depth psychology before the child had any access to that language.

## Magus

Bruce Alderman's retrospective exegesis of his own adolescent cosmogony constitutes a striking case study in what might be called autochthonous depth psychology — symbolic work performed by the psyche upon itself prior to any encounter with the interpretive traditions that would later render it legible. The mythology in question features a goddess, Rhea, whose radiance into infinite openness generates a disturbance she cannot resolve, prompting self-enclosure; she then fails to recognize her own firstborn as her own, summoning a Tamer to exile the child into a lunar realm of torment. The structural homology with dissociative dynamics is precise: creative-mystical interiority, experienced as threat by the self-protective functions of the psyche, is subjected to exile by an internalized authoritative force.

What elevates this beyond autobiographical curiosity is the hermeneutic move Alderman performs. The adolescent mythmaker had no access to Jungian, object-relational, or transpersonal frameworks, yet the narrative encodes their core insights with remarkable fidelity. The goddess-who-cannot-recognize-her-child is simultaneously a figure for failed mirroring, for the ego's dissociation from its own numinous ground, and for the cultural suppression of imaginal cognition. The retrospective reading does not impose meaning but discovers the meaning the psyche had already articulated in its own idiom.

This carries implications for understanding the developmental function of elaborate world-building and mythopoeic activity in children whose interiority exceeds the metabolic capacity of their social environment. Such activity is neither compensatory fantasy nor mere cognitive play. It operates as a form of symbolic self-witnessing — the psyche generating narrative structures adequate to realities it is undergoing but cannot yet name. The imaginal, in this framing, is not a retreat from the real but a more rigorous phenomenology of it, conducted in the only language available.
