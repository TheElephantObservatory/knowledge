# Stage Models Confuse Cognitive Structure with Historical Consciousness

You have not been where they are.

---

> Stage models of cultural development confuse cognitive structure with historical content — the ability to think at higher levels of abstraction doesn't mean you've actually experienced or understood premodern consciousness, any more than evolving from early mammals lets you know what it was like to be one.

One of the deepest problems with stage models of cultural development — whether Wilber's integral theory or Hanzi Freyja's metamodern framework — is what we might call the confusion of structure and content. The model of hierarchical complexity describes something real: a linear process of recursively coordinating mental schema into higher-order abstractions. That's a structural claim about cognition. But 'premodern' is a content claim — it bundles together specific historical worldviews, social arrangements, mythologies, and material conditions into a single label. When you conflate these two things, you end up saying something like 'I have access to premodern consciousness because I've moved through that developmental stage' — which is absurd. Just as evolving from early mammals doesn't give you access to what it was like to be an early mammal, moving through higher levels of cognitive complexity doesn't give you access to the lived experience of a Bronze Age forager. This confusion generates a kind of epistemic hubris: the assumption that you already understand someone else's worldview because you've 'been there' on the developmental ladder. It removes the hard work of actually trying to understand another perspective from the inside. The decentration move, properly understood, is about structural flexibility — the ability to toggle between levels of abstraction — not about having colonized all prior historical forms of consciousness.

## Apprentice

Many popular theories of human development arrange cultures and worldviews on a ladder — from "premodern" to "modern" to "postmodern" and beyond. The idea is that as individuals grow, they pass through these stages and eventually gain a higher vantage point that includes everything below. But there's a deep problem hiding in this picture.

The ladder part — the idea that our thinking can become more complex and abstract over time — is real enough. But labeling each rung with a historical era like "premodern" smuggles in something very different. It suggests that by reaching a higher stage, you somehow understand what it was like to live in the Bronze Age or hold a mythological worldview. That's like saying that because humans evolved from earlier mammals, we know what it felt like to be one.

This mix-up breeds a quiet arrogance. If you believe you've already "passed through" someone else's worldview on your way up the ladder, you lose the motivation to actually listen to them or understand their experience on its own terms. Real intellectual flexibility isn't about having absorbed all prior ways of thinking — it's about being able to shift between different levels of complexity without assuming you've mastered perspectives you've never truly inhabited.

## Adept

A persistent vulnerability in developmental stage models — from Ken Wilber's integral theory to Hanzi Freyja's metamodern framework — is the conflation of cognitive structure with cultural content. The Model of Hierarchical Complexity describes a genuine phenomenon: the recursive coordination of mental schemas into progressively higher-order abstractions. This is a structural claim about how cognition operates. But labels like "premodern," "modern," or "postmodern" are content claims — they bundle specific historical worldviews, material conditions, mythologies, and social arrangements into single developmental markers. The conflation of these two registers produces a category error with serious consequences.

The most damaging consequence is a form of epistemic hubris. If developmental progression is understood as literally passing through prior cultural formations, then someone at a "higher" stage can claim implicit access to the lived experience of, say, a Bronze Age forager or a medieval mystic. This is structurally analogous to claiming that because Homo sapiens evolved from early mammals, we have experiential access to early mammalian consciousness. The analogy exposes the absurdity: phylogenetic succession doesn't confer phenomenological access, and neither does cognitive-developmental succession.

This confusion also short-circuits genuine perspective-taking. If you assume you've already "been there" on the developmental ladder, the difficult hermeneutic labor of understanding another worldview from the inside becomes unnecessary. The decentration capacity that stage models rightly value is better understood as structural flexibility — the ability to toggle between levels of abstraction and coordinate multiple frames — rather than as the successful colonization of all prior historical forms of consciousness. Properly disambiguated, the structural insight remains valuable; the content claim collapses.

## Magus

The most consequential category error embedded in hierarchical stage models of cultural development — whether Wilber's integral AQAL framework or Freyja's metamodern developmental schema — is the systematic conflation of structural complexity with experiential-historical content. The Model of Hierarchical Complexity (Commons & Richards) identifies a formally defensible sequence: the recursive coordination of task-schemas into meta-systematic and paradigmatic orders of abstraction. This is a claim about the architecture of cognition, not about the phenomenology of inhabiting a particular lifeworld. Yet when stages are labeled "premodern," "modern," "postmodern," each rung absorbs into itself an entire civilizational gestalt — specific cosmologies, material infrastructures, affective textures, epistemic norms — and treats this absorption as entailed by the structural transition.

The result is a distinctive species of epistemic hubris operating under the guise of developmental inclusivity. The "transcend and include" formula implies that higher-order cognition metabolizes prior stages such that their content becomes available as lived understanding. But this trades on an equivocation between formal subsumption and hermeneutic access. Phylogenetic analogy sharpens the point: cladistic descent from therapsids confers no phenomenological access to therapsid experience. Likewise, the capacity to perform meta-systematic operations on schemas that can be abstractly labeled "mythic" or "traditional" does not constitute having inhabited mythic or traditional consciousness. The structural operation and the historical lifeworld are incommensurable registers.

What is forfeited in this conflation is precisely the epistemic humility that genuine decentration requires. If developmental attainment is mistaken for perspectival omniscience, the arduous interpretive work of entering another framework on its own terms — what Gadamer would call the fusion of horizons — is preemptively foreclosed. Decentration, rigorously conceived, names a structural capacity for toggling between orders of abstraction, not the colonial annexation of historically situated consciousness into a developmental taxonomy.
