# Value Is Not Arbitrary: Its Thermodynamic Roots in Existence Itself

Things either continue, or they don't.

---

> Value is not a human invention projected onto a neutral world. It emerges from the basic fact that any organized structure either persists or dissolves — an asymmetry that is intrinsic, not arbitrary — and this thermodynamic root complexifies through evolution without ever becoming merely subjective.

The relationship between value and meaning is one of the most philosophically underexplored areas in contemporary discourse, and the dominant postmodern framing — that values are arbitrary social constructs, purely subjective, culturally invented — has contributed directly to the meaning crisis we now find ourselves in. But there is a way out of this that doesn't require metaphysical hand-waving. When we talk about something being 'good' or 'bad,' we are almost always implicitly talking about 'good for' or 'bad for' — and that relational framing is the key. For any informationally organized entity that exists as a distinct configuration in an environment, there are conditions under which it continues to exist and conditions under which it does not. That asymmetry is not invented. It is not projected. It is intrinsic to the structure of being a thing at all. A dissipative structure — a whirlpool, a flame, a cell — has a normative orientation toward its own persistence not because it is conscious or because we attribute value to it, but simply because it is a thing, and things either continue or they don't. This is the thermodynamic root of value. What is remarkable is that this same basic dynamic complexifies through every level of cosmic evolution, from matter to life to mind to culture, taking on richer and richer forms without ever losing its connection to that original thermodynamic ground. By the time we reach human beings embedded in language and collective justification systems, value has become extraordinarily complex and socially mediated — but it has not become arbitrary. It has become more, not less, real.

## Apprentice

When people say something is 'good' or 'bad,' they almost always mean good or bad *for* something — for a person, a community, an ecosystem. This relational quality is a clue. Anything that exists as an organized structure in the world — a flame, a cell, an organism — faces a basic reality: there are conditions that keep it going and conditions that destroy it. That asymmetry isn't something humans invented. It's built into what it means to be a thing at all.

The dominant intellectual mood of recent decades has treated values as arbitrary — mere social constructs with no grounding in reality. This perspective has contributed to a widespread sense that life lacks inherent meaning. But the insight here is that value doesn't begin with human opinion. It begins with the physics of organized systems and their relationship to their environments.

What's striking is that this basic dynamic doesn't stay simple. As the universe produces more complex forms — from molecules to living cells to conscious minds to entire cultures — value takes on richer and more layered expressions. By the time it reaches human beings who argue about justice and beauty, it has become enormously complex and shaped by language and society. But it hasn't become fake. It has become *more* real, not less.

## Adept

This line of thinking identifies a critical gap in contemporary philosophy: the relationship between value and meaning has been inadequately theorized, and the prevailing postmodern consensus — that values are arbitrary social constructions — has directly fueled the modern meaning crisis. The proposed corrective begins with a deceptively simple observation: evaluative language is almost always implicitly relational. 'Good' means 'good for.' This relational structure points toward something ontologically grounded rather than merely projected.

The argument locates the origin of normativity in thermodynamics. Any informationally organized entity — any dissipative structure maintaining itself far from equilibrium — exists in an asymmetric relationship with its environment: some conditions sustain its configuration, others dissolve it. This asymmetry is intrinsic to the structure's mode of being. A whirlpool, a cell, a flame each have a normative orientation toward persistence not because consciousness or human attribution grants it, but because the alternative to persistence is dissolution. Value, in its most primitive form, is this structural asymmetry.

The deeper claim is that this thermodynamic ground of value does not get left behind as complexity increases. Through the successive emergent transitions of cosmic evolution — matter to life, life to mind, mind to culture — normativity complexifies and enriches without severing its connection to that original ground. Human values, embedded in language, collective justification, and cultural narrative, are extraordinarily mediated and layered. But they are not thereby arbitrary. The postmodern error was to mistake complexity and social mediation for groundlessness. The reality is the opposite: the more richly value is elaborated through emergent levels, the more — not less — real it becomes.

## Magus

This argument intervenes at the intersection of metaethics, thermodynamic ontology, and the philosophy of emergence to address what it identifies as a catastrophic lacuna in postmodern thought: the conflation of social mediation with ontological groundlessness in the domain of value. The prevailing constructivist consensus — that normativity is exhaustively explained by contingent cultural production — is diagnosed not merely as philosophically inadequate but as causally implicated in the contemporary meaning crisis. The corrective begins with the phenomenology of evaluative language itself: the relational structure of 'good for' and 'bad for' is not a pragmatic shorthand but an ontological signature.

The foundational move is to locate proto-normativity in the thermodynamics of dissipative structures. Any entity that maintains itself as a distinct informational configuration far from equilibrium instantiates an asymmetry between persistence-conducive and dissolution-conducive conditions. This asymmetry is neither observer-dependent nor attributively constituted; it is intrinsic to the entity's mode of being qua organized structure. Normativity, on this account, is coextensive with the existence of bounded, self-maintaining systems — it is as old as organization itself.

The architectonic claim is that this thermodynamic normativity undergoes successive complexification through each major transition in cosmic evolution — from autocatalytic chemistry to autopoietic life, from sentience to sapience, from individual cognition to collective justification systems — without undergoing ontological rupture. Each emergent level adds layers of mediation, abstraction, and reflexivity, but none introduces the discontinuity that would license treating higher-order values as free-floating constructs. Human meaning-making, saturated with language and intersubjective negotiation, represents not the point at which value becomes arbitrary but the point at which its reality achieves maximal depth. The postmodern error is precisely to read richness as rootlessness.
