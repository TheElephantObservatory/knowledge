# Three Metaphysical Claims That Make Post-Postmodern Spirituality Possible

Goodness pulls the universe forward.

---

> Steve McIntosh argues that three metaphysical claims — the reality of interior experience, the objective pull of values like goodness and truth, and the universe's inherent purposiveness — form the philosophical foundation for a worldview that transcends both religious dogma and secular materialism.

The fifth proposed agreement — affirming the interior universe, value realism, and the universe's purposiveness — represents what Steve McIntosh calls the philosophical foundation that makes the next cultural emergence possible at all. These three claims are not arbitrary spiritual preferences; they are the metaphysical preconditions for the kind of worldview that can transcend both religious fundamentalism and secular reductionism. The interior universe claim is that consciousness and intersubjectivity have genuine ontological weight — that worldviews are not merely analytical categories but actual intergenerational systems with a metabolism, sustained by values as a kind of interior energy. Value realism is the claim that goodness, truth, and beauty are not purely subjective preferences but have real causal efficacy in the world — they function as what McIntosh calls a cosmic attractor, the direction of evolution itself. And purposiveness is the claim that the universe is not a pointless accident — evidenced by the fine-tuning of physical constants, the inherent striving of all life, and the explosive creativity of the noosphere over fifty thousand years. Crucially, McIntosh reframes value not as a static object waiting to be discovered but as the direction of evolutionary progress — something that plays above the categories of objective and subjective, arising from their interaction. Together these three affirmations create what he describes as spiritual solidarity without requiring spiritual conformity — a shared orientation toward the good, the true, and the beautiful that can unite diverse spiritual paths without demanding doctrinal agreement, and that gives post-postmodern spirituality its primary superpower.

## Apprentice

Steve McIntosh proposes that any worldview capable of moving beyond both religious fundamentalism and flat materialism needs three foundational commitments. First, that our inner lives — consciousness, meaning, shared experience — are genuinely real, not just brain chemistry. Second, that values like goodness, truth, and beauty aren't just personal opinions but actually shape the world. Third, that the universe isn't a meaningless accident but shows signs of direction and creativity.

What makes this framework distinctive is that McIntosh doesn't treat values as fixed objects sitting out there waiting to be found. Instead, he sees them as the direction evolution is heading — something that emerges from the interplay between our inner experience and the outer world. Values pull us forward rather than sitting still.

Taken together, these three ideas create what McIntosh calls spiritual solidarity without spiritual conformity. People from very different traditions can share an orientation toward what is good, true, and beautiful without having to agree on doctrine or theology. This gives a post-secular spirituality real power: it can honor diverse paths while still standing on common philosophical ground.

## Adept

Steve McIntosh identifies three metaphysical affirmations as the necessary preconditions for a genuinely post-postmodern worldview: the ontological reality of the interior universe, value realism, and cosmic purposiveness. These are not spiritual preferences but structural requirements — without them, cultural evolution remains trapped in the oscillation between premodern dogmatism and postmodern deconstruction. The interior universe claim grants genuine ontological weight to consciousness and intersubjectivity, treating worldviews not as mere analytical categories but as intergenerational systems with their own metabolism, sustained by values functioning as a kind of interior energy.

Value realism, in McIntosh's formulation, holds that goodness, truth, and beauty possess real causal efficacy — they function as what he terms a cosmic attractor, the directional pull of evolution itself. Crucially, he reframes value not as a static Platonic object but as the direction of evolutionary progress, operating above the subject-object divide and arising from their dynamic interaction. This move sidesteps both naive objectivism and postmodern relativism.

Purposiveness completes the triad: the universe exhibits directionality evidenced by fine-tuned physical constants, the inherent striving of biological life, and the explosive creativity of the noosphere across fifty millennia. Together, these three affirmations generate what McIntosh describes as spiritual solidarity without spiritual conformity — a shared orientation toward transcendent values that can unite diverse contemplative and religious paths without demanding doctrinal agreement, providing the philosophical engine for the next stage of cultural emergence.

## Magus

McIntosh's fifth proposed agreement constitutes the metaphysical infrastructure for post-postmodern emergence: three interlocking claims — the ontological reality of interiority, value realism, and cosmic purposiveness — that function not as optional spiritual commitments but as the transcendental conditions for any worldview capable of dialectically integrating premodern depth and postmodern critique. The interior universe thesis asserts that consciousness and intersubjectivity possess genuine ontological standing, rendering worldviews actual intergenerational systems with metabolic dynamics sustained by values operating as interior energy. This is not phenomenological description but ontological claim: interiority is causally efficacious and irreducible.

Value realism, as McIntosh deploys it, performs a decisive philosophical maneuver. Goodness, truth, and beauty are neither Platonic forms awaiting discovery nor subjective projections — they are the direction of evolutionary process itself, functioning as a cosmic attractor. Value plays above the categories of objective and subjective, emergent from their interaction yet irreducible to either pole. This positions McIntosh's axiological framework as a process-relational alternative to both classical realism and constructivism, echoing Whitehead's treatment of value as constitutive of actual occasions while grounding it in observable evolutionary directionality.

Purposiveness completes the architectonic: fine-tuning arguments, biological teleonomy, and the explosive complexification of the noosphere across fifty thousand years converge as evidence for inherent cosmic directionality. The synthesis of all three affirmations yields what McIntosh terms spiritual solidarity without spiritual conformity — a shared axiological orientation that provides the binding force for diverse contemplative lineages without requiring doctrinal convergence. This is the primary superpower of the emerging cultural structure: metaphysical commitment sufficient to ground moral realism and spiritual seriousness, yet pluralistic enough to resist the totalizing closure of any single tradition.
