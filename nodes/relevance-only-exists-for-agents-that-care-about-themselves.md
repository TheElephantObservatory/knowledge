# Relevance Only Exists for Agents That Care About Themselves

Matter means weight; weight means you.

---

> Relevance is not a feature of the world but arises only for agents that sustain themselves. Intelligence and consciousness therefore rest on recursive relevance realization, which is inseparable from autopoietic agency — making all relevance ultimately self-relevance.

At the core of intelligence and consciousness lies what can be called recursive relevance realization — the capacity of an agent to zero in on relevant information from a combinatorially explosive field of possibilities. But relevance is not a property of the world in itself. Whether something is relevant depends entirely on who it is happening to and what they care about. A fly does not find this conversation relevant. A couch cannot care about it at all. This means relevance realization is grounded in agency — specifically in autopoietic, self-making agency. Only a system that is taking care of itself has needs, and only a system with needs can find anything relevant. The paramecium seeks food because food matters to it as a self-sustaining organism. Relevance realization and agency are therefore not two separate things — they co-define, co-determine, and co-depend on each other. There is no agency without recursive relevance realization, and there is no relevance realization without autopoietic agents. This leads to a crucial insight: all relevance is ultimately self-relevance. Not relevance to a self in the folk-psychological sense, but relevance to an organism that is sustaining itself. This is why our language for relevance is saturated with self-referential metaphors — we speak of things that 'matter,' of 'importing' information, of 'paying' attention. The word 'matter' literally means this thing has weight for me, it imports into my world. The convergent evidence from cognitive psychology suggests that self-relevance is the glue of cognition and perception — it is what binds information together and makes it stick. This architecture then becomes the theoretical bridge between the science of intelligence and consciousness on one side, and the psychology of the self on the other.

## Apprentice

How does any living thing decide what matters? Out of the infinite things happening around it, a creature somehow zeroes in on what is important and ignores the rest. This ability — called relevance realization — is arguably the deepest engine of intelligence. But here is the key move: relevance is not built into the world like gravity or temperature. Whether something is relevant depends entirely on who is encountering it and what they need. A fly does not care about this sentence. A rock cannot care about anything at all.

This means only beings that are actively keeping themselves alive — organisms that feed, repair, and sustain themselves — can find anything relevant in the first place. A single-celled organism seeks food because food matters to something that must maintain itself. Relevance and the capacity to act as a living agent are not two separate things; they arise together and depend on each other completely.

The striking conclusion is that all relevance is, at bottom, self-relevance — not in a narcissistic sense, but in the sense that information only "matters" to a system that is taking care of its own existence. Even our everyday language reveals this: we say things "matter" (they have weight for me), we "import" information (bring it into our world), we "pay" attention (spend our limited resources). This insight offers a powerful bridge connecting the science of intelligence with the psychology of the self.

## Adept

Recursive relevance realization — the capacity to iteratively zero in on what matters from a combinatorially explosive space of possibilities — is proposed as the core mechanism underlying intelligence and consciousness. Crucially, relevance is not an observer-independent property of the environment. It is relational: it exists only for an agent with needs, goals, and stakes. A paramecium finds a glucose gradient relevant; a stone finds nothing relevant. This asymmetry points to a deep structural dependency: relevance realization requires autopoietic agency, and autopoietic agency requires relevance realization. The two co-define and co-determine each other.

The implication is that all relevance is ultimately self-relevance — not relevance to a folk-psychological "self" but relevance to a self-sustaining, self-making system. An organism that must maintain its own organization is the minimal unit for which anything can matter. This is reflected in the very language of cognition: "matter" implies weight-for-me, "import" means bringing into one's world, "paying" attention treats cognitive resources as finite and personally owned. These are not mere metaphors — they reveal the autopoietic ground of cognitive life.

Convergent evidence from cognitive psychology supports this architecture. Self-referential processing enhances memory encoding, perceptual binding, and attentional capture. The self-relevance effect is among the most robust findings in the field. This framework therefore provides a theoretical bridge between the science of relevance realization — central to theories of intelligence and consciousness — and the psychology of the self. Agency, relevance, and selfhood are not three separate research programs but three faces of a single underlying structure.

## Magus

The thesis advanced here is that recursive relevance realization and autopoietic agency stand in a relation of constitutive co-dependence: neither is ontologically or explanatorily prior. Relevance realization — the iterative, context-sensitive narrowing of a combinatorially explosive problem space to what is salient — cannot operate in the absence of a system with needs, and needs arise only for entities engaged in self-production. The paramecium's chemotaxis is not mere mechanism; it is the minimal case of a self-sustaining system for which environmental features acquire valence. Relevance is therefore not a mind-independent property awaiting detection but an enactive achievement of organismic agency.

The deeper entailment is that all relevance is structurally self-relevance — relevance to an autopoietic unity maintaining its own organization against thermodynamic dissolution. This is not the self of folk psychology but the operational self of biological autonomy. The linguistic archaeology is instructive: "matter" derives from weight and substance, "import" from carrying-into, "attention" from attending-to as expenditure. These convergent etymologies are not accidental metaphors but sedimented traces of the autopoietic ground of cognition. They encode the insight that cognitive salience is always salience-for-an-organism-sustaining-itself.

This architecture yields a non-trivial theoretical unification. The self-reference effect in cognitive psychology — the robust enhancement of encoding, binding, and retrieval for self-relevant stimuli — ceases to be a curious empirical regularity and becomes a predictable consequence of the deep structure of relevance realization itself. The framework thus bridges the explanatory gap between the science of intelligence and consciousness, grounded in relevance realization, and the psychology of selfhood, grounded in self-referential processing. Agency, relevance, and self are revealed as three aspects of a single constitutive nexus rather than independent explananda requiring separate theoretical machinery.
