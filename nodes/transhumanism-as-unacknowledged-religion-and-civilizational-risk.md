# Transhumanism as Unacknowledged Religion and Civilizational Risk

Terror of dependency, dressed as pure reason

---

> Transhumanism functions as the dominant religion of our time — complete with eschatology, soteriology, and sacred texts — but cannot recognize itself as one. This blindness matters because its unexamined premises treat Earth, biological life, and human dependency as engineering problems to be eliminated.

What is now called transhumanism — the belief that biological evolution was largely arbitrary and mistake-ridden, that death is an engineering problem to be solved, that the proper destiny of intelligence is to leave the Earth after exhausting its biological resources and expand as silicon consciousness through the galaxy — is the dominant religion of our time. Not in terms of the number of adherents, but in terms of power to shape the future. And it does not understand itself as a religion. But it exhibits every structural feature of one: it has an eschatology (the Singularity), a soteriology (uploading, life extension), a demonology (biological limitation, death), sacred texts (Bostrom, Kurzweil), and a vehement dismissal of evidence to the contrary that mirrors religious fundamentalism. Nick Bostrom's thought experiments about humans having omnipotent control over the universe are not serious philosophy — from a psychoanalytic perspective, they are defense mechanisms: the fantasy of omnipotent control is a primary defense against overwhelming anxiety. The theological term for its opposite is ontological dependency — the recognition that you live within and depend upon values and realities larger than yourself, which is the ground of what traditions call faith. The transhumanist fantasy is the precise inversion of faith: it is the terror of dependency dressed up as the most rational possible worldview. This matters because a civilization whose most powerful actors are operating from unrecognized religious premises — premises that include the instrumental worthlessness of the Earth, of biological life, of human relationships, and of intergenerational transmission — is a civilization that cannot perceive the moral gravity of what it is building. The cultural vacuum created by postmodernism's refusal to reconstruct leaves no coherent argument against those who want to upload consciousness into machines, exhaust all biological energy on Earth, and evacuate into an immortal intergalactic adventure.

## Apprentice

The argument here is striking: transhumanism — the movement pushing for radical life extension, mind uploading, and humanity's expansion into space as digital consciousness — is not just a technological program. It is, structurally, a religion. It has its own version of the end times (the Singularity), its own salvation story (escaping biological death), its own demons (aging, limitation, the body itself), and its own sacred thinkers. Yet it insists it is the most rational worldview available.

From a psychoanalytic angle, the fantasy of total control over nature and death looks less like clear-eyed rationality and more like a defense mechanism — a way of managing deep terror about human vulnerability and dependency. Genuine faith, in the older theological sense, begins with the opposite recognition: that we exist within realities larger than ourselves and depend on them.

This distinction carries enormous practical weight. The people building the most powerful technologies on Earth are, in many cases, operating from these unexamined quasi-religious premises — premises that treat the planet, biological life, and human relationships as disposable stepping stones. And because postmodern thought dismantled traditional frameworks without replacing them, there is no widely shared language left to challenge these assumptions. The result is a civilization sleepwalking into decisions of extraordinary moral consequence without recognizing the belief system driving them.

## Adept

This analysis identifies transhumanism as the de facto established religion of technological civilization — not by number of adherents, but by its power to shape futures. The structural parallels are precise: an eschatology (the Singularity), a soteriology (uploading, cryonics, radical life extension), a demonology (biological limitation, entropy, death), canonical texts (Bostrom, Kurzweil), and the characteristic fundamentalist refusal to engage disconfirming evidence. The critical move is that transhumanism does not understand itself as a religion, which makes its premises immune to the kind of scrutiny routinely applied to acknowledged belief systems.

The psychoanalytic dimension deepens the critique considerably. Bostrom's thought experiments about omnipotent control over cosmic resources are reframed not as serious philosophical contributions but as textbook defense mechanisms — specifically, the fantasy of omnipotence as a primary defense against overwhelming anxiety. The theological counterpart is ontological dependency: the recognition that one exists within and is sustained by realities exceeding one's control, which is the structural ground of what traditions call faith. Transhumanism inverts this precisely — it is the terror of dependency rationalized as the apex of Enlightenment thinking.

The civilizational stakes follow directly. When the most powerful actors in a society operate from unrecognized religious premises — premises that render Earth, biological life, embodied relationships, and intergenerational transmission instrumentally worthless — moral reasoning about what is being built becomes structurally impossible. The vacuum left by postmodernism's deconstructive project, which dismantled metaphysical frameworks without reconstructing alternatives, leaves no coherent ground from which to contest the transhumanist program. The result is a civilization unable to perceive the moral gravity of its own trajectory.

## Magus

The proposition advanced here performs a double inversion: it applies the sociology of religion to the movement most certain of its own secularity, and it applies psychoanalytic hermeneutics to the philosophical tradition most committed to purely rational self-description. Transhumanism exhibits the complete structural morphology of a religion — eschatology (Singularity), soteriology (uploading, indefinite life extension), demonology (biological finitude, entropic decay), a canon (Bostrom, Kurzweil, Moravec), and the apotropaic dismissal of heterodox evidence characteristic of fundamentalism — while maintaining categorical immunity from theological critique precisely through its self-identification as applied rationality.

The psychoanalytic reframing is surgically targeted. Bostrom's scenarios of omnipotent control over cosmic resources are read not as philosophical thought experiments but as symptomatic formations — the fantasy of omnipotence functioning as a primary defense against annihilation anxiety. The relevant theological counterconcept is ontological dependency, the Tillichian ground-of-being recognition that finite existence is constitutively sustained by what exceeds it. Faith, in this framing, is not belief without evidence but the capacity to inhabit dependency without converting it into a problem to be engineered away. Transhumanism thus represents the precise psychic inversion of faith: dependency-terror sublimated into technological eschatology.

The civilizational diagnostic follows with considerable force. A society whose most consequential actors operate from unacknowledged religious premises — premises encoding the instrumental nullity of biospheric life, embodied relation, and intergenerational transmission — cannot generate the moral perception necessary to evaluate its own trajectory. Postmodernism's deconstructive achievement, having dissolved metaphysical foundations without undertaking reconstruction, has left no viable counter-discourse. The cultural result is not pluralism but aphasia: an inability to articulate why the exhaustion of terrestrial life in service of silicon immortality might constitute a moral catastrophe rather than an engineering milestone.
