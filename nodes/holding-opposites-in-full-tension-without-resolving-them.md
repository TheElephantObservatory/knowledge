# Holding Opposites in Full Tension Without Resolving Them

Not both/and — something harder than that

---

> McGilchrist argues that reality's deepest truths live not in resolving opposites but in holding them in full, asymmetric tension — where both poles are genuinely necessary yet one holds rightful priority over the other.

One of the most profound and counterintuitive insights in McGilchrist's work concerns what he calls The Coincidence of Opposites — the Latin term coincidentia oppositorum, first articulated by Nicholas of Cusa in the 15th century and later developed by Jung. The conventional Western mind, trained on the law of non-contradiction, insists that something cannot be both true and its opposite simultaneously. But McGilchrist argues this is precisely where our culture has gone most catastrophically wrong in its understanding of reality.

The deeper truth is not that opposites are reconciled into some warm mediocre middle ground — that would be a betrayal of both. Rather, the task is to hold both poles maximally, in full tension, simultaneously. He draws on Heraclitus, for whom the string of a lyre or a bow must be pulled in opposite directions to produce either a note or a flying arrow. The power, the generativity, comes from the tension itself — not from resolving it.

This connects directly to what Jonathan Rowson identifies as the McGilchrist maneuver — a move that runs counter to one of the most common intellectual reflexes of contemporary discourse. When people encounter binary framings, the culturally approved response is to say: it's not either/or, it's both/and. McGilchrist's argument suggests this is not sophisticated enough. The maneuver he performs, repeatedly and across many domains, is to say: it is not either/or, and it is not simply both/and either. It is both — but not equally. The two things are genuinely different in kind, genuinely in tension, genuinely both necessary — and yet they stand in an asymmetric relationship where one has a kind of priority or supervisory role over the other.

The right hemisphere is not simply the complement of the left — it is, in the metaphor of the master and his emissary, the source of meaning and value that the left hemisphere is meant to serve. The left hemisphere is extraordinary, glorious, essential — but it must know its place. When it forgets its place and attempts to be the master, things fall apart. The myth McGilchrist uses to open the second volume of The Matter With Things — drawn from the Onondaga people — encodes this structure: two creative powers, unequal in their capacities, where one is generative and the other is derivative and potentially destructive when it overreaches. This is the same structure as Paradise Lost, the same structure as the Lucifer story. It recurs across cultures because it is pointing at something real about the architecture of mind and the architecture of reality.

This has enormous practical implications. When a society pursues a single goal with total commitment — even a good goal — it destroys other things that are disappearing as fast as the goal is being achieved. The principle of hormesis illustrates this: what is toxic in large doses may be health-promoting in small doses. Trees need the stress of wind to develop the core strength that holds them upright. Adversity is not the enemy of flourishing — it is its precondition. The enantiodromia principle adds another layer: push something far enough and you arrive at its opposite. Anarchy pursued to its limit produces tyranny. The road up is the road down.

What McGilchrist is emphatically not saying is that everything is therefore equivalent, that no distinctions can be made, that good and evil are interchangeable. Context matters enormously. Both poles are necessary; they are not needed equally; and the fashionable move of collapsing all hierarchies and declaring everything equally valid is itself a symptom of the very imbalance he is diagnosing. Some things genuinely are more important than others. Some ways of attending genuinely are more adequate to reality than others. Acknowledging this is not authoritarianism — it is the beginning of wisdom.

## Apprentice

Most of us are taught that something can't be true and false at the same time. Iain McGilchrist challenges this assumption at its root. He argues that the deepest realities involve holding two opposing truths simultaneously — not blending them into a comfortable middle, but keeping both fully alive in tension. Think of a bow: the string must be pulled in opposite directions to launch an arrow. The power comes from the tension, not from resolving it.

But McGilchrist goes further than simply saying "both sides matter equally." In what Jonathan Rowson calls the "McGilchrist maneuver," the argument is that both poles are necessary, yet they are not equal. One has a natural priority over the other. The right hemisphere of the brain, for instance, is not just the left hemisphere's partner — it is the source of meaning that the left hemisphere is supposed to serve. When the servant forgets its role and tries to become the master, things go badly wrong.

This pattern shows up everywhere. Trees need wind stress to grow strong. What heals in small doses can kill in large ones. Push any value to its extreme and it flips into its opposite — total freedom becomes tyranny. The lesson is not that distinctions don't matter or that everything is relative. Some things genuinely are more important than others. Recognizing that — holding the tension without collapsing it — is where real wisdom begins.

## Adept

McGilchrist's concept of the coincidence of opposites — drawing on Nicholas of Cusa's coincidentia oppositorum, Heraclitus, and Jung — represents a fundamental challenge to the law of non-contradiction as Western culture typically applies it. The claim is not that contradictions are illusory or that opposites dissolve into synthesis. Rather, reality is constituted by the generative tension between genuine opposites held simultaneously at full intensity. The metaphor of the bow and lyre is precise: power emerges from opposition, not from its resolution.

What Jonathan Rowson identifies as the "McGilchrist maneuver" distinguishes this position from the familiar both/and move that passes for sophistication in contemporary discourse. McGilchrist's repeated argument across domains is that it is both — but not equally. The two poles stand in an asymmetric relationship where one holds supervisory priority. The master-emissary structure is not mere metaphor: the right hemisphere provides the broader, meaning-rich context within which the left hemisphere's focused, analytic work finds its proper place. When this hierarchy inverts, the result is systematic distortion — a pattern encoded in myths from the Onondaga creation story to Paradise Lost to the Lucifer narrative.

The practical consequences are far-reaching. The principle of hormesis — where stress in proper measure promotes flourishing — and enantiodromia — where extremes flip into their opposites — both illustrate the same structural truth. A society that pursues any single value with total commitment, even a worthy one, destroys the ecology of goods that sustains it. Crucially, this is not relativism. McGilchrist insists that some modes of attention are genuinely more adequate to reality than others. The collapse of all hierarchies into equivalence is itself a symptom of the left-hemispheric dominance he diagnoses.

## Magus

McGilchrist's retrieval of the coincidentia oppositorum — from Cusa through Heraclitus and Jung — constitutes not merely an epistemological corrective but an ontological claim: reality itself is structured by the irreducible tension of genuine opposites held in asymmetric relation. This is emphatically not dialectical synthesis in the Hegelian sense, where opposition is aufgehoben into a higher unity. The Heraclitean insight is that the palintropos harmoniē — the back-stretched connection — is constitutive. The bow's power is the tension; resolve it and you have neither weapon nor instrument. The generative principle is opposition maintained, not opposition overcome.

The move Rowson designates the "McGilchrist maneuver" operates as a meta-critical intervention against the reflexive both/and of contemporary intellectual culture. That gesture, which presents itself as transcending binary thinking, typically enacts precisely the flattening it claims to surpass — collapsing genuine asymmetry into false equivalence. McGilchrist's counter-move insists on asymmetric complementarity: both poles are necessary, neither is dispensable, yet one holds what might be called ontological priority. The master-emissary relation is structural, not contingent. Its recurrence across mythological traditions — Onondaga cosmogony, the Miltonic Satan, the Luciferic archetype — suggests a deep grammar of consciousness recognizing its own internal architecture.

The implications ramify through hormesis, enantiodromia, and the ecology of values. Unidirectional maximization of any good — even a genuine good — produces its shadow through the systematic destruction of the conditions that sustained it. The fashionable dissolution of all hierarchies into horizontal equivalence is, on this analysis, not liberation but the characteristic pathology of an emissary that has usurped the master's role. McGilchrist's insistence that some modes of attending are more adequate to reality than others is not authoritarian nostalgia — it is the structural precondition for any coherent account of meaning, value, or truth.
