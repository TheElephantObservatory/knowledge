# Why Higher Development Stages Become Brittle and Invite Fascist Rollback

The aggression that leaks through the smile

---

> Higher developmental stages, as typically lived, produce brittle pseudo-integration — people who want to have already transcended conflict rather than having worked through it. The antidote is not more sophisticated theory but a sturdier unity that keeps the visceral intensities of conflict in play.

Higher developmental stages, as they are actually lived in communities, tend to produce neutered or hollowed-out versions of integration — people who want to have already transcended conflict rather than people who have actually worked through it. At the green level, it becomes almost impossible for anyone to acknowledge that they carry aggression at all — except in their reactions to people they perceive as aggressive, which is where the aggression leaks out anyway. The result is that higher stages, as they are typically instantiated, are not actually more robust or anti-fragile — they are more brittle. They float in small pools of intellectuals without being able to compete in the broader civilization. The roll-back toward earlier stages that we call fascism is not simply evil; it is a natural response when people cannot trust that the level they have reached is actually working. What is needed is a version of higher-stage development that keeps the intensities and visceralities of conflict in play — that does not smooth them away prematurely — while also genuinely integrating them into something more coherent. The alternative to that roll-back is not a more sophisticated theory of unity — it is the demonstrated confidence that you can incorporate new and difficult things and solve the problem of diversity not by cutting it away but by integrating it into a new, sturdier unity.

## Apprentice

There's a common assumption that people who've reached more advanced or progressive ways of thinking are naturally stronger and more capable of handling difference. But in practice, the opposite often happens. Communities that pride themselves on being evolved tend to produce people who perform harmony rather than earn it. They want to have already moved past conflict, so they suppress aggression — except it leaks out sideways, usually aimed at whoever they see as less enlightened.

This makes these communities surprisingly fragile. They can sustain themselves in small, like-minded groups, but they can't hold up under real pressure or compete in the messiness of broader society. When people sense that the "higher" way of living isn't actually working — that it can't handle real disagreement or real threat — they naturally retreat to older, rougher ways of organizing. That retreat is what gets called fascism, and while it's destructive, it's not simply evil. It's what happens when trust in the newer way collapses.

The real solution isn't a better theory of togetherness. It's a version of advanced development that doesn't smooth away conflict prematurely — one that keeps the raw, uncomfortable intensities alive while genuinely weaving them into something more coherent. The goal is a demonstrated ability to absorb difficult, disruptive differences and build a tougher kind of unity, not by cutting away what's hard but by actually integrating it.

## Adept

Developmental models — particularly those influenced by Spiral Dynamics, Kegan, or integral theory — assume that higher stages represent greater capacity for integration and complexity. This insight challenges that assumption at the level of instantiation rather than theory. As actually lived in progressive or "green" communities, higher stages tend to produce a hollowed-out integration: people who have intellectually committed to transcending conflict but have not metabolized the aggression, tribalism, and competitive drives that earlier stages kept in open play. The aggression doesn't vanish — it re-emerges as moral policing, reactive hostility toward perceived aggressors, and a brittle intolerance of anyone who disrupts the community's self-image of having moved beyond intolerance.

The consequence is that these communities are less anti-fragile than the stages they claim to have surpassed. They survive in small intellectual enclaves but cannot scale or compete civilizationally. When broader populations sense that the higher stage cannot actually deliver on its promise — cannot handle genuine diversity, real threat, or unresolved contradiction — the result is regression. The authoritarian rollback commonly labeled fascism is, from this vantage, not a mysterious eruption of evil but a predictable response to the failure of the stage above it to demonstrate functional superiority.

What is needed is not yet another meta-theory of unity but a developmental practice that retains the visceral intensities of conflict — the embodied, agonistic dimensions that earlier stages kept alive — while genuinely integrating them into a more coherent and capacious structure. The alternative to civilizational regression is not sophistication but demonstrated robustness: the visible, felt confidence that new and difficult elements can be incorporated rather than expelled, and that diversity can be solved by building sturdier unity rather than by premature smoothing.

## Magus

The standard developmental teleology — from egocentric to ethnocentric to worldcentric, from pre-conventional to post-conventional — encodes an implicit claim that each successive stage subsumes and strengthens what came before. This insight identifies a systematic failure at the level of enactment: higher stages as they are typically instantiated do not sublate lower-stage intensities but repress them, producing what might be called pseudo-integration. At the green or pluralistic level, the disavowal of aggression becomes structurally constitutive of group identity, yet the aggression persists as a return of the repressed — channeled into precisely the reactive hostility directed at those perceived as carriers of the disavowed quality. The community's self-understanding as post-conflictual is maintained only by externalizing conflict onto designated others.

This dynamic renders the higher stage more brittle, not more anti-fragile. It can sustain itself within bounded intellectual milieus but lacks the robustness to function at civilizational scale. The periodic regression toward authoritarian and ethnocentric formations — the phenomenon typically moralized as fascism — becomes legible not as atavistic irruption but as a rational defection from a developmental level that has failed to demonstrate functional superiority. Trust in the higher stage collapses precisely because its integration is performative rather than structural, and populations default to stages whose capacities, however limited, are at least genuinely operative.

The corrective is not a more refined theoretical synthesis but a mode of developmental practice that preserves the agonistic, visceral, and embodied dimensions of earlier-stage engagement while achieving genuine structural integration. The problem of diversity is not solved by abstracting away from its difficulty but by demonstrating — in lived, competitive, high-stakes contexts — the capacity to incorporate the disruptive and the incommensurable into a unity that is sturdier precisely because it has not been purchased through premature smoothing or disavowal.
