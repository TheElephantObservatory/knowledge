# Meaning in the Noise: Relevance Realization and Optimal Grip

John Vervaeke's theory of relevance realization proposes that the fundamental criterion of cognition is not computation in the abstract, but the capacity to intelligently ignore most of the available information in an environment and home in on what actually matters. The world contains, in Shannon's sense, an effectively infinite amount of information — no organism could process all of it. The cognitive challenge is not to process more, but to filter better: to develop mechanisms that reliably distinguish meaningful signals from noise in ways that serve the organism's goals and survival. Vervaeke and colleagues identify a set of opponent processes built into cognitive dynamics — compression versus particularization, assimilation versus accommodation, integration versus differentiation — that together allow the brain to find what he calls the optimal grip on its context. A system that simultaneously integrates and differentiates its functions is complexifying, and complex systems develop emergent functions; cognitive learning is therefore not merely the accumulation of information but a genuine developmental process in which the brain becomes more complex, more capable, and more finely tuned to the structure of its world. Vervaeke captures this with the concept of transjectivity — meaning is neither purely subjective nor purely objective, but arises in the dynamic relational exchange between an agent and its arena, a formulation that applies not just to minded animals but to every level of complexity at which entity-field relationships generate meaning. This bridges directly with Friston's free energy principle: both frameworks converge on the question of which subset of the infinite sea of environmental information is relevant, which signals the nervous system should attend to, and which can be safely ignored.

## Apprentice

Every moment, your brain is bombarded with an overwhelming flood of information — sights, sounds, smells, memories, possibilities. The strange thing is that thinking well has almost nothing to do with processing more of this flood. It has everything to do with knowing what to ignore. John Vervaeke's theory of relevance realization proposes that this filtering capacity is the very heart of cognition. The brain is not a computer crunching through data; it is a system that has learned, over a lifetime and over evolution, to find what actually matters in any given situation. This is harder than it sounds. What matters shifts depending on your goals, your context, and what you already know. Vervaeke argues that the brain manages this through a set of competing pressures — compressing patterns while staying alert to exceptions, absorbing new information while also revising old assumptions. These tensions, held in balance, allow the mind to develop an increasingly refined sense of what is relevant. Meaning, in this view, is not something the world hands you, nor something you invent alone — it emerges from the live relationship between you and your environment.

## Adept

Vervaeke's theory of relevance realization addresses what he identifies as the frame problem at the heart of cognition: given an environment containing effectively infinite information in Shannon's sense, no finite system can process everything, yet intelligent agents reliably home in on what matters. The question is how. Vervaeke's answer draws on a set of opponent processing dynamics — compression versus particularization, assimilation versus accommodation, integration versus differentiation — that together constitute the brain's mechanism for achieving what he calls an optimal grip on its context. These are not sequential operations but simultaneous tensions whose balance is continuously renegotiated as the agent moves through its environment. Crucially, a system that integrates and differentiates its functions at the same time is complexifying in a technical sense: it is generating emergent functional organization. Cognitive development is therefore not the accumulation of more information but a genuine increase in systemic complexity, producing finer and finer sensitivity to the structure of the world. Vervaeke introduces the concept of transjectivity to capture the ontological status of meaning in this framework: meaning is neither a property of the subject nor a feature of the object, but arises in the dynamic relational exchange between agent and arena. This formulation generalizes beyond minded animals to any level of organization at which entity-field relationships produce adaptive salience. The convergence with Friston's free energy principle is significant: both frameworks center on the same core question — which subset of environmental information a system should model, minimize surprise about, and attend to — arriving from different directions at a shared account of adaptive relevance.

## Magus

Vervaeke's relevance realization framework intervenes at the intersection of cognitive science, philosophy of mind, and complex systems theory to dissolve what he regards as a foundational confusion in classical computationalism: the assumption that cognition is well-characterized as information processing over explicitly represented content. The frame problem — the inability of any finite representational system to determine, from within its own resources, which facts are relevant to a given inference — is not a technical limitation awaiting a better algorithm but a structural feature of the relationship between finite agents and informationally unbounded environments. Relevance realization proposes that the brain's solution is not representational but dynamical: a set of opponent processes — compression/particularization, assimilation/accommodation, integration/differentiation — whose continuous co-regulation produces adaptive salience without recourse to a meta-level relevance detector, which would merely reproduce the problem at a higher order. The theoretical weight here falls on the integration-differentiation axis: a system that simultaneously increases its internal differentiation and its integrative coherence is, in Edelman and Tononi's sense, complexifying — generating irreducible informational structure and, with it, emergent functional capacities. Learning, on this account, is not epistemic accumulation but ontogenetic complexification of the cognitive system itself. Vervaeke's concept of transjectivity formalizes the relational ontology implicit in this picture: meaning supervenes neither on agent-internal states nor on environment-external properties but on the dynamical coupling between agent and arena, a formulation that is scale-invariant across levels of biological organization. The structural homology with Friston's free energy principle is non-trivial: both frameworks converge on a variational account of adaptive relevance in which the organism's generative model is continuously revised to minimize the divergence between predicted and actual sensory states, effectively operationalizing relevance realization as the gradient descent of surprise across the agent-environment interface.
