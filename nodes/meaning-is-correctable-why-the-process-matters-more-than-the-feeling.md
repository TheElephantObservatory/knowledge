# Meaning Is Correctable: Why the Process Matters More Than the Feeling

The sacred lives at the edge of collapse

---

> The feeling of meaning can be wrong — you can discover your life's meaning was built on a lie. What actually matters is not the sense of meaning but the process that corrects it when it fails. That self-correcting process, operating at the edge of order and chaos, is what we experience as sacred.

The subjective sense of meaning in life and the process by which that sense is updated when it fails are not the same thing, and conflating them produces a distorted picture of what meaning is and where its value lies. The sense of meaning is subjective in a precise sense: you can be wrong about it. A person can invest twenty years of meaning in a relationship that turns out to have been built on betrayal — and in retrospect, that meaning was an illusion. If you can be wrong about meaning, there must be something non-subjective that provides the normative standard by which the error is recognized. Meaning cannot be equated to the feeling of meaning, because there has to be something that corrects that feeling when it is false. What carries the real value, then, is not the achieved sense of meaning but the process by which meaning is updated in the face of anomalies — the process of dissolution and reintegration when the prior frame fails. This is why, in empirical research on mystical experience and meaning in life, it is not the phenomenological content of the experience that predicts lasting change in meaning, but the insight factor — the degree to which the experience involved genuine realization. The sacred, on this account, is not a particular content or a stable state. It is the inexhaustible process of intelligibility itself: the ongoing capacity to update, integrate, and re-grip reality when prior framings break down. That process, when undergone optimally, is what takes place at the border between order and chaos — which is why it is felt as sacred.

## Apprentice

Most people think meaning is a feeling — if your life feels meaningful, it is. But consider someone who pours twenty years into a relationship only to discover it was built on betrayal. In hindsight, the meaning they felt was an illusion. If you can be wrong about meaning, then meaning can't just be the feeling of meaning. There has to be something deeper that lets you recognize the error.

That something deeper is the process of updating. When your framework for making sense of life breaks down, you go through a painful dissolution and then, if things go well, a reintegration — a new way of seeing that accounts for what the old way couldn't. This process, not any particular settled feeling, is where the real value lives.

This also explains something interesting from research on mystical experiences: what predicts lasting positive change isn't the content of the experience — the visions or emotions — but the degree of genuine insight involved. The sacred isn't a thing you arrive at and hold onto. It's the ongoing capacity to fall apart and come back together with a truer grip on reality. That's why it always feels like it lives at the border between order and chaos.

## Adept

This insight draws a sharp distinction between the subjective sense of meaning and the normative process that governs its correction. The sense of meaning is subjective in a precise, philosophically loaded way: it admits of error. A person can experience profound meaning in a context later revealed as fraudulent, which means the phenomenology of meaning and meaning itself cannot be identical. If meaning were reducible to its felt quality, there would be no standard by which to recognize that the feeling was mistaken. The existence of meaning-error implies a non-subjective normative dimension — something that adjudicates between genuine and illusory meaning.

The argument then locates the real locus of value not in any achieved state of meaningfulness but in the process by which meaning is dissolved and reconstituted when prior framings fail. This is a process-relational account: meaning is not a stable possession but an ongoing dynamic of integration, breakdown, and reintegration. Empirical support comes from research on mystical and transformative experiences, where the "insight factor" — the degree of genuine realization — predicts lasting changes in meaning, while phenomenological intensity alone does not.

The sacred, on this account, is reconceived as the inexhaustible process of intelligibility itself: the capacity to update one's grip on reality at the edge where established order meets anomaly. This reframes sacredness not as a content, an object, or a terminal state, but as the optimal functioning of the self-correcting, self-transcending process that operates precisely at the border between order and chaos — which is why encounters with it carry the phenomenological signature of the numinous.

## Magus

The argument hinges on a deceptively simple observation with far-reaching consequences: the sense of meaning is fallible, and fallibility entails a normative gap between phenomenology and truth-conditions. If one can be wrong about meaning — as when decades of existential investment are retroactively voided by the revelation of betrayal — then meaning cannot be constituted by its felt quality. There must be a normative standard external to the phenomenological state that renders the judgment of error intelligible. This is not merely an epistemic point but an ontological one: meaning has mind-independent conditions of adequacy, even though it is always experienced from within a participatory frame.

What fills this normative role, the argument proposes, is not a Platonic form or a fixed telos but a dynamic process — specifically, the process of dissolution and reintegration that occurs when an existing framework of intelligibility encounters recalcitrant anomaly. John Vervaeke's relevance realization framework and related 4E cognitive science provide the theoretical scaffolding here: meaning is constituted by the ongoing optimization of the agent-arena relationship, and its value resides in the self-correcting, self-transcending capacity of that process rather than in any particular equilibrium state it achieves. The empirical literature on psilocybin-occasioned mystical experience corroborates this — the insight factor, not phenomenological intensity, predicts durable restructuring of meaning.

The sacred is thus identified with the inexhaustible generativity of intelligibility itself: the meta-systematic process that operates at the phase transition between order and chaos, perpetually capable of integrating what prior frames could not accommodate. This positions sacredness not as a domain, substance, or experience-type, but as the optimal functioning of the participatory process by which reality discloses itself through the very breakdown and reconstitution of the agent's grip upon it.
