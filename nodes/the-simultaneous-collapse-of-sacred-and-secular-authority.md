# The Simultaneous Collapse of Sacred and Secular Authority

The hollow sound of a broken compass

---

> Two of humanity's great meaning-making systems — organized religion and liberal modernity — are collapsing at the same time, leaving a vacuum that extremism and nihilism are rushing to fill.

We are in the midst of a dual collapse — both of meaning 1.0 and meaning 2.0 — happening almost simultaneously. Meaning 1.0 is organized religion, the oldest and longest-standing framework humans have used to make sense of the world. Meaning 2.0 is modern liberalism: everything from the French Enlightenment through civil society, democracy, civil rights, and open markets. What's striking is that these two pillars are crumbling at the same time, and in that vacuum, culture abhors emptiness just as much as nature does. We're seeing people get sucked toward the extremes — fundamentalism on one end, and nihilism on the other. The Pew Research Foundation has documented the dramatic rise of the 'nones' — people who are spiritual but not religious, or outright atheist or agnostic — now the largest and fastest-growing denomination in North America. But it's not just religious authority that's collapsed. Benign secular authority has too: the Walter Cronkites are gone, no one trusts fact-checkers, the journals of record have become politicized, academia is rocked by replication crises and admissions scandals, medicine has been implicated in the opioid epidemic, Goldman Sachs and McKinsey have been caught acting in bad faith, and Silicon Valley went from 'making the world a better place' to 'breaking democracy.' The core heuristic humans use — what should I think? Let me look at what smarter, more credible, more established people think — has been systematically undermined across every major institution. The result is a meaning crisis: not just a political or economic crisis, but a crisis in the shared substrate of reality itself.

## Apprentice

For most of human history, people relied on two big systems to make sense of the world. The first was organized religion, which provided answers about why we're here and how to live. The second was the modern liberal order — democracy, science, trusted institutions, a shared sense of progress — which took over as religion faded for many people. The unsettling argument here is that both are now failing at once.

The evidence is everywhere. Record numbers of people are leaving organized religion entirely. But secular authority is also crumbling: the trusted news anchors are gone, fact-checkers are dismissed as partisan, universities are mired in scandal, doctors got caught fueling the opioid crisis, and the tech industry abandoned its idealism. The basic human habit of looking to credible, established sources for guidance has been broken across nearly every institution simultaneously.

When both systems fail together, the result isn't just confusion — it's a meaning crisis. People don't simply stop believing; they get pulled toward the edges. Some retreat into rigid fundamentalism. Others slide into nihilism, the feeling that nothing matters. The danger isn't just political polarization. It's the loss of a shared sense of reality itself.

## Adept

The concept of a 'meaning crisis' — developed most rigorously by philosopher John Vervaeke — typically traces the erosion of existential frameworks to a long arc beginning with the Copernican revolution and accelerating through Nietzsche's diagnosis of nihilism. This framing extends that analysis by identifying two discrete layers of meaning-making infrastructure that are now collapsing in close succession: Meaning 1.0, the cosmological and moral architecture of organized religion, and Meaning 2.0, the Enlightenment-derived liberal order encompassing democratic institutions, epistemic authorities, and market-based civil society.

What makes the current moment distinctive is the simultaneity of these failures. The secularization thesis long predicted the decline of Meaning 1.0, but assumed Meaning 2.0 would serve as a stable successor. Instead, institutional trust has collapsed across every major domain at once — journalism, academia, medicine, finance, and technology — undermining the core epistemic heuristic by which individuals defer to credentialed, established authorities. Pew's data on the 'nones' captures the religious dimension, but the deeper phenomenon is the delegitimization of benign secular authority in parallel.

The resulting vacuum follows a predictable cultural logic: nature abhors a vacuum, and so does the human psyche. The poles that rush in are fundamentalism — a regression to Meaning 1.0 with added rigidity — and nihilism, the affective collapse into meaninglessness. The crisis is therefore not merely sociological or political but epistemological: it concerns the shared substrate of reality-construction that makes coordinated social life possible.

## Magus

The argument advanced here maps onto, but significantly extends, the secularization literature and the meaning-crisis discourse associated with Vervaeke, Charles Taylor's 'A Secular Age,' and Robert Bellah's work on civil religion. The analytical move is to periodize meaning-making infrastructure into two successive regimes — Meaning 1.0 as the pre-modern cosmological-religious complex, and Meaning 2.0 as the post-Enlightenment liberal epistemic order — and to identify their concurrent delegitimization as the defining civilizational condition of the present moment.

Taylor's 'immanent frame' and Habermas's account of communicative rationality both presupposed that the disenchantment of Meaning 1.0 would be absorbed by robust secular institutions capable of sustaining intersubjective norms. What this framing contests is that presumption of sequential substitution. The empirical record — institutional replication failures, the opioid-pharmaceutical complex, the financialization scandals implicating Goldman Sachs and McKinsey, the democratic destabilization wrought by platform capitalism — suggests instead a cascading delegitimization of the very authorities that constituted Meaning 2.0's epistemic backbone. The Cronkite-era 'view from nowhere' was not merely a journalistic convention but a load-bearing fiction of liberal epistemology; its collapse is symptomatic, not incidental.

The psychodynamic consequence follows Durkheimian anomie logic but with greater structural determinism: when both the sacred and the civic-rational orders lose their legitimating force simultaneously, the individual is not liberated into reflexive autonomy but is instead subjected to a centrifugal pull toward regressive certainty (neo-fundamentalism) or affective dissolution (nihilism). The crisis is, at its root, an onto-epistemic one — a fracture in the shared construction of the real that precedes and subtends political, economic, and cultural dysfunction.
