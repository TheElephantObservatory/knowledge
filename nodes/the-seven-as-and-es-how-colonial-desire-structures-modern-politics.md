# The Seven A's and E's: How Colonial Desire Structures Modern Politics

Every savior wears the same grammar.

---

> Modern politics — left and right alike — runs on a hidden operating system of colonial desire: seven drives (Authority, Arbitration, Autonomy, Affirmation, Appropriation, Accumulation, Acceleration) that translate into predictable political patterns (Exceptionalism, Exaltedness, Externalization, Expansion, Emancipation from entanglement). Any movement blind to this architecture will reproduce it.

The dominant political grammar of our moment — across left and right, progressive and reactionary — is structured by what might be called the seven A's and their political translation into seven E's. The seven A's describe the ontological architecture of colonial desire: Authority (the claim to moral and epistemic superiority), Arbitration (the right to determine what counts as truth, beauty, and common sense), Autonomy (unrestricted and unaccountable freedom granted by that authority), Affirmation (the need for validation of one's goodness and virtue to sustain the whole structure), Appropriation and Accumulation (the entitlement to extract capital that flows from the above), and Acceleration (the drive to advance a particular vision of progress that places oneself at the apex). When these A's translate into politics, they produce seven E's: Exceptionalism (one group or leader has the unique legitimacy to save us), Exaltedness (that group must be elevated beyond contradiction and paradox, their complexity erased), Externalization of culpability (we are the good team, they are the problem — innocence as the source of moral authority), Expansion of entitlement (politics becomes about more rights for my group, never expanded responsibilities), and Emancipation from entanglement (a politics that reproduces separability rather than relational accountability). This framework applies equally to reactionary nationalism and to progressive romanticization of indigenous peoples — whenever a group is exalted as having the answers, the moment they show complexity, the motivation to support them evaporates. Understanding these patterns is essential because any politics that does not see this architecture will simply reproduce it under new branding, regardless of its stated values.

## Apprentice

There is a recurring pattern in politics that cuts across left and right, progressive and conservative. Whether it's a nationalist leader claiming to save the country or an activist movement claiming moral purity, the same underlying drives keep showing up: the desire to be the authority, to decide what counts as true, to act without accountability, and to be affirmed as good. These drives — called the seven A's — include Authority, Arbitration, Autonomy, Affirmation, Appropriation, Accumulation, and Acceleration.

When these desires enter the political arena, they produce recognizable behaviors — the seven E's. A group declares itself uniquely qualified to lead (Exceptionalism). It demands to be seen as beyond criticism (Exaltedness). It blames all problems on outsiders (Externalization). It seeks ever-expanding rights without matching responsibilities (Expansion of entitlement). And it pursues a kind of freedom that means cutting ties rather than building honest relationships (Emancipation from entanglement).

The uncomfortable insight is that this pattern applies just as much to movements that see themselves as liberatory. When indigenous peoples or marginalized groups are romanticized as having all the answers, the moment they reveal human complexity — contradictions, disagreements, flaws — support dries up. The framework suggests that without recognizing this deep architecture, any political project will end up recreating the very dynamics it claims to oppose, just under a different banner.

## Adept

This framework identifies a structural grammar underlying contemporary political formations across the ideological spectrum. It names seven ontological drives — the seven A's — that constitute the architecture of colonial desire: Authority (claims to moral and epistemic superiority), Arbitration (the power to determine what counts as truth and common sense), Autonomy (unaccountable freedom derived from that authority), Affirmation (the need for validation that sustains the entire structure), Appropriation and Accumulation (entitlements to extraction flowing from the above), and Acceleration (the drive to advance a teleological vision of progress with oneself at the apex). These are not incidental features of colonialism but its generative code.

The political translation of these drives produces seven E's: Exceptionalism (the claim that one group or leader holds unique legitimacy), Exaltedness (the demand that this group be elevated beyond contradiction), Externalization of culpability (innocence as the foundation of moral authority), Expansion of entitlement (politics reduced to rights-claims without relational responsibilities), and Emancipation from entanglement (a politics of separability rather than accountability). The critical move here is showing that these patterns operate symmetrically across reactionary and progressive formations.

The framework's sharpest edge cuts against progressive movements themselves. The romanticization of indigenous peoples or other marginalized groups reproduces the same exaltation dynamic: the moment a group is placed beyond complexity — beyond paradox, internal disagreement, and contradiction — the structure of colonial desire is already operative. Support becomes conditional on the exalted group performing its assigned role. This analysis insists that any emancipatory politics unaware of this architecture will inevitably reproduce it, regardless of its stated commitments to justice, decolonization, or liberation.

## Magus

This intervention maps the generative grammar of colonial desire as a set of ontological drives — the seven A's — whose political instantiation produces a predictable morphology of power across ostensibly opposed ideological formations. Authority, Arbitration, Autonomy, Affirmation, Appropriation, Accumulation, and Acceleration constitute not a taxonomy of colonial effects but the libidinal-epistemic architecture through which coloniality reproduces itself as common sense. The claim is structural: these drives precede and condition political subjectivity, operating as the unconscious syntax of modern political imagination regardless of its declared orientation toward justice or tradition.

Their political translation — Exceptionalism, Exaltedness, Externalization of culpability, Expansion of entitlement, Emancipation from entanglement — reveals the isomorphism between reactionary nationalism and progressive identity politics at the level of form. Both require a subject position of innocence; both demand exaltation beyond paradox; both pursue separability rather than relational accountability. The framework thus refuses the comfort of oppositional self-positioning, insisting that the colonial matrix operates precisely through the conviction that one has escaped it. Innocence here functions not as a moral condition but as a structural requirement for the reproduction of authority.

The most consequential implication concerns the politics of solidarity and romanticization. When any group — indigenous, subaltern, diasporic — is exalted as repository of alternative wisdom, the exaltation itself reinstantiates the A's: it arbitrates their value, appropriates their epistemic resources, and demands their affirmation of the exalter's virtue. The moment complexity, internal contradiction, or refusal of the assigned role emerges, the libidinal investment collapses. This analysis suggests that decolonial and emancipatory projects require not better content but a fundamentally different relationship to desire, authority, and the seductions of moral certainty.
