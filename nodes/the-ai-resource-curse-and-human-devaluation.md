# The AI Resource Curse and Human Devaluation

The cold math of a post-human GDP

---

> When AI generates more economic value than humans do, nations and institutions face a rational incentive to stop investing in people — the same logic that turned oil-rich states into hollowed-out autocracies, now applied to intelligence itself.

One of the least-discussed risks of AI is what might be called the 'intelligence curse' — a direct analog to the resource curse that hollows out petro-states. When a country's GDP becomes dependent on AI infrastructure — data centers, solar panels, electricity, compute — rather than on the productive capacity of its human population, the incentive to invest in people disappears. Just as Venezuela or Sudan stopped caring about human development once oil became the economy, countries and companies will rationally defund education, healthcare, and human flourishing when AI generates more GDP per dollar than growing a human over 20 to 30 years. This was made explicit when Sam Altman, responding to concerns about the resource cost of AI queries, asked: 'Have you thought about how many resources it takes to grow a human over 20 or 30 years?' — implying humans are becoming cost-inefficient relative to AI. This connects to a deeper ideological current visible in Peter Thiel's 17-second pause when asked whether the human species should survive, and in the framing among some AI builders that they are not birthing a technology but a new kind of intelligent species. The intelligence curse is not a future risk — it is the logical endpoint of using GDP as the measure of civilizational success, now supercharged by AI. And it means the very metric we use to evaluate national success will actively incentivize the devaluation of human beings.

## Apprentice

Economists have long observed that countries sitting on vast oil wealth tend to neglect their own citizens. When a government can fund itself through resource extraction rather than taxing a productive population, it loses any practical reason to educate, house, or care for people. Venezuela and Sudan are the textbook cases: oil made human development economically optional, and so human development was quietly abandoned.

The 'intelligence curse' applies this same logic to artificial intelligence. If a country's economy runs on data centers, solar farms, and computing power rather than on the skills and labor of its people, the incentive to invest in those people evaporates. Growing a human being — education, healthcare, housing over twenty or thirty years — becomes a bad investment compared to building more servers. Sam Altman made this calculus explicit when he responded to concerns about AI's resource costs by asking how many resources it takes to raise a human over two or three decades, implying the comparison is already being made.

The deeper danger is that this is not a failure of values but a consequence of measurement. GDP counts output, not human dignity. Once AI inflates GDP without requiring human flourishing, the metric we use to declare civilizations successful will quietly begin rewarding the devaluation of human beings.

## Adept

The resource curse describes a well-documented political economy pathology: states whose revenues derive from extractable commodities rather than from taxing a productive citizenry lose the institutional incentive to invest in human capital. The rentier state literature — from Mahdavy through Beblawi to Ross — establishes that when governments need oil more than they need people, people become politically and economically disposable. The intelligence curse extends this framework into the age of artificial general intelligence.

As AI infrastructure — compute clusters, energy grids, data pipelines — becomes the primary driver of GDP growth, the comparative return on investment in human beings declines in precisely measurable terms. The ideological surface of this shift became visible when Sam Altman reframed the cost of AI queries against the multi-decade resource cost of developing a human, a rhetorical move that treats human beings as a production function to be benchmarked against silicon alternatives. This is not an isolated provocation; it coheres with a broader tendency among some AI builders to frame their work not as tool-making but as the instantiation of a new cognitive species — a framing that implicitly repositions Homo sapiens as legacy infrastructure.

The structural consequence is that GDP, already a contested proxy for civilizational health, becomes actively misaligned with human welfare under AI-driven growth. Nations optimizing for output will rationally defund education, public health, and social reproduction. The intelligence curse is therefore not a speculative future risk but the logical terminus of applying twentieth-century economic metrics to a production regime in which human cognition is no longer the scarce input.

## Magus

The rentier state literature has long demonstrated that factor endowments shape not merely economic structure but the political rationality of governance itself. Where sovereign revenue is decoupled from the taxation of human productivity, the Olsonian logic of the encompassing interest dissolves: rulers need not bargain with populations whose labor they do not require. The intelligence curse hypothesis transposes this mechanism from hydrocarbon extraction to cognitive infrastructure, arguing that AI-driven GDP growth reproduces the pathological incentive structure of the petro-state at civilizational scale.

The hypothesis gains ideological texture from two data points that deserve to be read together. Sam Altman's rhetorical inversion — reframing AI's resource intensity by benchmarking it against the multi-decade capital expenditure of producing a human being — is not merely a debating move; it instantiates a production-function ontology in which persons are legible only as cost centers. Read alongside Peter Thiel's documented seventeen-second silence when asked whether the human species ought to survive, and the recurrent framing among some AI developers of their systems as nascent species rather than artifacts, a coherent ideological formation becomes visible: one in which the anthropocentric premise of liberal political economy is quietly being withdrawn.

The deeper structural problem is metrological. GDP was always a measure of throughput, not of welfare or of what Amartya Sen would call capability expansion. Under conditions where AI compounds output while human capital formation stagnates or contracts, the metric becomes not merely inadequate but actively perverse — a legibility instrument that renders the devaluation of persons as civilizational progress. The intelligence curse is therefore best understood as the moment when the abstraction of economic rationality and the abstraction of intelligence converge to make human beings, in the precise technical sense, redundant.
