# How the Brain Repurposes Old Circuits for Social and Cultural Life

Evolution never starts from scratch.

---

> The brain didn't build new machinery for social selfhood — it repurposed the agent-modeling systems evolution had already shaped for navigating social mammal life, just as the tongue was repurposed for speech. Cultural personhood is a cognitive exaptation, not a novel invention.

Neural reuse — also called cognitive exaptation — offers a crucial explanatory bridge between the biological machinery of agency and the emergence of genuinely social and cultural selfhood. The biological concept of exaptation, illustrated by the tongue, captures the core idea: evolution does not build from scratch. The tongue evolved for poison detection, taste, and mastication, and because of the contingent fact that it sits in the airway and is highly flexible and richly innervated, it was pre-adapted for speech. Evolution did not design a speech organ; it repurposed an existing structure whose accumulated properties happened to make it suited for a new function. Michael Anderson extends this logic into the brain itself, arguing that neural circuits routinely get recruited for functions far removed from those that originally shaped them. The same circuitry that supports fine motor dexterity in the right hand turns out to be well-designed for syntactic articulation — which is why we gesture, especially with the dominant hand, when we speak. But the deeper application of this principle is to the entire machinery of mutual modeling. The brain built sophisticated systems for modeling other agents in order to navigate the cooperative and competitive dynamics of social mammal life. That machinery — with all its capacity for tracking relational value, social rank, alliance, and the intentions of others — turns out to be exactly what is needed, with relatively modest modification, to support the explosion of social-cognitive and eventually cultural existence that defines human personhood. The exaptation is not just of individual circuits but of entire functional architectures. Crucially, this means that the emergence of the self into the social and cultural domain does not require the invention of entirely new cognitive machinery — it requires the repurposing of machinery that was already doing something structurally analogous at a lower level of complexity.

## Apprentice

Evolution rarely invents from scratch. Consider the tongue: it evolved for tasting and chewing food, but because it happened to be flexible, sensitive, and sitting right in the airway, it turned out to be perfectly suited for speech. Evolution didn't design a speech organ — it recycled one that was already there.

Michael Anderson argues the same thing happens inside the brain. Neural circuits that evolved for one purpose routinely get recruited for entirely different tasks. The brain regions that control fine hand movements, for example, also help organize the structure of language — which is why people gesture when they talk, especially with their dominant hand.

The most profound application of this idea concerns how humans became social and cultural beings. The brain already had powerful systems for reading other people — tracking who's an ally, who's a rival, what someone intends to do next. These systems evolved to help social mammals survive together. The key insight is that this same machinery, with relatively small adjustments, is what powers the far richer world of human selfhood, culture, and meaning-making. Becoming a person embedded in culture didn't require the brain to develop something radically new. It required repurposing something it was already doing — modeling other minds — and scaling it up to meet the demands of a far more complex social world.

## Adept

Neural reuse — or cognitive exaptation — provides a powerful framework for understanding how biological agency scaffolds the emergence of social and cultural selfhood without requiring the wholesale invention of new cognitive architecture. The canonical illustration is the tongue: evolved for gustation and mastication, its contingent properties (flexibility, rich innervation, airway placement) pre-adapted it for articulatory speech. Evolution did not design a speech organ; it co-opted existing structure for a novel function. Michael Anderson generalizes this principle to cortical organization, demonstrating that neural circuits are routinely recruited for tasks far removed from their original selective pressures. The overlap between right-hand fine motor circuitry and syntactic processing — visible in the tight coupling of speech and dominant-hand gesture — exemplifies this reuse at the circuit level.

The deeper claim, however, extends beyond individual circuits to entire functional architectures. The sophisticated systems mammals evolved for mutual modeling — tracking relational value, social rank, alliance structures, and the intentions of conspecifics — constitute precisely the computational substrate needed to support the explosion of social-cognitive complexity that characterizes human personhood. The transition from social mammal cognition to cultural selfhood is not a discontinuous leap but an exaptation of agent-modeling machinery operating at a higher level of recursive complexity.

This reframing carries significant implications. It means the cultural self is not layered on top of biology as something categorically different; it is biology repurposed. The capacity for narrative identity, institutional reasoning, and symbolic meaning-making emerges from the same relational-modeling architecture that once tracked who groomed whom. The continuity is structural, not merely metaphorical.

## Magus

Cognitive exaptation — neural reuse scaled from circuit-level co-optation to the repurposing of entire functional architectures — offers perhaps the most parsimonious account of how biological agency gives rise to genuinely cultural selfhood without invoking emergentist discontinuity. The tongue serves as the paradigmatic illustration: a structure shaped by selection for chemoreception and mastication whose contingent biomechanical and neuroanatomical properties (flexibility, dense innervation, airway adjacency) rendered it pre-adapted for the articulatory demands of speech. No dedicated speech organ was designed; existing structure was exapted. Anderson's extension of this logic into cortical organization reveals that neural populations are promiscuously redeployed across functionally disparate domains — the well-documented overlap between right-hand fine motor control circuitry and syntactic processing being one striking instance, phenomenologically visible in the obligatory coupling of dominant-hand gesture with speech production.

The critical move is to apply this principle not merely to discrete circuits but to the deep architecture of mutual modeling. Social mammals evolved computationally rich systems for tracking relational valence, dominance hierarchies, coalition structure, and intentional states of conspecifics. This architecture — already performing sophisticated recursive inference over agent-models — constitutes the structural precondition for the cultural-cognitive explosion characteristic of human personhood. The exaptation thesis holds that the transition requires not novel machinery but the recursive deepening and contextual redeployment of existing agent-modeling capacities.

What makes this framing theoretically consequential is its dissolution of the apparent explanatory gap between biological mechanism and cultural selfhood. Narrative identity, institutional cognition, and symbolic meaning-making are not sui generis capacities superadded to a biological substrate; they are the same relational-modeling architecture operating over increasingly abstract representational domains. The continuity is computational and architectural, rendering the emergence of the cultural self a matter of exaptive elaboration rather than ontological novelty.
