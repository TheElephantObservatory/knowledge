# Replacing Economic Return with Human Flourishing as Society's Core Metric

When the village decides what winning means.

---

> Modern civilization optimizes for a single metric — short-term financial return — at the expense of everything else. A viable alternative requires replacing this with a recursive fitness function: self-actualization that expresses itself through building coherent community, which in turn deepens the conditions for further self-actualization.

One of the deepest structural problems with Game A civilization is that it has only one fitness function: short-term money-on-money return. Everything else — the wellbeing of children, the stability of families, ecological limits, community coherence — gets systematically subordinated to that single metric. Game B requires replacing this with human flourishing as the primary optimization target, but crucially, each community must define what human flourishing actually means through its own consensus process. One possibility is purely individualistic: self-actualization in the Maslovian sense. Another is collective: high social coherence. But the most compelling formulation is a recursive one — self-actualization that actualizes itself by building high-coherence community, which in turn increases the conditions for further self-actualization. These two levels operate simultaneously and reinforce each other. The design implication is significant: every governance decision, every economic arrangement, every architectural choice in a proto-B community should be evaluated against this fitness function rather than against economic efficiency alone. The kibbutz model is instructive here — child care workers were paid identically to engineers and lawyers, because the community had made an explicit value judgment that raising children well was not a second-class contribution. That is what it looks like when a society actually operationalizes a different fitness function rather than just declaring one rhetorically.

## Apprentice

Our current civilization essentially runs on one measure of success: making money in the short term. Everything else — how well we raise children, whether communities hold together, the health of the natural world — gets pushed aside whenever it conflicts with that bottom line. This isn't a bug in the system; it's the system working exactly as designed.

The alternative isn't just to declare different values. It requires actually building communities that measure every decision — how governance works, how people are paid, how buildings are designed — against a different standard: human flourishing. But what does flourishing mean? One answer is individual growth and self-realization. Another is strong, cohesive community. The most powerful answer combines both: people grow by helping build communities that, in turn, help more people grow. These two levels feed each other.

The Israeli kibbutz offers a real-world example. Child care workers were paid the same as engineers and lawyers — not as charity, but because the community genuinely valued raising children as highly as any other work. That's what it looks like when a society doesn't just talk about different values but actually reorganizes itself around them.

## Adept

The Game A / Game B framework identifies a core structural failure in existing civilization: the collapse of all value into a single fitness function — short-term money-on-money return. This monoculture of optimization systematically subordinates child welfare, ecological integrity, family stability, and community coherence to financial extraction. The problem is not that economic metrics exist, but that they have become the sole arbiter of institutional decision-making, crowding out every competing signal.

Game B's proposed remedy is not merely rhetorical revaluation but structural replacement of the fitness function itself. Human flourishing becomes the primary optimization target — but with a critical caveat: each proto-B community must arrive at its own operationalized definition through genuine consensus. The most robust formulation is recursive rather than linear. Individual self-actualization in the Maslovian sense expresses itself through the construction of high-coherence community, which reciprocally generates the conditions for deeper self-actualization. These two levels are not sequential but simultaneous and mutually reinforcing.

The design implications cascade through every domain. Governance structures, economic arrangements, and even architectural choices must be evaluated against this dual fitness function rather than defaulting to efficiency metrics. The kibbutz model provides an instructive historical case: compensating child care workers identically to engineers and lawyers represented an explicit, operationalized value judgment — not a subsidy or concession, but a direct expression of an alternative fitness function embedded in institutional design. The distinction between declaring a value and operationalizing it is precisely where most reform efforts fail.

## Magus

The deepest structural critique within the Game A / Game B discourse concerns not policy failure but fitness-function monoculture: the reduction of civilizational optimization to a single scalar — short-term money-on-money return. This is not merely an economic observation but a systems-theoretic one. When a complex adaptive system collapses its evaluation landscape to one dimension, it necessarily generates systematic externalities across every orthogonal axis — ecological, relational, developmental, communal. The subordination is not incidental but architecturally entailed.

The Game B response demands not value pluralism in the liberal sense but fitness-function replacement — a move from financial return to human flourishing as the primary optimization target. Crucially, this formulation resists universalist prescription: each proto-B community must derive its own operationalization through endogenous consensus. The most theoretically compelling candidate is recursive: Maslovian self-actualization that actualizes itself precisely through the construction of high-coherence social fields, which in turn expand the possibility space for further individuation. This is neither the atomistic self of liberal theory nor the dissolved self of collectivism, but a strange loop in which individual and collective flourishing are co-constitutive and simultaneously operative.

The implications are architectural in the strongest sense — every governance mechanism, compensation structure, and spatial design becomes a site of fitness-function expression. The kibbutz model serves as a concrete instantiation: identical compensation for child care workers and engineers was not redistributive policy but ontological commitment — an explicit judgment that developmental labor carries equivalent dignity. This distinction between rhetorical declaration and institutional operationalization marks the precise failure point of most reformist projects, which modify outputs while leaving the underlying optimization function intact.
