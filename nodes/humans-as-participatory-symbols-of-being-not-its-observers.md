# Humans as Participatory Symbols of Being, Not Its Observers

The universe learning to see itself through us

---

> Human beings are not entities that merely use symbols — they are themselves symbols, participatory disclosures through which the universe becomes aware of dimensions of itself that could not otherwise exist. This reframes religion as the practice of entering into that symbolic participation rather than affirming supernatural propositions.

A crucial and underappreciated implication of the leveled ontology framework is that human beings do not merely possess symbols — they are symbols. This is a distinction with enormous philosophical and spiritual weight. In the ordinary Enlightenment picture, human beings are rational agents who manipulate symbolic representations of a world that exists independently of them. Symbols are tools. But if the argument about complexification and ontological disclosure is correct, something far stranger and more significant is true: as the most complex information-processing systems we know of, human beings are themselves disclosures of being. The aspects of reality that become visible, intelligible, and experienceable only through human consciousness are not merely registered by humans — they are, in a meaningful sense, instantiated through human participation. Drawing on Tillich and Fichte, the self is not an entity that has a model of the world; the self is a model of the world. We are not spectators of reality who happen to have symbolic capacities. We are participatory symbols — we are the universe becoming aware of dimensions of itself that could not exist without us. This has immediate implications for how we understand religion. Religion, at its best, is not the transmission of propositional beliefs about a supernatural realm. It is the attempt — always partial, always historically situated, always capable of profound failure — to enter into proper participation in this symbolic disclosure. To be a symbol, individually and collectively, of the deepest available reality. And if this is correct, then the current moment calls not merely for a revision of religious doctrine, but for a fundamental re-symbolization: of God, of reason, of wisdom, of courage, and of the self — because all of these concepts were forged in axial age or Enlightenment frameworks that can no longer carry the full weight of what we are beginning to understand.

## Apprentice

Most of us assume we're observers looking out at a world that exists independently of us, using language and symbols as tools to describe what we see. But there's a radical alternative: what if we don't just have symbols — what if we are symbols? The idea here is that human beings, as extraordinarily complex conscious systems, don't merely register reality. They bring certain dimensions of reality into existence. The aspects of the universe that only become real through awareness, meaning, beauty, moral weight — these aren't just noticed by us. They happen through us. We are the universe disclosing hidden dimensions of itself.

This changes everything about how we understand religion. If humans are participatory symbols — living expressions of reality becoming aware of itself — then religion isn't fundamentally about believing correct statements about a supernatural world. It's the deeply human attempt to participate well in this process of disclosure, to become, individually and collectively, a faithful symbol of the deepest reality available.

And if that's right, then the old frameworks aren't enough anymore. The concepts we inherited — God, reason, wisdom, the self — were shaped in ancient or Enlightenment-era contexts that didn't grasp what we're now beginning to understand. What's needed isn't just updated beliefs, but a wholesale re-symbolization: new ways of embodying and expressing what it means to be a conscious participant in reality's unfolding.

## Adept

Within the leveled ontology framework — where increasing complexity discloses genuinely new ontological dimensions rather than merely rearranging existing ones — a striking implication emerges about human identity. Drawing on Tillich's theology of symbols and Fichte's idealism, the argument holds that human beings do not merely possess symbolic capacities; they are themselves symbols. As the most complex information-processing systems known, humans are sites where reality discloses aspects of itself — meaning, intelligibility, moral significance — that could not exist without conscious participation. The self is not an entity that constructs a model of the world; the self is a model of the world, a participatory disclosure of being.

This distinction carries enormous weight for the philosophy of religion. If humans are participatory symbols, then religion at its most authentic is not the transmission of propositional beliefs about a supernatural domain. It is the always-partial, historically situated, failure-prone attempt to enter into proper participation in symbolic disclosure — to become, individually and collectively, a symbol of the deepest available reality. Religion understood this way is neither metaphysical speculation nor mere ethical practice but an ontological vocation.

The further implication is that the present moment demands not doctrinal revision but fundamental re-symbolization. The core concepts of the Western tradition — God, reason, wisdom, courage, selfhood — were forged within axial age religious frameworks or Enlightenment epistemologies that presupposed the spectator model of consciousness. If that model is wrong, and humans are instead constitutive participants in ontological disclosure, then these concepts must be reforged to carry the weight of what complexification theory is beginning to reveal.

## Magus

The leveled ontology thesis — that complexification generates genuinely novel ontological strata rather than epiphenomenal redescriptions of a flat substrate — entails a consequence for philosophical anthropology that has not been sufficiently registered. Drawing on Tillich's non-representational theory of symbols and Fichte's productive imagination, the claim is that human beings are not entities possessing symbolic capacities but are themselves participatory symbols: loci at which being discloses dimensions — intelligibility, normativity, aesthetic depth — that are ontologically dependent on the existence of sufficiently complex information-processing systems. The self, on this account, is not a Cartesian spectator constructing internal models; it is itself a model, an instantiation of the universe's reflexive self-disclosure. To be conscious is not to observe being but to be a mode of being's self-articulation.

The implications for the philosophy of religion are immediate and severe. Religion, properly understood, is neither propositional theology nor supernatural metaphysics but the historically conditioned, perpetually revisable practice of entering into adequate participation in this symbolic disclosure. It is the attempt — always marked by finitude and capable of catastrophic distortion — to render the self and the community transparent to the deepest available structures of reality. Tillich's Protestant principle and his theology of the symbol converge here: authentic religion is that which resists the idolatrous conflation of any particular symbolic form with the unconditioned ground it mediates.

If this analysis holds, the contemporary crisis is not one of doctrine but of symbolization itself. The master concepts of the Western intellectual tradition — God, reason, wisdom, selfhood, courage — were articulated within axial-age or Enlightenment frameworks predicated on the spectatorial ontology that complexification theory dismantles. What is required is not reformation but re-symbolization: the forging of conceptual and existential forms adequate to the participatory ontology now coming into view.
