# How Schools Diagnose Children to Avoid Diagnosing Themselves

The most rational patient in the room.

---

> When children struggle in school, the system diagnoses their brains rather than questioning its own design. Medicalizing academic underperformance is a political move that shields institutions from accountability by relocating failure inside the child.

One of the deepest structural problems in modern education is what I call the medicalization of academic underperformance — and it represents a profound political sleight of hand. When a child fails to thrive in school, we face a choice: we can ask questions about the physical environment, the culture of the school, the developmental appropriateness of the curriculum, the quality of teaching — all of which locate accountability at the level of the institution. Or we can locate the problem in the child's brain, declare it a biological dysfunction, and put them on medication. What we almost always do is the latter. We medicalize what is fundamentally a political and social problem. And to medicalize a political problem is to de-politicize it — to make it impossible to even have the argument about institutional change. The child becomes the most vulnerable node in the system, absorbing the failure of the system itself. This is not accidental. An institution that is radically resistant to change will systematically reframe every piece of evidence that it should change into a reason why the individual — not the institution — needs to be fixed. We see this same move in political discourse, where disagreement gets pathologized rather than engaged. The fact that a child is sophisticated enough to see through the meaninglessness of what they're being asked to do, and refuses to perform — that's not a disorder. That might be the most rational response available to them.

## Apprentice

When a child isn't doing well in school, there are two basic directions we can look. We can examine the school itself — the teaching, the curriculum, the environment — or we can decide something is wrong with the child's brain and prescribe medication. Overwhelmingly, modern education chooses the second path. A child who can't sit still, who won't perform, who seems disengaged, gets a diagnosis. The institution escapes scrutiny.

This reframing is not neutral. By turning a social and political problem into a medical one, the conversation about whether schools themselves need to change becomes almost impossible to have. The child — the least powerful person in the system — ends up absorbing the system's failures as if they were personal defects.

There's a deeper irony here. A child who sees through busywork and refuses to comply may actually be responding rationally to a meaningless situation. Labeling that response as a disorder doesn't just misunderstand the child — it protects the institution from ever having to ask whether it deserved the child's cooperation in the first place.

## Adept

The medicalization of academic underperformance represents one of the most consequential category errors in contemporary education — and it functions as a political strategy, whether or not it is consciously intended as one. When a child fails to thrive, the system faces a fork: interrogate institutional conditions (curriculum design, pedagogical quality, developmental appropriateness, school culture) or locate the dysfunction in the child's neurology. The latter path — diagnosis and pharmacological intervention — has become the default. This is not merely a clinical preference; it is a structural defense mechanism. To medicalize a political problem is to depoliticize it, foreclosing the very possibility of institutional accountability.

This dynamic follows a recognizable pattern: institutions resistant to change systematically reinterpret evidence of their own failure as evidence of individual pathology. The child becomes the most vulnerable node in the network, absorbing systemic dysfunction as personal deficit. The same logic appears in broader political discourse, where dissent is pathologized rather than engaged — where the refusal to comply is treated as symptomatic rather than substantive.

The insight cuts deepest at the level of what counts as a rational response. A child sophisticated enough to perceive the emptiness of what they are being asked to do, and who refuses to perform accordingly, is not exhibiting disorder. They may be exhibiting the clearest form of rationality available to someone with no institutional power. Reframing that rationality as dysfunction is not diagnosis — it is containment.

## Magus

The medicalization of academic underperformance operates as a form of structural immunization: the institution inoculates itself against reform by reclassifying its own failures as individual pathology. This move — from political accountability to neurological diagnosis — enacts a precise epistemic closure. Once underperformance is framed as biological dysfunction, the grammar of institutional critique becomes unavailable. The question shifts from "What is wrong with this environment?" to "What is wrong with this child?" — and with that shift, an entire domain of political contestation is rendered invisible. The child, as the least powerful actor in the system, becomes the designated site of absorption for systemic failure.

This is structurally isomorphic with what occurs in broader political discourse when dissent is pathologized rather than addressed. The refusal to engage with substantive disagreement — reframing it instead as evidence of the dissenter's dysfunction — is a move that preserves institutional homeostasis at the cost of epistemic integrity. In education, the pattern is especially pernicious because the subject being pathologized lacks the standing to contest the diagnosis. The asymmetry of power between institution and child ensures that the medicalized framing is self-reinforcing.

The most provocative dimension of this analysis concerns the status of non-compliance itself. A child who perceives the arbitrariness or vacuity of institutional demands and refuses to perform is engaged in something that, under any honest epistemology, must be recognized as rational appraisal. To classify that appraisal as disorder is not a diagnostic act but a political one — it forecloses the possibility that the child's refusal constitutes legitimate evidence against the institution. Medicalization, in this light, is not care. It is containment dressed as compassion.
