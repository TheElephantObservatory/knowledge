# How Social Media Replaced Being Loved with Being Followed

The market has no word for belonging.

---

> Gregg Henriques argues that modern culture has replaced the human need to be genuinely known and loved — relational value — with the pursuit of social influence metrics like followers and status, producing mass psychological suffering that is structural, not mysterious.

Henriques offers a precise and clinically grounded diagnosis of the meaning crisis through the lens of what he calls the Influence Matrix — a model of the human relational system that tracks two distinct but intertwined dimensions: social influence and relational value. Social influence is the instrumental capacity to move other people in accordance with your interests — it is about power, status, and control. Relational value is something categorically different: it is the experience of being seen, known, and valued by important others. These are not two points on the same spectrum; they are different kinds of human need. The pathology of contemporary culture, Henriques argues, is that capitalism and social media have elevated social influence to the status of the primary currency of human worth — followers, money, status, likes — while systematically stripping the structural conditions that allow relational value to flourish. Children are isolated in rooms, performing for audiences of strangers, chasing metrics of influence that are entirely decoupled from the embodied, intimate, long-term relationships in which genuine relational value is generated. Even those who succeed at accumulating influence — the so-called influencers — are trapped by audience capture, compelled to perform a persona regardless of their inner reality. The result is a population that is offered either social invisibility or social performance, with authentic relational nourishment available to almost no one. The 57% of teenage girls reporting clinically significant depression, Henriques argues, is not a mystery. It is the predictable output of a social architecture that has confused the soul's need to be loved with the market's demand to be followed.

## Apprentice

Psychologist Gregg Henriques draws a sharp distinction between two things people need from their social world. The first is social influence — the ability to affect others, gain status, and accumulate power. The second is relational value — the deep experience of being truly seen, known, and cherished by people who matter to you. These are not the same thing, and confusing them has devastating consequences.

Henriques argues that modern capitalism and social media have made social influence the dominant measure of human worth. Followers, likes, money, and visibility have become the currency people chase. Meanwhile, the conditions that allow relational value to grow — long-term intimacy, embodied presence, genuine mutual knowledge — have been systematically eroded. Children perform for strangers online instead of being nourished by close relationships. Even successful influencers find themselves trapped, performing personas disconnected from who they actually are.

The result is a culture that offers people two options: be invisible or perform. Authentic relational nourishment is available to almost no one. The staggering rates of depression among teenage girls, Henriques suggests, are not a puzzle — they are the predictable outcome of a world that has confused the soul's need to be loved with the market's demand to be followed.

## Adept

Gregg Henriques's Influence Matrix provides a clinically grounded framework for diagnosing the meaning crisis by distinguishing two orthogonal dimensions of the human relational system: social influence and relational value. Social influence refers to the instrumental capacity to move others in accordance with one's interests — encompassing power, status, and control. Relational value is categorically distinct: it is the felt experience of being seen, known, and valued by significant others within sustained, intimate bonds. These dimensions are intertwined but irreducible to one another, and the failure to distinguish them constitutes a core pathology of contemporary life.

Henriques argues that capitalism and social media have systematically elevated social influence to the status of primary currency for human worth — followers, likes, wealth, visibility — while degrading the structural conditions under which relational value can be generated. The architecture of digital social life decouples metrics of influence from embodied, long-term relational contexts. Children optimize for audience attention rather than developing secure relational bonds. Even those who succeed at accumulating influence fall prey to audience capture, compelled to maintain performative personas regardless of inner reality.

The cultural outcome is a forced choice between social invisibility and social performance, with authentic relational nourishment structurally foreclosed. The epidemic of adolescent depression — 57% of teenage girls reporting clinically significant symptoms — is, on this analysis, not an anomaly requiring explanation but the predictable output of a social architecture that has substituted the market logic of being followed for the psychological necessity of being loved. The crisis is not one of individual pathology but of systemic design.

## Magus

Henriques's Influence Matrix operationalizes a distinction of considerable theoretical consequence: between social influence — the instrumental capacity to shape others' behavior in accordance with one's interests, tracking power, status, and hierarchical positioning — and relational value — the phenomenological experience of being known, recognized, and valued within sustained intersubjective bonds. These are not poles of a single continuum but orthogonal dimensions of the human relational system, each with distinct developmental trajectories, neurobiological substrates, and failure modes. The conflation of these dimensions, Henriques contends, constitutes the psychodynamic core of the contemporary meaning crisis.

The argument is structural rather than moralistic. Late capitalism and algorithmically mediated sociality have instantiated social influence as the hegemonic metric of human worth — operationalized through followers, engagement metrics, financial capital, and visibility — while simultaneously dismantling the ecological conditions prerequisite for relational value: temporal continuity, embodied co-presence, reciprocal vulnerability, and freedom from performative demand. The digital social field offers what amounts to a forced choice between invisibility and performance, neither of which satisfies the relational system's requirement for authentic recognition. Even maximal accumulation of influence — the condition of the so-called influencer — generates audience capture dynamics that decouple public persona from subjective interiority, producing alienation at the point of apparent social success.

The epidemiological data — 57% of adolescent girls meeting thresholds for clinical depression — becomes, under this framework, not an explanatory puzzle but a predictive entailment. The pathology is architectural: a civilization-scale substitution of market-legible influence metrics for the soul's constitutive need to be relationally valued. Henriques's contribution is to render this substitution analytically precise, grounding cultural critique in a falsifiable model of the relational system's dual-process structure.
