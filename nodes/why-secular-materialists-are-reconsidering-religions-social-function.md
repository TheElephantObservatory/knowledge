# Why Secular Materialists Are Reconsidering Religion's Social Function

The cathedral held something we haven't replaced.

---

> Two self-described materialists — Chris Rutt and Bret Weinstein — independently observe that religious communities have proven functionally superior at resisting the corrosive pressures of financialized capitalism, suggesting the New Atheist critique committed a category error by treating religion as mere proposition rather than evolved adaptation.

Both Rutt and Weinstein, identifying as materialists and evolutionary thinkers with no personal religious belief, arrive independently at a troubling observation about the adaptive value of religious community. Their argument is not that religious claims are true — both are explicit that they are not — but that the atheist critique of religion made a category error by treating it purely as a set of propositions to be falsified rather than as an evolved adaptation that solved real problems of human coordination, courage, and resistance to cultural corruption. The specific observation that drives this is empirical rather than theoretical: under the pressures of late-stage financialized capitalism — with its incentives toward short-termism, status competition, and moral flexibility — the people who have most consistently resisted those pressures, maintained community integrity, and demonstrated the courage to confront institutional wrongdoing have disproportionately been people embedded in religious communities. Rutt cites a weekend spent with Jordan Hall's church community as a concrete data point. Weinstein frames it in evolutionary terms: nature is sending a signal, and the obligation of honest thinkers is to receive that signal rather than dismiss it on ideological grounds. The implication is not a call to adopt religious belief but a recognition that the hole left by the decline of the Abrahamic God — which Nietzsche diagnosed and which John Vervaeke has mapped in detail — has not been filled by anything with equivalent functional power, and that the transition period may be longer and more costly than the Enlightenment's optimists assumed.

## Apprentice

Chris Rutt and Bret Weinstein are both non-religious, evolution-minded thinkers who have arrived at an uncomfortable conclusion: religious communities seem to work better than secular ones at helping people resist the worst pressures of modern life. Neither is saying God is real. What they're saying is that the atheist movement missed something important by focusing only on whether religious claims are factually true, while ignoring what religion actually does for people.

The evidence they point to is practical, not theological. In a world dominated by short-term financial thinking, status games, and moral compromise, the people who most reliably hold their ground — who maintain real community, show courage, and push back against institutional corruption — tend to be people rooted in religious communities. Rutt describes spending a weekend with a church community and being struck by its resilience. Weinstein frames the pattern in evolutionary terms: if something keeps working, honest thinkers should pay attention rather than dismiss it.

The takeaway isn't that everyone should start going to church. It's that when Nietzsche warned about the death of God, he was pointing to a real problem. The structures that religion provided — meaning, belonging, moral backbone — haven't been replaced by anything equally powerful. The gap is real, and filling it may take far longer and cost far more than the optimists of the Enlightenment tradition expected.

## Adept

Rutt and Weinstein represent a specific and increasingly visible position: materialist thinkers who, without abandoning their epistemological commitments, recognize that the New Atheist project committed a category error. By treating religion exclusively as a set of falsifiable propositions, that movement failed to engage with religion's function as an evolved coordination technology — one that solved problems of group cohesion, moral courage, and resistance to defection under pressure. The critique is not that Dawkins or Hitchens were wrong about religious truth-claims, but that they were answering the wrong question.

The argument is grounded in empirical observation rather than theoretical speculation. Under the specific conditions of late-stage financialized capitalism — characterized by pervasive short-termism, status competition, and incentive structures that reward moral flexibility — religious communities have disproportionately produced individuals and groups capable of maintaining integrity, sustaining genuine community, and confronting institutional corruption. Rutt points to direct experience with Jordan Hall's church community as a concrete instance. Weinstein invokes an evolutionary heuristic: persistent functional advantage constitutes a signal that demands honest reception, regardless of one's priors about the metaphysical substrate.

The implication connects directly to the lineage running from Nietzsche's diagnosis of God's death through John Vervaeke's detailed mapping of the resulting "meaning crisis." The functional niche that Abrahamic religion occupied — providing existential orientation, communal binding, and a framework for moral resistance — remains largely unfilled. Secular modernity has not produced an equivalent adaptive structure. This suggests the transition period between religious and post-religious civilization may be substantially longer, more dangerous, and more costly than Enlightenment optimism assumed, and that intellectual honesty requires sitting with that discomfort rather than resolving it prematurely.

## Magus

The convergence of Rutt and Weinstein on this point is methodologically significant precisely because it is arrived at from within a materialist-evolutionary framework rather than imposed upon it. Both thinkers are explicit about their lack of personal religious commitment, which sharpens the epistemological stakes: this is not apologetics but a recognition that the New Atheist critique, whatever its propositional validity, operated at the wrong level of analysis. Religion-as-adaptation and religion-as-truth-claim occupy distinct explanatory registers, and collapsing them — as the Dawkins-Hitchens program systematically did — constitutes a category error with real civilizational consequences.

The empirical substrate of the argument is specific to the selection pressures of financialized late modernity. The incentive architecture of contemporary capitalism — its optimization for short-term extraction, its gamification of status, its structural rewards for moral flexibility — functions as a fitness landscape against which different social technologies can be evaluated. Against this landscape, religious communities have demonstrated disproportionate capacity for maintaining coordination integrity, producing individuals willing to bear costs for confronting institutional corruption, and resisting the atomization that erodes collective agency. Rutt's weekend with Jordan Hall's church community serves as a phenomenological data point; Weinstein's framing is more abstract, invoking the evolutionary obligation to receive persistent signals of functional advantage without ideological filtering.

The deeper theoretical implication engages the Nietzsche-to-Vervaeke lineage directly. The death of God was never merely a propositional event but a structural one — the removal of a keystone adaptation around which meaning-making, communal binding, and moral resistance were organized. That the resulting niche remains functionally unfilled after more than a century suggests the Enlightenment's implicit timeline for civilizational transition was radically optimistic, and that the interregnum carries costs — in coordination failure, moral drift, and susceptibility to memetic capture — that secular frameworks have yet to adequately theorize, let alone address.
