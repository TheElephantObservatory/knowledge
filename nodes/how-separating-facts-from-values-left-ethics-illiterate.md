# How Separating Facts from Values Left Ethics Illiterate

The surgery succeeded; the patient forgot how to speak.

---

> The separation of facts from values was a genuine intellectual achievement — it stopped religious ontology from dictating what exists. But the separation hardened into a dismissal: value discourse was declared irrational, leaving modern culture fluent in fact-stating and nearly illiterate in ethical reasoning.

The is-ought distinction did not arrive in history as a neutral logical observation. It arrived as part of a broader cultural project — the differentiation of value spheres, the separation of scientific discourse from ethical and religious discourse — and that project had genuine emancipatory force. It stopped the use of religious ontology, which fuses descriptive and evaluative claims simultaneously, to foreclose the question of what actually exists as separate from how we should value it. Justifying a normative claim is genuinely different from justifying a scientific one, and collapsing that difference has real costs — it allows descriptive language to smuggle in crypto-normative assumptions. This is precisely what happens in psychology and education: the label of ADHD is a descriptive claim, but whether it is bad, what it means for a child's life, what ought to be done — those are separate questions requiring a different kind of justification. The problem is not the differentiation itself. The problem is what happened after: the differentiation became a dismissal. The normative, the evaluative, the ethical was effectively declared irrational, the domain of emotion rather than reason. And so we ended up with a culture that is technically sophisticated at fact-stating discourse and almost entirely illiterate in the discourse of value. The resuscitation of that conversation — not by collapsing it into science, but by recovering its own evidentiary standards and forms of justification — is one of the central intellectual tasks of our moment.

## Apprentice

There was a moment in intellectual history when thinkers drew a sharp line between describing the world and saying what we should do about it. This was genuinely important. Before that distinction, religious authorities could declare that the way things are is the way they should be, shutting down both scientific inquiry and moral debate in one move. Separating "is" from "ought" opened space for science to investigate reality on its own terms and for ethics to be argued rather than dictated.

But something went wrong after the separation. Instead of treating questions of value as a different kind of serious reasoning, modern culture effectively demoted them. Values got filed under "feelings" or "personal preference" — something you can't really argue about rationally. Science kept developing rigorous methods, while ethical reasoning was left without any recognized standards of evidence or argument.

The consequences show up everywhere. Consider how a diagnosis like ADHD works: the label describes a pattern of behavior, but whether that pattern is a problem, what it means for a child's future, what should be done — those are value questions that require their own careful thinking. When we lack the tools for that thinking, descriptive labels quietly do normative work nobody examines. The task now is not to collapse facts and values back together, but to rebuild our capacity for serious ethical reasoning — to recover the idea that value claims can be justified, debated, and held to standards, just different ones than scientific claims.

## Adept

The is-ought distinction, as it emerged historically, was not a bloodless logical point. It was part of a larger emancipatory project — the differentiation of value spheres that Max Weber described — which broke the grip of religious ontology over both empirical inquiry and normative deliberation. When a metaphysical framework fuses descriptive and evaluative claims into a single package, it forecloses the question of what actually exists independently of how we should relate to it. The separation of scientific from ethical discourse was a genuine achievement: it recognized that justifying a normative claim requires a fundamentally different evidentiary structure than justifying an empirical one.

The pathology set in not with the differentiation but with what followed. The normative domain, once distinguished, was progressively delegitimized — treated as the residue of emotion, preference, or cultural conditioning rather than as a domain with its own rational standards. Modernity became technically sophisticated at fact-stating discourse while losing almost all literacy in the discourse of value. This asymmetry has concrete consequences: in fields like psychology and education, ostensibly descriptive categories — ADHD being a paradigmatic case — carry crypto-normative freight. The diagnostic label presents as empirical description, but the questions of whether the described pattern constitutes a deficit, what it means for flourishing, and what interventions are warranted are evaluative questions that require their own forms of justification.

The intellectual task, then, is neither to re-collapse the distinction nor to accept the post-differentiation dismissal. It is to resuscitate normative reasoning on its own terms — to recover or construct evidentiary standards appropriate to evaluative claims, recognizing that the discourse of value is not science done poorly but a different and equally demanding form of rational inquiry.

## Magus

The is-ought distinction entered intellectual history bearing the full weight of the differentiation of value spheres — a project whose emancipatory force lay precisely in its capacity to dissolve the totalizing fusion of descriptive and normative claims characteristic of pre-modern religious ontology. That fusion operated as a closure mechanism: by embedding evaluative commitments within ontological assertions, it rendered both empirical investigation and normative contestation structurally impossible. The Humean fork, whatever its analytic merits, functioned historically as a wedge driven into this closure, creating the discursive space in which scientific inquiry and ethical deliberation could each develop their own justificatory logics.

The dialectical reversal is well known but insufficiently diagnosed. The differentiation, having liberated the empirical from normative overdetermination, proceeded to delegitimize the normative altogether — not by argument but by institutional and epistemic marginalization. What Weber identified as rationalization became, in practice, the monopolization of rational discourse by instrumental and empirical modes. The evaluative was consigned to the domain of the arational: preference, sentiment, Weltanschauung. The result is a culture that has refined fact-stating discourse to extraordinary precision while possessing almost no functioning apparatus for the rational adjudication of value claims. This deficit is not abstract. In psychology and education, putatively descriptive categories like ADHD perform unacknowledged normative work — the diagnostic label smuggles evaluative assumptions about cognitive normativity, childhood flourishing, and institutional accommodation under the cover of clinical description.

The recovery project cannot proceed by collapsing the distinction — that would merely reinstate pre-modern closure in new guise. It requires instead the reconstruction of evaluative discourse as a domain with its own evidentiary standards, modes of justification, and criteria of rational adequacy: not science conducted by other means, but a parallel and equally rigorous form of inquiry into what matters and why.
