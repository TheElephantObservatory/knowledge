# Separating Consciousness as Property from Mindedness as Structure

Everyone is solving a different puzzle in the dark.

---

> Consciousness studies conflate two distinct things: the abstract property of consciousness (awareness, subjectivity, self-reflection) and the concrete biological structure of mindedness. Until the field develops vocabulary to separate these questions, researchers will keep talking past each other.

A critical conceptual problem in consciousness studies is the conflation of two genuinely distinct things: the property of consciousness and the structure of mindedness. Mindedness is bound to a specific biological architecture — the complex active body, the nervous system, the sensory-motor loop that emerged with the Cambrian explosion. Consciousness, by contrast, refers to a set of properties that can be abstracted away from any particular substrate: functional awareness and responsiveness (consciousness one), the subjective experience of being a self experiencing contents (consciousness two), and self-recursive awareness of that subject (consciousness three). The confusion arises because scientists — neuroscientists and psychologists — are almost entirely doing the science of mindedness, specifically what UTOK calls Mind 2, while philosophers and physicists are debating the abstract property of consciousness without necessarily anchoring it to structure. These are genuinely different questions. Anil Seth's 'real problem of consciousness' is, more precisely, the real problem of Mind 2: how does subjective experience arise from and relate to the neurocognitive processes of Mind 1a and the overt behavioral field of Mind 1b? Until we have vocabulary that separates the structural question from the property question, the field will continue talking past itself. The Map of Mind is an attempt to provide exactly that vocabulary — not to resolve the hard problem, but to clarify which problem you are actually working on.

## Apprentice

When scientists study consciousness, they're usually studying something more specific than they realize: how brains produce experience. When philosophers debate consciousness, they're often asking something far more general: what is it for anything to be aware at all? These sound like the same question, but they aren't. The first is about a particular biological structure — the nervous system, the body, the sensory loops that evolved hundreds of millions of years ago. The second is about an abstract property that might not depend on any particular structure at all.

This confusion runs deep. Neuroscientists map brain activity and call it consciousness research. Philosophers propose theories about awareness in the universe and call it the same thing. They end up arguing not because one side is wrong, but because they're answering different questions with the same word.

The insight here is that we need clearer vocabulary. "Mindedness" can refer to the specific biological architecture that produces human experience. "Consciousness" can refer to the more abstract properties — basic awareness, subjective experience, and self-reflection — which may or may not require that architecture. With that distinction in hand, a researcher can finally ask: which problem am I actually working on?

## Adept

A persistent source of confusion in consciousness studies is the failure to distinguish between consciousness as an abstract property and mindedness as a concrete biological structure. Mindedness — what UTOK's framework calls Mind 2 — is bound to a specific architecture: the complex active body, the nervous system, and the sensory-motor loops that emerged during the Cambrian explosion. Consciousness, by contrast, can be decomposed into at least three layers of abstraction: functional awareness and responsiveness (consciousness one), the subjective experience of being a self encountering contents (consciousness two), and self-recursive awareness of that subjectivity (consciousness three). These layers describe properties that can, in principle, be discussed independently of any particular substrate.

The consequence of this conflation is that entire research programs talk past one another. Neuroscientists and psychologists are overwhelmingly doing the science of mindedness — investigating how subjective experience arises from neurocognitive processes (Mind 1a) and relates to the overt behavioral field (Mind 1b). Meanwhile, philosophers and physicists debate the abstract property of consciousness without necessarily anchoring it to any biological structure. Anil Seth's "real problem of consciousness" is, more precisely, the real problem of Mind 2: mapping the relationship between subjective phenomenology and its neural correlates.

The Map of Mind framework attempts to supply the missing vocabulary. Its purpose is not to resolve the hard problem but to make explicit which problem a given researcher is actually addressing. Without this structural-versus-property distinction, the field remains trapped in a cycle where genuine progress on mindedness is mistaken for progress on consciousness writ large, and vice versa.

## Magus

The central diagnostic offered here is that consciousness studies suffers from a systematic category error: the conflation of consciousness qua abstract property with mindedness qua instantiated biological structure. Mindedness is architecturally specific — it designates the integrated complex of nervous system, active body, and sensory-motor coupling that constitutes what UTOK terms Mind 2, a formation with a datable evolutionary origin in the Cambrian explosion. Consciousness, decomposed into functional awareness-responsiveness (C1), subjective phenomenal experience of a self encountering contents (C2), and self-recursive meta-awareness (C3), names a set of properties that admit of substrate-independent characterization. The failure to maintain this distinction generates pseudo-disagreements across disciplinary lines.

Consider the actual research landscape. Neuroscience and psychology operate almost exclusively within the domain of mindedness — their explananda are the neurocognitive processes (Mind 1a) and behavioral expressions (Mind 1b) that subtend and express Mind 2. Seth's "real problem of consciousness" is best understood as the real problem of Mind 2: articulating the bridging relations between phenomenal subjectivity and its neural implementation. Philosophers and physicists engaging the hard problem or panpsychist alternatives are, by contrast, interrogating consciousness as a property — asking whether and how C1, C2, or C3 might obtain in systems lacking the architecture of mindedness altogether. These are genuinely orthogonal inquiries that happen to share a lexicon.

The Map of Mind framework functions as a disambiguation instrument. It does not claim to dissolve the hard problem; it claims to render visible which problem is being worked on in any given investigation. Without such vocabulary, the field perpetuates a structural ambiguity in which empirical advances in the science of mindedness are alternately inflated into solutions to the property question or dismissed as irrelevant to it — both errors traceable to the same conflation.
