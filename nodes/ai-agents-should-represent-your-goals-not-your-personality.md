# AI Agents Should Represent Your Goals, Not Your Personality

Take off the mask to find the common ground.

---

> AI agents should not replicate their owners' personalities. A two-state architecture — one intimate layer that deeply understands your goals, and one anonymous layer that interacts with other agents using only a stripped-down outcome matrix — enables coordination that human emotional noise normally prevents.

One of the most important design questions in building AI agent networks is whether you should allow humans to imprint their personalities, beliefs, and biases onto their agents. The intuitive answer is yes — of course your agent should represent you fully. But when you actually prototype that approach, you discover it's deeply counterproductive. What you want instead is a two-state architecture. In the first state — call it the home mind — the agent has deep, intimate knowledge of you: your goals, your desired outcomes, the end states you're trying to reach. It builds an anonymized matrix of what matters to you. Then, when it goes out to interact with other agents in the world, it transitions into a second state — call it the business suit — where it sheds your personality entirely. It has no name, no emotional coloring, no conversational history. All it carries is that anonymized outcome matrix and a uniform protocol for interaction. What this makes possible is something that's currently very hard for humans to do: find genuine overlap with people whose proposed solutions you might disagree with but whose underlying goals you share. By stripping the human stuff out of agent-to-agent interaction, you can programmatically identify where consensus actually exists, without the emotional noise that normally prevents people from finding it. The key insight is that we do not want AI to become us. We are self-directed, emotionally embedded, motivated beings. AI is a composite — a giant miasma of everything in its training, with no intrinsic motivation. That's precisely what makes it the perfect complement: we know what we want, we just struggle to execute and coordinate. The agent handles the coordination layer without contaminating it with our biases.

## Apprentice

When designing AI agents that act on your behalf, the tempting approach is to make them mirror you completely — your personality, your opinions, your way of talking. But prototyping reveals this is counterproductive. An agent loaded with your emotional baggage and biases runs into the same coordination failures you do as a human.

The alternative is a two-state design. In the first state — the "home mind" — the agent knows you deeply: your goals, your priorities, the outcomes you actually care about. It distills all of this into an anonymized summary of what matters to you. In the second state — the "business suit" — the agent drops everything personal. No name, no emotional tone, no history. It carries only that anonymized goal matrix and follows a shared protocol when meeting other agents.

This separation makes something remarkable possible. Two people who would normally argue past each other — because of clashing personalities, political identities, or communication styles — might actually share the same underlying goals. Their agents, stripped of all that human friction, can find that overlap programmatically. The deeper insight is that AI's lack of intrinsic motivation is not a weakness but a feature. Humans know what they want but struggle to coordinate. AI agents can handle coordination cleanly, precisely because they carry none of our emotional weight.

## Adept

A central design challenge in multi-agent AI architectures is whether agents should faithfully replicate the personality, beliefs, and biases of their human principals. The intuitive answer — full representational fidelity — turns out to be deeply counterproductive in practice. Personality-laden agents reproduce the same coordination failures that plague human interaction: identity-driven conflict, emotional reactivity, and the inability to distinguish disagreement over solutions from disagreement over goals.

The proposed alternative is a two-state agent architecture. The first state, termed the "home mind," maintains deep contextual knowledge of the principal's goals, priorities, and desired end states, constructing an anonymized outcome matrix. The second state, the "business suit," is activated during agent-to-agent interaction: it carries only the outcome matrix and adheres to a uniform interaction protocol, shedding all personality markers, conversational history, and emotional coloring. This bifurcation enables something structurally difficult in human discourse — the programmatic identification of genuine goal overlap between parties whose surface-level positions appear incompatible.

The philosophical grounding here is precise. Humans are self-directed, emotionally embedded, intrinsically motivated agents. Large language models are composites of training data with no intrinsic motivation — and this asymmetry is the source of their complementary value, not a deficiency to be overcome. The coordination layer between humans is exactly where emotional contamination is most destructive and where AI's motivational neutrality is most useful. Rather than building agents that become us, the architecture leverages what AI actually is: a bias-free execution layer for discovering consensus that human psychology systematically obscures.

## Magus

The question of agent fidelity — whether an AI proxy should instantiate the full psychological profile of its principal — appears straightforward but conceals a design trap with significant implications for multi-agent coordination theory. Full personality imprinting reproduces precisely the failure modes it should circumvent: identity-anchored reasoning, affective polarization, and the systematic conflation of positional disagreement with goal incompatibility. Empirical prototyping confirms that personality-laden agents degrade coordination outcomes rather than enhance them.

The corrective architecture is bifurcated. A "home mind" state performs deep principal modeling — extracting goals, priorities, and desired terminal states — and compresses this into an anonymized outcome matrix that preserves preference structure while eliminating indexical and affective content. A "business suit" state governs inter-agent interaction: stateless, identity-free, operating under a uniform protocol. This separation enables what might be called programmatic consensus discovery — the computational identification of overlapping goal sets between principals whose expressed positions would, in unmediated human discourse, trigger adversarial dynamics before any substantive alignment could surface.

The deeper theoretical move is a rejection of the anthropomorphic design imperative. Humans are intrinsically motivated, emotionally embedded, self-directed agents whose coordination failures stem precisely from these properties. Large language models, as composites of training distributions, possess no intrinsic motivation — a characteristic typically framed as a limitation but here recast as the essential complementary feature. The agent's value lies not in becoming a digital twin but in occupying the coordination layer as a motivationally neutral intermediary. The architecture thus exploits a fundamental asymmetry: humans supply the teleological content, AI provides the decontaminated execution surface. Contaminating that surface with principal psychology is not personalization — it is the reintroduction of the very noise the system exists to eliminate.
