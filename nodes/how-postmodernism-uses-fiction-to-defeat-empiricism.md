# How Postmodernism Uses Fiction to Defeat Empiricism

A joke about dragons, generalized to everything

---

> Postmodern thinkers like Foucault use fictional examples rather than empirical evidence to argue that all knowledge categories are arbitrary — an elegant but suspect rhetorical move. The metamodern counter-thread: many frameworks exist, but plurality does not mean equivalence.

There is a recurring philosophical move in postmodern thought that deserves to be named and examined: the shift from empirical example to fictional narrative as a way of making a general epistemological claim. Hicks points to Foucault's opening of *The Order of Things*, where the primary exhibit is a Borges fiction — an invented Chinese encyclopedia with an absurd classification system. The rhetorical move is elegant: if even a fictional taxonomy can feel internally coherent, then perhaps all taxonomies, including scientific ones, are just stories we tell. There is no in-principle distinction between fact and fiction; the scientist's categories are just a more socially powerful version of Borges's joke. But notice what has happened: a philosophical argument about the nature of all knowledge has been grounded not in an empirical investigation of actual different cultures and their actual conceptual schemes, but in a literary device. The modernist alternative would be to go visit the Indonesians, to study the subsaharan conceptual frameworks, to ask rigorously whether their categories can be mapped onto ours, whether one is more or less adequate to reality. That is hard, slow, empirical work. The postmodern move substitutes a fictional shortcut and then generalizes from it to a sweeping conclusion about the impossibility of objective knowledge. The sleight of hand is in the generalization — from 'here is a funny story about arbitrary categories' to 'therefore all categories are arbitrary.' This connects to a deeper question Brendan Graham Dempsey presses: the difference between recognizing that different cultures have different customs and concepts, and recognizing that those differences constitute whole *structures of thought* — epistemes, in Foucault's language — that shape what counts as a valid question, not just a valid answer. Hicks largely resists this, arguing the modern tradition already contains sensitivity to the problem. But Dempsey's point is subtler: it took a certain historical distance before the modern episteme itself could be seen *as* an episteme rather than simply as reason. The metamodern response is not to embrace incommensurability but to attempt a developmental meta-model — drawing on figures like Habermas and developmental psychology — that can situate different epistemic structures in relation to each other without collapsing into relativism. The needle being threaded is: plurality of frameworks is real, but plurality does not entail equivalence.

## Apprentice

There is a clever trick that shows up repeatedly in postmodern philosophy. Instead of studying how real cultures actually organize their knowledge, a thinker will point to a made-up story — like Borges's invented Chinese encyclopedia with absurd categories — and use it to argue that all ways of sorting the world are equally made up, including science. The leap from "here's a funny fictional example" to "therefore no classification system is more valid than any other" skips over the hard work of actually comparing how different peoples understand reality.

The alternative is genuinely empirical: go study how different cultures think, compare their categories carefully, and ask whether some ways of organizing knowledge work better than others. That kind of investigation is slow and difficult, which may be why the fictional shortcut is so tempting.

A deeper question emerges from this critique. It is one thing to notice that cultures differ; it is another to recognize that entire structures of thought shape what questions can even be asked. The metamodern response tries to hold both insights at once: yes, there are genuinely different frameworks for understanding the world, but that does not mean they are all equally good. The goal is to map how these frameworks relate to each other developmentally — acknowledging real diversity without falling into the claim that everything is relative.

## Adept

Stephen Hicks identifies a recurring rhetorical strategy in postmodern epistemology: the substitution of fictional narrative for empirical investigation as grounds for sweeping claims about knowledge. Foucault's famous opening of *The Order of Things* exemplifies this — the primary exhibit is not an anthropological study of actual divergent classification systems but a Borges fiction, an invented Chinese encyclopedia with deliberately absurd categories. The argumentative structure runs: if a fictional taxonomy can appear internally coherent, then coherence alone cannot distinguish scientific categories from literary invention; therefore, all taxonomies are conventional impositions rather than discoveries about reality. The sleight of hand lies in the generalization — moving from the observation that arbitrary categories can be imagined to the conclusion that all categories are arbitrary.

Brendan Graham Dempsey presses a subtler point against Hicks's critique. Recognizing cultural variation in customs and concepts is not the same as recognizing that entire epistemic structures — Foucault's epistemes — determine what counts as a legitimate question, not merely a legitimate answer. Hicks argues that the modern tradition already contains this sensitivity, but Dempsey counters that it required historical distance before modernity's own episteme could be perceived as an episteme rather than as transparent rationality itself. This is a genuinely important philosophical development, even if Foucault's method of establishing it is suspect.

The metamodern synthesis attempts to thread the needle: drawing on Habermasian communicative rationality and developmental psychology, it seeks a meta-framework that can situate different epistemic structures in developmental relation to one another. Plurality of frameworks is acknowledged as real and philosophically significant, but plurality is not taken to entail equivalence. The project is to build a model capacious enough to honor genuine difference without collapsing into the relativism that the postmodern fictional shortcut was designed to justify.

## Magus

Hicks's critique isolates a precise rhetorical operation in Foucault's epistemology: the displacement of empirical comparativism by literary exemplification as the evidentiary basis for claims about epistemic incommensurability. The locus classicus is the opening of *The Order of Things*, where a Borges fiction — not an ethnographic study of actually divergent classificatory practices — serves as the primary exhibit for the thesis that taxonomic order is imposed rather than discovered. The argumentative topology is revealing: internal coherence of an admittedly fictional scheme is taken to demonstrate that coherence per se cannot adjudicate between knowledge systems, licensing the conclusion that scientific categories enjoy no privileged epistemic status. What remains unexamined is precisely the empirical question — whether actual cross-cultural conceptual variation supports strong incommensurability or merely documents the expected plurality of adequate-but-partial mappings of a shared reality.

Dempsey's intervention complicates Hicks's defense of the modern tradition by distinguishing between first-order recognition of cultural difference and second-order recognition that entire structures of intelligibility — epistemes — constitute the conditions of possibility for inquiry itself. Hicks maintains that modern empiricism already accommodates conceptual diversity, but Dempsey's genealogical point is that modernity's self-recognition as one episteme among others required precisely the kind of historical reflexivity that poststructuralism, however problematically, made available. The modern episteme could not see itself as an episteme from within its own self-understanding as universal reason.

The metamodern response refuses both the postmodern collapse into equivalence and the modern refusal of epistemic plurality. Drawing on Habermasian reconstructive science and developmental-structural models, it proposes a meta-framework in which different epistemic configurations are situated relationally and hierarchically — not as incommensurable language games but as developmental achievements of varying scope and adequacy. The core thesis: plurality is ontologically real but axiologically differentiable. Frameworks differ, and some differences matter directionally.
