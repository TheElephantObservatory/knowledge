# Naturalizing the Sacred Without Evacuating Its Meaning

The real refuses to be reduced.

---

> Stripping religion down to what science can verify empties it of meaning. The real project is expanding our sense of what counts as knowledge — subjective, intersubjective, and mythic alongside objective — so that the sacred becomes not irrational but the most real thing we can point toward.

There is a crucial difference between making religion rational in the Cartesian, scientific sense — demythologizing it until only empirically verifiable propositions remain — and the deeper project of naturalizing transcendence in a way that preserves the full epistemic stack. The first project retains an epistemology that systematically excludes the subjective, the intersubjective, and the collective-mythic, and then wonders why the result feels evacuated of meaning. The second project — transcendent naturalism — recognizes that naturalism names the broader metaphysical context within which science operates, but that science as currently practiced carries a limited epistemology that cannot account for the physicist, only for physics. It cannot ground qualitative subjective knowing, cannot adequately theorize the intersubjective narrative dimension of human life, and cannot provide the consilient picture that would put scientific logos, subjective pathos, and collective mythos into right relation with one another. What is needed is not the reduction of religion to science but the expansion of our epistemological framework to a transactive one — objective, subjective, intersubjective, and collective vectors recognized as genuinely complementary, each disclosing aspects of reality the others cannot reach. In this expanded frame, the sacred is not irrational; it is, if anything, the most real domain we can gesture toward — the intimation toward further realness — and any adequate account of it must be rational in the broad sense of tracking intelligibility while refusing the narrowness that would make it disappear.

## Apprentice

There's a common assumption that making religion reasonable means trimming it down until only scientifically testable claims remain. But this approach quietly throws out everything that made religion matter in the first place — the felt sense of awe, the shared stories that bind communities, the personal experiences of meaning that no lab can measure. The result feels hollow, and that hollowness isn't a failure of religion; it's a failure of the filter.

The deeper alternative isn't to reject science but to recognize that science is one powerful way of knowing among several. Human beings also know things through direct personal experience, through the meanings that emerge between people in relationship, and through the grand stories cultures tell about who they are and what matters. Each of these ways of knowing reveals something the others miss.

When all these ways of knowing are taken seriously together, the sacred doesn't look irrational at all. It starts to look like a pointer toward something profoundly real — something our usual tools are too narrow to capture on their own. The goal isn't to reduce the sacred to equations, but to build a bigger picture of knowledge where science, personal experience, shared meaning, and collective story each have a genuine seat at the table.

## Adept

A persistent modern impulse seeks to rationalize religion by subjecting it to Cartesian-scientific epistemology — stripping away myth, symbol, and subjective depth until only empirically verifiable propositions remain. This demythologizing project operates under the assumption that scientific method exhausts legitimate knowing. But the result is predictably evacuated: an epistemology that can account for physics but not the physicist, for measurable phenomena but not the qualitative texture of experience or the narrative structures through which human communities make meaning. The problem isn't that religion resists reason; it's that the operative definition of reason is too narrow.

Transcendent naturalism offers a different path. It accepts naturalism as the broad metaphysical context — there is no appeal to the supernatural — but insists that science as currently practiced carries a limited epistemology within that context. A genuinely adequate framework must be transactive: recognizing objective, subjective, intersubjective, and collective-mythic vectors as complementary modes of disclosure, each accessing dimensions of reality the others structurally cannot reach. Logos, pathos, and mythos are not competitors but co-constitutive layers of a full epistemic stack.

Within this expanded frame, the sacred is reconceived not as the irrational residue that science hasn't yet explained away, but as an intimation toward further realness — the domain where intelligibility deepens beyond any single epistemic mode. Rationality, properly understood, means tracking intelligibility wherever it leads, not amputating the inquiry at the boundary of the measurable. The task is not the reduction of religion to science but the construction of a consilient epistemology capacious enough to hold both without collapsing either.

## Magus

The Enlightenment project of rationalizing religion has largely proceeded by epistemological subtraction — demythologizing until only empirically adjudicable propositions survive. This move presupposes that Cartesian-scientific epistemology is coextensive with rationality as such, a conflation that systematically excludes first-person qualitative knowing, second-person intersubjective meaning-constitution, and the collective-mythic register through which communities enact coherence across time. The resulting "rational religion" is not purified but amputated: it retains the epistemology that produces the explanatory gap between physics and the physicist, then treats the gap as a feature rather than an artifact of methodological constraint.

Transcendent naturalism reframes the problem at the level of metaphysical and epistemological architecture. Naturalism names the broadest ontological commitment — no recourse to the supernatural — but within that commitment, the epistemology adequate to the full scope of natural reality must be transactive rather than reductive. Objective, subjective, intersubjective, and collective vectors function as genuinely complementary modes of epistemic access, each disclosing strata of reality structurally opaque to the others. Scientific logos, experiential pathos, and collective mythos stand not in hierarchical relation but in consilient tension, and the adequacy of any framework is measured by its capacity to hold all three without premature collapse.

On this account, the sacred is neither supernatural addendum nor pre-scientific residue awaiting dissolution. It names the intimation toward further realness — the horizon where intelligibility intensifies beyond the carrying capacity of any single epistemic mode. Rationality in the broad sense means fidelity to intelligibility as such, which requires refusing the narrowness that would render the most integrative dimensions of human experience invisible. The task is not reduction but epistemic expansion: constructing a framework where the full stack — from measurable regularity to mythic depth — is recognized as disclosive of the real.
