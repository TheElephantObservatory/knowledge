# Why Complicated Systems Have Short Theories and Complex Systems Do Not

The gears do not learn from turning.

---

> The real difference between complicated and complex systems isn't whether you can take them apart and reassemble them — it's whether the components adapt. In complicated systems, a short theory covers everything; in complex systems, the theory itself must keep growing because adaptation generates irreducible novelty.

The complicated and the complex are genuinely distinct categories, but the distinction is not about whether something can be reassembled — that criterion is fatally subjective, depending on the skill of the observer rather than the nature of the system. The real distinction is this: in complicated systems, the components are stationary — they do not adapt, learn, or evolve. The engineer can redesign the clock, but the clock's gears do not become better gears through experience. This means theories of complicated systems scale perfectly: Newton's and Einstein's equations apply to all masses everywhere in the universe, and the theory does not need to grow as the system grows. Complex systems, by contrast, have what might be called extensivity — as the system grows, the description length of the theory must grow too. The same equations do not apply at every scale because the components adapt, and adaptation generates contingency, history, and broken symmetry that must be encoded in the theory itself. This is why there is no Einstein equation for the economy, the brain, or life. Crucially, this distinction also separates the generating process from the output: a three-line system of differential equations can generate chaotic, near-random behavior — but the theory of the generating process is short, so chaos is complicated, not complex. Complexity science is fundamentally about systems where the theory of the generating process itself must expand to match the irreducible richness of what the adaptive components have become.

## Apprentice

There's a popular way of distinguishing complicated things (like clocks) from complex things (like economies): complicated things can be taken apart and put back together, while complex things can't. But this distinction is flawed — it depends on who's doing the reassembling, not on anything real about the system itself. A skilled watchmaker can reassemble a clock; a novice can't. That tells us about the person, not the clock.

The deeper distinction is about whether the parts of a system adapt. A clock's gears don't learn or evolve. They sit there, doing what they've always done. Because of this, the rules governing the clock are beautifully compact — the same equations of physics work for every gear, every pendulum, everywhere in the universe. You don't need a bigger theory for a bigger clock.

Complex systems are fundamentally different. Their components — people in an economy, neurons in a brain, organisms in an ecosystem — change in response to each other. As the system grows, its history accumulates, and no short set of equations can capture what it has become. The theory has to grow alongside the system. This is also why chaos, despite looking wild and unpredictable, is actually complicated rather than complex: a few short equations generate all that apparent randomness. True complexity arises when even the generating rules can't stay short.

## Adept

The standard heuristic for separating the complicated from the complex — that complicated systems can be disassembled and reassembled while complex ones cannot — fails on its own terms. The criterion is observer-dependent: reassembly is a function of the skill brought to the task, not an intrinsic property of the system. A more rigorous distinction focuses on the nature of the components themselves. In complicated systems, components are stationary — they do not adapt, learn, or co-evolve. This stationarity is what allows theories of complicated systems to be compact and universal. Newtonian mechanics and general relativity apply to all masses at all scales; the description length of the theory does not grow as the system grows.

Complex systems exhibit what can be termed extensivity of theory: as the system scales, the minimal description of its generating process must scale with it. This happens because adaptive components introduce contingency, path-dependence, and broken symmetry that cannot be compressed away. Each adaptation event adds information that the theory must encode. There is no Einstein equation for the economy or the brain precisely because the irreducible history of adaptive interactions defeats any fixed-length formalism.

This framing also cleanly separates complexity from chaos. A low-dimensional system of differential equations can produce trajectories that are effectively indistinguishable from randomness — but the generating process remains short. Chaos is complicated, not complex. Complexity science, properly understood, concerns systems where the generating process itself must expand to faithfully represent what the adaptive components have become. The richness is not in the output but in the irreducible growth of the mechanism.

## Magus

The conventional demarcation between the complicated and the complex via reassembly is epistemically vacuous: it indexes observer competence rather than system ontology. The more productive criterion is stationarity of components. Complicated systems are composed of non-adaptive elements whose interactions are governed by time-invariant rules. This stationarity underwrites the compressibility of physical theory — Newtonian and relativistic mechanics achieve universality precisely because the entities they describe do not learn, and the Kolmogorov complexity of the theory remains constant as the system scales. The theory is intensive, not extensive.

Complex systems violate this intensivity. Their components adapt, and adaptation is an information-generating process: each co-evolutionary step breaks symmetries, creates contingent structure, and deposits historical residue that resists compression. The minimal description length of the generating process grows with the system — a property that might be formalized as theoretical extensivity. This is the deep reason no fixed-dimensional dynamical system captures an economy, a genome-phenotype map, or a neural architecture at arbitrary scale. The broken symmetries are not noise to be averaged over; they are constitutive of the phenomenon.

This framework yields a sharp classification of chaos as complicated rather than complex. Strange attractors generated by low-dimensional ODEs produce output of high algorithmic randomness, yet the generating process has negligible description length — the complexity is in the trajectory, not the mechanism. Genuine complexity, by contrast, demands that the mechanism itself be irreducibly rich. Complexity science is therefore not the study of systems that produce surprising behavior from simple rules; it is the study of systems whose rules cannot remain simple because the components that instantiate them are themselves evolving repositories of encoded history. The theory must grow because the world it describes has not stopped becoming.
