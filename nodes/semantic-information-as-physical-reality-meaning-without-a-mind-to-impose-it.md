# Semantic Information as Physical Reality: Meaning Without a Mind to Impose It

The deer already knows what matters.

---

> Shannon's theory measures whether information arrives intact, but says nothing about whether it matters. A new framework grounded in far-from-equilibrium thermodynamics shows that meaning arises naturally when an entity's survival depends on the information it holds about itself and its world.

One of the most important distinctions in this conversation is between Shannon information and semantic information — and why conflating them has caused so much confusion. Claude Shannon gave us a way to measure information as accuracy: if I send you an email with a billion random numbers and you receive it intact, you have, in the Shannon sense, received information. But that email is useless. It means nothing to you. It has no bearing on your life, your decisions, your survival. Shannon information says nothing about what information is *for*. It has no aboutness. The breakthrough that comes from thinkers like Emy Kolchinsky and David Wolpert, working at the intersection of complexity science and far-from-equilibrium thermodynamics, is that information can have intrinsic semantic content when we think about entities in context — entities that are informationally distinct from their environments and that need to maintain that distinction through time. In that framing, meaningful information is the information an entity has about itself and its environment that bears on its continued existence. This is not meaning imposed from above by a conscious observer. It is meaning that arises from the structure of the situation itself. The deer responding to the lightening sky at dawn is processing semantically meaningful information — not because we say so, but because that information causally bears on its viability. This reframing allows us to ground meaning in physics without reducing it to mere mechanism, and it opens the door to tracking how meaning complexifies all the way from dissipative structures to human culture.

## Apprentice

Claude Shannon revolutionized communication by figuring out how to measure information — but his measure only tracks accuracy, not meaning. A billion random numbers transmitted perfectly count as high information in Shannon's framework, even though they're completely useless to the person receiving them. This leaves a massive gap: how do we account for the difference between data that's merely accurate and data that actually matters?

The key move comes from recognizing that some entities in the world — a cell, a deer, a person — are distinct from their surroundings and need to stay that way to survive. For these entities, certain information genuinely matters: it bears on whether they keep existing. A deer reading the lightening sky at dawn isn't just processing signals — that information is directly relevant to its life. The meaning isn't something a human observer projects onto the situation. It's built into the relationship between the deer and its world.

This insight, developed by thinkers like Artemy Kolchinsky and David Wolpert, offers a way to talk about meaning that starts in physics and scales all the way up to human culture — without ever needing to invoke a mysterious extra ingredient.

## Adept

Shannon information theory provides a powerful framework for quantifying the fidelity of signal transmission, but it is deliberately agnostic about meaning. A perfectly transmitted string of random digits carries maximal Shannon information while conveying zero semantic content. This deliberate omission — the exclusion of "aboutness" — has been enormously productive for engineering but has generated persistent confusion whenever information theory is imported into biology, cognitive science, or philosophy of mind.

The critical advance comes from work by Artemy Kolchinsky, David Wolpert, and others operating at the intersection of complexity science and nonequilibrium thermodynamics. Their framework identifies semantic information as information that an individuated entity — one that maintains informational boundaries distinguishing it from its environment — possesses about itself and its surroundings insofar as that information bears on the entity's continued viability. Meaning, in this account, is not observer-dependent attribution but an intrinsic feature of the structural coupling between an entity and its context. The deer processing the lightening sky at dawn exemplifies this: the causal relevance of that signal to the deer's survival constitutes its semantic content.

This reframing accomplishes something philosophically significant. It grounds meaning in physics — specifically in the thermodynamic requirements of far-from-equilibrium self-maintenance — without collapsing it into mere mechanism. It also provides a principled way to trace how semantic complexity scales from minimal dissipative structures through biological organisms to the elaborate meaning-making of human culture, offering a continuous naturalistic account where previously there was only a gap between syntax and semantics.

## Magus

The Shannon-semantic conflation represents one of the most consequential category errors in contemporary interdisciplinary discourse. Shannon's mathematical theory of communication explicitly brackets semantics — his measure quantifies surprise relative to a probability distribution over signals, remaining orthogonal to any question of reference, relevance, or functional consequence. Yet the cultural authority of information theory has repeatedly licensed the implicit assumption that Shannon entropy exhausts what information is, leaving meaning either epiphenomenal or inexplicable.

The decisive intervention, articulated most rigorously by Kolchinsky and Wolpert, reconceives semantic information through the lens of nonequilibrium statistical mechanics. An entity individuated from its environment — maintaining a viability set within phase space through continuous thermodynamic work — possesses semantic information to the extent that its internal states carry mutual information with environmental variables that are causally relevant to its persistence. This is not meaning conferred by an external interpreter but meaning constituted by the dynamical coupling between an autonomous system and the boundary conditions on which its far-from-equilibrium existence depends. The crepuscular deer processing photic gradients at dawn instantiates genuine semantic processing: the information's aboutness is underwritten by its causal bearing on the organism's viability, not by any observer's hermeneutic act.

What makes this framework philosophically potent is its capacity to naturalize meaning without eliminativism. By anchoring semantics in the thermodynamic imperatives of self-individuating systems, it provides a graded, physically grounded account of how aboutness complexifies — from autocatalytic dissipative structures maintaining compositional closure, through organisms with increasingly sophisticated environmental models, to cultural systems whose semantic structures decouple partially from immediate viability constraints while remaining genealogically rooted in them. The syntax-semantics gap does not require a bridge from outside physics; it requires recognizing that physics, properly understood at the nonequilibrium regime, already contains the resources for meaning's emergence.
