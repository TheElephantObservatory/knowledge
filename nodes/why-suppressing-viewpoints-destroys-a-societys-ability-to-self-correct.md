# Why Suppressing Viewpoints Destroys a Society's Ability to Self-Correct

He knew. He bent the knee anyway.

---

> Suppressing viewpoints doesn't just silence dissenters — it destroys the parallax effect that lets societies triangulate toward truth. When someone like Zuckerberg articulates this principle and then violates it anyway, the failure is not intellectual but moral.

There is a moral argument, distinct from the technical one, about why platforms like Facebook should not be in the business of policing viewpoints at all — and it begins with the open society tradition running from Kant through Karl Popper. The core insight is that we do not experience reality directly. We see it through the filters of our perceptions, our tools, our frameworks. This means that the more distinct perspectives we can bring to bear on any question — including aberrant, heterodox, even apparently crazy ones — the better our collective ability to triangulate toward reality. Parallax requires distance between viewpoints. A society that suppresses perspectives loses not just those perspectives but the error-correcting function that comes from their friction with other perspectives. Popper's argument in 'The Open Society and Its Enemies' is that a society tolerating diverse viewpoints is dramatically more self-correcting and evolutionarily capable than one that suppresses them. The practical implication is that even QAnon, even Marxist-Leninism, even the most apparently dangerous ideas, serve a function in the epistemic ecosystem — they are data points against which better ideas can sharpen themselves. The moral failure of Zuckerberg is not merely technical incompetence. It is that he demonstrably understands this argument — he articulated a sophisticated defense of free speech at Georgetown — and then bent the knee anyway to social pressure. That is not confusion or error. That is knowing the right thing and doing the wrong thing. That is the essence of immorality in the classical sense.

## Apprentice

We never see reality perfectly. Everything we know comes filtered through our senses, our assumptions, our tools. Because of this, the best way to get closer to the truth is to gather as many different perspectives as possible — even ones that seem wrong or dangerous. Just as you need two eyes spaced apart to perceive depth, a society needs distant and conflicting viewpoints to perceive reality more accurately. When you shut down a perspective, you don't just lose that voice — you lose the ability to test your own ideas against it.

This line of thinking runs from Kant through Karl Popper, who argued in 'The Open Society and Its Enemies' that societies tolerating diverse viewpoints are far better at correcting their own mistakes than societies that suppress dissent. The practical consequence is striking: even ideas most people find repugnant — conspiracy theories, extremist ideologies — serve a function by forcing better ideas to prove themselves.

This reframes the debate about social media censorship. The charge against Mark Zuckerberg is not that he failed to understand this argument — he laid out a sophisticated version of it in a speech at Georgetown. The charge is that he understood it and then abandoned it under social pressure. In classical moral terms, that is not ignorance. Knowing the right thing and choosing not to do it is the definition of moral failure.

## Adept

The epistemological tradition from Kant through Popper rests on a foundational claim: human beings do not have unmediated access to reality. Perception is always filtered through cognitive frameworks, instruments, and cultural assumptions. From this it follows that epistemic progress depends on maximizing the diversity of perspectives brought to bear on any question. The metaphor of parallax is instructive — depth perception requires distance between vantage points. A society that narrows the range of permissible viewpoints does not merely silence dissidents; it degrades its own capacity for error correction. Popper formalized this in 'The Open Society and Its Enemies,' arguing that open societies possess a decisive evolutionary advantage precisely because they institutionalize the friction between competing perspectives.

The implications for platform governance are severe. If the parallax model holds, then even ideologies widely regarded as dangerous — QAnon, Marxist-Leninism, various forms of denialism — perform a necessary function in the epistemic ecosystem. They are the data points against which more robust ideas refine themselves. Removing them doesn't eliminate error; it eliminates the mechanism by which error is detected.

This frames Zuckerberg's capitulation to censorship pressure as something more than a policy misstep. In his 2019 Georgetown address, he articulated a philosophically literate defense of free expression that tracks closely with the Popperian argument. His subsequent reversal under political and social pressure therefore cannot be attributed to ignorance or confusion. It is the deliberate abandonment of a principle one has publicly endorsed and demonstrably understands. In the classical moral tradition — from Aristotle's akrasia to Kant's categorical imperative — this is not mere weakness. It is the paradigmatic form of moral failure: knowing the good and choosing against it.

## Magus

The Kantian insight that phenomena are always mediated — that the Ding an sich remains inaccessible behind the structuring activity of cognition — carries implications far beyond epistemology proper. When extended through Popper's critical rationalism, it yields a powerful normative argument for viewpoint pluralism: if no single perspective can claim unmediated access to reality, then the reliability of collective knowledge production is a function of the heterogeneity of perspectives in play. Parallax — the displacement of apparent position caused by a change in the point of observation — becomes the operative metaphor. Epistemic triangulation requires maximal baseline distance between observers. Popper's 'Open Society' thesis is precisely that societies institutionalizing this parallax function outperform closed societies not contingently but structurally, because they embed error correction into their social epistemology.

The radical corollary is that heterodox, aberrant, even prima facie dangerous ideologies are not noise to be filtered but signal essential to the system's self-correcting dynamics. QAnon and Marxist-Leninism function as stress tests for the prevailing epistemic order. Their suppression does not eliminate the errors they embody; it eliminates the friction through which those errors become legible and through which competing frameworks are forced to articulate their own justificatory structures more rigorously.

This analysis recasts Zuckerberg's trajectory from his Georgetown speech to his subsequent accommodation of censorship demands as a case study in classical akrasia — moral incontinence in the Aristotelian sense. The Georgetown address demonstrates sophisticated comprehension of the Popperian framework. The subsequent capitulation cannot therefore be diagnosed as epistemic failure. It is praxis contradicting articulated principle under external pressure, which places it squarely within the domain of moral culpability rather than intellectual limitation. The distinction matters: error is correctable through better reasoning, but the deliberate betrayal of understood principle corrodes the very faculty of judgment on which open society depends.
