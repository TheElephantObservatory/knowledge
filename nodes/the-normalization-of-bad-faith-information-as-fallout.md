# The Normalization of Bad Faith: Information as Fallout

We've reached a point where the basic informational surround — the ecosystem in which we socialize — has been so thoroughly overrun with strategic communication that bad faith communication is becoming normalized. Think of it like dropping the equivalent of an atomic bomb in the informational warfare space, which happened roughly around 2016. What followed is a kind of background radiation seeping into the lifeworld. Computational propaganda, coupled with AI and peak advertising, has created informational weapons of mass destruction. And the insidious consequence isn't just that bad actors are deploying these weapons — it's that the basic frame of social media itself has come to accept strategic communication as the only viable form of communication. So we fall back habitually into bad faith tactics, not because we're malicious, but because we've been socialized in an environment where that's simply the water we swim in. This is the context the Consilience Project is trying to name and address before any other serious civilizational conversation can even take place.

## Apprentice

Something significant shifted around 2016. The online spaces where people talk, argue, and form opinions became flooded with a new kind of communication — one designed not to share truth or build understanding, but to manipulate, persuade, and win. Bots, targeted advertising, coordinated disinformation campaigns, and eventually AI all poured into the same streams where ordinary people were just trying to connect and make sense of the world. The damage wasn't only from the bad actors running these operations. The deeper problem is what happened to everyone else. When manipulation becomes the dominant mode of communication in a space, people start absorbing its habits. Spin, misdirection, and rhetorical tricks stop feeling like cheating and start feeling like just how conversation works. Nobody decided to become a bad-faith communicator — they were simply trained by their environment to be one. This is the argument the Consilience Project advances: before society can solve its biggest problems, it needs to honestly reckon with the fact that the informational ground beneath those conversations has been poisoned. Recognizing that poisoning is the necessary first step.

## Adept

The Consilience Project's framing identifies a structural rupture in the shared informational environment, locating its approximate origin around 2016, when the convergence of computational propaganda, algorithmic amplification, and industrialized advertising reached a kind of critical mass. The metaphor deployed is deliberately stark: an informational equivalent of an atomic detonation, followed not by immediate devastation but by persistent background radiation seeping into the lifeworld — a term drawn from Habermasian social theory referring to the pre-theoretical horizon of shared meanings within which communicative action takes place. The core claim is that strategic communication — communication oriented toward winning rather than toward mutual understanding — has ceased to be an aberration and has become the default grammar of digitally mediated social life. This is distinct from claiming that most people are cynically manipulative. The more unsettling argument is that bad-faith communicative habits are now being reproduced structurally, through socialization in environments where authenticity is systematically disadvantaged. Social media platforms, optimized for engagement rather than epistemic quality, function as training grounds for rhetorical manipulation. The consequence is a degradation of the communicative preconditions that deliberative democracy and collective sensemaking require. This analysis positions informational ecology as a foundational civilizational concern — one that must be named and addressed before substantive discourse on governance, science, or ethics can be conducted on reliable ground.

## Magus

The thesis advanced here operates at the intersection of critical theory, media ecology, and epistemology of testimony. Drawing implicitly on Habermas's distinction between communicative and strategic action, the argument holds that the lifeworld — that intersubjectively constituted background of tacit norms, shared meanings, and communicative trust upon which all higher-order discourse depends — has undergone a colonization not merely by systemic imperatives of money and power, as Habermas originally theorized, but by a technologically weaponized information environment that has structurally inverted the communicative norms of the public sphere. The 2016 threshold functions as a periodizing marker for the maturation of what might be termed informational weapons of mass destruction: the synthesis of computational propaganda, psychographic microtargeting, adversarial AI, and platform architectures optimized for affective engagement over epistemic fidelity. The insidious mechanism is not primarily one of deliberate bad-faith agency but of what could be called communicative habituation under adversarial conditions — a process by which strategic, manipulative, and epistemically irresponsible communicative behaviors become normalized through iterative socialization in degraded informational ecologies. This resonates with Bourdieu's concept of habitus: dispositions toward communication are not chosen but acquired, and when the field of acquisition is systematically distorted, the resulting habitus encodes distortion as competence. The civilizational stakes are high because the preconditions for Habermasian ideal speech — sincerity, comprehensibility, legitimacy, truth-orientation — are precisely what this environment erodes. Any serious project of collective sensemaking, democratic deliberation, or coordinated response to existential risk must therefore begin with a diagnostic account of this epistemic substrate before its substantive agenda can be coherently pursued.
