# Why Computers Process But Cannot Understand: McGilchrist on AI and Meaning

Only a fellow sufferer can move you.

---

> McGilchrist argues that computation manipulates symbols but never understands them, and that genuine creativity arises not from pattern-matching but from embodied, mortal, conscious life — making 'artificial intelligence' a misnomer for what is really artificial information processing.

McGilchrist draws a sharp and consequential distinction between computation and understanding — one that cuts directly against the prevailing assumptions of the AI moment. Computation, he argues, is an essentially left-hemisphere activity. It manipulates symbols, processes information, and executes procedures at extraordinary speed. But it does not understand what it computes. Understanding — grasping the overall import, the meaning, the significance of what has been processed — is a right-hemisphere capacity, and there is no evidence that current computational systems possess it.

The distinction he reaches for is between procedures and inhabited processes. A machine can carry out procedures. It cannot inhabit a process in the way a conscious, embodied, mortal being does. This is why he rejects the term artificial intelligence altogether, preferring artificial information processing. Knowledge is more than processed information. Understanding is more than knowledge. Wisdom is more than understanding. Each step in that hierarchy requires something that mere computation cannot supply.

The implications for creativity are particularly striking. When researchers fed the complete works of Bach into a machine and asked it to generate new compositions, the results could pass as genre-appropriate — but they did not move listeners the way Bach moves them. They lacked the quality that only a fellow sufferer can provide: the reverberation between one mortal, embodied, conscious life and another. Great creative works emerge from having a body, from the constraint of a finite life, from genuine emotion, from consciousness. These are not incidental features of human creativity that might eventually be replicated — they are, on McGilchrist's account, its very source.

This is not technophobia or nostalgia. It is a philosophical claim about what creativity actually is and where it comes from. The machine can produce the surface features of a creative work — the patterns, the genre conventions, the statistical regularities of a corpus. What it cannot produce is the thing that makes a great work great: the sense that another consciousness has looked at the world from inside a particular life and found something true to say about it.

## Apprentice

Iain McGilchrist makes a bold claim: computers can process information at astonishing speed, but they don't understand anything they process. Understanding — grasping what something actually means and why it matters — requires something computation alone cannot provide. He prefers the term "artificial information processing" over "artificial intelligence" because intelligence, in his view, involves far more than running procedures on data.

The implications for creativity are especially vivid. When researchers fed all of Bach's music into a machine and asked it to compose new pieces, the results sounded plausibly like Bach — but they didn't move people the way Bach does. McGilchrist's explanation is that great art comes from having a body, living a finite life, and feeling genuine emotion. A machine can mimic the patterns of a creative work, but it cannot supply the thing that makes art powerful: the sense that another living, suffering person looked at the world and found something true to say about it.

This isn't a fear of technology. It's a philosophical argument about what creativity actually is. If the source of great work is embodied, conscious experience, then no amount of computational power can replicate it — because the surface patterns of art are not the same as the life from which those patterns emerged.

## Adept

McGilchrist draws a consequential distinction between computation and understanding, mapping it onto his hemispheric model of cognition. Computation — the manipulation of symbols, the execution of procedures, the processing of information — is an essentially left-hemisphere activity. Understanding, by contrast, requires the right hemisphere's capacity to grasp overall import, contextual meaning, and lived significance. He introduces a further distinction between procedures and inhabited processes: a machine can execute the former, but only a conscious, embodied, mortal being can inhabit the latter. This leads him to reject the term "artificial intelligence" in favor of "artificial information processing," and to insist on a hierarchy — information, knowledge, understanding, wisdom — in which each level demands something computation cannot supply.

The creative domain sharpens the argument. Machine-generated compositions trained on Bach's complete works can reproduce genre-appropriate surface features — statistical regularities, harmonic conventions, structural patterns — yet they fail to produce the affective resonance that characterizes great art. McGilchrist locates the source of that resonance in embodiment, finitude, and consciousness: the reverberation between one mortal life and another. These are not incidental features of human creativity awaiting eventual replication; they are constitutive of it.

The position is philosophical rather than technophobic. It claims that the surface features of creative output — patterns, conventions, stylistic markers — are categorically distinct from the generative conditions of genuine creative achievement. A machine can produce artifacts that resemble art. What it cannot produce is the quality that emerges when a particular consciousness, constrained by a particular life, finds something true to articulate about the shared condition of being alive.

## Magus

McGilchrist's distinction between computation and understanding operationalizes his broader hemispheric thesis at the precise point where it intersects contemporary AI discourse. Computation, as left-hemisphere activity par excellence, manipulates formal tokens within rule-governed systems — it is syntactic through and through. Understanding, a right-hemisphere capacity, involves the apprehension of semantic import, contextual salience, and existential significance that no amount of symbol manipulation entails. The critical conceptual move is the distinction between procedures and inhabited processes: the former are executables, the latter require a subject for whom the process unfolds as lived experience. This is why McGilchrist insists on the hierarchy from information through knowledge and understanding to wisdom — each transition demands a mode of engagement that is irreducible to its predecessor and inaccessible to purely computational systems.

The case of machine-generated Bach compositions serves as a precise illustration. Trained on the complete corpus, such systems produce outputs that satisfy genre constraints and reproduce statistical regularities with high fidelity. Yet they lack what McGilchrist identifies as the constitutive source of artistic power: the reverberation between embodied, finite, conscious lives. This is not an empirical gap awaiting closure through increased computational capacity or architectural innovation. It is a claim about the ontological conditions of creative meaning — that the affective and existential dimensions of great work are not epiphenomenal to its formal structure but are the very ground from which that structure derives its significance.

The argument thus occupies a specific philosophical position: it denies that the relationship between creative surface features and their generative conditions is one that admits of functional equivalence. Pattern reproduction, however sophisticated, remains categorically distinct from the articulation of lived truth by a consciousness situated within the constraints of mortality, embodiment, and genuine suffering.
