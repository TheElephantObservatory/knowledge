# How Religions Are Born, Spread, and Captured by Power

A hundred messiahs walked into history.

---

> The sheer number of religions — founded by charismatic individuals, surviving by narrative fitness and historical accident, then captured by power structures — is itself the strongest evidence that none of them are true.

At age 11, after systematically reading through hundreds of religions at the county library, Jim Rutt had an epiphany that religion is fundamentally a human construction — not designed to control people, as he initially believed, but typically founded by charismatic individuals who were, in his words, 'ravers.' The ones we've heard of are probably one in a hundred that survived. Jesus, for instance, was one of roughly a hundred people claiming to be the Messiah at the time. What made Christianity persist wasn't divine truth but contingency — a near-run thing, with only a thousand Christians by 100 AD, saved partly by the salesmanship of St. Paul. Later, the powers that be — the Roman Empire and others — captured these movements and turned them into instruments of control. The key insight isn't that religion is evil, but that it's a natural human phenomenon: charismatic founders, survival of the most compelling narrative, and eventual institutional capture. This framework applies equally to Christianity, Islam, Hinduism, and Buddhism. The implication is that the diversity of religions — thousands of them, with mutually exclusive metaphysical claims — is itself the strongest evidence against any of them being true. If Presbyterians believe in predestination, Catholics believe in priestly magic, and Methodists believe in neither, they can't all be right, and the most parsimonious explanation is that none of them are.

## Apprentice

Jim Rutt arrived at a striking conclusion as an eleven-year-old after reading about hundreds of religions in his county library: religion is a human construction. Not a conspiracy designed to control people, but something more organic — charismatic individuals throughout history have founded spiritual movements, and the ones we remember are simply the tiny fraction that survived. Jesus, for example, was one of roughly a hundred people claiming to be the Messiah in his era. Christianity's persistence owed less to divine truth than to historical accident and the salesmanship of figures like St. Paul.

What happens next is institutional capture. Once a movement gains enough followers, power structures — the Roman Empire, caliphates, monarchies — co-opt it and reshape it into an instrument of social control. This pattern repeats across Christianity, Islam, Hinduism, and Buddhism alike.

The deepest implication is simple but powerful: thousands of religions exist, making mutually exclusive claims about the nature of reality. Presbyterians insist on predestination, Catholics on priestly sacramental power, Methodists on neither. They cannot all be right. And the most straightforward explanation for this diversity isn't that one got lucky and found the truth — it's that none of them did. The multiplicity of religions is itself the strongest argument against any single one being correct.

## Adept

Jim Rutt's framework for understanding religion, developed precociously through systematic comparative study, treats religious movements as natural phenomena subject to evolutionary dynamics rather than as either divine revelations or deliberate conspiracies. The model identifies three distinct phases: origination by charismatic founders (what Rutt bluntly calls "ravers"), differential survival through narrative fitness and historical contingency, and eventual institutional capture by existing power structures. Christianity serves as the paradigm case — Jesus was one of approximately a hundred messianic claimants in first-century Palestine, the movement numbered only about a thousand adherents by 100 AD, and its survival depended critically on the rhetorical genius of Paul of Tarsus before being co-opted as Roman state religion under Constantine.

This is essentially a cultural-evolutionary account of religion that parallels biological selection: many variants arise, most go extinct, survivors persist not because they are "true" but because they possess features — compelling narrative, social cohesion mechanisms, adaptability to power — that confer differential replication advantages. The framework applies with equal force across traditions: Christianity, Islam, Hinduism, and Buddhism all exhibit the same pattern of charismatic founding, contingent survival, and institutional capture.

The epistemological payoff is a parsimony argument against religious truth claims. The existence of thousands of religions making mutually exclusive metaphysical assertions — predestination versus free will, priestly sacramental power versus its absence, reincarnation versus resurrection — constitutes a massive evidential problem for any single tradition's claim to unique truth. The most economical explanation for this diversity is not that one tradition stumbled onto reality while all others erred, but that the entire phenomenon is human-generated, with no tradition possessing privileged access to metaphysical truth.

## Magus

Rutt's autodidactic synthesis, reached through exhaustive comparative reading at age eleven, constitutes a proto-evolutionary epistemology of religion that anticipates later work by Dennett, Atran, and Boyer while arriving at its conclusions through a distinct inferential pathway. The model is tripartite: stochastic origination by charismatic founders operating within what Weber would term prophetic authority; differential survival governed not by veridical correspondence to metaphysical reality but by narrative compellingness, institutional adaptability, and sheer historical contingency; and subsequent capture by hegemonic power structures that instrumentalize surviving movements for social control. The Christianity case study is instructive precisely in its contingency — one messianic claimant among approximately a hundred, a movement of perhaps a thousand adherents a century after its founding, rescued from obscurity largely through Pauline rhetorical innovation before its Constantinian co-optation.

The epistemological move is a sophisticated application of the principle of parsimony to the problem of religious pluralism. Thousands of extant and extinct traditions advance mutually exclusive metaphysical claims — Calvinist predestination, Catholic sacramental realism, Methodist Arminianism represent merely intra-Christian contradictions before one even addresses inter-traditional incompatibilities regarding soteriology, cosmogony, and eschatology. The logical space permits at most one tradition to be veridical; the prior probability assignable to any given tradition is vanishingly small.

Critically, Rutt's framework resists the reductive temptation to characterize religion as mere false consciousness or elite conspiracy. The phenomenon is naturalized without being demonized — charismatic founding is treated as a recurrent feature of human social cognition, not as pathology. Institutional capture is a secondary process, parasitic upon movements that originate organically. This distinction between origination and instrumentalization preserves explanatory nuance while still delivering the deflationary conclusion: the diversity of religious claims, understood through their generative mechanisms, constitutes the strongest available evidence against the truth of any particular one.
