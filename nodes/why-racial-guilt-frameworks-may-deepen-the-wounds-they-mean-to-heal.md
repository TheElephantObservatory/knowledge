# Why Racial Guilt Frameworks May Deepen the Wounds They Mean to Heal

The medicine that feeds the disease

---

> Identity-based frameworks for historical healing may inadvertently reinforce the very racial categories that produced the original harm. A more generative path holds specific atrocities within the full arc of human brutality, enabling shared reckoning rather than guilt between fixed groups.

There is something both important and insufficient about the way American culture currently approaches historical healing — particularly around slavery and the genocide of indigenous peoples. The impulse to reckon with these wounds is genuine and necessary. But the way it is often practiced may actually reinforce the very divisions it seeks to heal. When the frame is 'I am a white American, therefore I must heal the transatlantic slave trade,' the identity categories that produced the harm are being reified and re-inscribed in the very act of trying to address it. A potentially more generative approach would be to hold these particular historical tragedies within the much broader context of human suffering across all civilizations and all of recorded history — the slavery that existed across cultures, the genocides, the wars, the human sacrifices. This isn't a move to relativize or minimize specific harms. It's a move toward a deeper and more universal identity as humans who share a long and often brutal collective story. From that vantage point, healing becomes less about guilt and reparation between fixed racial categories, and more about a shared reckoning with what human beings have done to each other across time. The chicken-and-egg problem is real: you need a grounded sense of universal human identity to do that kind of healing, but doing that kind of healing is part of how you build that identity. But the direction matters, and the current direction seems to be making things harder rather than easier.

## Apprentice

American culture has begun to seriously engage with the wounds of slavery and indigenous genocide, and this impulse is genuine and important. But there's a subtle problem with how this healing often works in practice. When someone approaches it as "I'm white, so I need to atone for slavery," they're reinforcing the same racial categories that created the harm in the first place. The very act of healing keeps the old divisions alive.

A different approach would place these specific tragedies within the much larger story of human suffering — slavery existed across many civilizations, genocides have occurred on every continent, and cruelty is woven through all of recorded history. This isn't about minimizing what happened in America. It's about finding a deeper starting point: our shared identity as human beings with a brutal collective past.

The challenge is circular. You need a sense of universal human identity to do this kind of healing well, but doing this kind of healing is part of how you develop that identity. Still, the direction matters. The current approach, rooted in fixed group identities, seems to be deepening divisions rather than resolving them.

## Adept

There is a structural paradox embedded in contemporary American approaches to historical trauma — particularly around the transatlantic slave trade and indigenous genocide. The reckoning impulse is legitimate, but the dominant frameworks may be self-undermining. When historical healing is organized around the same racial identity categories that produced the original violence, those categories are reified in the very act of attempting repair. The oppressor-oppressed dyad becomes a permanent architecture rather than a historical condition to be transcended.

The alternative proposed here is not relativism but contextualization at a different scale. Placing American slavery and genocide within the full panorama of human atrocity — slavery across civilizations, ritual human sacrifice, ethnic cleansing on every continent — doesn't diminish specific harms. It shifts the locus of identity from racial particularity to shared humanity. Healing then becomes less a transaction between fixed groups defined by guilt and grievance, and more a collective confrontation with what our species has repeatedly done to itself.

This reframing encounters a genuine chicken-and-egg problem: the universal human identity required to hold this broader frame is itself partly constituted through the practice of inclusive historical reckoning. The circularity is real but not paralyzing — what matters is directionality. The current trajectory, which deepens identification with racial categories as the primary vehicle for healing, appears to entrench the very divisions it aims to dissolve. The insight is that the unit of analysis for historical healing needs to be larger than the nation-state and deeper than any single axis of identity.

## Magus

The prevailing praxis of historical healing in American public life exhibits a performative contradiction: it seeks to transcend racialized violence while operating entirely within the epistemic frame that racialization itself produced. When the subject position from which one undertakes reparative work is "white American" confronting "Black suffering" or "settler" confronting "indigenous erasure," the identity categories forged through domination are not interrogated but reinscribed. The wound is dressed with the instrument that made it.

This critique does not counsel amnesia or false equivalence. Rather, it proposes a scalar shift in the horizon of moral reckoning. The transatlantic slave trade and the genocide of indigenous peoples are situated within the deep-historical continuum of human predation — chattel systems across Mesopotamia, Rome, West Africa, and the Mongol steppe; sacrificial economies in Mesoamerica and Bronze Age Europe; ethnic annihilation from Carthage to Cambodia. The move is genealogical rather than relativizing: it locates the capacity for atrocity in the species rather than the phenotype, thereby opening a subject position — shared humanity — from which genuine integration of historical trauma becomes possible without the recursive reproduction of group antagonism.

The epistemological difficulty is frankly acknowledged: the universal identity that would ground this broader reckoning is not prior to the reckoning but co-constituted by it. This is a hermeneutic circle, not a logical refutation. What remains decisive is vector, not origin. The current vector — intensifying identification with racial particularity as the precondition for historical engagement — appears to generate increasing fragmentation and moral rigidity. The counterproposal is that healing at civilizational scale requires a subject commensurate with that scale: not the racial self but the human one, confronting its own full and terrible record.
