# How Each Mode of Knowing Tests Contact with Reality

To name a thing truly is to love it.

---

> Each way of knowing has its own signal of realness — conviction, power, or presence. Presence matters because it recovers what propositions hide: the inexhaustible depth of everything we encounter. Attending to that inexhaustibility is what it means to love something.

Each mode of knowing has its own criterion of realness — its own signal that you are in genuine contact with reality rather than merely processing representations. For propositional knowing, the criterion is conviction: we believe we have grasped something true. For procedural knowing, the criterion is power: we sense we can cause things to happen, that we are empowered. For perspectival knowing, the criterion is presence — that felt sense of being fully here, which does not require propositional verisimilitude. You can experience profound presence playing Tetris. But why do we seek presence so urgently? Cognitively, we are training insight and implicit learning. Existentially, presence serves to remember what our propositions systematically blind us to: the suchness and the moreness of everything we encounter. Propositions work by placing things into containers — categories, concepts, marshaled together into claims. This is enormously powerful and we could not live without it. But notice what language itself does to compensate: we have proper names. When I call something a cat, I am aware only of its categorical features. When I call you Taylor, I cannot categorize you — I have to tailor the name just to you, because you exceed all possible categorizations I can bring to bear. This inexhaustibility is not a quirk of persons; everything has it. And the moreness that exceeds any individual thing's suchness is not that thing's alone — every moreness is interwoven and interconnected. What makes something real is precisely its inexhaustible intelligibility, the fact that we can always go further into it. When you begin to know something in that way — attending to its inexhaustibility rather than merely its categorical features — you are loving it. Presence is the practice of recovering that loving attention.

## Apprentice

We have different ways of knowing things, and each one comes with its own feeling of contact with reality. When we know facts, the signal is conviction — we feel sure something is true. When we know how to do things, the signal is a sense of power — we can make things happen. But there is a third kind of knowing that shows up as presence: the vivid feeling of being fully here. You can feel it playing a simple video game. It does not require believing anything in particular.

Why do we crave that feeling so much? One reason is practical — presence trains our ability to notice patterns and learn without conscious effort. But there is a deeper reason. The words and categories we use to understand the world are incredibly useful, yet they always leave something out. When you call an animal "a cat," you see only its general features. But when you call a friend by name, you are acknowledging that they overflow every label you could attach to them. This inexhaustibility is not unique to people — everything has more to it than our descriptions can capture, and all of that "moreness" is woven together.

What makes something real, on this view, is precisely the fact that you can always go deeper into it. When you start paying attention to that depth instead of just sorting things into boxes, something shifts. That kind of attention is, in a meaningful sense, love. And presence is the practice of returning to it.

## Adept

John Vervaeke's framework distinguishes three participatory modes of knowing — propositional, procedural, and perspectival — each carrying its own criterion of genuine contact with reality. Propositional knowing signals itself through conviction (the sense of having grasped truth), procedural knowing through power (the felt capacity to intervene causally), and perspectival knowing through presence — the phenomenological sense of being fully situated, which is independent of propositional accuracy. The urgency with which we seek presence has both a cognitive and an existential explanation: cognitively, it trains insight and implicit learning; existentially, it serves as a corrective to the systematic blindness built into propositional cognition.

That blindness arises because propositions work by categorical containment — placing things into conceptual containers and marshaling those containers into claims. This is indispensable, but it necessarily strips away what Vervaeke calls the "suchness" (the particular, irreducible thisness) and the "moreness" (the inexhaustible depth) of whatever is encountered. Language itself gestures toward this problem through the device of proper names: calling something "a cat" foregrounds categorical features, while calling someone "Taylor" acknowledges that the person exceeds every categorization one could bring to bear. Crucially, this inexhaustibility is not a special property of persons — it belongs to everything.

The moreness of any given thing is not isolated but interwoven with the moreness of everything else. What constitutes the realness of something, on this account, is precisely its inexhaustible intelligibility — the fact that knowing can always go further. To attend to that inexhaustibility rather than merely to categorical features is to engage in what can properly be called love. Presence, then, is not merely a pleasant phenomenological state; it is the disciplined recovery of loving attention to the real.

## Magus

Within Vervaeke's participatory framework, each mode of knowing carries an intrinsic criterion of realness — a phenomenological marker distinguishing genuine cognitive contact from mere representational processing. Propositional knowing indexes itself through conviction (verisimilitude to truth), procedural knowing through agentive power (causal grip on the world), and perspectival knowing through presence — an irreducible sense of situatedness that is orthogonal to propositional accuracy. The cognitive function of presence is the cultivation of insight and implicit learning; its existential function is anamnesis — the recovery of what propositional cognition structurally occludes.

The occlusion is not accidental but constitutive. Propositions operate through categorical containment: entities are subsumed under concepts, concepts marshaled into truth-apt claims. This yields enormous representational power at the cost of systematically effacing what might be termed, following the phenomenological and contemplative traditions, the "suchness" (haecceity, tathātā) and the "moreness" (inexhaustible intelligibility) of the encountered. Language itself encodes a compensatory gesture in the institution of proper names: "cat" activates categorical features, while "Taylor" functions as an index that cannot be resolved into any finite set of predicates — it marks the point where categorization confesses its own insufficiency. The argument is that this inexhaustibility is not an anthropocentric privilege but an ontological universal; everything possesses moreness, and every instance of moreness is relationally interwoven with every other.

The metaphysical claim follows: what constitutes the realness of an entity is its inexhaustible intelligibility — its capacity to sustain ever-deeper cognitive penetration. To orient one's attention toward that inexhaustibility rather than toward categorical reduction is to enact what can be rigorously identified as love, understood not sentimentally but as a mode of participatory knowing. Presence, accordingly, is not epiphenomenal affect but the practiced recovery of agapic attention — the epistemic stance in which the knower is continuously transformed by the known's refusal to be exhausted.
