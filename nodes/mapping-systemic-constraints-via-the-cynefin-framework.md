# Mapping Systemic Constraints via the Cynefin Framework

When the map strangles the territory

---

> Tightening rules on messy human systems often makes them worse, not better. The Cynefin framework explains why: some systems are genuinely complex, and forcing rigid order onto them produces creative subversion, not compliance.

The Cynefin framework is built on a fundamental divide into three types of system — ordered, complex, and chaotic — separated not by gradations but by phase shifts. Within ordered systems, there are two sub-types: 'obvious' and 'complicated.' In the obvious domain, cause and effect are self-evident to everyone, constraints are rigid, and there is a single best practice — you sense, categorize, and respond. In the complicated domain, cause and effect exist but are not self-evident to the decision-maker, requiring expert analysis — you sense, analyze, and respond — and the answer is discoverable, but within a range, which is why it's the domain of good practice rather than best practice. The critical insight is that over-constraining a system that isn't naturally constrainable doesn't make it more ordered — it causes it to fragment or break. IBM's absurd expense system, where people invented workarounds like claiming taxi fares to cover staff meals, is a perfect example: the system's rigidity didn't produce compliance, it produced creative subversion. This distinction matters enormously for organizational design and policy, because the instinct to impose tighter rules on messy human systems often accelerates their dysfunction rather than resolving it.

## Apprentice

Not all problems are the same kind of problem. Some situations are straightforward — the right answer is obvious, everyone can see it, and you just follow the standard procedure. Others are complicated: you need an expert to figure it out, but there is still a correct answer waiting to be found. And then there are situations that are genuinely complex — messy, unpredictable, shaped by human behaviour — where no single right answer exists at all.

The Cynefin framework, developed by Dave Snowden, maps these different types of situation. Its key warning is about what happens when leaders misread which type they're in. When a system is genuinely complex, adding more rules doesn't bring order — it creates chaos of a different kind. IBM once built an expense system so rigid that employees started claiming taxi fares to cover the cost of staff meals. The rules didn't produce compliance; they produced inventive workarounds.

This matters enormously for anyone designing organisations, policies, or institutions. The instinct to clamp down harder when things go wrong is deeply human — but if the underlying system is complex rather than merely complicated, tighter constraints tend to accelerate dysfunction rather than fix it. The first diagnostic question isn't 'what rule do we need?' but 'what kind of system are we actually dealing with?'

## Adept

The Cynefin framework partitions decision environments not along a smooth continuum but across categorical phase shifts. Within the broad class of ordered systems, Snowden distinguishes two sub-domains: the obvious (formerly 'simple'), where cause-and-effect relationships are self-evident and best practice governs; and the complicated, where causal structure exists but requires expert analysis to surface, making it the domain of good practice — a range of valid responses rather than a single optimum. The transition from complicated to complex marks a more fundamental boundary: complex systems are not merely difficult ordered systems, they are ontologically different, characterised by emergent behaviour, non-linear causality, and sensitivity to initial conditions.

The framework's most operationally significant insight concerns the failure mode of misclassification. Imposing the governance logic of ordered systems onto complex ones — rigid process controls, exhaustive rule sets, single-pathway compliance requirements — does not produce the intended constraint. Instead, it induces fragmentation: the system's agents route around the imposed structure, generating informal workarounds that hollow out the formal architecture. Snowden's IBM expense system example is instructive: procedural over-specification didn't eliminate discretionary behaviour, it merely displaced it into less visible and less accountable channels.

For organisational design, this reframes the standard managerial reflex. When a complex system produces unwanted variance, the diagnostic question is not 'which additional constraint will close the gap?' but 'is this system capable of being constrained in the first place?' Applying complicated-domain solutions to complex-domain problems is not just ineffective — it is actively destabilising, converting manageable emergence into brittle, subversion-prone structures.

## Magus

Cynefin's ontological architecture rests on a non-gradualist topology: the boundaries between domains are phase transitions, not scalar thresholds, which means that movement across them involves qualitative discontinuity rather than incremental change. Within the ordered regime, the obvious/complicated distinction tracks epistemic accessibility — in the obvious domain, causal transparency is universal and constraint is self-executing; in the complicated domain, causal structure is latent and requires expert mediation to render actionable, producing a solution space characterised by equipotentiality among valid responses rather than a unique optimum. The ordered/complex boundary, however, marks a deeper shift: from systems whose behaviour is in principle deducible from their structure to systems whose behaviour is constitutively emergent, path-dependent, and resistant to decomposition into stable input-output mappings.

The critical theoretical contribution here concerns the failure mode Snowden terms over-constraining: the imposition of ordered-domain governance logic onto complex-domain phenomena. This is not merely a category error in the Rylean sense but a generative pathology. Rigid procedural architectures applied to inherently complex adaptive systems do not produce the intended attractor states; they produce what complexity theorists would recognise as forced bifurcation — the system's agents, denied legitimate degrees of freedom, exercise agency through informal channels, producing shadow practices that are structurally invisible to the governance apparatus. The IBM expense system case is paradigmatic: procedural hyper-specification displaced discretionary behaviour rather than eliminating it, relocating it into domains beyond audit and accountability.

The normative implication for institutional design is epistemically demanding. It requires practitioners to resist the sensemaking heuristic that treats disorder as a deficit of constraint, and to instead perform prior ontological classification — distinguishing systems that are complicated-but-orderable from those that are complex-and-only-governable-through-enabling-constraints. Failure to make this distinction before intervening tends to convert manageable emergence into brittle, subversion-saturated structures whose dysfunction is then misread as evidence that still more constraint is required.
