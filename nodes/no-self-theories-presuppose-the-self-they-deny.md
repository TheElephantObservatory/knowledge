# No-Self Theories Presuppose the Self They Deny

The mirror cannot unsee its own reflection.

---

> Philosophers who use psychedelic experiences to argue the self doesn't exist rely on an analogy to perceptual binding — but that analogy secretly requires a unified agent doing the binding, undermining their own conclusion. A false self-image is not the same as a nonexistent self.

The no-self theorists who use psychedelic and mystical experiences as their evidence are caught in a subtle but serious internal contradiction. They invoke the entire machinery of relevance realization, self-relevance, adaptivity, salience, and self-modeling throughout their argument — they cannot deny these because the empirical and theoretical evidence is too strong. They also cannot deny that this machinery constitutes a set of transcendental preconditions: necessary conditions for the possibility of unified conscious agency, extended moral selfhood, and coherent experience over time. They agree all of that exists. What they want to claim is that the self — understood as a Cartesian enduring substance behind all of this — does not exist, and that the psychedelic-to-mystical continuum proves it. But here is the problem with their central analogy. They compare the self to perceptual binding: just as we bind features into a unified object and might mistakenly posit a substance behind the binding, so we bind experiences into a unified self and mistakenly posit a substance behind that. But for perceptual binding to produce a unified object, all of the acts of the knower must themselves be bound together — there has to be a unified integrating process doing the binding. They cannot deny this — it is presupposed in the very analogy they are using. So the analogy actually points toward the reality of something doing the self-binding, not away from it. What they seem to want to say is: yes, there is a transcendental precondition on agency and the unity of consciousness, but the image you form of that in conscious experience does not accurately point to it. I can accept that. But that is a very different claim from saying the self does not exist. The content of the self-model may be false without the referent of the self-model being nonexistent. These are not the same thing.

## Apprentice

Some thinkers argue that psychedelic and mystical experiences prove there is no self — that the feeling of being a unified "someone" is an illusion, like mistaking a bundle of visual features for a solid object. They accept that our minds constantly organize experience, track what matters, and model ourselves over time. They just deny there is any real self behind all that activity.

But there is a hidden problem in their reasoning. Their favorite analogy — comparing the self to how we bind scattered features into a single perceived object — actually works against them. For that binding to happen at all, something has to be doing the integrating. All the separate acts of knowing have to come together in a unified process. You cannot explain how we construct the illusion of a self without quietly relying on something that functions like a self.

What these thinkers may actually be showing is something more modest: that our conscious picture of who we are is not perfectly accurate. The mental image we carry of ourselves might be distorted or incomplete. But a wrong self-portrait is not the same thing as having no one sitting for the portrait. Confusing these two claims — "our self-image is flawed" and "the self does not exist" — is where the argument quietly falls apart.

## Adept

No-self theorists who draw on psychedelic and mystical phenomenology face a structural contradiction at the heart of their argument. They accept the robust empirical evidence for relevance realization, self-modeling, salience landscapes, and adaptive self-relevance — mechanisms they cannot deny without abandoning the cognitive science they depend on. They further accept that these mechanisms constitute transcendental preconditions for unified conscious agency, moral selfhood, and diachronic coherence. Their target is narrower: the Cartesian substantial self, an enduring thing behind the process. Psychedelic ego dissolution, they claim, reveals this substance to be a construction.

The problem emerges from their own central analogy. They liken the self to perceptual object binding: just as we mistakenly reify a substance behind bound features, we mistakenly reify a substance behind bound experiences. But perceptual binding itself presupposes a unified integrating process — all acts of the knower must be bound together for any object to appear as unified. The analogy does not point away from a real integrator; it points toward one. The very machinery invoked to debunk the self quietly reinstates something functioning as a self at the transcendental level.

What the argument can legitimately establish is that the phenomenal self-model — the conscious image we form of ourselves — may not accurately represent the transcendental preconditions it tracks. The content of the self-model may be false. But falsity of content does not entail nonexistence of referent. A distorted map does not prove there is no territory. Collapsing the distinction between "the self-model is inaccurate" and "the self does not exist" is the equivocation that quietly sustains the no-self position, and once it is made explicit, the stronger eliminativist claim dissolves.

## Magus

The no-self position grounded in psychedelic and contemplative phenomenology harbors an internal contradiction that becomes visible only when its transcendental commitments are made explicit. Its proponents accept the full apparatus of relevance realization, self-relevance processing, adaptive salience, and predictive self-modeling — they must, given the convergent evidence from 4E cognitive science, neurophenomenology, and enactivism. They further concede that this apparatus constitutes a set of transcendental preconditions: necessary conditions for the possibility of unified conscious agency, extended moral selfhood, and diachronically coherent experience. Their eliminativist claim targets only the Cartesian residuum — a substantial self persisting behind the integrative process.

The fatal difficulty lies in their governing analogy. The self, they argue, is to experiential unity what a reified substance is to perceptual binding: an illegitimate hypostatization of a constructive process. Yet perceptual binding itself presupposes the synthetic unity of the acts of the knower — a transcendental requirement they cannot discharge without circularity. For features to be bound into a unified percept, the binding operations must themselves be integrated within a single locus of agency. The analogy, rigorously pursued, does not dissolve the self; it relocates it from the phenomenal to the transcendental register. What is revealed is not the absence of a self but the non-identity of the self-model's content with its transcendental referent.

The defensible claim is therefore epistemological, not ontological: the phenomenal self-model may systematically misrepresent the transcendental preconditions it indexes. Content falsity, however, is not referent elimination. Conflating these — treating the inaccuracy of the self-image as evidence for the nonexistence of that which the image tracks — constitutes a scope error that vitiates the stronger eliminativist conclusion while leaving the more modest representational thesis intact.
