# Drives Come Before Myths: On Living Your Own Story

The fossil record of someone else's abyss.

---

> Myths are the fossilized residue of someone else's lived encounter with existence. If you start from your own drive, myth forms retroactively around your life; if you start from myth, you are living in someone else's story.

The distinction between drives and myths is one of the most practically consequential ideas in this conversation. The claim is that myths — whether biblical stories, Jungian archetypes, or cultural narratives — are the concretized residue of other people's drives. They are the fossilized record of someone else's lived encounter with existence. When Jesus was alive, Christianity didn't exist. Jesus had his drive, and the abyss of that experience. The myth came afterward, retroactively, as a transmissible form of what was originally irreducibly singular. The problem arises when we reverse this order — when we place myth before drive, when we use inherited stories as the primary container for our own existence rather than living from the singularity of our own encounter with life. Peterson does this with biblical archetypes; Dawkins, paradoxically, does something structurally similar by placing the myth of scientific rationality before the drive of inquiry itself. The Hegelian-Lacanian standpoint insists that drives mythify the real, not the other way around. What this means practically is that if you are genuinely committed to your own drive — to the abyss of your own existence — and that drive is socially real, meaning it produces something of genuine value to others, then the myth will happen retroactively. Your story will be transmissible because it needed to be told. But if you begin with the myth, you are living in someone else's story. The story is already written. You can find comfort in it, and that comfort has real value, but you cannot write your own story from within it. This is not an argument against myth. It is an argument about sequence, about what comes first in a life that is genuinely one's own.

## Apprentice

There is a crucial difference between living from your own deepest impulse and living inside a story someone else already wrote. Myths — whether religious narratives, cultural archetypes, or even the story of scientific progress — are really the crystallized leftovers of another person's raw encounter with life. When Jesus was alive, there was no Christianity. He had his experience, his confrontation with existence. The myth came later, built by others to transmit what he had lived through.

The problem is not myth itself. Myths carry real comfort and real wisdom. The problem is sequence. When someone adopts a pre-existing story as the primary framework for their own life — whether that story is biblical or scientific — they are placing the finished product before the living process. They are trying to live forward through a narrative that was constructed backward.

The alternative is to trust that if you are genuinely committed to your own encounter with life, and that commitment produces something of real value to others, the story will write itself after the fact. Your life becomes transmissible because it needed to be. But this requires tolerating the uncertainty of not having a script — of facing existence without the comfort of a pre-written ending.

## Adept

This insight draws a sharp line between drives and myths, arguing that the distinction is not merely theoretical but has immediate consequences for how a life is lived. Myths — biblical narratives, Jungian archetypes, the Enlightenment story of rational progress — are understood here as the concretized, transmissible residue of someone else's drive. They are what remains after a singular encounter with existence has been retroactively narrativized. Jesus had his drive; Christianity came afterward. The myth is always a backward construction imposed on what was originally irreducibly singular.

The critical move is identifying a structural error shared by seemingly opposed thinkers. Jordan Peterson places biblical archetypes before individual experience, using inherited narrative as the primary container for existence. Richard Dawkins, despite his anti-religious stance, does something structurally identical by placing the myth of scientific rationality before the drive of inquiry itself. Both reverse the proper sequence: they begin with the transmissible form rather than the lived encounter that generates it.

From a Hegelian-Lacanian standpoint, drives mythify the real — not the reverse. This means that genuine commitment to one's own drive, to the abyss of one's own existence, will produce myth retroactively if that drive is socially real, meaning it generates something of authentic value to others. The story becomes transmissible because it needed to be told. But beginning from myth means inhabiting a story already written. There is comfort in that, and the comfort is not dismissed, but it forecloses the possibility of writing one's own narrative from within the singularity of one's own life.

## Magus

The drive-myth distinction articulated here operates as a thesis about temporal ordering with ontological stakes. Myths — whether scriptural, archetypal, or epistemological — are theorized as the retroactive concretization of drives: the fossilized trace of another subject's encounter with the Real. The claim is rigorously Hegelian in structure: the universal (myth) does not precede the particular (drive) but is constituted retroactively through it. Christianity did not exist during Christ's life; it is the nachträglich narrativization of a singular confrontation with the abyss. The myth is always-already a secondary formation, a transmissible encoding of what was, in its originary moment, irreducibly non-narrativizable.

What gives this distinction its critical force is the identification of a shared structural error across ostensibly antagonistic positions. Peterson's deployment of biblical archetypes as existential scaffolding and Dawkins's elevation of scientific rationality to a mythic frame both perform the same inversion: they place the already-symbolized before the act of symbolization, the product before the production. In Lacanian terms, both substitute the discourse of the Other for the subject's own traversal of the fantasy. The imaginary coherence of inherited narrative replaces the encounter with the drive's constitutive lack.

The practical consequence is precise: if a subject remains faithful to their drive — to the point where that drive is socially real, producing genuine use-value for others — mythification occurs retroactively as a structural necessity. The narrative becomes transmissible because the symbolic order requires it. But to begin from myth is to foreclose this process, to inhabit a pre-written signifying chain. This is not an iconoclastic rejection of myth but a thesis about sequence: the subject who lives from drive authors myth; the subject who lives from myth is authored by it.
