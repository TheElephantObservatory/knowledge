# Whitehead's Asymmetry: Internal Relations to the Past, External Relations to the Future

Before the law of contradiction applies, something decides.

---

> Whitehead resolves the classic standoff between internal and external relations by making the distinction temporal: every entity is internally related to its past and externally related to its future, and the moment of becoming that unifies these is prior to logic itself.

One of the most profound moves in Whitehead's process philosophy is his temporalization of the distinction between internal and external relations — a move that dissolves the classic dilemma between idealism and analytic philosophy. Russell and the analytic tradition insisted we need external relations to say anything meaningful about the world. The British idealists like F.H. Bradley insisted everything is internally related to everything else — but Bradley's actual point was subtler: the very concept of relation violates the law of non-contradiction, leading to an infinite regress. Whitehead's response is neither to collapse into the absolute nor to abandon internal relations. Instead, he says every entity is internally related to everything in its past — it literally incorporates its past into itself — but externally related to everything in its future, to possibility. This asymmetry is the key to his entire ontology. It means that soul, or interiority, is not a Cartesian substance that exists independently of all relations, nor is it merely a Hegelian network of moral commitments and social recognitions. It is this Nexus — the point where the weight of all internal relations from the past meets the openness of external relations toward future possibility. And what holds these together — what makes the internal and external cohere in a single actual entity — is what Whitehead calls concrescence. During concrescence, the laws of non-contradiction and identity do not yet apply. The entity is not yet in space and time. It is in a process of decision, of unification, resolving the conflicts between all the prehensions — all the feelings of past entities — into a coherent whole. Only when concrescence completes does the entity become determinate, measurable, propositionally describable. This is why Whitehead says there is an imaginative leap required to understand his philosophy: the generative moment of becoming is inherently ineffable to propositional language, because propositional language presupposes the determinate entities that concrescence produces.

## Apprentice

There's a long-standing philosophical fight about how things relate to each other. One side says things are fundamentally separate and only connected from the outside — like billiard balls bumping into each other. The other side says everything is woven together so deeply that nothing can be understood in isolation. Both views seem to capture something real, but each leads to serious problems on its own.

Whitehead's key move is to notice that the answer depends on time. Looking backward, every entity genuinely contains its past — your experiences, your history, the causes that shaped you are literally part of what you are. But looking forward, toward what hasn't happened yet, the relationship is open and external. You don't yet contain your future. This simple asymmetry — closed behind, open ahead — dissolves the old argument.

The moment where past and future meet is what Whitehead calls "concrescence" — the process by which something new comes into being. During this process, all the influences from the past are being woven together into a unified whole. Crucially, this creative moment can't be fully captured in words or logic, because language and logic only work on things that are already finished. The act of becoming is more fundamental than the rules we use to describe what has already become.

## Adept

Whitehead's temporalization of the internal/external relations distinction represents one of the most consequential moves in twentieth-century metaphysics. The analytic tradition, following Russell, demanded external relations to preserve the independence of terms and the possibility of analysis. Bradley's idealism countered that all relations are internal, though his deeper argument was that the very concept of relation generates an infinite regress that violates non-contradiction. Whitehead sidesteps this impasse by introducing temporal asymmetry: every actual entity is internally related to its settled past — it prehends and literally incorporates antecedent actualities — while remaining externally related to its future, the domain of real potentiality. This asymmetry is not incidental but architectonic; it grounds the irreversibility of process and the reality of novelty.

The locus where internal and external relations converge is concrescence — the self-constituting process of an actual occasion. During concrescence, the entity is not yet spatiotemporally located, not yet subject to the laws of identity and non-contradiction that govern determinate beings. It is resolving the multiplicity of its prehensions — feelings inherited from the entire settled world — into a novel unity of experience. Concrescence is thus the metaphysical ground of decision, where "decision" carries its etymological weight of cutting off alternatives.

This framework redefines interiority. Soul or subjectivity is neither Cartesian substance independent of relations nor Hegelian spirit constituted entirely by social and logical mediation. It is the nexus where the weight of inherited actuality meets the lure of unrealized possibility — and the creative unification of these is inherently prior to propositional description, since propositions presuppose the determinate entities that concrescence produces.

## Magus

Whitehead's temporalization of the internal/external relations problematic constitutes a genuine speculative advance beyond the antinomy that structured early twentieth-century metaphysics. Russell's insistence on external relations was not merely logical but ontological — a defense of the irreducible plurality of terms against monistic absorption. Bradley's counter, often caricatured, was in fact a reductio: relation as such, requiring terms that must be both independent of and constituted by their relating, generates a vicious regress that exposes a contradiction at the heart of relational thought itself. Whitehead neither dismisses Bradley's regress nor capitulates to Russell's atomism. His resolution is genetic: actual entities are internally related to the entirety of their actual world — the settled past prehended with conformal feeling — and externally related to the indeterminate future, the realm of eternal objects as pure potentials. The asymmetry is not epistemic but cosmological, grounding both the cumulative character of process and the ontological openness that makes novelty possible.

Concrescence names the phase of becoming in which this asymmetry is operative but not yet resolved into determinacy. Here the entity exists as a subject of experience prior to its satisfaction — prior, that is, to its achievement of the definite character that renders it available for propositional predication and spatiotemporal location. The laws of non-contradiction and excluded middle, on this account, are features of the extensive continuum as already atomized by completed occasions; they do not govern the internal constitution of becoming itself. This is the precise sense in which Whitehead's metaphysics requires an imaginative leap: the generative phase of reality is categorially prior to the logical apparatus we deploy to describe its products.

Interiority thus emerges as the concrescent nexus itself — where the vector feelings carrying the efficient causality of the past undergo integration under the final causality of the subjective aim. This is Whitehead's relocation of soul: neither res cogitans nor Geist, but the living unity of prehensive inheritance and appetitive projection, whose very actuality is the perishing of its subjectivity into objective immortality.
