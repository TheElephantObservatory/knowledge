# Why Bret Weinstein Broke His Third-Party Voting Principle in 2024

When the future election never comes.

---

> Bret Weinstein argues that voting third party as a future signal only works if future elections remain meaningful — and that the blue team's constitutional norm-breaking around Biden's competency has destroyed that assumption, making defeating them a prerequisite for preserving the mechanisms of democratic course-correction.

Bret Weinstein articulates a principled distinction between his usual voting strategy and the urgency he perceives in 2024. Ordinarily, he argues, if the candidate you believe should be president isn't running, you vote in the future election — meaning you vote third party or write-in as a signal, not a capitulation. He has done this consistently, including writing in Tulsi Gabbard in 2020. But he argues this principle has a hidden assumption: that there will be a meaningful future election in which to register that signal. His case is that the blue team's behavior — specifically his reading of the 25th Amendment being wielded as a threat to remove Biden from the race without then actually invoking it to remove him from the office — represents a willingness to break the foundational rules of consent of the governed. The result, he argues, is that the United States is currently operating without a functional commander-in-chief while simultaneously engaging in nuclear brinksmanship with Russia. Kamala Harris, in his framing, is complicit: she had the constitutional mechanism to act on behalf of the American people and chose not to, then sought the presidency anyway. This is not merely a policy disagreement — it is, in his view, a demonstration that the blue team is comfortable operating outside constitutional norms when it serves their retention of power. The argument is not that Trump is good. It is that the preconditions for future course-correction require defeating the team that is dismantling the mechanisms of course-correction themselves.

## Apprentice

Bret Weinstein has long argued that if no candidate deserves your vote, you should vote third party or write someone in — treating your ballot as a message aimed at future elections rather than a concession to the lesser evil. He practiced this himself, writing in Tulsi Gabbard in 2020. But he identifies a hidden assumption in this strategy: it only works if future elections will still be fair and meaningful enough to receive that signal.

His argument for abandoning this principle in 2024 centers on what he sees as a constitutional crisis. Democratic Party leaders, he contends, used the threat of the 25th Amendment to pressure Biden out of the presidential race — effectively admitting he was unfit — but then never actually invoked it to remove him from office. This means, in his reading, that the country has been left without a genuinely functioning commander-in-chief during a period of dangerous nuclear tensions with Russia.

Kamala Harris, he argues, bears particular responsibility: she held the constitutional power to act on this situation and chose not to, then pursued the presidency herself. For Weinstein, this isn't a policy disagreement — it's evidence that one political faction is willing to operate outside the rules that make self-governance possible. His conclusion is not that Trump is the right president, but that defeating the faction dismantling democratic safeguards is the necessary first step before any future course-correction becomes possible again.

## Adept

Bret Weinstein draws a sharp distinction between strategic and emergency voting. His longstanding position holds that when no viable candidate merits genuine support, the principled move is to vote third party or write in a preferred candidate — effectively casting a ballot in a future election by signaling demand for better options. He has practiced this consistently, including a write-in for Tulsi Gabbard in 2020. The critical insight he surfaces is that this strategy contains a load-bearing assumption: that the institutional architecture of democratic accountability will persist, making future signal-reception possible.

His 2024 argument hinges on what he interprets as a constitutional violation of extraordinary significance. The Democratic establishment, he contends, leveraged the 25th Amendment as a political threat to force Biden from the race — an implicit acknowledgment of incapacity — while deliberately declining to invoke it to remove him from office. The result is a period in which the United States lacks a functional commander-in-chief while engaged in nuclear brinksmanship with Russia. Harris, possessing the constitutional mechanism to address this gap, chose inaction followed by personal ambition. Weinstein reads this as not mere hypocrisy but as a revealed preference for operating outside constitutional norms when institutional compliance threatens power retention.

The structure of the argument is deliberately meta-political. It does not rest on Trump's merits or policy positions. Instead, it identifies a precondition problem: democratic self-correction requires intact mechanisms of consent and accountability. If one faction demonstrates willingness to hollow out those mechanisms, then defeating that faction becomes logically prior to all other political considerations — including the ordinary calculus of candidate quality. The vote becomes defensive rather than aspirational, aimed at preserving the possibility space within which future principled votes can function.

## Magus

Weinstein's 2024 electoral argument operates at the level of constitutional meta-strategy, distinguishing between first-order candidate evaluation and the preservation of the decision-theoretic framework within which such evaluation retains meaning. His established heuristic — treating a ballot cast for a non-viable candidate as a temporally displaced signal aimed at future electoral cycles — is revealed to contain an unexamined axiom: the continuity of institutional conditions under which signals propagate. The argument's force derives from identifying the precise moment this axiom fails.

The evidentiary core is the 25th Amendment paradox he constructs around Biden's withdrawal from the race. The Democratic establishment's deployment of Section 4 as political leverage — sufficient to compel withdrawal from candidacy — without subsequent invocation to address the underlying incapacity constitutes, in Weinstein's analysis, a performative contradiction that exposes the instrumental relationship between this faction and constitutional governance. The implications compound: a nation engaged in nuclear brinksmanship operates without genuine executive authority, while the officer constitutionally positioned to remedy this — Harris — elected strategic passivity followed by personal advancement. This is read not as incompetence but as a revealed preference function: constitutional mechanisms are activated or suppressed according to their utility for power retention, not their intended protective function.

The resulting decision calculus is structurally analogous to a game-theoretic precommitment problem. If the mechanism-space within which democratic correction operates is itself being degraded by one actor, then all first-order policy preferences become subordinate to the preservation of that space. Voting against the norm-violating faction is not an endorsement of the alternative but a necessary condition for the continued coherence of endorsement itself. Weinstein frames this as the collapse of his own prior strategy's validity — the moment when voting for the future requires, paradoxically, abandoning the future-oriented vote.
