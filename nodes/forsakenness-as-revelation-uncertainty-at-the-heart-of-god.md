# Forsakenness as Revelation: Uncertainty at the Heart of God

The question mark that prays back

---

> Christ's cry of forsakenness on the cross may reveal not God's absence but God's deepest nature as irreducible openness. The question mark at the heart of divinity transforms worship from certainty-seeking into participation in grief, dissolution, and generative mystery.

Layman Pascal offers a striking reinterpretation of Christ's cry of dereliction — 'My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?' — not as a theological problem to be resolved but as a mythological event of transfiguration. In this reading, Christ on the cross is not simply expressing abandonment; he is undergoing a transformation in his understanding of the very God he embodies. He has been relating to God as certainty, as Covenant, as a stable ground of promise and protection. In the moment of forsakenness, that structure dissolves. But Pascal's move is to suggest that this dissolution is not a failure of the Divine — it is a revelation of the Divine's deepest nature. The question mark is not our lack of understanding of an exclamation mark; it is our true relationship to a question mark that lives at the heart of God itself. This reframes worship entirely: we are not worshiping a being of perfect certainty who occasionally withholds himself, but entering into relationship with a ground of being that is itself open, uncertain, generative. The pain in the cry is real — it is the pain of disconnection from a covenantal structure that had been sustaining and defining. But Pascal connects this to a non-dual insight: disconnection is also the site of connection; the separator is the connector. The grief of forsakenness, held without resolution, becomes the very place where a deeper mode of participation becomes possible. Bruce Alderman deepens this motif by noting that Chöd — the Tibetan shamanic practice of offering one's dismembered body to all beings — is structurally identical to the sacrificial logic at the heart of Christianity, and that practices like Tonglen extended to planetary scale share this same movement: from individual identity into participation with the broader living sphere, with grief, not just gratitude, as the medium of that participation.

## Apprentice

When Christ cries out 'My God, why have you forsaken me?' most people hear it as a moment of tragic abandonment — God turning away from his own son. Layman Pascal offers a different reading: what if this isn't a breakdown but a breakthrough? Christ had been relating to God as a source of certainty, protection, and promise. On the cross, that familiar structure falls apart. But the collapse isn't a failure — it's a revelation. The deepest truth about the Divine might not be solid ground but living openness, a question mark rather than an exclamation point.

This changes what worship means. Instead of approaching God as a being who has all the answers and occasionally withholds them, we enter into relationship with something fundamentally open and generative. The pain of feeling forsaken is real, but it becomes the doorway to a deeper kind of connection — one that doesn't depend on certainty or comfort.

Bruce Alderman extends this insight by pointing to the Tibetan practice of Chöd, where practitioners visualize offering their own dismembered body to all beings. This shares the same deep structure as Christ's sacrifice: letting go of individual identity in order to participate more fully in the life of the whole. Grief, not just gratitude, becomes the medium through which we connect to something larger than ourselves.

## Adept

Layman Pascal reframes Christ's cry of dereliction — 'My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?' — as a mythological event of transfiguration rather than a theological problem requiring resolution. In this reading, Christ is not merely abandoned; he is undergoing a transformation in his relationship to the Divine itself. The covenantal God of certainty, promise, and stable ground dissolves in the moment of forsakenness. Pascal's critical move is to argue that this dissolution reveals God's deepest nature: the question mark is not a deficiency in our understanding of an exclamation mark but the authentic character of the Divine as irreducibly open, uncertain, and generative. Worship thus shifts from orientation toward a being of perfect certainty to participation in a ground of being that is itself questioning.

The non-dual structure of the insight is precise: disconnection is simultaneously the site of connection; the separator is the connector. The grief of forsakenness, held without premature resolution, becomes the locus where a deeper mode of participation emerges — one that does not depend on covenantal guarantees but inhabits the open texture of reality itself.

Bruce Alderman deepens this trajectory by identifying structural isomorphism between Christ's sacrifice and the Tibetan shamanic practice of Chöd, in which the practitioner offers their dismembered body to all beings. Both enact the same sacrificial logic: dissolution of bounded identity as the mechanism of expanded participation. Alderman further suggests that practices like Tonglen, extended to planetary scale, share this movement — with grief, not merely gratitude, serving as the medium through which individual identity opens into the broader living sphere.

## Magus

Layman Pascal's hermeneutic of the cry of dereliction displaces the standard soteriological and theodical framings by treating Christ's forsakenness as a mythological event of transfiguration — a real-time mutation in the structure of divine-human relation. The covenantal God who guarantees, promises, and grounds is precisely what dissolves on the cross. Pascal's decisive claim is ontological rather than merely experiential: the question mark is not epistemological deficit relative to a hidden exclamation mark but the authentic modality of the Divine itself. God's deepest nature is irreducible openness, and the cry reveals this by enacting it. The pain is not incidental — it is the felt dissolution of a relational structure (Covenant as stable ground) that had been constitutive of identity and orientation.

The non-dual logic operating here is rigorous: the separator is the connector, disconnection is the site of connection. This is not dialectical sublation — the grief is not aufgehoben into resolution — but a holding of forsakenness as the very medium through which a deeper participatory mode becomes available. Worship reconfigured along these lines is no longer orientation toward certainty but inhabitation of generative indeterminacy at the heart of being.

Bruce Alderman extends this sacrificial topology cross-traditionally by identifying structural identity between the Crucifixion and Chöd — the Tibetan practice of offering one's dismembered body to all beings. Both instantiate the same logic: bounded identity dissolved as the operative mechanism of expanded participation. Alderman's further suggestion that Tonglen practiced at planetary scale shares this movement reframes contemplative practice as ecological sacrifice, with grief — not gratitude alone — as the affective medium through which individual identity opens into the living whole. The convergence implies a deep grammar of sacrificial participation operative across traditions.
