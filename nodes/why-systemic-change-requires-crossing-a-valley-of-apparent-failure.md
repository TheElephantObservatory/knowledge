# Why Systemic Change Requires Crossing a Valley of Apparent Failure

You have to look worse before you can be better.

---

> Capitalism is stuck at a local optimum on a fitness landscape. No internal reform can cross the valley to a genuinely better system — only a shift in meaning powerful enough to make people accept apparent loss by current metrics can bridge that gap.

One of the most important arguments for why the second renaissance cannot be achieved through economic or structural reform alone comes from evolutionary theory — specifically the concept of fitness landscapes. Imagine capitalism as a highly optimized system sitting at the bottom of a fitness valley — it is extraordinarily good at what it does within its own logic. The problem is that to reach a genuinely better social and economic arrangement — one that accounts for the value of rainforests, that doesn't externalize ecological destruction onto future generations, that enables genuine cooperation and shared knowledge — you would first have to get worse before you get better. You'd have to climb out of the current optimum, cross a valley of reduced productivity, and ascend a different and higher peak. That transition looks like failure by the metrics of the current system. No proximate tweak to capitalism — no green new deal, no stakeholder capitalism, no ESG framework — can bridge that valley, because all of those reforms are still optimizing within the existing fitness landscape. What can bridge it is a shift in values so profound that people are willing to accept apparent loss by current metrics in exchange for something the current system cannot offer at all. This is exactly what happened at the end of the Roman Empire. Christianity did not out-compete Rome economically — standards of living in Western Europe did not recover to late Roman levels until around 1300 AD. But Christianity offered something Rome could not: meaning. And that meaning was sufficient to motivate an entirely different mode of social organization — one that, over centuries, turned out to be far more generative than what it replaced. David Sloan Wilson tells a resonant version of this story: imagine a rich young Roman who goes to the amphitheater to watch Christians being fed to the lions, and before they die, he sees in their eyes a peace and quality of presence that no amount of money or power in Rome will ever give him. They have something the system cannot produce. The meaning crisis that thinkers like John Vervaeke describe is not a fringe phenomenon — it is the civilizational condition of late modernity. What the second renaissance offers is not a better economic arrangement. It is meaning. It is the possibility of seeing yourself as part of an unfolding cultural evolution that is real, not guaranteed, and that you could give your life to. These are not things the market can counterfeit indefinitely. And they are the most powerful lever for collective transformation available to us.

## Apprentice

Think of capitalism as a system that has gotten incredibly good at doing what it does — so good that any small change actually makes things worse by its own standards. This is what evolutionary biologists call a "local optimum" on a fitness landscape. The trouble is that a truly better way of organizing society — one that protects ecosystems, shares knowledge freely, and doesn't dump problems on future generations — sits on a different peak entirely. To get there, you'd first have to go through a valley where everything looks like it's failing.

No amount of tweaking the current system — green investment programs, corporate responsibility pledges, sustainability metrics — can jump that gap, because all those reforms are still playing by the rules of the existing game. What can jump it is something much deeper: a change in what people actually care about, powerful enough that they'll accept short-term loss for something the current system simply cannot provide.

This has happened before. Christianity didn't beat the Roman Empire by being more productive — living standards in Europe actually collapsed for centuries. But it offered meaning so compelling that a rich Roman watching Christians die in the arena could see in their faces a quality of peace that no wealth could buy. The meaning crisis described by thinkers like John Vervaeke suggests late modernity is in a similar moment. The most powerful force for civilizational transformation isn't a better policy — it's a source of meaning worth reorganizing life around.

## Adept

The fitness landscape metaphor from evolutionary theory provides one of the sharpest framings for why incremental reform cannot produce civilizational transformation. Capitalism occupies a deep local optimum — a configuration so internally coherent that any proximate deviation reduces fitness by the system's own metrics. A genuinely superior attractor state — one that internalizes ecological costs, enables cooperative knowledge commons, and operates on longer time horizons — exists on a separate peak, reachable only by traversing a valley of apparent regression. Green New Deals, stakeholder capitalism, and ESG frameworks all fail this test: they optimize within the existing landscape rather than crossing between peaks.

The only force capable of bridging such a valley is a shift in the evaluative framework itself — a transformation in values so fundamental that what registers as loss under current metrics becomes acceptable or even desirable. David Sloan Wilson's parable of the rich Roman at the amphitheater captures this precisely: the Christians being fed to lions possessed a quality of presence that no amount of Roman wealth could generate. Christianity did not out-compete Rome economically — European living standards did not recover to late-Roman levels until roughly 1300 AD — but it offered meaning sufficient to reorganize social life entirely.

This argument converges with John Vervaeke's diagnosis of the meaning crisis as the defining condition of late modernity. The implication is that the so-called second renaissance is not primarily an economic or institutional project. It is a meaning project. The capacity to offer people genuine participation in an unfolding cultural evolution — something experienced as real, consequential, and worthy of devotion — constitutes the actual mechanism of phase transition. Markets can simulate purpose temporarily, but they cannot manufacture the kind of meaning that motivates valley-crossing at civilizational scale.

## Magus

The fitness landscape formalism, drawn from evolutionary biology and applied here to civilizational dynamics, yields a rigorous account of why capitalism's local optimum is structurally unreformable from within. The system occupies a deep basin of attraction whose gradient uniformly penalizes deviation by endogenous metrics — productivity, GDP growth, competitive advantage. Any candidate successor regime that internalizes currently externalized costs (ecological destruction, intergenerational debt, epistemic enclosure) necessarily occupies a distinct peak separated by a fitness valley: a region where performance degrades by every measure the incumbent system recognizes. Proximate interventions — carbon pricing, stakeholder governance, ESG integration — remain confined to the topology of the existing landscape and cannot effectuate inter-peak transition.

The historical analogue is precise rather than merely suggestive. Christianity's displacement of Roman imperial civilization involved centuries of measurable economic regression — material living standards in Western Europe did not return to late-antique levels until approximately 1300 AD. Yet the transition occurred because Christianity supplied a participatory framework of meaning that Roman civic religion and imperial ideology could not generate. David Sloan Wilson's vignette of the Roman aristocrat at the amphitheater distills the mechanism: the martyrs' equanimity indexes access to an evaluative dimension orthogonal to the system's reward structure. What compels valley-crossing is not superior performance on existing metrics but the revelation of a metric the current system cannot satisfy.

This analysis converges with John Vervaeke's account of the meaning crisis as the deepest structural vulnerability of late-modern civilization. The lever for phase transition is not institutional redesign but the emergence of a meaning structure sufficiently robust to sustain collective action through prolonged apparent loss. The second renaissance, on this reading, is fundamentally a project in the ecology of meaning — one whose success depends on whether it can offer forms of participatory sense-making that markets can neither produce nor convincingly counterfeit at scale.
