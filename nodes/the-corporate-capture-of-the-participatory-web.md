# The Corporate Capture of the Participatory Web

Unlocking the world only to sell the keys

---

> The early internet promised to make culture participatory and writable by everyone — then Wall Street turned it into a machine for harvesting attention. The tragedy is that digital technology didn't corrupt something pure; it accelerated an extractive logic that was already there.

The internet was originally a genuinely emancipatory technology — it broke the tyranny of top-down broadcast media and turned the cultural landscape back into something participatory, collective, and social. In the early days of online services like The Source in 1980, people already had chat, shopping, news, bulletin boards, and rudimentary social media — all of it pointing toward a world where ordinary people could make and share content with one another rather than simply receiving it from above. The metaphor that crystallized this was the distinction between read-only and read-write files. When you first encountered a computer and realized that the only difference between a file anyone could edit and one that was locked was simply your own choice, it reframed the entire world. Television, money, laws, institutions — all of these were read-only files in the real world that could just as easily have been made read-write. The early internet felt like the moment when all of those locked files were suddenly being opened. But by the late 1990s, that dream was surrendered to Wall Street's vision of something entirely different: creating more predictable and manipulable consumers by any means necessary. What had been a peer-to-peer renaissance became a mechanism for extracting value from human attention and behavior. The tragedy isn't just that a good technology was misused — it's that the deeply anti-human ethos that was already embedded in older institutions like corporations and extractive capitalism was simply turbocharged by going digital. It became corporatism on digital steroids, doing faster and more efficiently what assembly lines and resource extraction had always done.

## Apprentice

The internet, in its earliest form, felt like a revolution in who got to speak. Before it, culture mostly flowed one way — from television networks, newspapers, and corporations down to passive audiences. Online services in the 1980s already offered chat rooms, bulletin boards, and shared spaces where ordinary people could create and respond. It was genuinely new.

A useful way to understand what that felt like is the computer concept of read-only versus read-write files. A read-only file can be seen but not changed; a read-write file is open to anyone with access. The insight is that television, laws, money, and institutions are treated as read-only in everyday life — fixed, handed down from above — but there's no deep reason they have to be. The early internet felt like someone had quietly unlocked all those files.

That promise didn't last. By the late 1990s, the commercial internet had been reshaped around a different goal: turning users into predictable, manipulable consumers. The peer-to-peer energy was replaced by platforms designed to extract value from attention and behavior. The argument here is that this wasn't simply a good technology being misused — the extractive logic was borrowed wholesale from older institutions like corporations and industrial capitalism, and digital tools simply made it faster and more efficient.

## Adept

This perspective locates the internet's original promise in a structural inversion of broadcast media. The dominant 20th-century information architecture was asymmetric: a small number of producers transmitted content to a large, passive audience. Early networked computing — already visible in services like The Source by 1980 — disrupted that asymmetry by enabling peer-to-peer exchange, user-generated content, and participatory culture at scale. The shift was not merely technical but political, redistributing the means of cultural production.

The read-only/read-write distinction serves as the argument's central metaphor. In computing, the difference between a locked and an editable file is a permission setting — an arbitrary human choice, not a natural law. Extending this logic, the argument reframes social institutions (legal codes, monetary systems, corporate structures) as contingently locked rather than inherently fixed. The early internet appeared to be the moment when those permissions were being revised, opening previously closed systems to collective authorship and modification.

The critique of what followed is not simply that commercialization corrupted a neutral technology. The deeper claim is that the extractive, anti-human ethos embedded in industrial capitalism and corporate organization was ported directly into the digital environment and amplified. Platform capitalism — with its surveillance infrastructure, attention markets, and behavioral manipulation — is understood here not as an aberration but as corporatism running on faster hardware. The tragedy is one of acceleration and intensification, not deviation.

## Magus

The argument reconstructs the internet's genealogy through the lens of media theory and political economy, positioning early networked computing as a genuine rupture in the hegemonic broadcast paradigm. The Habermasian public sphere, long colonized by mass media's one-to-many architecture, appeared briefly recoverable through the distributed, many-to-many topology of early online services. The participatory affordances already present in 1980s platforms — asynchronous messaging, bulletin board systems, rudimentary social exchange — prefigured a communicative infrastructure that could, in principle, have sustained a post-broadcast democratic culture.

The read-only/read-write heuristic functions here as more than analogy; it operates as a denaturalization device. By foregrounding the contingency of access permissions in file systems, the argument defamiliarizes the apparent fixity of social and juridical structures — money, law, institutional authority — revealing them as historically sedimented permission states rather than ontological givens. The early internet's emancipatory charge derived precisely from this denaturalizing effect: the felt sense that the write-protection on inherited social forms was being lifted.

The subsequent critique draws on a tradition running from Marcuse's one-dimensional society through Zuboff's surveillance capitalism: the claim that digital platforms did not corrupt an innocent technology but rather provided new infrastructure for an extractive rationality already constitutive of industrial capitalism. The platform economy is theorized as a phase transition rather than a deviation — corporatism achieving greater thermodynamic efficiency through digital mediation, optimizing the same core operations of attention capture, behavioral prediction, and surplus extraction that the assembly line and resource enclosure had always performed, now at algorithmic speed and planetary scale.
