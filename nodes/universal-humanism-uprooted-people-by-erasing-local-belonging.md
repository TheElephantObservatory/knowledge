# Universal Humanism Uprooted People by Erasing Local Belonging

Your feet already know where you are.

---

> Identity politics on both left and right are failed substitutes for indigeneity — the felt sense of belonging to a place and a people. Liberal universalism gave us dignity but stripped us of roots. The real remedy is embodied, local belonging: your feet on the actual ground beneath you.

The crisis of identity politics — both the intersectional left and the blood-and-soil right — is best understood not as a political failure but as a failure of indigeneity. Liberal Universal humanism won, at least in the American mainstream, somewhere between the 1940s and the 1960s. Martin Luther King's vision — that every human being regardless of color or origin deserves equal dignity — became the dominant moral framework. And it was a genuine achievement. But it left something out. It gave people abstract universal belonging and denied them local, embodied, particular belonging. It gave them principles and took away roots. What people are desperately reaching for when they over-identify with intersectional categories or with racial mythologies is a substitute for indigeneity — the felt sense of being from somewhere, of belonging to a place and a people in a way that is real and not just conceptual. Indigenous peoples have this. They are of the land. Modern secular Westerners largely do not, and they feel the absence as a kind of groundlessness. The tragedy is that both substitutes — intersectionality and race — are fictions. They are social constructions with no biological or ontological reality. They cannot actually provide what people are seeking. The real answer is almost embarrassingly simple: your feet on the ground. Wherever you are standing — on asphalt behind a 7-Eleven, on a farm, in a city — you are there. Your body is there. Your nervous system is embedded in that place and those relationships. The project of Team Human, as Rushkoff frames it, is to reunite liberal Universal humanism with the embodied reality of our indigeneity as earthlings — beings who belong to this planet, to this biosphere, in a way that no algorithm or ideology can replicate or replace.

## Apprentice

When Martin Luther King's vision of universal human dignity became America's dominant moral framework, it was a genuine triumph. But something got lost in the process. People gained abstract equality — the idea that everyone matters — but lost the feeling of truly belonging somewhere specific, to a particular place and community. That sense of rootedness, of being *from* somewhere in your bones, is what indigenous peoples have always had.

This is the lens through which identity politics makes the most sense — not as a political movement but as a desperate search for belonging. Whether people cling to intersectional categories on the left or racial mythologies on the right, they are reaching for something to fill the hole where rootedness used to be. The problem is that neither substitute actually works. These categories are social inventions, not deep truths, and they cannot deliver the grounding people crave.

The answer, as thinkers like Douglas Rushkoff suggest, is almost comically direct: look down. Wherever you are standing — behind a convenience store, in a suburb, on a farm — your body is there. Your nervous system is woven into that place and those relationships. The real project is to keep the moral gains of universal humanism while recovering the embodied fact that we are earthlings, creatures who belong to this planet in a way no ideology can replicate.

## Adept

Liberal universal humanism achieved cultural dominance in the mid-twentieth-century United States, culminating in the civil rights era's insistence on equal dignity regardless of origin. This was a genuine moral advance. But the framework carried a structural deficit: it offered universal belonging at the cost of particular belonging. It provided principles but evacuated roots. The result is a modern secular subject who possesses abstract moral standing yet experiences a persistent groundlessness — the absence of indigeneity, understood here not as ethnic identity but as the felt sense of being embedded in a specific place, ecology, and relational web.

From this vantage, both intersectional identity politics and ethno-nationalist movements represent compensatory strategies for the same underlying loss. Intersectionality attempts to reconstruct belonging through taxonomies of social position; blood-and-soil nationalism attempts it through racial mythology. Both are social constructions lacking biological or ontological substrate, which is precisely why neither can deliver the embodied rootedness they promise. They are maps mistaken for territory — symbolic systems that cannot substitute for somatic and ecological emplacement.

The corrective, as Douglas Rushkoff's "Team Human" framework suggests, is a reunification of universalist ethics with embodied indigeneity. Not indigeneity as ethnic claim, but as the irreducible fact of organismic presence: a nervous system situated in a particular place, embedded in particular relationships, belonging to a biosphere. The radical simplicity of this move — your feet on the ground, wherever that ground happens to be — is both its strength and the reason it is so easily overlooked by political frameworks that operate entirely at the level of abstraction.

## Magus

The mid-century triumph of liberal universal humanism — consolidated between the 1940s and 1960s in the American moral imagination — resolved the problem of exclusion by extending dignity universally, but in doing so it performed an unacknowledged subtraction. Universal belonging, predicated on abstract personhood, systematically displaced particular belonging: the pre-reflective, somatically grounded sense of being *of* a place, a people, an ecology. What was gained in moral extensibility was lost in ontological thickness. The modern secular subject thus inhabits a paradox: maximal moral inclusion coinciding with existential groundlessness.

This framework reinterprets the contemporary crisis of identity politics — both the intersectional left's proliferating subject-positions and the ethno-nationalist right's atavistic blood-myths — as symptomatic not of political failure but of a failure of indigeneity. Both formations attempt to manufacture through ideological construction what can only be received through embodied emplacement. Intersectionality hypostasizes social categories into quasi-ontological identities; racial nationalism biologizes cultural memory into fictive kinship. Neither possesses the ontological density required to satisfy the hunger they address, because both remain operations within the symbolic order rather than returns to the phenomenological ground.

The resolution proposed — aligned with Rushkoff's "Team Human" thesis — is deceptively elementary: the recuperation of indigeneity not as ethnic patrimony but as the basal condition of organismic existence. Every human nervous system is already emplaced, already ecological, already indigenous to the biosphere it inhabits. The project becomes one of reuniting universalist ethical commitments with this irreducible somatic facticity — acknowledging that the body standing on asphalt behind a convenience store is no less *of the earth* than one standing on ancestral land. The algorithmic and ideological cannot replicate this ground; they can only obscure it.
