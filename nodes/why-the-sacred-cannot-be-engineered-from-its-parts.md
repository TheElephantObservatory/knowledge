# Why the Sacred Cannot Be Engineered from Its Parts

You cannot manufacture an arrival.

---

> You cannot engineer the sacred, because engineering demands you already know what you are building toward — but the sacred, by definition, arrives as what you did not already possess. Meaning recovers not through synthesis but through genuine dialogue between living traditions.

There is a profound mistake that even serious thinkers make when they try to address the meaning crisis, and John Vervaeke has admitted making it himself: the attempt to engineer religion. The idea was to identify all the functions that religion performs — the community, the practices, the sense of transcendence, the ecology of wisdom cultivation — and extract them from the existing traditions to build something new, something without the historical baggage. He now recognizes this as a profound and ironically modernist error. You cannot manufacture logos. If you try, you throttle it, because logos has to show up of its own accord and take on a life of its own. The moments that matter most are not moments of complete control. When you turn a corner and there is an unexpected snowfall, when someone says something in deep dialogue that calls you beyond where you could go on your own — these are moments of what Hartmut Rosa calls resonance, which is characterized precisely by uncontrollability. The world must be able to surprise you. The sacred cannot be engineered because engineering requires that you already know what you are building toward. But the advent of the sacred is, by definition, an arrival of something you did not already possess. What this means practically is that the recovery of meaning requires bringing all the traditions — their ecologies of practices, their cultivation of wisdom, their enhancement of religio — into genuine dialogos with each other, not as a manufacturing project but as a way of affording the conditions in which the sacred can show up. The goal is not a new synthetic religion built from parts. The goal is that Buddhism might help Christians recover their Christianity, and Christianity might help Buddhists recover their Buddhism — each tradition deepened by genuine encounter with the other, none dissolved into a universal method.

## Apprentice

There is a tempting idea that keeps surfacing among people who take the modern meaning crisis seriously: why not study what religion does well — the community, the practices, the sense of something greater — and build a new version without all the historical baggage? John Vervaeke, a cognitive scientist who has thought deeply about wisdom and meaning, admits he once pursued exactly this project. He now considers it a profound mistake, and ironically, a deeply modernist one.

The error is subtle. You cannot manufacture the sacred the way you manufacture a product, because engineering requires knowing in advance what you are building. But the moments that matter most — an unexpected snowfall, a line in conversation that takes you somewhere you could not have gone alone — are defined by the fact that they surprise you. The philosopher Hartmut Rosa calls this quality "resonance," and its essential feature is that it cannot be controlled. If you try to control it, you destroy the very thing you are reaching for.

What this suggests is that the path forward is not to build a new religion from spare parts, but to bring existing traditions into real conversation with each other. Buddhism might help Christians see something they have forgotten about their own tradition, and Christianity might do the same for Buddhists. Each tradition is deepened by genuine encounter, not dissolved into a universal method. The sacred is not assembled — it is afforded the conditions to arrive.

## Adept

John Vervaeke has publicly acknowledged what he now considers a profound error in his earlier approach to the meaning crisis: the attempt to reverse-engineer religion by extracting its functional components — community, transformative practices, ecology of wisdom cultivation, the enhancement of religio — and reassembling them into something new, stripped of historical and doctrinal baggage. He recognizes this as an ironically modernist move, one that reproduces the very instrumental rationality responsible for the crisis it seeks to address. The core problem is that logos cannot be manufactured. If you attempt to engineer it, you throttle it, because logos must emerge of its own accord and take on a life of its own.

This connects directly to Hartmut Rosa's concept of resonance, whose defining characteristic is uncontrollability. The moments of deepest significance — an unexpected encounter, a turn in genuine dialogos that calls you beyond your current horizon — are precisely those that cannot be predetermined. The sacred, by structural necessity, arrives as that which you did not already possess. Engineering presupposes a known target; the advent of the sacred presupposes the absence of one. These two orientations are fundamentally incompatible.

The practical implication is that addressing the meaning crisis requires not a synthetic construction project but the cultivation of conditions under which the sacred can show up. This means bringing the living traditions — their ecologies of practices, their wisdom cultivation, their capacities for religio — into genuine dialogos with one another. The aim is mutual deepening, not dissolution: Buddhism helping Christians recover dimensions of their own tradition, Christianity doing the same for Buddhism. No tradition is reduced to a component in a universal method. Each is renewed through the encounter itself.

## Magus

Vervaeke's self-correction on the project of engineering religion constitutes a significant reflexive moment in contemporary meaning-crisis discourse. The original impulse — to perform a functional decomposition of religion, isolating its roles in community formation, participatory knowing, wisdom ecology, and the cultivation of religio, then reconstituting these functions in a tradition-neutral framework — recapitulates precisely the procedural rationality it sought to overcome. It is the Cartesian gesture applied to the very domain that resists Cartesian treatment. The insight is that logos is not the kind of thing that submits to fabrication; it must, in Vervaeke's formulation, show up of its own accord and take on a life of its own. To throttle this emergence through engineering is to foreclose on the very phenomenon one seeks.

The theoretical backbone here draws on Rosa's account of resonance as constitutively uncontrollable. Resonance is not a subjective state to be induced but a relational event whose possibility depends on the genuine openness of the axis along which it occurs. The sacred, understood through this lens, is structurally adventitious — it arrives as what was not already contained within the horizon of the agent. Engineering, by contrast, operates within a closed teleological frame: you must already know what you are building toward. The incompatibility is not contingent but formal.

The resulting methodological reorientation moves from construction to affordance. Rather than assembling a synthetic religion from extracted components, the task becomes one of facilitating genuine dialogos among living traditions — each bringing its full ecology of practices, its cultivated capacities for wisdom and religio, into encounter with the other. The telos is not convergence but mutual anamnesis: Buddhism recovering dimensions of Christianity for Christians, Christianity performing the same service for Buddhism. Traditions are deepened through alterity, not dissolved into method. The sacred is not built; it is given room to arrive.
