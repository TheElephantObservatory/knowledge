# Exponential Curves Running in Opposite Directions

E.O. Wilson, the Harvard biologist, offered a pithy summation that captures our civilizational predicament perfectly: we have Paleolithic emotions — our neurophysiology and root psychology are roughly 50,000 years out of date — we have medieval institutions, and we have god-like technology. We are in a period of exponential change, but no one has articulated a framework for exponential meaning to help us make sense of all this and steer it. And that mismatch would probably have overtaxed both religion and modern liberalism even at their healthiest, fullest versions. But we're now encountering this challenge in a period of decline, decay, and collapse. What makes this especially disorienting is a frame that Daniel Schmachtenberger first shared: things are getting exponentially better — that's the Steven Pinker, Matt Ridley, Hans Rosling view, the under-reported uptick in the benefits of the Enlightenment experiment — and simultaneously, undeniably, things are also getting exponentially worse. Both of these things are happening at once. And typically, people cannot hold that tension. We glom onto whichever one resonates with our existing disposition. If you're prone to doom and gloom, you latch onto extinction narratives. If you're prone to optimism, you cling to the good news. But the fairly obvious answer is that both curves are real — and when you're trying to map a linear outcome to the intersection of two exponential curves running in opposite directions, it becomes genuinely crazy-making. That is the epistemic and existential situation we are actually in.

## Apprentice

E.O. Wilson, the Harvard biologist, once described humanity's core problem in a single sentence: we have ancient emotions, medieval institutions, and godlike technology. Our feelings and instincts are roughly 50,000 years old. Our governments and religions were designed for a much slower world. But our tools — nuclear weapons, artificial intelligence, genetic engineering — have the power of gods. That mismatch alone would be hard enough to navigate. But there is a second layer of difficulty. Things are genuinely getting better and worse at the same time. More people are lifted out of poverty than ever before. Diseases are being cured. Lifespans are lengthening. And simultaneously, ecosystems are collapsing, weapons are proliferating, and social trust is eroding. Most people cannot hold both of those truths at once. They pick a side — either relentless optimism or creeping doom — because the tension between the two is almost unbearable. But both curves are real. And when two opposite trends are both accelerating, the future becomes genuinely difficult to think about clearly. That disorientation is not a personal failing. It is the actual shape of the moment we are living through.

## Adept

E.O. Wilson's tripartite diagnosis — Paleolithic emotions, medieval institutions, godlike technology — names a structural lag that sits at the heart of the modern predicament. Human neurophysiology evolved under conditions of scarcity, tribalism, and slow environmental feedback. The institutional architectures of liberal democracy and organized religion were designed for a pre-industrial tempo of change. Yet the technological substrate of civilization is now operating on an exponential curve that neither our psychology nor our governance frameworks were built to process. What Daniel Schmachtenberger adds to Wilson's frame is a crucial complication: the exponential is not running in one direction. The empirical optimists — Pinker, Ridley, Rosling — are correct that by many measurable indices, human welfare has improved dramatically. And the catastrophists are equally correct that systemic risks, ecological degradation, and civilizational fragility are accelerating. These are not competing narratives where one must be wrong. They are simultaneous realities produced by the same underlying dynamic: the compounding power of human coordination and technology generating both enormous benefit and enormous destructive potential at once. The epistemic problem this creates is severe. Linear cognitive intuitions cannot easily model the intersection of two diverging exponential curves. The result is a kind of rational disorientation — not confusion born of ignorance, but confusion that is the appropriate response to a genuinely novel structural situation. What is conspicuously absent is any framework for exponential meaning: a coherent interpretive and ethical architecture scaled to the actual complexity of the moment.

## Magus

Wilson's tripartite formulation encodes a theory of civilizational phase mismatch: the temporal registers of neurobiological evolution, institutional sedimentation, and technological acceleration have become so radically decoupled that the integrative capacity of any single framework — religious, liberal, or scientific — is structurally overwhelmed. The Paleolithic emotional substrate operates on evolutionary timescales; the institutional layer on historical ones; the technological layer on something closer to geological compression. No prior civilizational moment has required the simultaneous navigation of all three registers at maximum divergence. Schmachtenberger's intervention sharpens the epistemological stakes considerably. The standard debate between Pinker-style meliorism and collapse-oriented catastrophism is revealed as a false disjunction produced by a shared cognitive limitation: the inability to hold two orthogonal exponential trajectories in superposition. Both the progress narrative and the decline narrative are empirically defensible precisely because they are tracking different dimensions of the same underlying dynamic — the recursive amplification of human coordinative and technological capacity, which simultaneously compounds welfare gains and systemic fragility. The resulting epistemic condition is not mere uncertainty but something closer to what one might call exponential aporia: a state in which the standard heuristics for adjudicating between competing worldviews fail, because the phenomenon in question is not amenable to linear decomposition. What is structurally absent — and what neither late liberalism nor post-secular spirituality has yet produced — is a framework for exponential meaning: an interpretive, ethical, and ontological architecture capable of orienting agency at the intersection of accelerating goods and accelerating catastrophe, without collapsing into either triumphalism or nihilism.
