# The Inseparable Trinity of Economic Expansion

The More We Save, the More We Burn

---

> Every modern economy rests on three inseparable foundations — energy, materials, and information — and efficiency gains don't shrink consumption, they accelerate it. Technological progress, as currently structured, is an accelerant of resource use, not a cure.

There is an economic trinity at the foundation of all modern growth — energy, materials, and information — and removing any one collapses the entire system. Energy is 99 percent correlated with economic output. Materials are 100 percent correlated — there is no dematerialization, only the illusion of it. Information, in the form of ideas, blueprints, technology, and cultural transmission, directs and transforms the other two. This trinity also reveals why efficiency gains do not reduce overall consumption in a growth-oriented economy: the global economy has been getting more efficient by about 1 percent per year for 60 years, but those savings get funneled back to grow consumption further. This is the rebound effect — the Jevons Paradox — meaning technological progress, as currently structured, is not a solution to resource depletion but an accelerant of it. If we plan to grow the economy by 10 percent, we need 10 percent more physical materials. There is no escape from this constraint within the current growth paradigm. The trinity is a diagnostic framework revealing the deep structural reasons why incremental technological solutions cannot resolve the human predicament without a fundamental change in the goals of the economic system itself.

## Apprentice

Modern economies run on three things that cannot be separated: energy, materials, and information. Take away any one of them and the whole system stops. Energy use tracks economic output almost perfectly. Materials use tracks it even more closely — despite talk of a 'weightless economy' or 'dematerialization,' the total weight of stuff the global economy consumes keeps rising. Information — in the form of ideas, designs, and know-how — is what directs and transforms the other two.

Here is the uncomfortable part: getting more efficient doesn't reduce how much we consume. For sixty years, the global economy has improved its efficiency by roughly one percent per year. But those savings don't sit idle — they get reinvested to produce and consume more. This is called the rebound effect, or the Jevons Paradox. A more fuel-efficient car makes driving cheaper, so people drive more.

The implication is stark. If an economy is committed to growing by ten percent, it needs roughly ten percent more physical stuff — no matter how clever the technology. Efficiency improvements, within a growth-oriented system, function as an accelerant rather than a brake. Solving the resource problem requires changing the goals of the system itself, not just improving its tools.

## Adept

A structural analysis of modern economies reveals a foundational trinity: energy, materials, and information. Energy consumption correlates with GDP at approximately 0.99; material throughput correlates even more tightly, falsifying the widely circulated hypothesis of absolute dematerialization. Information — encompassing codified knowledge, technology, institutional design, and cultural transmission — functions as the organizational layer that directs and transforms flows of the other two. Remove any vertex of this trinity and economic output collapses. The framework is diagnostic, not merely descriptive: it identifies the load-bearing columns of industrial civilization.

The trinity's most consequential implication concerns efficiency. The Jevons Paradox, first articulated in the context of coal consumption in nineteenth-century Britain, holds that improvements in resource efficiency tend to increase rather than decrease total resource consumption within a growth-oriented system. The empirical record bears this out: roughly one percent annual improvement in global energy intensity over six decades has not reduced aggregate energy demand — savings have been absorbed by expanded output. This is the rebound effect operating at macroeconomic scale.

The diagnostic power of the trinity lies in what it forecloses. A ten-percent growth target entails approximately ten-percent greater material throughput, regardless of technological sophistication. Incremental efficiency gains are structurally incapable of decoupling growth from physical resource consumption in absolute terms. The framework therefore shifts the analytical burden: the question is not how to make the current system more efficient, but whether the growth imperative itself is negotiable.

## Magus

The energy-materials-information trinity constitutes a biophysical accounting identity for industrial economies. The near-unity correlation between primary energy consumption and GDP (~0.99) reflects the thermodynamic substrate of economic production; work, in the physical sense, is not metaphorically related to economic output but causally constitutive of it. Material throughput exhibits an even tighter coupling, with empirical studies of metabolic flows consistently falsifying the environmental Kuznets curve hypothesis at the level of absolute, rather than relative, decoupling. The information vertex — encompassing Schumpeterian knowledge capital, codified technique, institutional grammar, and Veblenian cultural transmission — operates as the configurational layer: it does not substitute for energy or materials but determines the efficiency and directionality of their transformation.

The Jevons Paradox, formalized in contemporary ecological economics as the rebound effect, operates as the dynamic mechanism that prevents efficiency gains from translating into reduced throughput within a growth-maximizing system. At the microeconomic level, price elasticity of demand responds to efficiency-induced cost reductions; at the macroeconomic level, Solow residuals are reinvested rather than banked as slack. The empirical signature — approximately one percent annual improvement in global energy intensity sustained over six decades with no attendant reduction in absolute consumption — is precisely what the paradox predicts. Technological progress, endogenous to the growth system, functions as a throughput accelerant rather than a dematerialization mechanism.

The trinity's epistemological contribution is to reframe the standard techno-optimist position as a category error. The claim that innovation can decouple growth from biophysical constraint conflates relative efficiency with absolute decoupling and mistakes a component variable — information — for a substitute for the system's material substrate. Within a growth-imperative framework, a ten-percent output target is a ten-percent material throughput target, modulo marginal efficiency gains that are themselves consumed by the growth dynamic. The trinity thus functions as a falsifiability criterion: any proposed solution to ecological overshoot that does not address the growth imperative is structurally insufficient by definition.
