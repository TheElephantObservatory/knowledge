# Social Atomization as Precursor to Conscious Retribalization

The clearing had to happen first.

---

> The radical individualism of recent decades was not a cultural dead end but a clearing operation — dissolving inherited myths so people could consciously choose new collective affiliations. The chaotic retribalization now underway is a Cambrian explosion of social forms, most of which will fail, and that failure is the mechanism by which genuinely new scaffolding emerges.

The atomization produced by hyperindividualism and the internet was not the endpoint of cultural evolution but a necessary precursor to retribalization — and the retribalization now underway, however chaotic and sometimes deranged it appears, may contain within it the seeds of a genuinely new social scaffolding. Collective myths had to be dissolved before people could autonomously choose new collective affiliations. The Emersonian self-reliance, the Randian individual, the decade of Me — these were not just pathologies but a kind of clearing operation, unmooring people from inherited collective delusions so they could re-enter collective life more consciously. The internet then provided the infrastructure for mass retribalization at speed. What looks like chaos — QAnon, network states, intentional communities, religious revivals, localist movements — is actually a Cambrian explosion of social membranes, most of which will fail. But failure is the mechanism, not the problem. Just as Luca, the last universal common ancestor, had countless failed predecessors and competitors, the current proliferation of retribalizations is the substrate from which something viable might emerge. Indeterminacy is not a sign of collapse but a marker of emergence. Moments of ontological flooding — when the old scaffolding is gone and the new one is not yet built — are precisely the moments when genuinely new forms become possible. The prescription is not to return to previous scaffolding, which is always a nostalgic fiction, but to tend the membranes carefully, insist that each one actually works, and remain open to the possibility that one of these strange experiments becomes the Luca of the next phase of human social evolution.

## Apprentice

For decades, modern culture pushed people toward extreme individualism — self-reliance, personal branding, the idea that you don't need anyone else. This felt like fragmentation, and in many ways it was. But this perspective reframes that atomization as a necessary step: old group identities built on inherited myths had to dissolve before people could freely choose new ones. You can't consciously join a tribe if you've never left the one you were born into.

Now, thanks largely to the internet, people are rapidly forming new groups — online movements, intentional communities, network states, religious revivals, localist experiments, and yes, some conspiracy-driven collectives. It looks messy, even alarming. But the argument here is that this mess is productive. Most of these new groups will fail. That's not a crisis — it's how evolution works. Countless organisms had to fail before viable life took hold.

The key insight is that the current moment of confusion, where old structures are gone and new ones haven't solidified, is not collapse. It's the only kind of moment when genuinely new social forms can appear. The task isn't to rebuild what was lost or to panic at the chaos, but to pay close attention to which experiments actually sustain the people inside them — because one of these strange new formations might become the ancestor of whatever comes next.

## Adept

This argument reframes hyperindividualism — from Emersonian self-reliance through Randian libertarianism to the therapeutic culture of self-actualization — not as a terminal cultural pathology but as a clearing operation. Inherited collective mythologies had to be dissolved at the level of individual consciousness before autonomous re-affiliation became possible. The atomized self was never the telos; it was the necessary interstitial phase between involuntary tribalism and conscious collective formation. The internet then provided the infrastructure for mass retribalization at unprecedented speed, compressing what might have taken generations into years.

What results — QAnon, network states, intentional communities, neo-religious movements, localist experiments — appears chaotic precisely because it is a Cambrian explosion of social membranes. The analogy to LUCA (Last Universal Common Ancestor) is instructive: viable forms emerge from a substrate of massive failure. Most of these retribalizations will not persist, but their proliferation is the evolutionary mechanism itself, not evidence of systemic breakdown. The indeterminacy of the present moment is recast as ontological flooding — a phase transition in which old scaffolding has been removed and new scaffolding has not yet crystallized.

The normative implication resists both nostalgic restoration and nihilistic resignation. The prescription is membrane-tending: evaluating each emergent social form by whether it actually sustains coherent collective life, remaining agnostic about which specific experiment will prove viable, and recognizing that the impulse to prematurely close the indeterminacy — by returning to prior structures or by declaring the whole process pathological — is itself the primary threat to genuine emergence.

## Magus

The genealogy proposed here inverts the standard declension narrative of modernity-as-atomization. Hyperindividualism — traced through its Emersonian, Randian, and therapeutic-narcissistic instantiations — is repositioned as a phase of necessary delamination: the systematic dissolution of involuntary collective mythic structures at the level of individual subjectivity. This delamination is the precondition for what follows, namely autonomous re-affiliation. The sequence is not fragmentation-then-loss but fragmentation-as-clearing, an ontological controlled burn that renders the substrate available for novel collective formation. The internet functions not as the cause of atomization but as the infrastructural accelerant of the subsequent retribalization, collapsing the temporal horizon of social morphogenesis.

The current proliferation of social membranes — conspiratorial, communitarian, techno-political, neo-sacral — is theorized as a Cambrian explosion operating at the level of social ontology. The invocation of LUCA is precise: just as the last universal common ancestor emerged from a substrate of innumerable failed protocellular experiments, viable new social forms require a phase of massive, undirected variation in which most experiments are eliminated. Failure is not noise but signal — the selective mechanism through which fitness landscapes are explored. The indeterminacy of the present is thus recharacterized as ontological flooding, a concept marking the interstitial phase between the collapse of one scaffolding regime and the crystallization of its successor.

The prescriptive dimension is deliberately anti-restorationist and anti-teleological. Nostalgia for prior scaffolding is diagnosed as a category error — the attempt to reimpose a dissolved ontological regime. Instead, the task is careful membrane-tending: empirical evaluation of which emergent social forms generate genuine collective coherence, coupled with epistemic humility about which, if any, will prove to be the ancestral form of subsequent social evolution. Premature closure of the indeterminate phase is identified as the gravest risk.
