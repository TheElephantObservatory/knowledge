# How Purpose Emerges from the Physics of Absence

The hollow space that moves the world

---

> Terence Deacon rescues purpose and meaning from the exile Darwin imposed, showing that goal-directedness is a real physical phenomenon grounded in absence — in what a system is organized toward but does not yet have.

Since Darwin, teleology had been summarily removed from naturalistic biology — replaced by blind chance and efficient material causation. But Terence Deacon's work represents a profound paradigm shift: he rehabilitates teleology not by smuggling in metaphysics, but by grounding it in the physical dynamics of incompleteness. His key insight is that concepts like information, function, purpose, meaning, intention, and value are all intrinsically defined by their fundamental incompleteness — they exist only in relation to something they are not. An absent goal state doesn't just describe these entities; it motivates them, in the most literal etymological sense — it causes them to move. This reframes teleology as a processual physical phenomenon rather than a speculative metaphysical one. Deacon coins the term 'ententional' to capture this: a class of entities — from simple organisms to conscious minds — that are organized around and driven toward an absent goal. These are expressions of Aristotle's final causality, but now placed firmly within a naturalistic, emergentist framework. The causal power of ententional processes is not located in any ultimate stuff, but in their dynamical organization itself. This allows us to trace a continuous thread from thermodynamic dissipative structures all the way up through life, mind, and culture — each level exhibiting increasingly complex forms of consequence-organized, goal-directed behavior, without ever requiring a transcendent designer or a ghost in the machine.

## Apprentice

Ever since Darwin, science has been uncomfortable with the idea of purpose in nature. If evolution is just random mutation and selection, where does genuine goal-directedness come from? How can a bacterium swimming toward food, or a person striving toward a dream, be doing something genuinely purposeful rather than just running a complicated mechanism?

Terence Deacon's answer is surprising: purpose is real, and its reality is grounded in absence. Information, meaning, and intention are all defined not by what they are, but by what they point toward — something that isn't present yet. That missing goal state isn't just a description; it actually causes the system to move. A seed doesn't just happen to grow into a tree; it is organized around a future state that pulls its development forward, without any designer required.

Deacon calls entities organized this way 'ententional' — a new word for a genuinely new concept. From the simplest self-organizing chemical systems all the way up to conscious human minds, there is a continuous thread of increasingly complex purpose-driven behavior. None of it requires magic or a ghost in the machine. Purpose turns out to be a natural consequence of how certain physical systems are organized around what they lack.

## Adept

Since Darwin, teleology was treated as a conceptual embarrassment in the natural sciences — a relic of pre-scientific thinking to be replaced entirely by efficient and material causation. Terence Deacon's project, most fully articulated in 'Incomplete Nature,' constitutes a serious attempt to rehabilitate final causality within a rigorously naturalistic and emergentist framework, without reintroducing vitalism or dualism.

Deacon's central move is to locate the causal efficacy of purpose, meaning, and intentionality in their intrinsic incompleteness. These phenomena are not defined by any positive substrate but by their constitutive orientation toward an absent referent or goal state. The absence is not merely descriptive — it is dynamically efficacious. A system organized around what it is not yet, or what it lacks, is genuinely caused to behave in consequence-organized ways. Deacon coins the term 'ententional' to name this class of phenomena, which includes everything from thermodynamic dissipative structures and homeostatic organisms to semiotic processes and conscious intentions.

This framework allows for a continuous, non-reductive account of emergence across levels of complexity. Each level — from chemistry to biology to mind to culture — exhibits increasingly sophisticated forms of absential causation, where the organizing principle is a future or ideal state rather than a prior efficient cause. The causal power is located not in any ultimate stuff but in dynamical organization itself, dissolving the hard boundary between the physical and the intentional without collapsing one into the other.

## Magus

The post-Darwinian settlement in philosophy of biology effectively bracketed Aristotelian final causality as either eliminable in principle or reducible without remainder to efficient-causal and selectionist accounts. Deacon's 'Incomplete Nature' mounts a systematic challenge to this consensus by arguing that ententional phenomena — information, function, reference, purpose, value, and consciousness — share a common logical and physical structure: they are constitutively defined by their intrinsic incompleteness, their orientation toward an absent, counterfactual, or ideal terminus.

The philosophical core of the argument is that absence can be causally efficacious without invoking non-physical agency. Drawing on thermodynamics, self-organization theory, and semiotics, Deacon constructs a hierarchical ontology of emergent constraint. Dissipative structures, morphodynamic processes, and teleodynamic systems represent successive levels at which the organizing principle is not a prior state but a far-from-equilibrium attractor — a regime of constraint that the system is, in a precise sense, about. The causal work is done by the geometry of constraint propagation, not by any supervening substance. This is Aristotelian final causality reconstructed in the idiom of non-equilibrium thermodynamics and dynamical systems theory.

The philosophical payoff is considerable. It dissolves the Cartesian hard problem at its root by denying that intentionality requires a non-physical medium while equally denying that it reduces to mere mechanism. Ententional organization is real, causally potent, and physically grounded — but irreducible to the substrate-level descriptions that efficient causation tracks. Deacon thus offers a third path between eliminative materialism and property dualism, one in which the normative and the natural are continuous without being identical.
