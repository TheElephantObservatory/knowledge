# A Secular Structure for the Sacred: Mattering, Love, and Ultimacy

Falling into what is most real

---

> The sacred can be fully explained without the supernatural: it arises when something matters beyond the self, draws us deeper into reality through love, and orients us toward what is ultimately real — a structure the great traditions named God, Dao, or Brahman.

The sacred, understood phenomenologically and functionally, has a structure that can be articulated without any appeal to the supernatural. It is not simply what we would refuse to trade for money — that criterion captures intrinsic value but could equally apply to a compulsion or an addiction. The sacred is better approached through the concept of meaning in life, which research consistently shows involves four factors: intelligibility, purpose, a sense of realness, and — perhaps most fundamental — mattering. Mattering means being connected to something that has reality and value beyond your egocentric existence. This is why children are prototypically sacred to parents: the relationship is agapic, not instrumental. You love them because you want them to exist even after you do not. But the sacred is not exhausted by mattering. There is a further structure: when we are connected to something that matters and we find ourselves falling in love with it — in the sense that it is taking us deeper into reality, disclosing more of what is real rather than distracting from it — the relationship intensifies. And there is a superlative register beyond even that: when the thing we are falling in love with, that is taking us deeper into reality, begins to bear us toward what is ultimately real — when we find ourselves in relationship to what might be called Ultimate Reality — then the sacred reaches its highest pitch. This is what the great religious traditions were attempting to articulate with terms like God, Dao, or Brahman: not a supernatural entity, but this structure of enhanced mattering, deepening love, and orientation toward ultimacy. The functional and phenomenological account is complete. None of it requires anything beyond nature. And all of it — the agapic concern, the falling in love with reality, the orientation toward ultimacy — can be grounded in what we are as primate mammals who are born from care, sustained by care, and who can exapt that care onto ever-wider circles of reality.

## Apprentice

Most people assume that calling something "sacred" means believing in the supernatural. But there is a way to understand the sacred that stays entirely within nature. It starts with mattering — the sense that you are connected to something real and valuable beyond just yourself. Children are a clear example: a parent's love for a child is not about what the child does for them. It is the desire for the child to exist and flourish even after the parent is gone.

But mattering alone does not capture the full shape of the sacred. There is a deeper layer: when the thing that matters to you also draws you closer to reality — when loving it opens your eyes rather than closing them — the experience intensifies. And at the furthest reach, when that deepening love begins to point toward what is ultimately real, the sacred hits its highest note.

This is what religious traditions have tried to name with words like God, Dao, or Brahman — not a supernatural being, but this layered experience of caring beyond yourself, falling in love with what is real, and being drawn toward the ultimate. The whole account can be grounded in what we already are: mammals born from care, sustained by care, and capable of extending that care outward to ever-wider circles of reality.

## Adept

The sacred, approached phenomenologically and functionally rather than dogmatically, reveals a layered structure that requires no supernatural commitments. The first layer is mattering — being connected to something with reality and value beyond one's egocentric frame. This is distinct from mere intrinsic value, which could equally describe a compulsion. Mattering draws on the meaning-in-life literature, which identifies intelligibility, purpose, a sense of realness, and mattering itself as core factors. The paradigm case is agapic love: a parent loves a child not instrumentally but because they want the child to exist beyond their own existence.

The structure deepens when what matters also discloses reality — when the relationship is not merely valued but revelatory, drawing one into deeper contact with what is real rather than functioning as distraction or illusion. This is the phenomenology of falling in love with reality through a particular connection. And there is a superlative register: when that which deepens our contact with reality begins to orient us toward what is ultimately real, the sacred reaches its highest intensity. This is the experiential referent behind terms like God, Dao, and Brahman — not supernatural entities but names for this culminating orientation toward ultimacy.

The account is naturalistic throughout. The capacity for agapic concern, for falling in love with reality, and for orientation toward ultimacy can be understood as exaptations of the care structures fundamental to primate mammalian life. Born from care, sustained by care, we extend care onto ever-wider domains. The sacred is not an exception to nature but its deepest expression.

## Magus

The phenomenological and functional analysis of the sacred reveals a triadic structure — mattering, deepening disclosure, and orientation toward ultimacy — that is internally complete without supernatural supplementation. The first stratum is mattering, which research in the meaning-in-life literature (alongside intelligibility, purpose, and felt realness) identifies as the sense of being connected to something possessing reality and value that transcends egocentric framing. This must be distinguished from intrinsic value simpliciter: compulsions and addictions resist commodification too, but they lack the agapic structure characteristic of genuine mattering. The paradigmatic instance is parental love — a non-instrumental desire for the beloved's existence beyond one's own, irreducible to hedonic or utilitarian calculus.

The second stratum emerges when the mattering relation is simultaneously alethic: when the connection does not merely hold value but functions as disclosure, drawing one deeper into reality rather than occluding it. This is the phenomenology of eros as reality-contact intensification, not mere attachment. The third and superlative stratum arises when that disclosive, mattering relationship orients the person toward what is ultimately real — toward ultimacy as such. The great axial traditions named this referent God, Dao, Brahman — terms best understood not as denoting supernatural entities but as indexing this culminating structural moment in the phenomenology of the sacred.

Crucially, the entire architecture is naturalizable. The agapic dispositions grounding mattering, the erotic capacity for deepening reality-contact, and the tropism toward ultimacy are all explicable as exaptations of mammalian care structures. Primates born from care and sustained by care possess the developmental and cognitive resources to extend care onto progressively wider and deeper domains of reality. The sacred, on this account, is not nature's other but its most intensified self-expression.
