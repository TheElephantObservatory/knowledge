# How Market Systems Erode the Cultural Norms That Once Constrained Them

The account was running low long before anyone checked.

---

> When doing the right thing becomes a competitive disadvantage, the system itself is broken. Game B's founding diagnosis traces how multipolar traps and cultural erosion turned market competition into a machine that systematically rewards bad behavior and punishes good.

The core diagnosis that gave birth to Game B can be reduced to a single sentence: if doing the right thing is the wrong thing, there is something fundamentally wrong with the system. Jim Rutt traces this through his own career. In 1980, certain behaviors were simply unthinkable — not because they were illegal, but because the cultural accords made them shameful. By 1994, the ethos had shifted to 'if it's arguably legal and profitable, you must do it.' By 2008, the logic had degraded further still: if you are caught, is the penalty less than the gain? Companies like Google and Facebook have been fined billions for scurrilous behavior and keep doing it — because they made five billion and were fined two. This is not individual moral failure. It is the multipolar trap — a multi-player prisoner's dilemma with high stakes. If one competitor moves toward the bad because it is financially advantageous, every other competitor is forced to follow or gets driven out. Gresham's Law, transposed into a new domain: bad behavior drives out good. Rufus Pollock deepens this diagnosis by pointing to the cultural substrate beneath the financial mechanics: what we are witnessing is a double attack — the erosion of the cultural base that once made the cost of bad behavior prohibitively shameful, combined with the intensification of competitive pressure that makes the reward for bad behavior ever larger. The ideological story — greed is good, market fundamentalism, shareholder value maximization — is not the cause but the symptom and accelerant of a much longer cultural decay. Just as the things that ultimately corrupted the medieval Catholic Church were present in embryonic form centuries before the crisis became visible, the things corrupting Game A were present in the rapacious capitalism of Rockefeller. The cultural inheritance that once constrained the worst impulses of the market system was being spent down long before anyone noticed the account was running low. The deeper engine beneath this is money-on-money return, which filters upward from actual financial returns to the competition among investable assets, which drives stock prices, which drives executive compensation. The result is a system that is now essentially amoral — where the only remaining taboo is putting a bullet in someone's head directly, but killing fifty people a year by skimping on brake rotors is a perfectly acceptable financial decision if the numbers work out. Game A, the civilizational system that emerged around 1700 combining the Westphalian state, real science, democracy, and modern finance, was probably the best compounding available at the time. But it has now consumed the cultural substrate that once constrained it, and what remains is a machine with no brakes. This framing makes clear that the solution cannot be purely institutional or financial — it requires the slow, difficult work of cultural rebuilding, of recovering a shared understanding of what is genuinely gained when individuals make voluntary collective commitments.

## Apprentice

Here is a simple but devastating observation: if a company tries to do the right thing and gets destroyed by competitors who don't, the problem isn't the company — it's the system. Jim Rutt traces this shift through decades of business culture. In 1980, certain predatory behaviors were unthinkable — not because of laws, but because shame made them impossible. By the mid-1990s, the rule had become: if it's legal and profitable, you must do it. By 2008, even legality was optional — the only question was whether the fine would be smaller than the profit.

This isn't about greedy individuals. It's a trap built into competition itself. If one player cheats and wins, everyone else must cheat or lose. Bad behavior drives out good, the same way counterfeit money drives out real currency. Rufus Pollock adds a crucial layer: the cultural traditions that once made bad behavior shameful have been eroding for centuries, while the financial rewards for cutting corners keep growing. The ideology of "greed is good" didn't cause the rot — it's a symptom of a much older decay.

The civilization that emerged around 1700 — combining science, democracy, nation-states, and modern finance — was a remarkable achievement. But it has burned through the cultural fuel that once kept it in check. What remains is a powerful engine with no brakes. Fixing this cannot be just about better rules or regulations. It requires the slow, hard work of rebuilding shared cultural commitments about what kind of behavior a society will and won't tolerate.

## Adept

The founding diagnosis of Game B reduces to a single proposition: when the system punishes ethical behavior and rewards defection, the system itself is the problem. Jim Rutt documents the degradation empirically — from the cultural accords of the 1980s where certain behaviors were unthinkable due to shame, through the 1990s shift to "if it's arguably legal and profitable, you must do it," to the post-2008 calculus where companies absorb billion-dollar fines as a cost of doing business because the profits exceed the penalties. This is the multipolar trap in action: a multi-player prisoner's dilemma where any single actor's move toward defection forces all competitors to follow or be eliminated. Gresham's Law, transposed from currency to conduct.

Rufus Pollock deepens the analysis by identifying a double attack: the intensification of competitive pressure that increases the reward for defection, combined with the erosion of the cultural substrate that once made the cost of defection prohibitively high. The ideological apparatus — shareholder value maximization, market fundamentalism — functions not as cause but as symptom and accelerant. The deeper engine is money-on-money return filtering upward through investable asset competition to stock prices to executive compensation, producing a system that is functionally amoral. The analogy to the medieval Catholic Church is precise: the corrupting dynamics were present in embryonic form long before the crisis became visible.

Game A — the civilizational operating system combining the Westphalian state, empirical science, democratic governance, and modern finance — was likely the best compounding strategy available circa 1700. But it has consumed the cultural inheritance that once constrained its worst tendencies. The implication is that no purely institutional or financial reform can suffice. The solution space necessarily includes the slow reconstruction of cultural norms — recovering the shared understanding that voluntary collective commitments represent genuine, not illusory, gains.

## Magus

The generative kernel of the Game B diagnosis is an inversion test applied to civilizational incentive structures: when prosocial behavior constitutes competitive disadvantage, the failure is systemic rather than moral. Rutt's periodization — from shame-constrained business culture (1980), through the juridification of ethics into mere legality-plus-profitability (1994), to the naked expected-value calculus where penalties are simply discounted against gains (2008) — traces not a decline in individual virtue but the progressive exhaustion of extra-economic constraints on market behavior. The mechanism is a generalized multipolar trap: an n-player iterated prisoner's dilemma in which defection, once initiated by any single actor, propagates through competitive selection until cooperation becomes structurally impossible. Gresham's dynamic, abstracted from monetary to behavioral domains.

Pollock's contribution identifies the trap as doubly determined. The competitive pressure intensifying the reward for defection operates simultaneously with the degradation of the cultural substrate that once rendered defection prohibitively costly through shame and social sanction. The ideological superstructure — Friedmanite shareholder primacy, the "greed is good" mythos — is epiphenomenal relative to the deeper engine: money-on-money return cascading through asset competition into equity pricing into executive compensation, producing a system whose amorality is bounded only at the extreme of direct physical violence. The analogy to pre-Reformation ecclesial corruption is structurally precise: the pathological dynamics were embryonically present in Rockefeller-era capitalism, spending down accumulated cultural capital long before the deficit became legible.

Game A — the civilizational stack assembled circa 1700 from Westphalian sovereignty, empirical science, representative governance, and modern financial instruments — represented optimal compounding given available cultural technology. Its terminal crisis is that it has metabolized the very cultural inheritance that constrained its extractive tendencies. The prescription follows necessarily from the diagnosis: no reconfiguration of institutional or financial architecture alone can restore systemic coherence. What is required is cultural reconstruction — the regeneration of shared normative frameworks within which voluntary collective commitment is recognized as genuine rather than sacrificial.
