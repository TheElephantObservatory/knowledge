# Relevance Bridges the Gap Between Fact and Value

The standard you never chose but cannot escape

---

> Relevance is not a fact about the world nor a mere preference — it is the pre-conceptual condition for any concept to work at all, revealing that the separation of fact and value collapses the moment cognition actually operates.

Relevance is one of the most philosophically revealing concepts we have, precisely because it refuses to sit cleanly on either side of the is-ought divide. Relevance is not a property that inheres in information the way mass inheres in an object. But neither is it purely a statement of preference, because we correct ourselves: we say things like 'I should have paid attention there, that was more relevant.' The capacity for self-correction implies a standard that is not merely internal to our preferences. The knowledge-claiming machinery and the value-assigning machinery are not separate systems — they are completely interdependent and interdefining. You cannot make an epistemic judgment without doing relevance realization, and relevance realization is already a normative act. This is the practical demonstration that the clean separation of fact and value is an impossible project when you try to actually do ontology or psychology. The concept of thick concepts, from Bernard Williams — terms like 'maturity,' 'wisdom,' or 'psychopathology' that are simultaneously descriptive and evaluative — captures part of this. But relevance goes deeper than thick concepts, because it is not just a concept that happens to be thick — it is the pre-conceptual condition for any concept to function at all. Getting relevance more and more coupled to realness — having what we find relevant track what is actually real — is perhaps the most concise formulation of what development and transformation mean.

## Apprentice

We tend to think facts and values are separate things — facts tell us what is, values tell us what matters. But relevance doesn't fit neatly into either category. Information isn't relevant the way a rock is heavy; relevance isn't just sitting there in the world waiting to be measured. Yet it's not purely a matter of taste either, because we correct ourselves. We say things like "I really should have paid attention to that" — which implies there's a standard beyond just what we happened to prefer in the moment.

This matters because relevance isn't just one concept among many that blurs the line between fact and value. It's the thing that has to happen before any concept can do its job at all. Before you can think about anything, something has to stand out as worth thinking about. That selection process — what gets noticed, what gets ignored — is already making judgments about what matters.

The deeper implication is striking: if relevance is both how we know things and how we value things, then the whole project of cleanly separating knowledge from values falls apart in practice. Development and growth, on this view, amount to getting better at finding relevant what is actually real — aligning our sense of what matters with what is genuinely there.

## Adept

The is-ought distinction — the supposed gap between descriptive facts and evaluative norms — has long been treated as a foundational boundary in philosophy. Relevance, however, refuses to respect it. Relevance is not a mind-independent property of information, yet it is not reducible to subjective preference either, because relevance judgments are self-correcting. The statement "I should have attended to that" presupposes a normative standard that transcends momentary inclination. This self-corrective structure reveals that the epistemic machinery (how we form knowledge) and the evaluative machinery (how we assign significance) are not parallel systems but deeply interdependent and mutually constituting.

Bernard Williams's notion of thick concepts — terms like "courage," "cruelty," or "wisdom" that are simultaneously descriptive and evaluative — captures something of this entanglement. But the insight here goes further. Relevance is not merely a thick concept; it is the pre-conceptual condition that enables any concept, thick or thin, to function. Before a concept can describe or evaluate, something must have already been selected as worth attending to. Relevance realization is the process that makes conceptual activity possible in the first place, and it is inherently normative.

This has profound implications for ontology and cognitive science alike. If relevance realization is both the ground of epistemic contact with reality and an irreducibly normative act, then the clean separation of fact and value is not just philosophically questionable — it is operationally impossible. Development and transformation, on this account, can be understood as the progressive coupling of relevance to realness: the ongoing calibration of what we find salient with what is genuinely there.

## Magus

The is-ought divide, canonized since Hume and reinforced by logical positivism's fact-value distinction, presupposes that epistemic and normative domains can be cleanly demarcated. Relevance constitutes a decisive counterexample — not as an anomaly but as a structural impossibility proof for the separation itself. Relevance is neither a mind-independent property (it does not inhere in information as mass inheres in matter) nor a projection of arbitrary preference, because relevance judgments exhibit a self-corrective normativity: "I should have attended to that" indexes a standard irreducible to first-order desire. The knowledge-claiming and value-assigning functions are not merely coupled but co-constitutive — each is unintelligible without the other.

Bernard Williams's thick concepts ("maturity," "psychopathology," "wisdom") demonstrate that certain terms resist decomposition into separable descriptive and evaluative components. Yet relevance is not simply another thick concept occupying the same logical space. It operates at a more fundamental stratum: relevance realization is the pre-conceptual selection process that determines what enters the space of reasons at all. It is the transcendental condition for conceptual functioning as such. Every epistemic act — perception, categorization, inference — presupposes a prior relevance landscape that is already normatively structured. To perform ontology or psychology without acknowledging this is to operate within an abstraction that cannot account for its own conditions of possibility.

The developmental and transformative implication follows directly: if relevance realization is the joint at which knowing and valuing are forged together, then cognitive and ethical maturation converge on a single trajectory — the progressive coupling of what is found relevant with what is real. This formulation compresses the aspirations of epistemology, phenomenology, and virtue theory into a single operative principle, one that is neither purely descriptive nor purely prescriptive but constitutively both.
