# Why Stripping Away the Social Self Reveals a Deeper Paradox

Into the desert, and back again.

---

> If the self is purely a social construction, why does stripping away social identity feel like encountering something more real, not less? The tension between constructed and essential selfhood is not a problem to solve but a recursive paradox that contemplative traditions have always inhabited.

When we say the self is socially constructed or socially attributed, many people feel existentially threatened — and there are serious reasons why that threat is legitimate, not merely defensive. One reason is arbitrariness: if given a different set of social conditions I could just as easily have become a different kind of self, or no self at all, then the self feels dissolute, without ground. But there is a second, deeper challenge. The intuitive experience of becoming oneself — of growing into who one is — seems to involve precisely the negation of social attribution. We go out into the desert, we withdraw from the currency of social valuation, we loosen ourselves from the conditions that constructed us. And in that withdrawal, something is confronted that feels more essential, not less. This is not merely a philosophical thought experiment — it is the structure of contemplative traditions across cultures, from Buddhist practice to Christian neoplatonism. These traditions report that when social attribution is stripped away, and even self-attribution is challenged, what remains is not nothing but something like pure agency, pure freedom. Yet here the paradox deepens from both directions: the Buddhist practitioner who realizes no-self is still called to return — to re-embed in the social world with compassion for all beings. But compassion for what, if there are no selves? The substantial self gets insinuated back in through the paradox, and so does the no-self. This dialectical movement — withdrawal from social attribution, confrontation with something more essential, and then return and re-embedding — suggests that the relationship between the intrinsic and the attributed self is not a simple opposition but a living, recursive tension that cannot be resolved by choosing one side.

## Apprentice

There's something unsettling about the idea that who you are is mostly a product of your social environment — that under different circumstances, you might have become an entirely different person. It can make identity feel accidental, even meaningless. But there's a deeper puzzle: when people actually do step away from social life — through meditation, solitude, or spiritual practice — they often report finding something that feels more real, not less. Stripping away what others think of you doesn't leave emptiness; it reveals what feels like a core of pure awareness or freedom.

Yet the story doesn't end there. Across traditions — Buddhist, Christian contemplative, and others — the person who withdraws and touches that deeper reality is always called to come back. The Buddhist who realizes there is no fixed self is still asked to feel compassion for all beings. But compassion for whom, if selves don't really exist? The very act of returning to care for others quietly reintroduces the idea of real, substantial selves.

This back-and-forth — leaving society to find something essential, then returning to re-engage with the social world — suggests that the question of whether the self is "really" constructed or "really" intrinsic can't be settled by picking a side. The two perspectives need each other and keep generating each other in an ongoing, living tension.

## Adept

The claim that selfhood is socially constructed provokes existential resistance for reasons that deserve philosophical respect, not dismissal. The first concern is arbitrariness: if contingent social conditions fully determine who one becomes, identity loses its ground. But a second, more structurally interesting challenge emerges from contemplative experience. The phenomenology of self-discovery — across Buddhist practice, Christian neoplatonism, and other apophatic traditions — involves precisely the negation of social attribution. One withdraws from the economy of social recognition, and what is encountered in that withdrawal registers not as absence but as something like pure agency or unconditioned awareness. This experiential datum resists reduction to mere ideology or defense mechanism.

The paradox, however, is bidirectional. The Buddhist practitioner who realizes anattā — no-self — is nonetheless enjoined to return to the social world and exercise compassion toward all sentient beings. But compassion presupposes subjects who suffer, which quietly reinstates the substantial selfhood that was just deconstructed. The no-self insight and the re-emergence of the self as morally real are not sequential stages that resolve into a final position; they form a dialectical loop.

This recursive movement — withdrawal from social attribution, confrontation with what appears intrinsic, and re-embedding in relational life — suggests that the constructed self and the essential self are not competing hypotheses between which one must adjudicate. They constitute a living tension, each pole generating the conditions for the other's intelligibility. The relationship is not oppositional but co-constitutive, and any account of selfhood that collapses into one side forfeits the phenomenological and ethical complexity the dialectic preserves.

## Magus

The existential threat posed by social constructionism about the self is typically read as defensive — a failure to relinquish metaphysical comfort. This analysis argues the threat is philosophically substantive on at least two axes. The first is the problem of arbitrariness: full social determination renders selfhood radically contingent, undermining the normative weight identity is asked to bear. The second, more penetrating challenge arises from the apophatic structure of contemplative self-inquiry across traditions. The via negativa of Buddhist vipassanā, the kenotic withdrawal in Christian neoplatonism, the Advaitic neti neti — each enacts a systematic stripping of socially attributed identity, and each reports that what remains at the limit of this subtraction is not nihil but something phenomenologically dense: pure awareness, unconditioned freedom, the ground prior to attribution. The very gesture of negating social construction yields an encounter that phenomenologically instantiates intrinsic selfhood.

Yet the dialectic does not terminate in essentialism. The practitioner who touches anattā or apophatic emptiness is structurally compelled to return — the bodhisattva vow, the Plotinian emanation back into multiplicity. Compassion for all beings presupposes beings capable of suffering, which reinscribes substantial selfhood at the moment of its ostensible dissolution. The no-self realization and the re-emergence of ethically salient personhood are not reconciled in a higher synthesis; they persist as an irreducible oscillation.

This dialectical recursion — negation of the attributed, encounter with the apparently intrinsic, return to relational embeddedness — reveals that constructed and essential selfhood are not rival ontological claims but co-constitutive moments in a single movement. Neither pole is intelligible without the other. Any theoretical framework that resolves the tension by eliminating one term sacrifices precisely the phenomenological and ethical complexity that makes the question of selfhood philosophically alive.
