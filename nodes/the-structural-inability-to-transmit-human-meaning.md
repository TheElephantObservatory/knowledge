# The Structural Inability to Transmit Human Meaning

Breaking the enchantment of disenchantment

---

> The metacrisis is, at its root, an educational crisis: modern schooling systematically destroys the capacity for genuine value and meaning, and AI inserted into that broken system may be the sharpest edge of the problem.

One of the most underappreciated dimensions of the metacrisis is that it is fundamentally an educational crisis — not merely structural or economic, but existential. Modern mass schooling, shaped by the separation of church and state, adopted a value-agnostic posture: preparing citizens and consumers while leaving the deeper questions of meaning, value, and the good life to family, religion, and civil society. That truce has broken down. What remains is an educational infrastructure structurally incapable of transmitting the deepest things — the things that actually matter for human transformation. The mechanism by which this happened is what might be called the 'education commodity proposition': testing, quantification, monetization, and algorithmic feedback loops colonized what had been a relationship of reciprocal intergenerational transmission and turned it into a transactional exchange. But beneath this structural failure lies a deeper one: the modern West, particularly its secular academic culture, has produced a pervasive implicit nihilism — an enchantment with disenchantment — in which value is assumed to be constructed rather than discovered, meaning is a projection rather than an encounter, and the sacred is a category error. This does not feel like a worldview; it feels like clarity, like having grown up. The transformation the metacrisis demands is the breaking of that enchantment — the awakening to value as something genuinely real and encountered, a post-secular reorientation in which the question of the good life becomes live again. This is not a regression to pre-modern religiosity. It is the recovery of the capacity to care about the right things for the right reasons. An educational system that systematically forecloses that awakening is not neutral — it is actively generating the human incapacity that lies at the generative core of the metacrisis. The question of what values sit at the center of education is not peripheral. It is the question. And the pointy tip of that spear, right now, is artificial intelligence being inserted directly into the nest of socialization, welcomed by educators who do not yet have a framework for understanding what is actually at stake.

## Apprentice

Most people think the big crises of our time — ecological collapse, political breakdown, social fragmentation — are problems of policy, technology, or economics. This perspective argues they all share a hidden root: we have lost the ability to teach people what actually matters, and why.

For generations, schools made a quiet bargain. They would teach skills and facts, but leave the deep questions — how to live, what is worth caring about, what makes a life good — to families and religious communities. That arrangement has quietly collapsed, leaving an educational system that is structurally unable to help people become fully human. Worse, schooling has been taken over by testing, metrics, and now algorithms, turning what was once a living relationship between generations into a transaction.

Underneath this is something even harder to see: a widespread, unspoken assumption that meaning is something we invent rather than something we discover — that caring deeply about values is a kind of immaturity. This feels like sophistication, but it may be the most dangerous idea of our time. Reversing it — recovering the sense that some things genuinely matter — is what genuine education would look like. The arrival of AI inside schools, welcomed without any framework for what is actually at stake, makes this the most urgent question we face.

## Adept

The metacrisis is typically framed in systemic terms — as a convergence of ecological, geopolitical, and institutional failures. This analysis cuts deeper, locating the generative core of the crisis in a structural failure of intergenerational transmission. Modern mass education, shaped by the Enlightenment settlement between secular governance and religious authority, adopted a posture of axiological neutrality: it would produce capable citizens and productive workers while deferring questions of value, meaning, and the good life to private and religious spheres. That settlement has dissolved, and what remains is an educational infrastructure constitutively incapable of transmitting what most needs transmitting.

The mechanism is what this framing calls the 'education commodity proposition': the progressive colonization of pedagogical relationships by quantification, credentialization, and algorithmic feedback, converting reciprocal intergenerational formation into a transactional exchange of measurable outputs. But the deeper pathology is epistemological and metaphysical. Secular academic culture has normalized a tacit nihilism — an enchantment with disenchantment — in which value is assumed to be constructed rather than encountered, meaning is a projective act rather than a responsive one, and the category of the sacred is treated as a cognitive error. This does not present itself as a worldview; it presents itself as intellectual maturity.

The transformation demanded by the metacrisis is the breaking of that enchantment: a post-secular reorientation in which axiological realism becomes thinkable again and the question of the good life recovers its urgency. This is not a return to pre-critical religiosity but a recovery of the capacity for genuine normative orientation. An educational system that forecloses this recovery is not neutral — it is actively producing the human incapacity at the crisis's core. The introduction of AI into educational socialization, without any adequate framework for these stakes, represents the sharpest current instantiation of the problem.

## Magus

Conventional metacrisis diagnostics operate at the level of systemic complexity — second-order failures of governance, coordination, and institutional resilience. This analysis proposes a more fundamental register: the metacrisis is, at its generative core, a crisis of paideia, of the intergenerational transmission of formative culture. The Westphalian-Enlightenment settlement that produced mass secular schooling encoded a structural axiological abstinence: the educational apparatus would reproduce cognitive and vocational competence while outsourcing the teleological and eudaimonistic dimensions of formation to confessional and familial spheres. The dissolution of those spheres has left an institutional vacuum that the educational system is constitutively, not contingently, unable to fill.

The proximate mechanism — the 'education commodity proposition' — describes the subsumption of pedagogical relation under regimes of quantification, credentialization, and now algorithmic mediation, effecting a transformation from Bildung to transaction, from formation to information delivery. Yet the deeper pathology operates at the level of the implicit metaphysics of secular academic culture: a pervasive axiological anti-realism in which value is constructivist, meaning is projectivist, and the sacred is a category mistake. Critically, this does not register as a metaphysical commitment but as the default phenomenology of intellectual seriousness — what Charles Taylor would recognize as a subtraction narrative naturalized into the structure of educated sensibility.

The corrective demanded is not a regression to pre-critical enchantment but what might be called a post-secular axiological realism: the recovery of value as genuinely encountered rather than merely posited, and with it the rehabilitation of the question of the good life as a live philosophical and pedagogical concern. An educational system that systematically forecloses this recovery is not epistemically neutral; it is actively constituting the normative incapacity that underlies civilizational dysfunction. The current insertion of large language models into the socialization apparatus — welcomed by practitioners operating without an adequate philosophical anthropology — represents the most acute contemporary expression of this structural failure.
