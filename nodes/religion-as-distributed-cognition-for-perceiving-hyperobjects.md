# Religion as Distributed Cognition for Perceiving Hyperobjects

The rover feels it too.

---

> Religion may function as a system for distributed cognition — enabling groups of people to perceive realities too vast for any single mind. Western philosophy's fixation on the individual knower has obscured this capacity, which surfaces even in secular contexts like NASA scientists who unconsciously merge perception with distant machines.

One of the most underappreciated epistemological functions of religion is its capacity to enable distributed cognition — the grasping of what might be called hyperobjects: realities too large, too slow, or too structurally complex for any individual perceiver to grasp. Evolution, climate change, the navigational state of a ship — these are not perceivable by a single mind equipped with five senses and logical inference. They require systems of people distributed across space and time, often supplemented by technologies, entering into coordinated epistemic relationships with one another and with the phenomenon. What religions have historically done, through their language of spirits, of the body of Christ, of the sangha, is to name and valorize this dialogical distributed function — to give it an honored place in how human beings can come into proper relationship with reality. The NASA scientists moving rovers on Mars illustrate this without any supernatural scaffolding: they naturally fall into imaginal practices — anthropomorphizing the rover, techno-morphizing themselves, rotating in their chairs as if embodying the machine — not because anyone taught them to but because these are the strategies human cognition reaches for when it must perceive at a distance. And some of them report uncanny sympathetic connections between their own bodies and the rover's behavior, connections they cannot name with available vocabulary. This is not magic and it is not mere metaphor: it is participation, the imaginal augmenting perception across vast distances. The absence of this emphasis on the dialogical and the distributed in much of the Western philosophical tradition — its accentuation of the individual knower from Descartes through Locke through Kant — may be one of the deepest structural failures of modern thought.

## Apprentice

Some realities are simply too big, too slow, or too complex for any one person to grasp alone. Climate change, evolution, or even the full state of a ship at sea — these things can only be understood when many minds work together across space and time, each contributing a piece of the picture. This kind of knowing-together is called distributed cognition, and it may be one of the most important things religions have ever done.

Religious traditions have long created frameworks — the body of Christ, the Buddhist sangha, the language of spirits — that honor and organize this collective way of perceiving. They give people roles, rituals, and shared language for entering into a knowing relationship with something no individual could see on their own. This isn't superstition; it's a sophisticated epistemic technology.

A striking secular example comes from NASA scientists who remotely operate Mars rovers. Without anyone teaching them, they begin to anthropomorphize the rover, physically rotate in their chairs as if they are the machine, and some even report feeling uncanny bodily connections to the rover's state. These are the same imaginative strategies religions have cultivated for millennia. Meanwhile, Western philosophy since Descartes has largely insisted that real knowledge belongs to the solitary thinker — and this may be one of its deepest blind spots.

## Adept

This insight identifies one of religion's most overlooked epistemological functions: its capacity to scaffold distributed cognition for engaging with what Timothy Morton has termed hyperobjects — phenomena whose scale, temporal duration, or structural complexity exceeds any individual perceiver's grasp. Evolution, climate systems, and large-scale navigational states are not objects available to a single mind armed with sensory input and logical inference. They require networks of knowers distributed across space and time, coordinated through shared practices and vocabularies, entering into structured epistemic relationships with the phenomenon and with each other.

Religious traditions have historically named and valorized precisely this function. The body of Christ, the Buddhist sangha, animistic spirit-languages — these are not merely social metaphors but frameworks that organize dialogical, distributed perception and grant it legitimacy as a mode of knowing. The insight here is that these traditions constitute epistemic technologies for collective apprehension of realities that resist individuated cognition.

The case of NASA scientists operating Mars rovers provides a compelling secular parallel. Without formal instruction, operators spontaneously adopt imaginal practices — anthropomorphizing the rover, techno-morphizing their own bodies, physically rotating as if embodying the machine at a distance. Some report sympathetic somatic connections to the rover's mechanical state that resist articulation in available conceptual vocabulary. This is participatory cognition, not metaphor and not magic. It reveals that the imaginal augmentation of perception across vast distances is a natural cognitive strategy — one that religions have cultivated systematically. The Western philosophical tradition's emphasis on the individual knower, from Descartes through Kant, may represent a profound structural failure: the systematic exclusion of distributed and dialogical knowing from the canon of legitimate epistemology.

## Magus

This argument locates a deep epistemological function within religious systems that has been systematically occluded by the Western philosophical tradition's commitment to the individual cogito as the locus of knowledge. The claim is that religions have historically served as architectures for distributed cognition — coordinating networks of perceivers across space and time to apprehend what Morton terms hyperobjects: phenomena whose spatiotemporal scale or structural complexity renders them constitutively inaccessible to any single epistemic agent equipped with sensory apparatus and inferential reason. The body of Christ, the sangha, animistic spirit-ontologies — these function not as representational metaphors but as participatory frameworks that organize dialogical epistemic relations between distributed knowers and between knowers and phenomena.

The NASA rover-operation case furnishes a naturalistic demonstration. Operators spontaneously generate imaginal practices — anthropomorphizing the machine, techno-morphizing their own embodiment, enacting somatic rotations that mirror the rover's orientation — without pedagogical instruction. Some report sympathetic proprioceptive correlations with the rover's mechanical states that exceed available descriptive vocabulary. This is neither magical thinking nor decorative metaphor; it is participation in the Barfieldian sense, the imaginal faculty augmenting perception across distances that defeat direct sensory contact. These strategies are precisely what religious traditions have cultivated, codified, and transmitted across millennia.

The structural failure identified here runs from Descartes' solitary meditator through Locke's tabula rasa to Kant's transcendental subject: each instantiation reinforces the individual knower as epistemology's fundamental unit, thereby rendering distributed, dialogical, and participatory cognition invisible or illegitimate. This exclusion is not incidental but architectonic — it shapes what counts as knowledge, what counts as evidence, and what cognitive strategies receive institutional support. Recovering the epistemic dignity of distributed knowing may require precisely the kind of vocabulary that religious traditions have always provided.
