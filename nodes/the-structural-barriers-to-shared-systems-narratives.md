# The Structural Barriers to Shared Systems Narratives

A truth too heavy for the elevator.

---

> Understanding the full scope of civilizational risk requires holding many complex, interacting systems in mind at once — and human brains resist exactly that. Building shared maps of this complexity may be a prerequisite for any coordinated response.

One of the deepest challenges in communicating a systems-level understanding of the human predicament is that the story only makes sense in its full form. There is no single one-hour conversation, no elevator pitch, no viral clip that can carry the weight of what's actually happening. Every simplified version loses something essential — whether it's Jevons paradox, the relationship between energy and financial claims, the distinction between symptoms and root causes, or the way ecological, economic, and behavioral dynamics are all entangled. And this runs directly into a fundamental feature of the human brain: we are wired for certainty, not nuance. We prefer clean narratives with clear villains and simple solutions. The meta-crisis, by contrast, is irreducibly complex. It requires holding multiple interacting systems in mind simultaneously, tolerating uncertainty, and resisting the urge to collapse the picture into something more comfortable. This is not just an intellectual challenge — it's a social and political one. Grand narratives that refuse to simplify, that insist on complexity, are experienced as threatening. They destabilize existing frameworks, challenge vested interests, and demand more cognitive and emotional labor than most people are prepared to give. And yet, the work of building shared understanding across this complexity — of finding the right series of conversations, the right sequencing of concepts, the right collaborative frameworks — may be one of the most important things that people working on civilizational risk can do. Not because a better story will automatically change behavior, but because without a shared map of the territory, coordinated action toward genuinely better futures becomes nearly impossible.

## Apprentice

Some problems are too big and too tangled to fit into a short explanation. When you try to summarize them, you end up cutting out the parts that matter most — and what's left starts to feel like a different problem entirely. This is especially true for the overlapping crises humanity faces today: ecological breakdown, economic fragility, and the behavioral patterns that connect them.

The trouble is that human minds are built for simple stories. We want clear causes, obvious villains, and straightforward fixes. Complexity feels uncomfortable, even threatening. So when someone tries to explain that everything is connected and there are no easy answers, people often push back — not because they're stupid, but because the brain is wired to prefer certainty.

This creates a real practical problem. If people working on big civilizational challenges can't build a shared understanding of what's actually going on, it becomes very hard to coordinate meaningful action. Finding better ways to sequence ideas, to guide people through layers of complexity without losing them — that work may be just as important as any technical solution.

## Adept

The meta-crisis — the interlocking set of ecological, energetic, economic, and social breakdowns now converging — resists compression. Any attempt to distill it into an elevator pitch or viral narrative necessarily strips out the dynamics that give it explanatory power: Jevons paradox, the structural relationship between energy flows and financial claims, the distinction between proximate symptoms and root-cause drivers. What remains after simplification is not a simplified version of the truth but a different, misleading picture.

This creates a deep tension with how human cognition actually works. People are pattern-matchers who favor narrative closure, clear agency, and actionable conclusions. Complexity that refuses to resolve into a clean story is experienced not merely as intellectually difficult but as socially and politically threatening — it destabilizes existing frameworks, implicates systems rather than actors, and demands sustained cognitive and emotional labor. Grand narratives of irreducible complexity are therefore structurally disadvantaged in public discourse, regardless of their accuracy.

The implication is that building shared understanding across this complexity is itself a strategic priority. Not because a better conceptual map automatically produces better behavior, but because coordinated action toward systemic change requires a sufficiently shared model of the system. The challenge is pedagogical and social as much as intellectual: finding the right sequencing of concepts, the right collaborative frameworks, and the right conversational contexts to move people through layers of understanding without triggering the defensive simplifications that short-circuit the process.

## Magus

The epistemological predicament at the heart of civilizational risk communication is structural, not merely rhetorical. Systems-level accounts of the meta-crisis are characterized by non-decomposability: the explanatory force of the whole cannot be recovered from any proper subset of its parts. Excise the biophysical constraints on economic growth, and the financial dynamics become mysterious; excise the behavioral and institutional feedback loops, and the persistence of maladaptive trajectories becomes inexplicable. Every lossy compression produces not a simplified truth but a category error — a narrative that actively misdirects.

This non-decomposability collides with well-documented features of human cognition and social epistemology. Kahneman's dual-process framework, Mercier and Sperber's argumentative theory of reasoning, and the broader literature on motivated cognition all converge on the same finding: minds under uncertainty default to heuristic closure, narrative coherence, and identity-protective cognition. Complexity that refuses resolution into agentive causation and tractable intervention is not merely cognitively costly — it is socially destabilizing, threatening the legitimacy of existing institutions and the coherence of established ideological frameworks. The result is a systematic selection pressure against accurate systemic accounts in public discourse.

The practical consequence is that sensemaking infrastructure — the scaffolding of sequenced concepts, shared ontologies, and collaborative interpretive frameworks — becomes a first-order strategic variable in any serious response to civilizational risk. This is not a claim about narrative persuasion or science communication in the conventional sense. It is a claim about the preconditions for collective intelligence: that coordinated adaptive action across complex sociotechnical systems requires a sufficiently convergent model of the system among the agents attempting to act. Without that shared map, even well-resourced and well-intentioned actors will optimize locally, talk past one another, and reproduce the fragmentation that characterizes the current moment.
