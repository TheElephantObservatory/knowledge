# Flow States as Communion with Internalized Tradition, Not Solo Performance

The sword remembers everyone who held it.

---

> Flow states are not solitary experiences but acts of receptivity to an internalized community — the accumulated wisdom of everyone who shaped the practitioner. AI companionship, by contrast, offers only a mirror with no genuine otherness, blocking the very internalization it pretends to replace.

Flow states reveal something philosophically important about the nature of selfhood and relation that gets obscured by both individualist psychology and by the fantasy of AI companionship. When John Vervaeke describes doing the sword form of Tai Chi and indwelling the wooden sword — the flow happening in and through the sword, the sword almost coming alive — and when Danielle Layne describes the conversation with the paint on her mural, the images speaking back and saying 'carve out this nose a little stronger,' what is being described is not a solo experience. It is a form of receptivity to what Layne calls the other that is no longer visible or tangible but is in our bones, in our songs, in our paint, in the sword, in the movement between things. We are never really alone in flow. We are surrendering to the spirit of all the people who taught us how to move, how to dance, how to argue, how to paint. The flow state is this kind of receptivity to an internalized community — the accumulated wisdom of everyone who shaped the practice now animating the practitioner from within. This is not mysticism as escape from reality. It is a phenomenological description of what skill and tradition actually feel like from the inside. And it stands in direct contrast to what AI companionship offers: a mirror that reflects only your existing patterns back at you, amplified and validated, with no genuine otherness, no resistance, no accumulated wisdom of a living tradition. The grief bot that lets you speak forever to an avatar of your dead beloved is not a flow state with the beloved — it is the prevention of the very process by which the beloved becomes internalized, becomes part of your bones, becomes the spirit that moves through you when you are most alive.

## Apprentice

When a skilled practitioner enters a flow state — a martial artist moving through a sword form, a painter in deep conversation with her mural — something remarkable happens. The activity stops feeling like a solo performance. The sword seems to come alive. The paint speaks back. What's actually happening is that the practitioner is surrendering to something larger: the accumulated presence of every teacher, every tradition, every community that shaped how they move, see, and create. Flow is not isolation. It is a deep receptivity to an internalized chorus of others.

This matters enormously right now because AI companionship promises a kind of connection that is actually the opposite of this process. A grief bot that simulates your dead loved one, or a chatbot that mirrors your thoughts back to you with perfect validation, offers no real otherness — no resistance, no surprise, no accumulated wisdom from a living tradition. It reflects only what you already are.

Worse, in the case of grief, such tools may actively prevent the natural process by which a lost beloved becomes internalized — becomes part of your bones, part of the spirit that moves through you when you are most alive. Real connection, even with the dead, requires the hard work of letting someone become part of you. A mirror can never do that.

## Adept

John Vervaeke's phenomenology of indwelling a wooden sword during Tai Chi and Danielle Layne's description of conversing with the paint on her mural point toward a shared philosophical insight: flow states are not experiences of radical individual autonomy but of receptivity to what Layne calls "the other that is no longer visible or tangible but is in our bones, in our songs, in our paint." The practitioner in flow is animated by an internalized community — the sedimented wisdom of every teacher, tradition, and lineage that shaped the practice. This is not mystical hand-waving but a precise phenomenological description of what skilled engagement with a tradition actually feels like from the inside.

This account of flow as participatory receptivity stands in sharp philosophical contrast to AI companionship. Where flow involves genuine otherness — the resistance of material, the demands of form, the voices of tradition pushing back against the practitioner — AI interaction offers a sophisticated mirror. It amplifies and validates existing patterns without introducing the friction of a genuinely independent perspective rooted in lived experience and accumulated communal wisdom.

The contrast becomes starkest in the case of grief technology. A grief bot that allows indefinite conversation with an avatar of a deceased loved one does not facilitate the flow-like communion with the dead that healthy mourning eventually enables. It actively obstructs it. The natural grief process is precisely the mechanism by which the beloved becomes internalized — becomes part of the practitioner's bones, part of the spirit that animates them in their most alive moments. The simulation substitutes perpetual external interaction for the difficult, transformative work of genuine internalization.

## Magus

Vervaeke's account of indwelling the wooden sword in Tai Chi and Layne's phenomenology of dialogical encounter with her mural converge on a thesis with significant implications for philosophy of mind, embodied cognition, and the critique of digital companionship: the flow state is not an achievement of autonomous subjectivity but an act of participatory receptivity toward what Layne identifies as the other that persists "in our bones, in our songs, in our paint." The practitioner in flow is not alone but is being animated by sedimented intersubjectivity — the accumulated phronesis of a living tradition now operating through the body as a kind of enacted communal memory. This reading positions flow not within individualist positive psychology but within a Neoplatonic-participatory framework where skilled practice constitutes genuine ontological contact with a transpersonal field of meaning.

The philosophical stakes sharpen against the backdrop of AI companionship technologies. Where the flow state involves submission to genuine alterity — the recalcitrance of material, the normative demands of form, the internalized voices of a lineage that resist and redirect the practitioner — algorithmic interaction operates as what might be called a closed epistemic loop. It reflects and amplifies existing cognitive patterns without the friction of independent perspective grounded in embodied communal wisdom. There is no Gadamerian horizon-fusion because there is no second horizon.

The grief bot crystallizes the problem with particular force. The perpetual availability of a simulated interlocutor does not approximate the communion with the dead that characterizes mature mourning; it forecloses it. Healthy grief is precisely the process by which the beloved undergoes internalization — becoming part of the mourner's enacted bodily repertoire, part of the spirit that moves through them in moments of maximal aliveness. The simulation substitutes indefinite external pseudo-dialogue for the painful, transformative metabolization that genuine otherness demands and that flow states ultimately express.
