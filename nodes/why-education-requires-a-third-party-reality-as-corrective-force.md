# Why Education Requires a Third Party: Reality as Corrective Force

The scaffold was never meant to be a wall.

---

> Real education differs from propaganda in a structural way: the teacher's authority is designed to dissolve, both parties answer to an independent reality, and the learner is meant to surpass the teacher. Digital environments threaten this by removing the shared reference point that keeps education honest.

One of the most reliable markers distinguishing education from propaganda is what we might call the unbridgeable epistemic asymmetry. In legitimate educational relationships, the teacher's authority is intended to make itself obsolete. The teacher looks over their shoulder at the same text the student is reading, grapples with it alongside them, and hopes the student will eventually surpass their understanding. The asymmetry of knowledge that grants teacherly authority is held provisionally — it is a scaffold, not a wall. Propaganda inverts this. It installs a permanent barrier between what the receiver can ever access and what the transmitter knows. The ideology is structured so that its simplifications cannot be questioned from within, and the thought-terminating clichés it offers are not invitations to inquiry but substitutes for it. There is also a third party in genuine education that is absent in propaganda: reality itself. Teacher and student are cooperating to understand something real — a phenomenon that exists independently of both of them, that can confirm or disconfirm their interpretations, that can make the teacher wrong in front of the student. This is what makes education self-correcting. The digital simulation environment is particularly dangerous here because it removes that third party. Two people arguing on social media are often arguing about the simulation, inside the simulation, with no shared handhold on anything outside it. The person you are addressing may not even be a person. In that context, the very conditions for legitimate teacherly authority dissolve.

## Apprentice

There is a useful test for telling education apart from propaganda. In genuine teaching, the teacher wants to become unnecessary. They know more than the student for now, but that gap is temporary — a ladder the student is meant to climb and eventually leave behind. In propaganda, the gap is permanent by design. The audience is never supposed to catch up, never supposed to question the simplified story they've been given.

But there's something even more important: in real education, both teacher and student are accountable to something outside themselves — the world, the evidence, the phenomenon they're trying to understand. That outside reality can prove the teacher wrong in front of the student, and that vulnerability is what keeps the whole process honest and self-correcting. Propaganda has no such check. It answers only to itself.

This matters urgently in digital spaces. Online, people often argue about representations of reality rather than reality itself, inside a system that offers no shared anchor to anything outside it. The person on the other end of the conversation might not even be a real person. Without that third party — the world itself, pushing back — the conditions that make honest teaching possible simply disappear.

## Adept

This insight draws a structural distinction between education and propaganda based on what might be called epistemic asymmetry and its intended trajectory. In authentic pedagogical relationships, the teacher's superior knowledge is provisional — a scaffold erected with the explicit purpose of being dismantled. The teacher's authority is self-liquidating; it succeeds precisely when the student no longer needs it. Propaganda inverts this architecture. It installs a permanent epistemic barrier, deploying thought-terminating clichés not as heuristic aids but as substitutes for inquiry, ensuring the receiver can never access the transmitter's actual epistemic position.

The deeper structural point concerns a crucial third party: reality itself. Genuine education is triadic — teacher, student, and an independent phenomenon that both are trying to understand. This third vertex disciplines the relationship because it can disconfirm the teacher's claims, making the teacher fallible in the student's presence. This fallibility is not a defect but the mechanism of self-correction. Propaganda is dyadic — transmitter and receiver with no external referent capable of generating disconfirmation.

The digital simulation environment intensifies this problem dramatically. Online discourse frequently operates entirely within a self-referential loop: arguments about representations, conducted inside the representational system, with no shared purchase on anything outside it. The interlocutor may be algorithmically generated. Under these conditions, the triadic structure of education collapses. Without an independent reality functioning as a shared constraint, the provisional authority that characterizes legitimate teaching cannot be established, and the distinction between education and propaganda becomes functionally undetectable.

## Magus

The taxonomy proposed here rests on a phenomenological distinction between two configurations of epistemic asymmetry. In the pedagogical configuration, asymmetry is diachronically self-negating: the teacher's authority is indexed to a knowledge differential whose telos is its own dissolution. The teacher and student share orientation toward the same object — they look over their shoulders at the same text — and the teacher's interpretive advantage is held under the normative expectation of eventual supersession. This is authority as scaffold. In the propagandistic configuration, asymmetry is synchronically stabilized: the transmitter's epistemic position is structurally inaccessible to the receiver, and the discursive apparatus — thought-terminating clichés, unfalsifiable simplifications — functions precisely to foreclose the recursive questioning that would erode the differential.

The more consequential claim concerns the triadic ontology of genuine education. Teacher and student are not merely in relation to each other but jointly in relation to a mind-independent phenomenon — a third vertex that exercises what might be called epistemic sovereignty over both parties. This third party can generate disconfirmation, rendering the teacher wrong coram discipulo, and it is this vulnerability that constitutes the self-correcting mechanism of legitimate pedagogy. Propaganda is structurally dyadic; it admits no external adjudicator.

The digital environment represents a novel epistemic threat because it systematically evacuates this third vertex. Discourse conducted within simulation — about the simulation, referencing only other simulations — operates without shared purchase on any extra-systemic referent. The interlocutor's ontological status is itself underdetermined. Under these conditions, the triadic structure collapses into a dyad indistinguishable from the propagandistic configuration, and the normative conditions underwriting provisional teacherly authority — fallibility before a shared real — become structurally unrealizable.
