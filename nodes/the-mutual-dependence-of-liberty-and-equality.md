# The Mutual Dependence of Liberty and Equality

Beyond the cold math of experts

---

> Danielle Allen argues that equality and liberty are not rivals but depend on each other — and that democracy itself, not courts or experts, is the true mechanism of justice.

Danielle Allen represents a crucial corrective to a dominant tendency in modern liberal political thought, which has so thoroughly privileged liberty that it has allowed equality to atrophy as a living political value. Her book Our Declaration makes the argument — grounded in a close, almost philological reading of the Declaration of Independence — that equality and liberty are not in tension but are co-constitutive. The Declaration does not subordinate equality to liberty; it presents them as mutually dependent. And yet the dominant tradition of liberal political philosophy, from Locke through much of contemporary libertarianism, has treated liberty as the master value and equality as, at best, a constraint on it. Allen's correction matters not just as a historical or textual point but as a philosophical and political one: a democracy that loses its grip on equality as a substantive value — not merely formal equality before the law, but genuine egalitarianism in the conditions of life and participation — is a democracy that is hollowing itself out. Her later work, Justice by Means of Democracy, extends this into a full account of how democratic practice is itself the mechanism of justice — not merely the arena in which justice is pursued, but the very means by which it is achieved. This is a deeply important idea because it refuses the technocratic temptation to outsource justice to experts, courts, or algorithms, and insists instead that the hard, antagonistic, cooperative work of democratic conversation is irreplaceable.

## Apprentice

Most people assume that freedom and equality pull in opposite directions — give people more liberty and you get less equality, or enforce equality and you restrict freedom. Danielle Allen challenges this assumption directly. Reading the Declaration of Independence with unusual care, she shows that its authors did not treat equality as a limit on freedom but as its foundation. The two values hold each other up.

This matters beyond history. Allen's argument is that a society which lets equality fade — not just legal equality, but real equality in how people live and participate in public life — is quietly undermining its own democracy. A democracy needs equality to function, not just as a nice ideal but as working political reality.

Her later work pushes further: justice is not something delivered by judges, technocrats, or clever systems. It is something that democratic communities produce through the difficult, sometimes contentious work of talking and deciding together. That process cannot be outsourced. The argument is a direct challenge to the modern habit of handing hard political questions to experts and courts, and a reminder that self-governance is irreplaceable.

## Adept

Liberal political philosophy has long operated with an implicit hierarchy of values in which liberty functions as the master concept and equality appears as either a derivative principle or a constraint. From Locke's natural equality as a precondition for individual rights through Nozick's libertarianism, equality tends to be formalized and then bracketed. Danielle Allen's reading of the Declaration of Independence in Our Declaration disrupts this hierarchy. Through close textual and rhetorical analysis, she demonstrates that the Declaration presents equality and liberty as co-constitutive rather than competing: neither can be fully realized without the other.

The philosophical stakes extend well beyond originalism or textual interpretation. Allen's claim is that substantive equality — equality in the conditions of participation and social life, not merely formal equality before the law — is a necessary condition for democratic legitimacy. A polity that retains the procedural architecture of democracy while allowing deep inequalities to erode genuine participation is engaged in a slow form of self-dissolution.

Her subsequent work, Justice by Means of Democracy, develops this into a positive political theory. Justice is not an external standard applied to democratic outcomes by courts or technocratic institutions; it is immanent to democratic practice itself. The antagonistic and cooperative labor of democratic deliberation is the mechanism through which justice is produced. This position carries a strong anti-technocratic implication: delegating political judgment to experts or algorithms does not refine democracy but displaces it.

## Magus

The subordination of equality to liberty within the liberal canon is not merely a contingent historical preference but reflects a deep structural feature of rights-based political theory: liberty, construed as non-interference or self-ownership, admits of individualist grounding, while equality requires relational and social specification that sits uneasily with atomistic foundations. Danielle Allen's intervention in Our Declaration is to contest this structure at its historical root. Her philological and rhetorical reconstruction of the Declaration of Independence argues that the document's logical architecture renders equality prior to, or at minimum co-original with, liberty — not as a constraint on rights but as the condition of their intelligibility within a self-governing political community.

The theoretical consequence is significant. If equality is co-constitutive rather than derivative, then the standard liberal move of satisfying egalitarian demands through formal procedural equality — equality before the law, equal basic liberties — is insufficient on its own terms, not merely by external egalitarian critique. Allen's egalitarianism is substantive: it concerns the actual conditions under which persons can exercise political agency and participate in collective self-determination. Democratic legitimacy, on this account, is indexed to the quality of those conditions, not merely to the presence of formal institutions.

Justice by Means of Democracy extends this into an immanentist theory of justice in which democratic praxis is not the arena but the generative mechanism of just outcomes. This places Allen in productive tension with both Rawlsian constructivism, which treats justice as a standard prior to and regulative of democratic procedure, and with deliberative democratic theories that risk aestheticizing discourse while leaving institutional power unaddressed. Her insistence on the irreducibility of democratic labor — its agonistic, cooperative, and irreplaceable character — constitutes a principled resistance to the technocratic displacement of political judgment.
