# Three Paradigms for What the Subtle Body Actually Is

Real relation, not real thing.

---

> There are three competing ways to understand subtle bodies: as a hidden physical force, as a cultural construction, or — following John Vervaeke — as an imaginally augmented realization of the fact that embodiment is neither subjective nor objective but a real relation that can be cultivated.

There are three competing paradigms for understanding subtle bodies and subtle energy, and they map onto genuinely different ontological commitments. The first holds that there is a real, physically detectable super-force — something like bioelectricity — that underlies all the cultural variations, and that rigorous science should be able to isolate and measure it. The second holds that subtle bodies are socially constructed ontologies, that the historical and mythopoetic frameworks are not windows onto something behind them but are themselves the phenomenon, and that attempts to get behind the narrative are doomed. The third — which John Vervaeke identifies as his own position — is that the subtle body is an imaginally augmented realization of the inherent transjectivity of embodiment. Transjectivity here means that embodiment is neither purely subjective nor purely objective but oscillates between and across those poles in ways that destabilize both of the other accounts. This third paradigm explains why the subtle body has such a strange ontology — why it seems real to the person experiencing it and yet resists third-person measurement — because it is a real relation, not a real thing. It explains the plethora of cultural metaphors — music, harmony, animal spirits, blessing, chi, prana — because those metaphors are tracking the dynamics of transjectivity, not naming a fixed physical entity. It explains why embodiment is so central to every tradition that works with this material. And it opens onto a relational ontology that Vervaeke argues is increasingly supported by evidence across multiple fields. The practical upshot is that when an imaginally augmented realization of transjectivity is properly ritualized — when it can be shared, repeated, and woven through the levels of the psyche and the kinds of knowing — it becomes a powerful vehicle for enhancing meaning, wisdom, and well-being. The subtle body, on this account, is not a thing out there to be detected; it is a real relation that can be cultivated, and cultivation is what makes it religiously and therapeutically significant.

## Apprentice

When people talk about subtle energy — chi, prana, animal spirits, or the feeling of aliveness in the body — there are basically three ways to make sense of what's going on. The first says there's a real physical force science hasn't fully measured yet, something like bioelectricity. The second says these are cultural stories, and the stories themselves are the whole phenomenon — there's nothing hidden behind them to discover.

John Vervaeke proposes a third option. He argues that the subtle body is a real relation rather than a real thing. Our embodiment isn't purely private (just in your head) or purely physical (just measurable matter). It constantly moves between those poles. Vervaeke calls this "transjectivity." The subtle body is what you get when that in-between quality of being embodied is heightened and enriched through imagination and practice.

This explains a lot. It explains why subtle-body experiences feel genuinely real to the person having them but can't be pinned down by a lab instrument. It explains why so many cultures use different metaphors — music, breath, harmony, blessing — because they're each tracking the same dynamic relationship, not naming a single hidden object. And it explains why these traditions always emphasize practice: the subtle body isn't waiting to be found, it's a relationship that deepens through cultivation, which is exactly what makes it meaningful and transformative.

## Adept

Three paradigms compete for explanatory authority over subtle bodies and subtle energy. The first is physicalist-reductionist: it posits a real super-force — bioelectricity, biophotons, or some yet-to-be-isolated field — that underlies every cultural variation and should ultimately yield to empirical measurement. The second is social-constructionist: subtle bodies are mythopoetic ontologies, and the cultural narratives are not windows onto a deeper reality but are themselves the entire phenomenon. Any attempt to get behind the metaphor is a category error.

John Vervaeke advances a third paradigm grounded in what he calls transjectivity — the claim that embodiment is irreducibly neither subjective nor objective but oscillates across those poles in ways that destabilize both reductionism and constructionism. On this account, the subtle body is an imaginally augmented realization of that transjectivity. It is a real relation, not a real thing. This distinction does significant philosophical work: it explains why subtle-body experience resists third-person measurement without being merely imaginary, and why the profusion of cultural metaphors — chi, prana, harmony, animal spirits, blessing — converges without reducing to a single referent. Each metaphor tracks the dynamics of transjectivity rather than naming a fixed physical entity.

The practical consequence is substantial. When this imaginally augmented realization is properly ritualized — made shareable, repeatable, and integrated across participatory, perspectival, procedural, and propositional knowing — it becomes a vehicle for meaning-making, wisdom, and well-being. The subtle body's religious and therapeutic significance lies not in detection but in cultivation: it is a relational reality that deepens through disciplined practice, and Vervaeke argues this relational ontology finds increasing convergent support across cognitive science, phenomenology, and ecological psychology.

## Magus

The ontological status of subtle bodies admits of three genuinely distinct paradigms, each carrying irreducible commitments. The physicalist paradigm posits a monistic super-force — bioelectromagnetic, biophotonic, or otherwise — subtending cultural variation and in principle amenable to third-person operationalization. The social-constructionist paradigm treats subtle-body ontologies as mythopoetic achievements: the narrative frameworks are not epistemic instruments disclosing a hidden referent but are themselves constitutive of the phenomenon, rendering any attempt to penetrate behind them self-defeating. Both paradigms stabilize the subject-object dichotomy — the first by collapsing the subtle body into the objective pole, the second by dissolving it into the socio-discursive.

John Vervaeke's third paradigm refuses this stabilization. Drawing on his account of transjectivity — the thesis that embodiment is constitutively neither subjective nor objective but oscillates across and between those poles — he identifies the subtle body as an imaginally augmented realization of embodiment's transjective character. The ontological strangeness of subtle bodies — their phenomenological robustness coupled with their resistance to reification — is thereby explained rather than explained away: what is at stake is a real relation, not a real substance. The heterogeneity of cultural metaphors (chi, prana, rūaḥ, harmonia, animal spirits) reflects not terminological confusion but the irreducible dynamism of transjectivity, which no single metaphor can totalize. Each tradition's insistence on embodied practice is thus not incidental but constitutive.

The paradigm's pragmatic yield is that when transjective realization is ritualized — rendered iterable, intersubjectively shareable, and integrated across the four kinds of knowing (participatory, perspectival, procedural, propositional) — it becomes a potent vehicle for the cultivation of meaning, wisdom, and flourishing. Vervaeke contends this relational ontology finds convergent warrant in 4E cognitive science, ecological psychology, and process-relational metaphysics, positioning the subtle body not as an anachronism awaiting debunking but as a lived enactment of a post-Cartesian understanding of mind and world.
