# Declining Interoception and the Somatic Roots of the Meaning Crisis

The body forgot before the mind did.

---

> Modern culture's loss of body-awareness is not a side effect of the meaning crisis but a structural cause. Without felt connection to the body's interior, the deeper flows of meaning become inaccessible — and no amount of seated mindfulness or propositional thinking can restore what only somatic practice can reopen.

Interoception — the felt sense of the body's interior — is declining across the population, and this decline is not incidental to the meaning crisis but structurally coupled to it. The tyranny of the propositional is not only a cognitive or linguistic phenomenon; it is a somatic one. When cognition becomes dominated by noun-based, object-property thinking — by the products of thought rather than the living process that generates them — the body recedes from awareness. And without the body, the deeper flows of meaning that Bohm called the holomovement become inaccessible. What Bohm recognized on his deathbed, in conversation with Lee Nichol ten days before he died, was that dialogue alone was insufficient: people needed preparatory somatic practices to ready their bodies for genuine participatory thinking. The RheoSoma practices that followed — attending to the moving body, aligning with the flows of fire, clouds, trees, rivers, and then attending to the micro-flows of meaning unfolding in speech — are an attempt to restore that lost continuity between bodily flow and cognitive flow. This connects to Bill Torbert's framework of triple-loop awareness and three-dimensional time: the capacity to inhabit a present that is poised between past and future, participating in the unfolding of orders and meanings rather than merely observing them sequentially. The reduction of mindfulness to seated breath meditation, stripped of the body and of the metacognitive attention to how attention itself works, has produced a domesticated version of contemplative practice — one that, in its corporate form, functions not to liberate but to make people more tolerant of the conditions generating the crisis. A revisioned religion must recover the full somatic-contemplative progression: from interoception, through attunement with larger flows, through participatory dialogue, to the kind of knowing that can actually transform the knower.

## Apprentice

Most people today have a weakened sense of what's happening inside their own bodies — the subtle feelings of tension, flow, energy, and aliveness that once informed how we made sense of the world. This isn't just a health issue. It's deeply connected to why so many people struggle to find genuine meaning in their lives. When thinking becomes all about fixed ideas and abstract categories, the living body drops out of the picture, and with it goes a whole dimension of knowing.

David Bohm, the physicist and philosopher, came to recognize this near the end of his life. He had spent decades developing dialogue as a practice for collective thinking, but he realized that people needed bodily preparation before they could truly participate. They needed to feel the flows in their own bodies first — the way fire moves, rivers run, clouds shift — and then notice those same qualities of flow in how meaning unfolds during conversation.

This insight also challenges what mindfulness has become in mainstream culture. Stripped down to quiet sitting and breath-counting, corporate mindfulness often helps people merely cope with stressful conditions rather than question them. A fuller practice would move from body awareness, through attunement with natural rhythms, into genuine dialogue — the kind of knowing that doesn't just inform the mind but transforms the whole person.

## Adept

The decline of interoception — the capacity to sense the body's internal states — is structurally coupled to the meaning crisis, not merely correlated with it. When cognition falls under what can be called the tyranny of the propositional, dominated by noun-based, object-property frameworks that privilege the products of thought over its living process, the body recedes from awareness. This is not only a cognitive narrowing but a somatic one. Without access to felt bodily flow, the deeper participatory knowing that Bohm described through his concept of the holomovement becomes functionally inaccessible.

Bohm himself came to this recognition in his final days. In conversation with Lee Nichol just ten days before his death, he acknowledged that dialogue practice alone was insufficient — participants needed preparatory somatic practices to ready their bodies for genuine thinking-together. The RheoSoma practices that emerged from this lineage address exactly this gap: attending first to the moving body, then aligning perception with natural flows — fire, water, clouds, trees — and finally tracking the micro-flows of meaning as they unfold in live speech. This progression restores continuity between bodily flow and cognitive flow.

This connects meaningfully to Bill Torbert's framework of triple-loop awareness and three-dimensional time: the capacity to inhabit a present poised between past and future, participating in the emergence of meaning rather than observing it sequentially. The reduction of mindfulness to seated breath meditation — particularly in its corporate domestication — strips away both the somatic depth and the metacognitive reflexivity that contemplative practice requires. A revisioned contemplative path must recover the full progression: interoception, attunement, participatory dialogue, and transformative knowing.

## Magus

The contemporary erosion of interoceptive capacity is not epiphenomenal to the meaning crisis but constitutive of it, operating through a structural coupling between somatic recession and propositional dominance. The tyranny of the propositional — cognition colonized by noun-based, object-property schemata that reify the products of thought while occluding its generative process — is simultaneously a linguistic, cognitive, and somatic phenomenon. As the body withdraws from the field of awareness, access to what Bohm theorized as the holomovement — the undivided flowing wholeness prior to the explicate order — becomes functionally foreclosed. Meaning, severed from its somatic ground, degrades into information.

Bohm's own trajectory enacts this recognition. His late acknowledgment to Lee Nichol, ten days before his death, that dialogue practice required preparatory somatic work marks a critical inflection point: the admission that participatory sense-making presupposes a body already attuned to flow. The RheoSoma practices that developed from this insight instantiate a phenomenological progression — from interoceptive recovery, through perceptual alignment with macroscopic flow patterns in natural phenomena, to the fine-grained tracking of meaning's micro-unfolding in dialogical speech — that recapitulates the movement from Bohm's implicate order toward participatory enactment.

Torbert's framework of triple-loop awareness and three-dimensional time provides a complementary architecture: the cultivation of a present that is not a knife-edge between past and future but a participatory field in which temporal orders interpenetrate. The domestication of mindfulness into seated breath-focused practice, stripped of somatic depth and metacognitive recursion, represents precisely the kind of reduction that renders contemplative technology complicit with the conditions it might otherwise transform. What is required is the recovery of a full somatic-contemplative progression capable of producing not merely altered states but transformed knowers — subjects reconstituted through their own participatory engagement with the flows of meaning.
