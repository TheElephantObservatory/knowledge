# Why Intentional Communities Must Be Designed Around Children and Security

What the 23-year-old doesn't know civilization is for

---

> Intentional communities dominated by young, unattached people tend to optimize for freedom and self-expression while neglecting civilization's core function: raising children well and guaranteeing no member falls through the floor. Childcare and housing security must be structural commitments, not afterthoughts.

One of the underappreciated failure modes of intentional communities — and of social change movements more broadly — is the demographic skew toward single young people who have not yet encountered the full weight of what civilization is actually for. When the median participant in a movement is 23 and unattached, the implicit optimization targets of that movement tend to reflect that life stage: personal freedom, resistance to authority, self-expression, social experimentation. These are not worthless values, but they are incomplete ones. The deepest purpose of any durable social operating system is the intergenerational transmission of culture — the raising of children into excellent future citizens, in ways that don't destroy the opportunities of the people doing the raising, particularly women. Any proto-B community that doesn't have this at the center of its design is probably wrongheaded. This means child care must be a built-in, first-class service — not an afterthought, not something individual families solve privately, but a structural commitment of the community equivalent in status and compensation to any other skilled profession. It also means the community must provide a baseline guarantee: no member, and no member's children, will ever be homeless. This is what extended families used to provide before geographic atomization dissolved them. The proto-B is in part an attempt to reconstruct that fallback security — the knowledge that no matter what happens to you economically, the community holds you — without necessarily replicating the neurotic entanglements that biological extended families sometimes produce.

## Apprentice

Most intentional communities and social movements are disproportionately filled with young, single people. There's nothing wrong with being young and single, but when that demographic dominates, the community's priorities naturally skew toward personal freedom, self-expression, and experimentation — values that matter but don't capture the full picture of what a lasting society needs to do.

The deeper purpose of any durable community is raising children into capable, flourishing people — without destroying the lives of those doing the raising, especially women. This insight argues that any serious community must treat childcare as a built-in, professional-grade service, not something families figure out on their own. It should carry the same status and compensation as any other skilled role.

Equally important is a bedrock guarantee: no member, and no member's child, will ever be homeless. Extended families used to provide this kind of safety net before people scattered across the map. The idea here is to reconstruct that fallback security — the deep knowledge that your community will catch you if you fall — without necessarily recreating the complicated dynamics that biological families sometimes bring along.

## Adept

A persistent failure mode of intentional communities is demographic capture by young, unattached participants whose life stage biases the community's implicit optimization targets toward personal autonomy, anti-authoritarianism, and social experimentation. These values are real but partial. The argument is that any community aspiring to civilizational durability must center itself on the intergenerational transmission of culture — specifically, on the problem of raising children into excellent future citizens without extracting ruinous costs from caregivers, particularly women.

This reframing has concrete structural implications. Childcare must be a first-class community service: professionally compensated, institutionally supported, and equivalent in status to any other skilled function. It cannot be relegated to the private sphere where individual families absorb its costs asymmetrically. Similarly, the community must provide a baseline guarantee against destitution — no member or member's child will ever lack housing. This is the functional equivalent of what extended kinship networks historically provided before geographic atomization dissolved them.

The proto-B community concept, as articulated here, is an attempt to reconstruct the fallback security of the extended family — the existential insurance that someone will hold you if everything else fails — while deliberately avoiding the enmeshment pathologies that biological kinship structures often produce. The design challenge is engineering that unconditional safety net within a community of voluntary association rather than blood obligation. Communities that skip this problem in favor of lifestyle experimentation are, by this analysis, probably wrongheaded from the start.

## Magus

The critique identifies a structural selection effect in intentional communities and social movements: when the modal participant is young, single, and pre-parental, the community's revealed preference function converges on liberty, self-expression, and resistance to constraint — values isomorphic to the developmental priorities of early adulthood rather than to the requirements of civilizational reproduction. The claim is not that these values are illegitimate but that they are radically incomplete as a social operating system. The irreducible function of any durable polity is intergenerational cultural transmission: the formation of children into competent successors, executed in a manner that does not systematically expropriate the labor and opportunity of caregivers — a cost historically borne disproportionately by women.

Two structural commitments follow as design axioms. First, childcare must be endogenous to the community's institutional architecture — a compensated, high-status profession rather than a privatized externality absorbed by individual households. Second, the community must guarantee a floor: no member or dependent faces homelessness under any economic contingency. This is functionally the insurance product that extended kinship networks provided prior to geographic atomization — unconditional material security rooted in membership rather than market position.

The proto-B concept thus positions itself as a deliberate reconstruction of extended-family fallback architecture within a framework of voluntary association. The design problem is non-trivial: replicating the unconditional holding environment of kin-based solidarity while excising the pathological enmeshment, obligation asymmetries, and exit costs that characterize biological extended families. Communities that fail to center this problem — that treat it as downstream of lifestyle or ideological concerns — are, on this analysis, optimizing for the wrong objective function entirely.
