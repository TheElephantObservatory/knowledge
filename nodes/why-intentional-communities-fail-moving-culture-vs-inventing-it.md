# Why Intentional Communities Fail: Moving Culture vs. Inventing It

You cannot engineer a people from scratch.

---

> Intentional communities fail because they try to invent culture from scratch rather than transplant or revive it. What these projects actually need — shared liturgy, a hierarchy of values, and ritual scaffolding — is functionally what religion provides, and refusing to acknowledge this is a primary reason they collapse.

One of the deepest lessons from failed attempts to build intentional communities is the distinction between moving a culture and innovating a culture. When historical communities migrated — Puritan settlements, Greek colonies — they carried their entire cultural architecture with them: the tools the blacksmith used, the rituals for dealing with death and marriage and conflict, the hierarchy of values, the liturgical practices that bound people together. These were not invented; they were transplanted. And even then, it took approximately six generations to build a functional cultural toolkit capable of surviving in a new environment. Contemporary intentional community projects attempt something categorically harder: they try to innovate at the level of culture itself, starting from the dysfunctional cultural material of people formed by late-stage cosmopolitan urbanism. Getting people who have been formed by a culture of minimum viable morality and anonymous market-and-state relationships to achieve genuine communion — the kind that holds under real stress, that shows up at funerals, that puts the addict in the attic — requires not just shared values but shared practices, shared history, and shared stakes that cannot be fabricated from scratch in a single generation. The implication is that the path to viable small-scale community is not invention but revivification: finding existing cultural root systems that are wilted but not dead, and pouring water on them rather than trying to grow new plants from engineered seeds. This connects directly to what religion, properly understood, actually provides: three distinct and mutually necessary functions that secular culture has largely lost the vocabulary to discuss. The first is liturgy — from the Greek meaning 'work together' — the embodied, cognitive, and behavioral practices through which people are woven into groups with a shared identity. The second is a hierarchy of values — what you actually worship in the functional sense, meaning what sits at the top of your value stack and governs your tradeoffs when things conflict. In cosmopolitan urbanism, the market and state project a flattened, tepid hierarchy that produces groups cohering around thin shared aesthetics but dissolving when anything genuinely difficult arises. The third is ritual: the scaffolding that makes it practically possible for people to live according to their values and respond to reality's impingements — war, famine, death, madness — without the community unraveling. The secular progressive project has largely attempted to retain the benefits of community while discarding the mechanisms that produce it. The civium project, properly understood, requires confronting the fact that what it is trying to build is, in the functional sense, a religion — and that the failure to acknowledge this has been one of the primary reasons these projects have failed.

## Apprentice

When people throughout history wanted to start a new community in a new place, they didn't invent a new culture — they packed up their existing one and moved it. Puritans brought their prayers, their trades, their ways of handling death and conflict. Even so, it took roughly six generations to fully adapt. Today's intentional communities attempt something much harder: they try to build a culture from scratch, using people shaped by modern urban life — people trained to relate through markets and institutions rather than deep personal bonds.

The core problem is that real community requires three things that modern secular life has largely abandoned. First, shared practices that bind people together — working, singing, eating, grieving as a group. Second, a clear ranking of what matters most, so that when hard choices arise, everyone knows what comes first. Third, rituals that help people face life's worst moments — death, addiction, disaster — without the group falling apart.

The insight is that these three elements are precisely what religion has historically provided. Intentional communities that refuse to recognize this end up trying to enjoy the fruits of deep community while rejecting the roots that produce it. The more promising path isn't to engineer new cultural systems but to find existing traditions that are weakened but still alive, and nurture them back to health.

## Adept

A crucial distinction separates historical community formation from contemporary intentional community projects: the difference between moving a culture and innovating one. Greek colonies and Puritan settlements transplanted entire cultural architectures — craft knowledge, conflict-resolution norms, liturgical practices, hierarchies of value — and even then required approximately six generations to produce a fully functional cultural toolkit adapted to new conditions. Modern intentional communities attempt something categorically more difficult: cultural innovation from the ground up, using human material formed by late-stage cosmopolitan urbanism, where relationships are mediated by anonymous market-and-state mechanisms and moral expectations hover at a contractual minimum.

The argument identifies three irreducible functions that viable communities require, functions historically bundled under the category of religion. Liturgy — embodied collective practices that weave individuals into a group with shared identity. A hierarchy of values — a functional ordering of commitments that governs tradeoffs under pressure, as opposed to the flattened aesthetic affinities that characterize urban social clusters. And ritual — the behavioral scaffolding that enables communities to absorb reality's impingements (death, madness, famine, addiction) without disintegrating.

The secular progressive project has systematically attempted to retain communal goods while discarding these generative mechanisms, treating them as vestigial rather than structural. The implication is that the path forward is not invention but revivification: identifying cultural root systems that are wilted but not dead and cultivating them, rather than attempting to grow new plants from engineered seeds. Any serious civium project must confront the fact that what it is functionally building is a religion — and that the persistent refusal to acknowledge this has been among the primary causes of failure.

## Magus

The genealogy of failed intentional communities reveals a categorical confusion between cultural translocation and cultural innovation. Historical precedents — Hellenic apoikiai, Puritan plantations, Anabaptist settlements — involved the transplantation of intact cultural architectures: craft epistemologies, liturgical calendars, adjudicatory norms, eschatological frameworks. Even under these favorable conditions, the ethnographic evidence suggests approximately six generations were required to produce a cultural toolkit capable of autonomous reproduction in novel environments. Contemporary projects operate under radically different constraints: they attempt de novo cultural synthesis from human material formed within the minimum-viable-morality regime of cosmopolitan urbanism, where social cohesion is outsourced to market pricing and state enforcement and genuine communion — the kind that absorbs psychotic breaks, buries the dead, and houses the addict — has no institutional substrate.

The analysis decomposes religion's communal function into three irreducible and mutually necessary components. Liturgy (leitourgia, 'public work'): the embodied cognitive-behavioral practices through which individual identity is subordinated to and constituted by collective identity. Hierarchical axiology: a functional worship-structure — what actually occupies the summit of the value stack and governs tradeoff resolution under existential pressure, as distinct from the flattened preference-orderings projected by liberal market culture. Ritual scaffolding: the practiced repertoires that enable communities to metabolize reality's impingements — war, famine, death, madness — without structural dissolution.

The secular progressive wager — that communal goods can be retained while their generative mechanisms are discarded as superstitious residue — stands refuted by the empirical record of intentional community failure. The viable path is revivification rather than invention: identifying cultural root systems that are dormant but not necrotic and providing conditions for regrowth. Any civium project that refuses to recognize itself as functionally religious is, in this framing, structurally incapable of producing what it promises.
