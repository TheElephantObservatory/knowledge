# Complexity Increases Over Time, But Progress Is Not Guaranteed

Directionality is a project, not a promise

---

> History may trend toward greater complexity while energy gradients persist, but this directionality guarantees nothing for any particular species or civilization. Human general intelligence and cultural evolution are genuinely new — and the trajectory ahead is not a destiny but a project.

The question of whether there is a directionality to deep history — a telos built into the structure of reality — deserves a careful empirical answer rather than either enthusiastic affirmation or reflexive dismissal. There is a reasonable argument that complexity tends to increase over time, at least while there is a net energy flux from stars providing the thermodynamic gradient that makes complexity possible. In ten trillion years when the stars burn out, we might see a reversal. But for now, the trend is plausible. However, this cannot be counted on to save any particular evolutionary branch. Evolution does not always move forward. The asteroid that ended the dinosaurs was an exogenous regression. And even without catastrophe, there is no guarantee of forward movement — homo sapiens have ten percent smaller brains than Cro-Magnon humans thirty thousand years ago, possibly because soft agricultural living relaxed the selection pressure for intelligence. The whales evolved from cattle-like ancestors back into the sea — is that forward, backward, or sideways? The honest answer is it's just different. What is genuinely new and significant is that humans are the first species in our evolutionary lineage to cross the threshold into general intelligence and cultural evolution — a mode of adaptation that is both very fast and very powerful. Cultural evolution can produce miraculous outcomes and can also produce self-destruction from a single wrong move. Stewart Brand's formulation is apt: we now have the power of gods and we better get good at it. The metacrisis is real. Worst case is extinction of all life. More likely bad case is collapse of advanced civilization and regression to warlordism. But the power to avoid those outcomes also exists. The directionality of history is not guaranteed — it is a project.

## Apprentice

Does history have a direction? There's a reasonable case that complexity has increased over deep time — atoms became molecules, molecules became cells, cells became organisms, organisms became societies. This trend seems to hold as long as stars pour energy into the universe, creating the conditions for complex things to emerge. But this broad pattern doesn't protect any particular branch of life. The asteroid that killed the dinosaurs was a brutal reset. Even without catastrophe, evolution doesn't always move "forward" — human brains have actually shrunk about ten percent over the last thirty thousand years, possibly because settled agricultural life reduced the pressure to be maximally clever.

What is genuinely unprecedented is that humans crossed a threshold into general intelligence and cultural evolution — a way of adapting that is extraordinarily fast and powerful compared to biological evolution. As Stewart Brand put it, we now have the power of gods and we'd better get good at it.

This power cuts both ways. Cultural evolution can produce miraculous breakthroughs, but a single catastrophic mistake could end advanced civilization or even all life. The worst outcomes are real possibilities, not science fiction. But so is the capacity to navigate past them. The direction of history isn't something we can sit back and count on — it's something that has to be actively built and defended.

## Adept

The question of teleology in deep history — whether reality has a built-in directionality toward greater complexity — deserves empirical scrutiny rather than ideological commitment. A defensible case exists that complexity increases over time, driven by thermodynamic gradients sustained by stellar energy flux. But this trend operates on cosmological timescales and offers no guarantees to particular lineages. The fossil record is littered with exogenous regressions (the K-Pg extinction) and ambiguous trajectories (cetacean re-adaptation to marine environments). Even within the human lineage, encephalization has reversed: modern humans carry roughly ten percent less cranial volume than Cro-Magnon populations, plausibly due to relaxed selection pressure under agricultural conditions. The notion that evolution reliably moves "forward" does not survive contact with the evidence.

What is genuinely novel is the emergence of general intelligence coupled with cultural evolution — a mode of adaptation that operates orders of magnitude faster than genetic selection and carries correspondingly greater leverage. This threshold crossing is without precedent in our evolutionary lineage and changes the nature of the game entirely. Stewart Brand's formulation captures the stakes: godlike power demands godlike competence.

The metacrisis — the convergence of existential and civilizational risks — is the direct consequence of this asymmetry between capability and wisdom. The failure modes range from total extinction to civilizational collapse and regression to warlordism. But the capacity to navigate these risks also exists within the same cultural-evolutionary toolkit. The directionality of history, to the extent it is real, is not an inheritance to be passively received. It is a project requiring deliberate construction — and one where the margin for error is narrowing.

## Magus

Whether deep history exhibits genuine teleological structure — an intrinsic directionality toward complexification — is an empirical question that resists both naïve progressivism and fashionable nihilism. The thermodynamic argument is coherent: sustained energy flux from stellar sources maintains far-from-equilibrium conditions under which complexity can ratchet upward across successive phase transitions — atomic, molecular, cellular, organismic, societal. Yet this cosmological trend dissolves into contingency at finer grain. Exogenous shocks (K-Pg bolide impact), niche-dependent reversals (cetacean re-marinization from artiodactyl ancestors), and relaxed selection dynamics (the approximately ten percent reduction in hominin cranial volume since the Upper Paleolithic, plausibly correlated with Neolithic domestication syndromes) all demonstrate that no particular lineage rides a guaranteed escalator. The arrow of complexification, if it exists, is statistical and thermodynamically bounded — it will reverse when stellar nucleosynthesis ceases in approximately ten trillion years.

The genuinely discontinuous development is the emergence of general intelligence yoked to cumulative cultural evolution — a Lamarckian transmission channel operating at rates that dwarf Darwinian selection by orders of magnitude. This threshold crossing introduces a qualitatively new dynamic: adaptation becomes reflexive, capable of modeling and modifying its own selection environment. The consequence, as Stewart Brand's aphorism distills, is that capability has outrun the governance structures meant to contain it.

The metacrisis is precisely this structural mismatch rendered concrete. The probability distribution of outcomes spans from the extinction of all terrestrial life to civilizational regression toward fragmented warlordism — but also includes successful navigation toward coordinated planetary stewardship. Crucially, the directionality that complexification theorists identify cannot be invoked as a guarantor. Whatever telos deep history may exhibit, it operates as a tendency within boundary conditions, not as providence. For a species wielding cultural-evolutionary power at planetary scale, directionality is not discovered but constructed — and the construction tolerates diminishing margins of error.
