# How Mystical States Reverse the Standard of What Is Real

Where being and ought share a single root

---

> Mystical experiences reverse our usual standard of what counts as real, making people judge ordinary life against the new experience rather than the reverse — a convergence of 'is' and 'ought' called onto-normativity, which can be understood as flow operating at the level of one's total capacity to grip reality.

When people undergo what we loosely call mystical experience or higher states of consciousness, something philosophically remarkable happens with their relationship to reality and normativity. Normally, people use the coherent intelligibility of their everyday world as the standard by which they judge what is real — dreams aren't real because they don't fit the maximal coherence of waking life, altered states aren't real because they fall outside the normative framework of ordinary experience. But in these higher states, people do something bizarre and philosophically significant: they reverse that judgment entirely. They enter experiences they have never had before, experiences that are almost lacking in ordinary content, and they say — this is more real than all of that. They take the new experience as the standard and use it to call the ordinary world into question. And crucially, this is not merely a statement — the research shows that people then attempt to transform their relationships, their roles, their identity, their understanding of the world, in order to come into increasing conformity with what they experienced as the really real. This is what I call onto-normativity: the is and the ought converging at a common origin. The mystical realization is precisely the experience in which that underlying oneness — where realness and rightness are indistinguishable — is exemplified in a way that drives people into profound transformation. This can be distinguished from the privative case of ego dissolution, like depersonalization, where the brain shows radical isolation of the insula rather than radical connectivity — the phenomenology differs, the neuroscience differs, and critically, the onto-normative pull is absent. The superlative case carries with it the sense of the really real; the privative case does not. Furthermore, there is a proposal worth taking seriously about what these states actually are through the lens of cognitive science and optimal grip: in ordinary flow states, a person flows within a limited domain, but in the highest states of consciousness, what may be happening is flow at the level of meta-optimal grip — not flowing in how to get an optimal grip in biology or in a relationship, but flowing in the capacity for optimal gripping as such, the very capacity to be in reality well. By the same logic as local flow — which reliably designates optimal experience and optimal performance simultaneously — the mystical state is similarly justifying: it reliably designates profound optimal experience and performance with respect to reality itself. And that is why, although people in these states may articulate very different metaphysical claims, the claim that they are enhancing their relationship to reality in a way that reliably improves their lives and makes them wiser can actually be justified. The metaphysical diversity does not undermine the functional convergence.

## Apprentice

Normally, people decide what's real by checking it against everyday waking life. Dreams don't count because they're less coherent than being awake. But in profound mystical experiences, something strange happens: people flip that judgment entirely. They enter an unfamiliar state — often stripped of ordinary content — and declare it more real than everything they've known. Then they restructure their lives to match what they glimpsed.

This reversal is not just a feeling. Research shows that people who undergo these experiences genuinely change — their relationships, their sense of identity, their understanding of what matters. The experience carries a built-in sense that what is most real is also what is most right. Realness and rightness feel like the same thing. This phenomenon has been called onto-normativity: the point where reality and value share a single root.

Importantly, this is different from experiences like depersonalization, where the self dissolves in a frightening, empty way. In those cases, the brain shows isolation rather than connectivity, and people don't come away feeling they've touched something more real. There's also a promising explanation from cognitive science: just as a musician in a flow state is simultaneously performing optimally and experiencing optimally within music, the mystical state may be flow operating at the deepest level — not flow within any single domain, but flow in the very capacity to connect with reality. That would explain why people from wildly different traditions describe different beliefs but converge on the same life-transforming effect.

## Adept

John Vervaeke's concept of onto-normativity identifies a striking feature of mystical and higher states of consciousness: the collapse of the distinction between what is real and what is normatively binding. Ordinarily, people use the coherent intelligibility of waking life as the benchmark for judging reality — dreams and altered states are dismissed because they fail to meet that standard. In mystical experience, this hierarchy inverts. People enter states largely devoid of ordinary content and yet judge them as more real than everyday life, then undertake profound transformation to bring their existence into conformity with what was revealed. The 'is' and the 'ought' converge at a common origin — the really real is simultaneously the really right.

This onto-normative pull distinguishes the superlative case of ego transcendence from the privative case of depersonalization or derealization. Neuroscientifically, the superlative case involves radical connectivity (particularly of the insula and default mode network), while the privative case involves radical isolation. Phenomenologically, one carries the sense of enhanced realness; the other carries the sense of unreality. Crucially, only the superlative case drives lasting transformation.

Vervaeke proposes that these states can be understood through the framework of optimal grip and flow. In ordinary flow, a person achieves simultaneous optimal experience and optimal performance within a bounded domain. The mystical state may represent meta-optimal grip — flow not within biology or relationships but in the very capacity for optimal gripping as such, the fundamental ability to be well-fitted to reality. Just as local flow reliably co-designates peak experience and peak performance, this meta-flow would reliably designate enhanced contact with reality itself. This explains the functional convergence across traditions: people articulate divergent metaphysical claims, yet the claim that they are genuinely improving their relationship to reality — and becoming measurably wiser — can be independently justified.

## Magus

Vervaeke's notion of onto-normativity names the phenomenon in which the ontological and the normative become indistinguishable at their shared root, a convergence most vividly exemplified in what the traditions call mystical experience. Under ordinary conditions, agents employ the maximal coherent intelligibility of the waking world as the normative standard against which candidate realities are measured — the dream is judged less real precisely because it fails the coherence criteria of waking life. The philosophically remarkable feature of higher states of consciousness is the complete reversal of this adjudicatory structure: subjects enter states largely evacuated of propositional and perceptual content and yet take those states as the new normative standard, retroactively calling the ordinary world into question. The ensuing transformation — of identity, relationships, worldview — constitutes behavioral evidence that the experience functions as an onto-normative attractor, not merely an altered phenomenology.

The distinction between superlative and privative dissolution is essential here. Depersonalization and derealization represent privative ego dissolution: the insula shows radical functional isolation, phenomenology is characterized by unreality and affective flattening, and critically, no onto-normative pull obtains. The superlative case — marked by radical neural connectivity and the phenomenology of enhanced realness — carries precisely the transformative demand that the privative case lacks. This is not a continuum but a qualitative divergence in both mechanism and meaning.

The explanatory framework draws on the logic of optimal grip extended to a meta-level. Flow states reliably co-designate optimal experience and optimal performance within domain-specific constraints; Vervaeke's proposal is that mystical states instantiate flow at the level of the capacity for optimal gripping as such — not grip within a frame but grip on the framing process itself. If local flow justifiably indicates enhanced agent-arena coupling within a domain, meta-flow justifiably indicates enhanced coupling with reality qua reality. This accounts for the well-documented paradox of metaphysical diversity amid functional convergence: practitioners articulate irreconcilable ontologies yet converge on measurable improvements in wisdom, meaning, and existential orientation. The justificatory force is preserved not despite the metaphysical pluralism but orthogonally to it.
