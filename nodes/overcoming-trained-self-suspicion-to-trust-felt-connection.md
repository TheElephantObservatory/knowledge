# Overcoming Trained Self-Suspicion to Trust Felt Connection

Permission was the missing ingredient all along.

---

> The main barrier to experiencing meaningful connection with the world is not ignorance but a culturally trained suspicion toward one's own deepest perceptions. Transformation happens less through new information than through frameworks that give people permission to trust what they already feel.

Rita Leduc articulates something that neither the philosophical argument nor the ecological narrative alone can accomplish: the phenomenology of the barrier that prevents people from accessing what oika and Transcendent Naturalism are pointing toward. She describes her own experience of having felt something real — an intuitive, participatory sense of connection — and then spending enormous cognitive and emotional energy doubting it, judging it, feeling foolish for having felt it. This is not merely personal psychology; it is, she suggests, a fractal of what the broader culture does. The hostility and skepticism she internalized is the same hostility and skepticism that culture has trained into people as a default response to any claim of meaning, sacredness, or felt intelligence in the world. What the scientific grounding of oika — and the philosophical scaffolding of Transcendent Naturalism — did for her was not to add new information but to remove the energy drain of self-doubt. Once the concepts were in place, the energy previously spent questioning and dismissing her own experience became available for actual participation. This points to a practical insight about transformation: the barrier is not primarily ignorance but a trained disposition of suspicion toward one's own deepest perceptions. The work of onboarding people into a new participatory relationship with the world is therefore not primarily informational — it is the work of giving people permission to trust what they already feel, backed by a framework rigorous enough to withstand the hermeneutics of suspicion.

## Apprentice

Rita Leduc describes a pattern many people will recognize: you feel something real — a sense of connection to the living world, a moment of meaning that seems to come from outside your own head — and then you immediately begin doubting it. You tell yourself you're being naive, unscientific, foolish. You spend enormous energy pushing the feeling away. Leduc suggests this isn't just a personal quirk. It's something our culture has trained into us. We've learned to treat any sense of sacredness or felt intelligence in the world as embarrassing, as something that needs to be explained away.

What changed for her was not learning new facts. It was encountering a framework — grounded in both ecological science and careful philosophy — that was rigorous enough to let her stop fighting her own experience. Once she no longer had to spend energy on self-doubt, that energy became available for actually participating in the connection she had always sensed.

This points to something important about how people change. The obstacle isn't that people lack information about the living world. The obstacle is a deeply ingrained habit of suspicion toward their own perceptions of meaning. Helping people into a new relationship with the world is therefore less about teaching and more about offering them credible permission to trust what they already feel.

## Magus

Rita Leduc's contribution is a precise phenomenology of epistemic self-suppression — the mechanism by which culturally internalized suspicion prevents participatory knowing from stabilizing into actionable orientation. She describes not a deficit of experience but a surplus of trained negation: the felt sense of meaningful connection with the living world is present, but the subject immediately mobilizes the hermeneutics of suspicion against her own perception, expending enormous cognitive and affective resources to maintain the culturally sanctioned posture of disenchantment. This self-policing is, she argues, fractal — isomorphic with the broader cultural operation that treats any attribution of intelligence, sacredness, or intrinsic meaning to the more-than-human world as epistemically illegitimate.

The function of oika's scientific grounding and Transcendent Naturalism's philosophical architecture was not informational but energetic: these frameworks removed the necessity of constant self-doubt by providing a structure rigorous enough to withstand precisely the debunking reflexes that modernity has installed as default epistemic hygiene. The experience preceded the framework; what the framework accomplished was the cessation of the energy drain that prevented the experience from becoming participatory rather than merely episodic. This is a critical distinction — transformation here is not the production of new states but the disinhibition of already-present capacities.

Leduc's account thus identifies the actual locus of resistance to participatory ontologies: not ignorance, not even disagreement, but a trained dispositional suspicion toward one's own deepest perceptions of meaning. The implication for any project of cultural reorientation is significant. The work is not primarily pedagogical but permissive — constructing scaffolding robust enough that subjects can relinquish the exhausting labor of self-negation and redirect that energy toward the participatory engagement their experience has always intimated was possible.
