# Why Western Culture Has Not Grieved the Death of God

The tears we never learned to cry

---

> Before any new spirituality can take root, the modern world must first genuinely grieve the death of God — a grief Nietzsche announced but never performed, and whose absence may quietly undermine every attempt to rebuild meaning.

One of the most underexamined obstacles to resolving the meaning crisis — and to any viable post-metaphysical spirituality — is the collective failure to grieve the death of God. Nietzsche announces the death of God with the parable of the madman, and the madman even asks the right question: have we felt this yet? But Nietzsche himself does not grieve. He converts the announcement almost immediately into a call for self-aggrandizement, for becoming worthy of the deed, for the will to power as the response to the void. John Vervaeke argues that this is his deepest failure — not philosophical but existential. The analogy to romantic relationships is instructive: you cannot move authentically into a new relationship until you have genuinely grieved the one you have lost. The positive project of building new ecologies of practice, new ways of falling in love with being, new participatory modes of seeing — all of that may be unavailable, or may quietly hollow out, until the prior grief has been done. What makes this culturally urgent is that so much of what looks like political acting out, ideological rage, and meaning-seeking in distorted forms may itself be the expression of unprocessed grief — grief that has nowhere to go because the culture has built elaborate interruptive mechanisms to prevent people from going there. David Michael Levin's phenomenological inquiry deepens this point: drawing on Heidegger's Gelassenheit, he examines how each of the human senses has been captured by a subject-object structure that sunders us from genuine participation in the world, and proposes that crying — understood not as mere emotional discharge but as a practice of the self — represents a threshold experience in which ordinary vision is interrupted and something deeper becomes possible. The example of Joanna Macy is instructive: years of intellectual engagement with ecological crisis suddenly broke through into hours of tears, and that rupture transformed her work — opening first into grief work, helping people feel what the culture trains them to suppress, and then into the broader project she calls the Work That Reconnects. The religion that is not a religion — whatever form it eventually takes — will succeed or fail on whether it can create the conditions for people to grieve the death of God deeply, respectfully, and fully, interrogating the cultural, psychological, and theological mechanisms that forestall that movement, so that they can arrive on the other side capable of falling in love with reality again.

## Apprentice

When Nietzsche declared that God is dead, he was describing something that had already happened — the slow collapse of the shared framework that once gave Western life its deepest sense of meaning. But here's the strange part: almost no one stopped to mourn. Nietzsche himself rushed past the loss toward grand visions of human self-overcoming. And the culture followed suit, filling the void with ideology, productivity, distraction — anything but sitting with what was actually lost.

The insight here is that grief is not optional. Just as you cannot authentically begin a new relationship without mourning the one that ended, a culture cannot build new ways of finding meaning until it has genuinely felt the absence of the old ones. Much of what looks like political rage, conspiracy thinking, or desperate meaning-seeking may actually be unprocessed grief with nowhere to go.

Joanna Macy's experience illustrates the point vividly: years of intellectual work on ecological crisis suddenly broke through into hours of weeping, and that rupture transformed everything that followed. The suggestion is that whatever comes after traditional religion — whatever new practices help people fall in love with being alive — will only work if people are first given permission and space to grieve what has been lost. The culture actively prevents this, and that prevention may be the deepest obstacle to resolving the meaning crisis.

## Adept

John Vervaeke identifies Nietzsche's deepest failure not as philosophical but as existential: having announced the death of God through the madman's parable — a figure who even asks whether we have felt this event yet — Nietzsche immediately converts the announcement into a program of self-aggrandizement and will to power. The grief is bypassed. This matters because the analogy to attachment and loss is precise: authentic movement into new relational configurations requires the completion of grief for what has been lost. The positive project of building ecologies of practice, participatory knowing, and post-metaphysical spirituality may be structurally unavailable until this prior work is done.

David Michael Levin's phenomenological work deepens the analysis. Drawing on Heidegger's Gelassenheit, Levin examines how each human sense has been captured by subject-object framing, severing participatory engagement with the world. He proposes that crying — not as emotional discharge but as a practice of the self — constitutes a threshold experience in which ordinary vision is interrupted and a more fundamental mode of seeing becomes possible. Joanna Macy's biography enacts this trajectory: years of intellectual engagement with ecological collapse broke through into sustained weeping, which opened first into grief work with others and then into the broader transformative project she calls the Work That Reconnects.

The culturally urgent dimension is that much of what presents as ideological rage, political acting out, and distorted meaning-seeking may be the surface expression of grief that has nowhere to go. The culture has constructed elaborate interruptive mechanisms — psychological, theological, social — that prevent people from entering the grief space. Any viable post-metaphysical spirituality will succeed or fail on whether it can dismantle those mechanisms and create conditions for the full mourning of God's death, so that genuine reenchantment becomes possible on the other side.

## Magus

The argument identifies an underexamined structural precondition for resolving the meaning crisis: the collective failure to grieve the death of God constitutes not merely a cultural oversight but an existential blockage that may render subsequent reconstructive projects — ecologies of practice, participatory knowing, religio without metaphysical commitment — internally hollow. Nietzsche's madman poses the decisive question ("Do we smell nothing as yet of the divine decomposition?") but Nietzsche himself performs an immediate sublimation, converting loss into the will to power and the project of self-overcoming. Vervaeke's diagnosis is that this represents Nietzsche's deepest failure, operating at the existential rather than philosophical register. The attachment-theoretic analogy is structurally precise: incomplete grief forecloses authentic new relational possibility, generating instead repetition compulsion and compensatory formations.

David Michael Levin's phenomenological inquiry, grounded in Heidegger's Gelassenheit, provides the philosophical infrastructure for understanding what grief might accomplish. Levin traces how each sensory modality has been colonized by the subject-object structure characteristic of the Gestell, and proposes that crying — reconceived not as affective discharge but as Selbstpraxis — enacts a threshold dissolution of the visual field's ordinary organization, opening toward what Heidegger calls Ereignis. Macy's trajectory from intellectual engagement with ecological catastrophe through sustained somatic rupture into the Work That Reconnects instantiates this phenomenological movement biographically.

What renders this culturally urgent is the hypothesis that much contemporary political acting out, ideological rigidity, and distorted meaning-seeking constitutes displaced grief — affect that has been denied its proper object and trajectory by elaborate cultural, psychological, and theological interruptive mechanisms. The religion that is not a religion will be tested precisely here: on its capacity to create conditions sufficient for the full mourning of the metaphysical framework's collapse, interrogating the foreclosure mechanisms with enough precision that genuine Wiederverzauberung — reenchantment as falling in love with the real — becomes structurally available rather than performatively simulated.
