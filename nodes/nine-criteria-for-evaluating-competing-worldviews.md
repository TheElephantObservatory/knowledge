# Nine Criteria for Evaluating Competing Worldviews

A scorecard for the questions that never close

---

> Philosophy lacks agreed-upon standards for judging whether one worldview is better than another. Clément Vidal proposes anchoring evaluation to the 'big questions' every worldview must answer, deriving nine criteria — objective, subjective, and intersubjective — that function as a structured checklist for honest comparison.

Philosophy has long lacked what science takes for granted: a rigorous set of criteria for evaluating whether one theory is better than another. In science, epistemology gives us clear standards — satisfactory structure, intrinsic and extrinsic explanatory power, observational and experimental support. But philosophy has no equivalent consensus, largely because philosophers cannot even agree on what philosophy is. This is not a trivial gap. If you can't say what philosophy is for, you can't say when it's doing its job well. The response Clément Vidal proposes is to anchor philosophy to the 'big questions' — the age-old questions that any serious worldview must answer: What is? Where does it all come from? Where are we going? What is good and what is evil? How should we act? What is true and what is false? These are not merely philosophical questions — they are also scientific, pragmatic, and existential. But they form the irreducible core of what a worldview must address. From this foundation, nine metaphilosophical criteria can be derived across three domains: objective criteria (scientificity, internal consistency, scope), subjective criteria (fit with personal experience, personal utility, emotional resonance), and intersubjective criteria (reduction of interpersonal conflict, narrativity, and mobilization toward social benefit). Together, these criteria function not as a deterministic algorithm for producing the correct worldview, but as a structured checklist — a way to identify where any given worldview is strong, where it is weak, and where it can be improved. The goal is not to crown a winner, but to make the dialogue between worldviews more rigorous and more honest.

## Apprentice

Science has well-established ways to test whether one theory is better than another — does it fit the evidence, is it internally consistent, does it explain more? Philosophy, surprisingly, has no equivalent. Philosophers can't even agree on what philosophy is supposed to do, which makes it nearly impossible to say when it's being done well.

Clément Vidal's response is to ground the whole enterprise in the big questions that any serious worldview must eventually face: What exists? Where did everything come from? What is good? How should we live? What counts as true? These aren't just abstract puzzles — they're the questions that shape how people actually navigate their lives.

From these questions, Vidal derives nine criteria for evaluating worldviews, organized into three groups: objective ones like scientific grounding and logical consistency, subjective ones like whether a worldview resonates with personal experience, and social ones like whether it helps reduce conflict between people. The point isn't to declare one worldview the winner. It's to give us a clear, structured way to see where any worldview is strong, where it falls short, and where honest conversation can begin.

## Adept

The sciences benefit from well-articulated epistemological standards — satisfactory structure, explanatory power, empirical support — that allow competing theories to be weighed against one another with some rigor. Philosophy possesses no comparable consensus. This is not merely an oversight; it stems from a deeper problem: the absence of agreement on what philosophy itself is for. Without a shared understanding of philosophy's purpose, criteria for evaluating philosophical success remain elusive.

Clément Vidal addresses this impasse by proposing that philosophy be anchored to the perennial 'big questions' — What is? Where does it all come from? Where are we going? What is good and evil? How should we act? What is true and false? These questions constitute the irreducible core that any comprehensive worldview must engage. They are simultaneously philosophical, scientific, and existential, and they provide the functional definition of philosophy's scope that has been missing.

From this foundation, Vidal derives nine metaphilosophical criteria distributed across three domains. Objective criteria include scientificity, internal consistency, and scope of explanatory reach. Subjective criteria address fit with lived experience, personal utility, and emotional adequacy. Intersubjective criteria evaluate a worldview's capacity to reduce interpersonal conflict, its narrative coherence for communities, and its power to mobilize collective action toward beneficial ends. These criteria do not function as an algorithm for producing the correct worldview. They serve instead as a diagnostic framework — a structured means of identifying strengths, exposing weaknesses, and making the comparative evaluation of worldviews more disciplined and transparent.

## Magus

The asymmetry between scientific and philosophical epistemology is striking: where the sciences operate with reasonably well-codified criteria for theory evaluation — empirical adequacy, internal consistency, explanatory scope, predictive power — philosophy lacks any analogous consensus. Vidal traces this deficit to a prior, more fundamental lacuna: the absence of a shared metaphilosophical account of what philosophy is. The circularity is vicious — without knowing what philosophy aims to accomplish, one cannot specify the conditions under which it accomplishes it well.

Vidal's intervention is to bypass definitional disputes by grounding philosophical evaluation in the set of 'big questions' that constitute the necessary conditions for worldview completeness: ontological (What is?), genetic-cosmological (Where does it all come from?), teleological (Where are we going?), axiological (What is good and evil?), praxeological (How should we act?), and epistemological (What is true and false?). These questions function as a transcultural invariant — the irreducible demand structure that any worldview, whether religious, secular, or syncretic, must satisfy to count as comprehensive.

From this demand structure, nine metaphilosophical criteria are systematically derived across three registers. The objective register encompasses scientificity, internal logical consistency, and breadth of scope. The subjective register evaluates experiential adequacy, personal utility, and emotional resonance. The intersubjective register assesses conflict-reduction capacity, narrative coherence for communal identity, and mobilizational efficacy toward collective flourishing. Crucially, these criteria are non-algorithmic; they do not converge on a unique rational worldview. They function instead as a multi-dimensional evaluation space within which worldviews can be rigorously compared, their trade-offs made explicit, and the dialogue between them rendered genuinely dialectical rather than merely rhetorical.
