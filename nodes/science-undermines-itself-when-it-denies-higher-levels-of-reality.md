# Science Undermines Itself When It Denies Higher Levels of Reality

You cannot use an illusion to prove the real.

---

> Science that declares only the bottom level of physical reality is real undermines itself, because scientific knowledge depends on higher-level processes like measurement, reasoning, and communication that cannot exist at the quantum level alone. Rigorous information theory proves these higher levels are causally real, not convenient fictions.

The dominant scientific worldview has produced a performative contradiction at its very core. Science, when it claims that only the bottom level of physical reality is truly real and everything above it is illusory or epiphenomenal, is sawing off the branch it sits on. Consider what science actually requires in order to function: real information transfer, real causal signaling, real measurement structures, and real computational processes happening at the level where scientists actually exist and work. None of these can be located at the quantum bottom level alone. If the upper levels — where scientists think, measure, communicate, and reason — are merely illusory epiphenomena, then the knowledge those scientists produce about the bottom level has no epistemic standing whatsoever. You cannot use an illusion to prove that something else is real. Furthermore, Erik Hoel's formal mathematical work through information theory provides a rigorous proof of this layered ontology. Shannon's theory of communication shows that reliable signal transmission requires redundancy — abstraction from the full quantum state — because noise overwhelms any purely bottom-level channel. For one part of an organism to causally coordinate with another, it must operate at a higher level of organization that compresses, transmits, and decodes information. This abstraction is not a cognitive convenience — it is causally necessary. The higher-level pattern has causal powers the lower level does not and cannot have. This is a formal proof that emergence is real: the levels are not where we happen to slice things for convenience, but where the causal action genuinely lives. Once you open the door to reductionism, following Ned Block's argument, there is no principled place to stop — neurons are just molecules, which are just atoms, which are just quarks, which are just probability distributions. And if you reach the absolute bottom, the laws of physics themselves break down entirely, as at the pre-Big Bang singularity. Science, properly understood, presupposes a layered ontology. Extended naturalism is not a retreat from scientific rigor; it is what scientific rigor actually demands.

## Apprentice

There is a deep contradiction hiding inside the common scientific assumption that only the smallest building blocks of reality — particles, quantum fields — are "truly real," and that everything built from them is just a convenient illusion. The problem is that science itself depends on things like thinking, measuring, and communicating, and none of these happen at the level of individual particles. If those higher-level activities are illusions, then the scientific conclusions they produce have no real standing. You cannot use something fake to prove something else is real.

The physicist and neuroscientist Erik Hoel has shown, using the mathematics of information theory, why higher levels of organization are not just useful descriptions but genuinely necessary for cause and effect to work. When one part of a living system needs to coordinate with another, it has to compress and transmit information in ways that only work above the quantum level. The noise at the bottom is simply too overwhelming. The higher-level pattern does something the lower level literally cannot do on its own.

Following the philosopher Ned Block's reasoning, once you start reducing everything downward — neurons to molecules, molecules to atoms, atoms to quarks — there is no natural stopping point, and at the very bottom, the laws of physics themselves dissolve. Science, to function honestly, must accept that reality has genuinely real layers. This is not a weakening of scientific thinking — it is what careful scientific thinking actually requires.

## Adept

A performative contradiction sits at the heart of eliminative reductionism: the claim that only fundamental physics is ontologically real delegitimizes the very epistemic processes — measurement, inference, communication — through which that claim is produced. Scientists operate at macroscopic levels of organization. If those levels are epiphenomenal, then the knowledge generated there about the microphysical has no epistemic warrant. The argument is self-defeating in a precise logical sense: it uses the causal efficacy of higher-level processes to deny that higher-level processes are causally efficacious.

Erik Hoel's work in causal emergence, grounded in Shannon information theory, provides formal teeth for this intuition. Reliable information transfer requires redundancy and error correction — operations that abstract away from the full microstate. A purely bottom-level communication channel is overwhelmed by noise; only by compressing into higher-level macrovariables can a system achieve effective causal coordination. This is not an observer-dependent choice of description. The higher-level variables possess causal powers — measured by effective information — that the lower-level variables provably lack. Emergence, on this account, is mathematically demonstrable, not merely philosophical assertion.

Ned Block's regress argument sharpens the point: reductionism offers no principled stopping place. Neurons reduce to molecules, molecules to atoms, atoms to subatomic particles, particles to quantum fields, and at the putative absolute bottom — the pre-Big Bang singularity — physical law itself becomes undefined. A coherent naturalism must therefore be a layered naturalism, one that grants genuine ontological standing to multiple levels of causal organization. Extended naturalism, understood this way, is not an expansion beyond science but a correction demanded by science's own operational presuppositions.

## Magus

Eliminative microphysicalism harbors a performative contradiction of considerable formal severity. The thesis that only the fundamental physical level possesses genuine ontological standing presupposes, for its own articulation and evidential support, the causal efficacy of macroscopic epistemic processes — observation, measurement, inference, intersubjective communication — whose reality it simultaneously denies. The epistemic bootstrapping problem is inescapable: if the levels at which scientific practice operates are epiphenomenal, then no product of that practice, including the reductionist thesis itself, carries epistemic warrant. The argument collapses under its own weight.

Erik Hoel's framework of causal emergence, formalized through effective information measures derived from Shannon's channel capacity theorems, renders this philosophical intuition mathematically rigorous. Shannon demonstrated that reliable signal transmission across noisy channels requires redundancy — necessarily a coarse-graining operation over microstates. Hoel extends this to show that macrovariables can exhibit strictly greater effective information than their microphysical substrates, meaning the higher-level description is not merely convenient but causally superior in a quantifiable sense. The causal joints of reality are not observer-relative projections; they are features of the informational topology of physical systems. Emergence is not epistemological shorthand but ontological fact, provable within information-theoretic formalism.

Ned Block's regress argument completes the picture by demonstrating that greedy reductionism admits no principled terminus — each putatively fundamental level dissolves into a still more fundamental one, until at the pre-Big Bang singularity, physical law itself becomes undefined. A scientifically coherent ontology must therefore be irreducibly stratified, granting genuine causal standing to multiple organizational levels simultaneously. What is sometimes called extended naturalism is thus not a concession to anti-scientific metaphysics but the fulfillment of naturalism's own internal logic — the ontology that scientific practice has always tacitly presupposed and that information theory now formally vindicates.
