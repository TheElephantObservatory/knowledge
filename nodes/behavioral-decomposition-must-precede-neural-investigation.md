# Behavioral Decomposition Must Precede Neural Investigation

The map is not the territory it cannot yet read.

---

> Neuroscience gets the order of operations wrong: you must first understand what a behavior is and decompose it into its component parts before searching for neural correlates. Without that prior analysis, brain data is uninterpretable.

A foundational methodological argument in neuroscience: behavioral decomposition must be epistemically prior to neural investigation. Before you go looking for neural correlates, you need a clear, principled account of what the behavior actually is and what its component parts are. Otherwise, you risk correlating neural activity with the wrong things — or with artifacts of your experimental setup rather than the natural behavior you care about. The classic trap is designing a lab task that activates the nervous system but no longer bears meaningful relation to what the animal actually does in its natural habitat. A spinalized cat can still walk on a treadmill, which tells you something real about spinal circuits — but it tells you nothing about balance, terrain navigation, or the behavioral switching between gaits. The reduced task is not the behavior. This matters because neuroscience has a deep habit of reverse-engineering: recording from neurons first and hoping the behavior falls out. David Marr's three-level framework (computational, algorithmic, implementational) is useful here not as a rigid doctrine but as a reminder of the correct direction of inquiry: understand what problem is being solved and how, before asking which neural circuits implement the solution. The complete connectome of C. elegans — every neuron, every synapse, fully mapped — does not allow us to predict the behavior of the worm, and the Jonas and Kording microprocessor paper makes the same point with a simpler system: standard neuroscientific methods applied to a microprocessor running video games would not recover the fetch-decode-execute architecture. A connectome is best understood as a library that becomes useful once you have a hypothesis-driven, algorithmically grounded question to bring to it — not a source of explanations in itself. The correct sequence remains: identify the behavior, decompose it algorithmically, generate either/or hypotheses, and then go to the neural data to break the tie. The sound localization example makes this concrete: once you know the animal needs to determine whether a sound is coming from left or right, and that there are two possible algorithmic strategies (spatial vs. temporal), you can design experiments to break the tie using neural data. Without that prior decomposition, the neural data is uninterpretable.

## Apprentice

There is a persistent temptation in brain science to start by recording neural activity and hope that understanding of behavior will follow. This argument flips that sequence: before you look at the brain, you need to know exactly what behavior you're trying to explain. What is the animal actually doing? What are the steps involved? Without answering these questions first, you risk finding brain patterns that correspond to artifacts of your experiment rather than anything the animal does in real life.

A vivid example makes this clear. A cat with its spinal cord severed can still walk on a treadmill — but that treadmill walking tells you about spinal reflexes, not about how a cat navigates rough terrain or decides to switch from walking to running. The lab task has stripped away the very thing you wanted to understand. Similarly, scientists have mapped every single neuron and connection in a tiny worm, yet that complete wiring diagram alone cannot predict what the worm will do. The map is not the explanation.

The productive path is to first identify the behavior, break it into logical steps, come up with competing explanations for how those steps work, and only then use brain recordings to decide between them. Sound localization illustrates this well: once you know an animal needs to figure out whether a sound comes from the left or right, and that there are two possible strategies it could use, you can design a targeted experiment. Without that groundwork, the neural data is just noise.

## Adept

This methodological argument asserts that behavioral decomposition is epistemically prior to neural investigation — a claim with deep consequences for how neuroscience should be practiced. The core problem is that the field habitually operates in reverse, recording from neurons and attempting to reconstruct behavioral explanations post hoc. David Marr's three-level framework — computational, algorithmic, implementational — serves as a corrective reminder: one must first specify what problem the organism is solving and what algorithmic strategies could solve it before asking which neural circuits implement the solution. Without this prior decomposition, neural data lacks an interpretive frame.

The examples are pointed. The spinalized cat walking on a treadmill reveals genuine properties of spinal central pattern generators, but it tells us nothing about balance, gait selection, or terrain navigation — the reduced preparation has excised the behavioral phenomenon of interest while preserving neural activation. The complete C. elegans connectome, despite cataloguing every neuron and synapse, does not yield behavioral prediction. Jonas and Kording's microprocessor study drives the point home with engineered precision: standard neuroscientific analysis methods applied to a known computational system fail to recover its actual architecture.

The proposed sequence is disciplined: identify the natural behavior, decompose it algorithmically, generate competing either/or hypotheses about mechanism, and then — and only then — use neural data to arbitrate. The sound localization case is paradigmatic. Knowing that an animal must lateralize a sound source, and that two distinct algorithmic strategies (spatial coding versus temporal coding) could accomplish this, permits the design of experiments where neural recordings become genuinely decisive. A connectome, in this view, functions as a library — enormously valuable once you arrive with the right question, but inert as a source of explanation on its own.

## Magus

The argument is foundational and deceptively simple: behavioral decomposition must be epistemically prior to neurophysiological investigation, and the systematic failure to honor this ordering accounts for a significant fraction of neuroscience's interpretive difficulties. The field's default mode — record first, interpret later — inverts the correct direction of inquiry. Marr's computational-algorithmic-implementational hierarchy is invoked here not as rigid doctrine but as a heuristic encoding a genuine epistemic dependency: implementation-level data cannot be interpreted absent a specification of the computational problem and the space of algorithmic solutions. Neural correlates discovered without this prior specification are correlates of the experimental apparatus, not of the behavior.

The exemplary failures are carefully chosen to span the range from biological to artificial systems. The spinalized cat on a treadmill isolates spinal CPG dynamics but has surgically removed the behavioral phenomena — postural control, gait switching, terrain adaptation — that constitute locomotion as an ethologically meaningful category. The C. elegans connectome, complete down to every synapse, remains predictively impotent with respect to behavior, demonstrating that structural completeness is not explanatory sufficiency. Jonas and Kording's application of standard neuroscientific methods to a microprocessor — a system whose computational architecture is fully known — yielded none of the actual organizational principles, exposing the inadequacy of the analytic toolkit when deployed without algorithmic grounding.

The prescribed methodology is hypothesis-driven in a specific sense: decompose the behavior into algorithmic alternatives that make distinguishable neural predictions, then use physiological data to arbitrate. The sound localization case is canonical — the computational problem (lateralization) admits two algorithmic solutions (place code versus temporal code) whose neural implementations are empirically distinguishable. The connectome becomes a library, not an oracle: its value is realized only in proportion to the specificity of the question brought to it. Without algorithmic hypotheses, the wiring diagram is a map of a territory one has not yet learned to see.
