# Nature's Primary Units: Atoms, Cells, Animals, Persons

The leap that cannot be explained from below

---

> Nature organizes itself into distinct planes — matter, life, mind, culture — each built around a primary unit (atom, cell, animal, person) that represents a genuine qualitative leap, not just increased complexity. The same triadic logic of parts, wholes, and groups repeats at every level, revealing both deep continuity and irreducible discontinuity.

The Periodic Table of Behaviors makes a structural claim that is easy to miss but enormously consequential: each plane of existence has a primary unit of organization — a metastable whole that is made up of parts beneath it and forms into groups above it. For the matter dimension, that primary unit is the atom. For life, it is the cell. For mind, it is the minded animal behaving in context. For culture, it is the justifying person behaving in context. This is not just a taxonomic convenience — it is a claim about how nature actually organizes itself. Each primary unit represents a qualitative leap in integrated differentiation: the atom is not just a pile of particles, the cell is not just a pile of molecules, the animal is not just a pile of cells, and the person is not just a pile of animal behaviors. The internal architecture of each plane follows a triadic structure — parts that compose the primary unit, the primary unit itself, and groups that the primary unit forms into — and this structure repeats across every dimension of complexification. Recognizing it allows us to see both the continuity and the discontinuity in nature's design: the continuity is the repeated logic of integration and differentiation, while the discontinuity is that each primary unit introduces a genuinely novel kind of entity that cannot be reduced to the logic of the plane below it.

## Apprentice

One of the most striking claims in the Periodic Table of Behaviors is that nature doesn't just get more complicated as you move from physics to chemistry to biology to psychology to culture. Instead, at each major level, there's a central "unit" that holds everything together — the atom for matter, the cell for life, the behaving animal for mind, and the justifying person for culture. Each of these units is genuinely new. A cell isn't just a bag of molecules, and a person isn't just a sophisticated animal. Something qualitatively different emerges at each level.

What makes this especially elegant is that the same basic pattern shows up every time. Each level has three layers: the smaller parts that make up the primary unit, the unit itself, and the larger groups that units form together. Atoms are made of subatomic particles and combine into molecules. Cells are made of organelles and combine into organisms. This three-part structure repeats across every plane of existence.

This framework helps explain why nature feels both connected and full of surprises. The repeating pattern is the connection — the same logic of building wholes from parts appears again and again. But the surprises are real too, because each new primary unit introduces something that simply didn't exist before and can't be fully explained by what came before it.

## Adept

The Periodic Table of Behaviors advances a structural ontological claim that each plane of existence — matter, life, mind, and culture — is organized around a primary unit that functions as a metastable whole: the atom, the cell, the minded animal behaving in context, and the justifying person behaving in context, respectively. These are not arbitrary taxonomic choices. Each primary unit represents a qualitative leap in integrated differentiation — a new kind of entity whose properties and organizational logic cannot be derived solely from the components of the plane beneath it. The cell is not reducible to molecular chemistry, and the person-in-culture is not reducible to animal behavioral ecology, even though each depends on and incorporates the plane below.

Critically, each plane exhibits a triadic internal architecture: parts that compose the primary unit, the primary unit itself, and groups that primary units form into. Subatomic particles compose atoms, which combine into molecules. Organelles compose cells, which combine into organisms. This triadic logic — parts, wholes, groups — is isomorphic across every dimension of complexification, providing a formal grammar for how nature scales.

This repeated structure reveals a dual character in nature's organization. The continuity lies in the recursive logic of integration and differentiation — the same compositional grammar operating at every level. The discontinuity lies in the irreducibility of each primary unit, which introduces emergent properties and causal powers that mark genuine ontological novelty. Grasping this duality is essential for avoiding both reductive flattening, which denies the novelty, and mystical dualism, which denies the continuity.

## Magus

The Periodic Table of Behaviors encodes a metatheoretical commitment with far-reaching implications: that each dimension of behavioral complexification — matter, life, mind, culture — is organized around a primary unit that functions as the metastable locus of integrated differentiation for that plane. The atom, the cell, the minded animal behaving in ecological context, and the justifying person behaving in sociocultural context each occupy this role. The claim is ontological, not merely heuristic. Each primary unit constitutes a genuinely novel kind of entity — one whose causal architecture, boundary conditions, and relational dynamics cannot be exhaustively specified by the compositional logic of the plane from which it emerges. This is emergence in the strong sense: not epistemic surprise but ontological novelty.

The framework's formal elegance lies in its identification of a triadic compositional grammar that recurs isomorphically across every plane. Each dimension resolves into parts that compose the primary unit, the primary unit as an integrated whole, and groups into which primary units aggregate. This is not a loose analogy but a structural invariant — a claim that nature's complexification follows a recursive logic of nested wholes, where each triad at one level becomes a compositional element within the triad above it. The grammar is scale-invariant even as the ontology is scale-dependent.

What this yields is a precise account of how continuity and discontinuity coexist in natural organization. The continuity is formal: the repeated triadic logic of integration and differentiation. The discontinuity is material and causal: each primary unit inaugurates a domain of phenomena — thermodynamic, autopoietic, sentient, justificatory — that obeys constraints irreducible to those governing the plane below. Recognizing this dual structure disciplines inquiry against both eliminative reductionism and inflationary holism, anchoring a naturalistic pluralism in the actual joints of nature's complexification.
