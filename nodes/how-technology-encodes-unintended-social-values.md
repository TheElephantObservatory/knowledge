# How Technology Encodes Unintended Social Values

The hammer that builds the builder

---

> Technologies are never value-neutral — they reshape societies, desires, and what feels 'natural' in ways their designers never intended. Ignoring this produces civilizations shaped by tools built with no moral compass.

There's a common belief that technology is values neutral — that you can use a hammer to kill somebody or to build a house, and it's really about the values of the person using it, not the tool itself. What the consilience project article is pushing back against is this idea, which they call 'nihilistic design' — the stance that it doesn't really matter what's going to occur in the realm of value as a result of a technology. This nihilistic design posture emerged from two related but distinct intellectual traditions: one is a naive optimism that technology always creates good value, and the other is a kind of Smithian invisible hand argument — the idea that the market will sort out which technologies do good and which don't. Both share the assumption that value concerns around technology will take care of themselves. But the critical flaw in the invisible hand argument is the leap of faith that profitable equals good — which is demonstrably wrong. What we need instead is a return to the common sense recognition that technologies encode practices and values into the societies that adopt them, often in ways that are unpredictable and unintentional, through second and third order effects. The automobile is the canonical example: built largely to solve the horseshit problem in cities, it ended up restructuring the entire libidinal economy of adolescence, making the sexual revolution more possible, reshaping the geography of the country through suburban sprawl, and even enabling the national parks system. None of these value consequences were designed in — they emerged from the affordances the technology created, interfacing with the deepest structures of human desire and psychology. This dynamic is further deepened by what happens when a novel technology becomes normal: technologies get absorbed into the basic presuppositions of social functioning, and once that happens, the values they encode stop appearing as values at all — they start appearing as just what humans are, as natural features of the human condition rather than contingent artifacts of a particular technological moment. Lewis Mumford understood this deeply, arguing that technological environments are educating us all the time — not just schools or media, but the built environment itself. A skyscraper is a piece of technology. A city is an ecosystem of technologies. These environments are constantly civilizing or de-civilizing us, forming our humanity, shaping what we take for granted, what we desire, what we think is possible. The more powerful technologies become, and the faster the pace of technological change, the more urgent it becomes to understand this dynamic consciously rather than letting it operate invisibly — because if we don't, we end up with a humanity shaped by technologies designed with no intentional value orientation, formed by nihilistic design at civilizational scale.

## Apprentice

Most people assume technology is just a tool — neutral in itself, good or bad only depending on who uses it. But this assumption quietly lets designers off the hook for thinking seriously about what their inventions will actually do to society. The argument here is that this hands-off attitude — call it nihilistic design — is a serious mistake with civilizational consequences.

The automobile is the clearest example. It was invented largely to clean up horse manure from city streets. Nobody planned for it to reshape teenage sexuality, enable suburban sprawl, or make national parks accessible to ordinary families. These consequences emerged anyway, because the car created new freedoms that interacted with deep human desires in unpredictable ways.

The deeper problem is what happens over time: once a technology becomes ordinary, the values baked into it stop looking like values at all. They start feeling like just human nature. Lewis Mumford saw this clearly — our built environments are constantly educating us, forming what we want, what we expect, what we think is possible. As technologies grow more powerful and change faster, letting this process run unconsciously becomes increasingly dangerous.

## Adept

The doctrine of technological neutrality — the hammer-as-tool argument — has long served as an implicit license for designers and engineers to bracket questions of value. The Consilience Project's critique targets what it calls 'nihilistic design': the posture that value consequences of technology are either irrelevant or self-correcting. This posture draws from two intellectual traditions: a naive progressivist optimism that technology inherently generates good, and a Smithian market-coordination argument that competitive selection will surface beneficial technologies and suppress harmful ones. Both traditions share a common assumption — that value concerns will take care of themselves.

The fatal flaw in the market-coordination version is the conflation of profitability with goodness — a leap that is empirically indefensible. What the critique recovers instead is a more classical understanding: technologies encode affordances that interact with human psychology, social structures, and existing institutions to produce second and third-order effects that are often unintended and unpredictable. The automobile's transformation of adolescent sexuality, urban geography, and national leisure culture illustrates how a technology designed for a narrow sanitation problem can restructure entire domains of human life through its affordances alone.

Mumford's insight deepens this analysis: technological environments function as continuous pedagogical systems, forming perception, desire, and the horizon of the possible. Crucially, as technologies become normalized, the values they instantiate cease to appear contingent — they naturalize into background assumptions about what humans simply are. This normalization dynamic means that the faster the pace of technological change, the more urgently conscious value-orientation in design becomes necessary, lest civilizational formation be ceded entirely to systems built without intentional moral architecture.

## Magus

The neutrality thesis in philosophy of technology — that artifacts are ontologically value-free, their moral valence determined solely by intentional use — has functioned less as a defensible philosophical position than as an ideological formation enabling the abdication of axiological responsibility in design. The Consilience Project's intervention identifies 'nihilistic design' as the operative stance: a compound of naive techno-progressivism and Smithian spontaneous-order theory, both of which externalize value questions from the design process itself. The market-coordination variant is particularly seductive because it appears empirically grounded, yet rests on a foundational equivocation — the substitution of exchange-value for normative value, profitability for goodness — that collapses under minimal scrutiny.

The alternative framework recovers a tradition running from Mumford through Winner and Ellul: technologies are not passive instruments but active structuring agents that encode affordance landscapes, which in turn interact with the deep grammar of human desire, institutional inertia, and social topology to generate emergent value consequences operating well beyond the intentional horizon of any designer. The automobile case is canonical precisely because its second and third-order effects — the restructuring of adolescent libidinal geography, the suburbanization of spatial identity, the democratization of wilderness — were not merely unintended but were generated through the interface of mechanical affordance with psychosexual and sociological structures that no engineering specification could have anticipated.

Mumford's pedagogical thesis adds a further critical dimension: technological environments operate as continuous, largely invisible formation systems, shaping the pre-reflective presuppositions through which subjects constitute their lifeworld. The normalization of a technology is simultaneously its axiological disappearance — values encoded in artifacts cease to appear as values and re-appear as anthropological constants, as features of human nature rather than contingent artifacts of a particular techno-historical moment. At accelerating rates of technological change, this normalization dynamic implies that the window for conscious value-orientation in design narrows precisely as the stakes of its absence expand to civilizational scale.
