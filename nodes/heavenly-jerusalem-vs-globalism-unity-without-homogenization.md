# Heavenly Jerusalem vs. Globalism: Unity Without Homogenization

The nations remain nations.

---

> True unity does not require homogenization. The Heavenly Jerusalem offers a fractal model where nations retain their distinctiveness while participating in a shared center — unlike the globalist project, which forces cohesion through fear and sacrifice, producing herds rather than communities.

The image of the Heavenly Jerusalem offers a different model of unity than the globalist project — and the distinction matters enormously. The globalist vision attempts to create a simulacrum of universal unity through fear, manipulation, incentive, and the sacrifice of increasingly valuable things to force cohesion. This is Moloch worship: binding people together through temptation and coercion, producing a herd rather than a community. The image of the Heavenly Jerusalem is fractal and differentiated: all the kings of the nations come and offer their crowns to the one who is simultaneously king and sacrificial victim — but the nations do not cease to be nations. Their differences are not annulled. Unity does not require homogenization. The alternative to Moloch worship is not a religion of religions that abstracts everyone out of their particular embodied tradition into some universal overview. That is not how human beings actually function. We participate in the world through our instantiated stories — through being Christian, or Jewish, or part of a particular lineage and community. The path forward is not to dissolve those particulars into a managed universal, but to find the fractal levels at which genuine participation, love, and mutual recognition can occur — and to let that spread outward from the local to the global, not be imposed from the global downward.

## Apprentice

There are two very different visions of how humanity might come together. One — the globalist model — tries to unify people from the top down, using fear, incentives, and pressure to flatten differences and force everyone into the same mold. This approach demands that people sacrifice more and more of what makes them distinct in order to belong. It produces compliance, not genuine community.

The alternative is captured in the ancient image of the Heavenly Jerusalem, where all the kings of the nations bring their crowns to a shared center — but the nations remain nations. Their cultures, stories, and traditions are not erased. Unity here is not about making everyone the same; it is about finding real points of connection between people who remain rooted in their own particular lives and communities.

This insight challenges the idea that the only way to avoid conflict is to abstract everyone out of their traditions into some universal framework. Human beings do not actually live that way. People participate in the world through specific stories, lineages, and communities. Genuine unity grows outward from the local — from love and mutual recognition between real, embodied people — rather than being imposed downward from some managed global system.

## Adept

This perspective draws a sharp distinction between two models of universal unity. The globalist project, as characterized here, operates through what Jonathan Pageau and others have called "Moloch worship" — a binding-together achieved through escalating sacrifice, coercion, and the management of incentives. It produces a simulacrum of unity: a herd held in place by fear rather than a community animated by love. The coherence it achieves is purchased at the cost of genuine particularity, flattening human difference into an administrable homogeneity.

Against this stands the image of the Heavenly Jerusalem as a fractal model of differentiated unity. In this vision, drawn from the Book of Revelation, the kings of the nations bring their glory into the city — but the nations persist as nations. The center holds not by abolishing difference but by being simultaneously sovereign and sacrificial, a pattern that invites free participation rather than demanding submission. Unity is not the erasure of hierarchy and distinction but their proper ordering around a self-giving center.

Critically, the argument rejects the notion that the corrective to top-down globalism is some syncretic "religion of religions" — an abstracted universal that lifts people out of their embodied traditions into a detached overview. Human beings participate in reality through instantiated stories: through being Christian, Jewish, rooted in a particular lineage. The path forward involves recognizing fractal levels of genuine participation and mutual recognition, allowing unity to propagate upward from the local and concrete rather than being imposed downward from the abstract and managerial.

## Magus

The distinction articulated here operates at the junction of political theology and symbolic ontology. The globalist project is identified not merely as a political program but as a liturgical structure — a form of Moloch worship in which cohesion is achieved through the progressive sacrifice of increasingly valuable particularities. The unity it produces is simulacral: a managed totality sustained by the very mechanisms of fear and incentive that preclude genuine communion. The herd is the ontological product of this sacrificial economy — subjects bound not by mutual recognition but by shared subjection to an abstracting sovereign principle.

The Heavenly Jerusalem functions here as a counter-image operating on fractal logic. In the Johannine apocalyptic vision, the kings of the nations process toward a center that is simultaneously royal and kenotic — the slain Lamb who is also sovereign. The nations do not undergo dissolution; their crowns are offered, not annihilated. This is differentiated unity: a participatory hierarchy in which each level retains its integrity precisely through its orientation toward a self-emptying center. The pattern is fractal because the same structure of sacrificial love and free offering repeats at every scale, from the household to the civilization.

The argument forecloses the perennialist escape route — the construction of a meta-religious universal that would abstract practitioners out of their instantiated traditions into a synoptic overview. Such a move merely reproduces the globalizing gesture at the spiritual level. Human participation in reality is always already embodied, storied, particular. The alternative to Moloch is not Babel's inverse but the organic propagation of genuine recognition across fractal boundaries — unity ascending from concrete, local acts of love rather than descending from managerial abstraction.
