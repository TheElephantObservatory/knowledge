# Why Information Abundance Produces Wisdom Famine

Where did you go for wisdom? Anxious silence.

---

> Modern civilization drowns in information yet starves for wisdom — and wisdom cannot be accumulated like data. It requires communal practices, trusted role models, and ongoing confrontation with self-deception, none of which can be privatized or individualized without destroying the very thing sought.

The distinction between information, knowledge, and wisdom is not merely a hierarchy of sophistication — it is a diagnosis of civilizational failure. T.S. Eliot captured it precisely in the Choruses from the Rock: 'Where is the knowledge we have lost in information? Where is the wisdom we have lost in knowledge?' The more information we accumulate, the more we need knowledge to organize it. The more knowledge we have, the more we need wisdom to know what to do with it. And wisdom is not simply more knowledge. Wisdom is the ongoing, disciplined confrontation with self-deception. The very cognitive processes that allow us to ignore irrelevant information — the processes that make us intelligent — also make us perennially prone to illusion and self-deception, every single moment. A child must become an adult for certain truths to be disclosed. But as the child is to the adult, the adult is to the sage. Why would we imagine that our capacity for self-deception simply ceases at age twenty-one? It does not. And yet modernity has produced a culture with a glut of information and a famine of wisdom. When John Vervaeke asks his students where they go for information, they hold up their phones. Where for knowledge? Perhaps the university. Where for wisdom? Anxious silence. That silence is the meaning crisis. Because wisdom requires binding and transformation. It is not a possession you accumulate; it is a way of being you undergo. It is imaginal and mythical and narrative in nature. It requires role models, communities, ecologies of practice — people doing things you do not yet understand, whose wisdom you trust enough to commit to, so that you can undergo the growth that discloses the truths they already inhabit. Meaning must be communally created, curated, and corrected — which is why the autodidactic, individualized, 'spiritual but not religious' approach cannot supply it. If you use terms in a completely idiosyncratic way, truth becomes unavailable and correction becomes impossible. That is not spirituality — it is the privatization of religio, and it is precisely what the meaning crisis looks like from the inside. The 'spiritual but not religious' position is, at its root, a category mistake: an attempt to individualize the binding, to make it a private possession. Malcolm Guite's analogy is apt: saying 'I like beer but I don't believe in organized breweries' captures the incoherence. What the spiritual-but-not-religious person is actually experiencing, however, deserves genuine sympathy: it is a symptom of the trust apocalypse. Trust in judicial, political, legal, and religious institutions has collapsed — but this is not unique to religion. Trust is restored not through legislation or credentialing but through credibility-enhancing displays — watching people do things you don't yet understand but trust reflect a wisdom you don't yet have, and committing to them so that you can undergo the transformation that discloses what they know. There is no panacea practice, no single technique that substitutes for a living community embedded in tradition.

## Apprentice

We live in a world overflowing with information but desperately short on wisdom. T.S. Eliot saw this coming nearly a century ago when he asked where the wisdom went that we lost in knowledge, and where the knowledge went that we lost in information. Today, anyone can pull out a phone for facts, maybe visit a university for deeper understanding — but when asked where to go for wisdom, most people draw a blank. That blank is the heart of what John Vervaeke calls the meaning crisis.

Wisdom is not just more knowledge stacked higher. It is the hard, ongoing work of catching yourself in your own self-deception. The same mental shortcuts that make us smart also make us prone to fooling ourselves — and that doesn't magically stop when we become adults. Just as a child must grow into an adult to understand certain truths, an adult must grow further still. That growth requires trusted guides, living communities, and shared practices — not just private reflection.

This is why the popular idea of being "spiritual but not religious" runs into trouble. As Malcolm Guite puts it, it is a bit like saying you enjoy beer but don't believe in breweries. Spirituality that is entirely private loses the communal correction and shared commitment that make real transformation possible. The impulse behind it is understandable — trust in institutions has collapsed nearly everywhere — but the solution is not to go it alone. It is to find and build communities where credibility is earned through lived example, where people undergo transformation together.

## Adept

T.S. Eliot's lament in the Choruses from the Rock — the loss of knowledge in information, and wisdom in knowledge — is not merely poetic nostalgia but a precise civilizational diagnosis. The information-knowledge-wisdom hierarchy is not a smooth continuum of increasing sophistication; each transition demands a qualitatively different capacity. Information requires filtering; knowledge requires integration; wisdom requires the disciplined, recursive confrontation with self-deception. As John Vervaeke argues, the very cognitive processes that grant us relevance realization — the ability to ignore irrelevant data — simultaneously render us perpetually vulnerable to illusion. Self-deception does not terminate at cognitive maturity. The child-to-adult transformation that discloses certain truths is not the final such transformation; the adult-to-sage transition is structurally analogous and equally necessary.

The meaning crisis, in Vervaeke's framing, is precisely the absence of legitimate wisdom ecologies. When students can identify sources for information and knowledge but fall silent when asked about wisdom, they reveal a cultural vacuum. Wisdom is not propositional but participatory and transformative — imaginal, mythical, narrative in nature. It requires binding: commitment to role models, communities, and ecologies of practice whose operations one does not yet fully comprehend but trusts enough to undergo.

This is why the "spiritual but not religious" stance constitutes a category mistake — an attempt to privatize religio, the very binding that makes wisdom transmission possible. Malcolm Guite's brewery analogy exposes the incoherence, but the impulse deserves sympathy: it is a symptom of the trust apocalypse affecting all institutions, not religion alone. Trust is rebuilt through credibility-enhancing displays — watching others embody what they profess — not through credentialing or legislation. Idiosyncratic spiritual vocabularies foreclose communal correction and render truth claims uncheckable. No panacea practice substitutes for a living tradition embedded in community.

## Magus

Eliot's interrogation in the Choruses from the Rock operates not as rhetorical flourish but as diagnostic taxonomy: the information-knowledge-wisdom distinction indexes qualitatively discontinuous epistemic and existential capacities, each requiring its own mode of cultivation and each vulnerable to its own characteristic pathology. The critical move, developed extensively in Vervaeke's cognitive scientific framework, is recognizing that relevance realization — the very machinery enabling intelligent behavior through the systematic ignoring of irrelevant information — is simultaneously the engine of perennial self-deception. Intelligence and illusion share a common root. Wisdom, then, is not an epistemic achievement but an ongoing existential practice: the recursive, disciplined confrontation with the self-deceptive tendencies intrinsic to cognition itself. The child-to-adult developmental disclosure is structurally iterable; there is no principled reason to posit a terminus at conventional maturity.

The meaning crisis, so understood, is the civilizational absence of legitimate ecologies of practice capable of facilitating transformative participation — what Vervaeke characterizes as the imaginal, mythical, and narrative dimensions of knowing that are irreducible to propositional content. Wisdom requires binding (religio in its etymological sense): commitment to communities, role models, and practices whose rationale is disclosed only through the transformation they occasion. This is participatory knowing in its strongest sense — not belief about but conformity to.

The "spiritual but not religious" position thus enacts a precise category error: the privatization of religio, severing the binding from its communal substrate. Guite's brewery analogy is apt but understates the epistemic stakes: idiosyncratic vocabularies do not merely impoverish practice, they render correction structurally impossible and truth systematically unavailable. The underlying driver — institutional trust collapse — is real but not unique to religion; it reflects a broader failure of credibility-enhancing displays across judicial, political, and epistemic institutions. Restoration demands not new propositional frameworks but renewed participatory ecologies where transformation is witnessed, trusted, and undergone communally. No technique substitutes for tradition.
