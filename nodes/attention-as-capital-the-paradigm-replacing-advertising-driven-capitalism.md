# Attention as Capital: The Paradigm Replacing Advertising-Driven Capitalism

Your calendar is the new deed of ownership.

---

> Attentionalism names the emerging paradigm beyond capitalism: whoever controls their own attention — understood as time — controls their real capital. Advertising, like slavery before it, may be enormously profitable yet increasingly unacceptable, as people develop the tools and will to refuse cognitive colonization.

Attentionalism is Bard's name for the emerging paradigm that supersedes capitalism — not by abolishing it, but by identifying a resource larger than capital that capital cannot fully colonize: human attention, understood as time. Capitalism required advertising because it needed to manufacture demand for an ever-expanding supply of goods. Advertising worked when people could not easily find alternatives and had not yet developed the cognitive immune response to recognize manipulation. All those conditions are now eroding. The parallel Bard draws is to slavery: slavery was not abolished because it was unprofitable — it was enormously profitable — but because a critical mass of people decided it was unacceptable, and because an alternative mode of production turned out to be more productive for new technological conditions. The same transition is underway with advertising. The attentionalist — the person who treats their calendar as their primary asset, refuses to have their attention colonized by algorithms serving others' interests, and demands that every tool they use serves their own purposes — is the emerging elite. Not necessarily wealthy, but cognitively sovereign. The theocrat, in Bard's vocabulary, is the attentionalist: someone who understands that data is a form of capital, attention is a form of data, and controlling your own attention is therefore controlling your own capital. Platforms like Perplexity, which provide information without advertising, are early evidence that this transition is underway.

## Apprentice

Alexander Bard argues that we are entering a new era he calls "attentionalism." The core idea is simple: your attention — how you spend your time and what you focus on — is now more valuable than money. Capitalism needed advertising to convince people to buy things they didn't know they wanted. For a long time, that worked because people had few alternatives and hadn't learned to recognize when they were being manipulated. Both of those conditions are changing fast.

Bard draws a striking comparison to slavery. Slavery wasn't ended because it stopped being profitable — it was wildly profitable. It ended because enough people decided it was morally unacceptable, and because new technology made a different kind of economy possible. Bard sees advertising heading down the same path. It still makes enormous money, but a growing number of people are rejecting it, and new tools are emerging that deliver information and services without demanding your attention as payment.

The person who thrives in this new world is what Bard calls the "attentionalist" — someone who guards their calendar fiercely, refuses to let algorithms dictate what they see and think, and insists that every tool they use works for them, not for an advertiser. This person may not be rich in the traditional sense, but they are sovereign over their own mind. That, Bard suggests, is the new form of power.

## Adept

Alexander Bard's concept of attentionalism identifies a paradigm shift that does not abolish capitalism but supersedes it by recognizing a resource that capital cannot fully colonize: human attention, understood fundamentally as time. Capitalism's dependence on advertising arose from a structural need to manufacture demand for ever-expanding production. Advertising functioned under specific conditions — limited consumer alternatives and an undeveloped cognitive immune response to persuasion. Bard argues that both conditions are now in terminal erosion, driven by information abundance and growing media literacy.

The historical parallel Bard deploys is deliberately provocative: slavery. The institution was not dismantled because it became unprofitable — it remained enormously so — but because moral consensus shifted and an alternative mode of production proved better suited to emerging technological conditions. Advertising, Bard contends, occupies an analogous position today. Its profitability is not in question; its legitimacy is. Tools like Perplexity, which deliver information without advertising intermediation, represent early structural evidence that post-advertising models are viable and preferred.

The figure at the center of this transition is the attentionalist — Bard's term for someone who treats their calendar as their primary asset and refuses algorithmic colonization of their cognitive resources. In Bard's broader vocabulary, this figure is the "theocrat": one who grasps that data is a form of capital, attention is a form of data, and therefore sovereignty over one's own attention constitutes sovereignty over one's own capital. The attentionalist is not necessarily wealthy but is cognitively sovereign — and that sovereignty, Bard argues, is becoming the defining marker of the emerging elite.

## Magus

Bard's attentionalism operates as a meta-economic thesis: not a negation of capitalism but its sublation, identifying in human attention — rigorously defined as temporality itself — a resource that exceeds capital's capacity for enclosure. The argument's architecture rests on a structural analysis of advertising as capitalism's necessary supplement. Overproduction demands manufactured demand; advertising fulfills this function only under conditions of informational asymmetry and pre-critical reception. Bard's claim is that both conditions are undergoing irreversible degradation — the first through networked information abundance, the second through what might be termed the immunological maturation of the cognitive subject under conditions of saturation.

The slavery analogy is not rhetorical decoration but a precise structural homology. Bard insists on the distinction between profitability and sustainability: slavery's abolition was overdetermined by moral delegitimation and the superior productivity of wage labor under industrial conditions, not by any internal economic failure. Advertising's trajectory, he argues, follows the same logic — still profitable, increasingly delegitimized, and facing displacement by tools that serve user sovereignty rather than advertiser extraction. Perplexity and analogous platforms function here as early instantiations of post-advertising information architecture.

The attentionalist — whom Bard identifies with the "theocrat" in his broader sociological taxonomy — is the subject position adequate to this transition. The syllogism is compact: data is capital; attention is data; therefore attentional sovereignty is capital sovereignty. The attentionalist treats the calendar as the primary ledger, refuses algorithmic intermediation that serves exogenous interests, and demands instrumental transparency from every platform and tool. This figure is defined not by wealth accumulation but by cognitive self-possession — a form of sovereignty that Bard positions as the emergent axis of social stratification, displacing financial capital as the primary determinant of elite status.
