# Value and Consciousness as Baseline Reality, Not Exceptions to Prove

They raised their children differently.

---

> The claim that consciousness and value are absent from reality is the extraordinary claim requiring extraordinary evidence — not the other way around. Most humans across history perceived a meaningful cosmos; reductive materialism is a recent, culturally narrow exception that its own proponents contradict in how they actually live.

The burden of proof, properly understood, lies with those who claim that value and consciousness are NOT intrinsic to reality — not with those who claim they are. For the vast majority of human history, across hundreds of thousands of years and cultures far more intellectually sophisticated than we tend to credit, the world was experienced as meaningful, humans were understood to be here for a purpose, and the cosmos was perceived as having preferences for human action. A relatively small number of people — concentrated in Western intellectual and techno-scientific elites — were gradually talked out of this belief. These are people who, in their serious philosophical work, claim that consciousness is an epiphenomenal blip, that we are deterministic meat machines, that value is a fiction. And yet these same people do not raise their children that way. That is a profound performative contradiction. Iain McGilchrist's work adds a neurological dimension: radical left-hemispheric dominance produces a phenomenological world genuinely absent of value — where zombie thought experiments feel philosophically serious, where empathy is attenuated, where the intrinsic beauty of a tree simply does not register as real. The epistemology of reductive materialism is not a neutral view from nowhere — it is the view from a particular kind of perceptual impoverishment. Anthro-ontology, the methodology at the heart of Cosmo-erotic humanism, says that a healthy, well-functioning human nervous system perceives value as readily and as accurately as it perceives space and time. You can tell a truer statement from a falser one. You can perceive a more beautiful thing from a less beautiful one. These are not arbitrary — they are as real as gauging the speed of a ball.

## Apprentice

For most of human history, people across every culture experienced the world as deeply meaningful. They felt that consciousness mattered, that beauty was real, and that the universe had something like preferences for how we should live. This wasn't naivety — these were often extraordinarily sophisticated civilizations. Only recently, and only within a narrow slice of Western intellectual culture, did the idea take hold that none of this is real — that we're essentially biological machines in a valueless void.

But here's the telling detail: the very people who argue this in their academic work don't raise their children that way. They still act as though love matters, beauty is real, and some choices are genuinely better than others. That gap between what they claim to believe and how they actually live is a profound contradiction.

The insight here is that the burden of proof has been placed on the wrong side. It should fall on those making the extraordinary claim — that the meaning virtually all humans have always perceived is an illusion. A healthy human nervous system perceives value as naturally as it perceives distance or speed. Denying that isn't neutrality — it's a particular kind of perceptual narrowing mistaken for objectivity.

## Adept

This line of thinking inverts a deeply entrenched assumption in modern philosophy: that those who claim reality is meaningful bear the burden of proof against a default position of valueless mechanism. In fact, the historical and cross-cultural default is overwhelmingly the opposite. Across hundreds of thousands of years and civilizations of remarkable intellectual depth, human beings perceived value, purpose, and consciousness as intrinsic features of the cosmos. Reductive materialism — the view that consciousness is epiphenomenal and value is fictive — is a recent, geographically concentrated anomaly. The burden of proof properly belongs to this minority position.

Iain McGilchrist's hemispheric analysis provides a neurological account of how this inversion occurred. Radical left-hemispheric dominance generates a phenomenological world genuinely denuded of qualitative richness — one where zombie thought experiments seem philosophically serious, where empathy is structurally attenuated, and where the intrinsic beauty of a living thing fails to register as ontologically real. The epistemology of reductive materialism is thus not a neutral view from nowhere but the view from a specific mode of perceptual impoverishment mistaken for rigor.

Anthro-ontology, the methodological core of Cosmo-erotic Humanism as developed by Marc Gafni and colleagues, responds by taking the well-functioning human nervous system as a legitimate instrument of ontological perception. Value perception — the capacity to distinguish truer from falser, more beautiful from less beautiful — operates with the same reliability as spatial or temporal perception. The performative contradiction of materialists who deny value in print yet affirm it in practice reveals not hypocrisy but the impossibility of actually inhabiting the worldview they profess.

## Magus

The standard framing of modern epistemology positions reductive materialism as the null hypothesis — a parsimonious baseline against which claims of intrinsic value, purpose, or consciousness must justify themselves. This argument demonstrates that such framing constitutes a profound misattribution of evidential burden. The anthropological and historical record reveals that the perception of a value-laden, purposive cosmos is the overwhelming default of human cognition across virtually all cultures and epochs, many of which possessed intellectual architectures of extraordinary sophistication. The mechanistic, disenchanted worldview is the anomaly — a late-arriving, culturally circumscribed deviation whose proponents betray its unlivability through systematic performative contradiction: they deny the reality of value in their philosophical work while presupposing it in every domain of serious human engagement, from child-rearing to moral judgment.

McGilchrist's lateralization thesis supplies a crucial explanatory mechanism. The phenomenological world generated by left-hemispheric hyper-dominance is not merely abstractive but ontologically impoverished — it is a world in which qualia, relational depth, and intrinsic significance are structurally absent from experience. Within this attentional mode, philosophical zombies appear conceivable, the hard problem seems intractable, and beauty registers as mere subjective preference. The epistemology of eliminative materialism thus reflects not a view from nowhere but the specific deliverances of a particular — and demonstrably partial — mode of hemispheric attention.

Anthro-ontology, as articulated within the framework of Cosmo-erotic Humanism, reclaims the integrated human nervous system as a calibrated instrument of ontological disclosure. Value perception — the discrimination of truer from falser, more beautiful from less beautiful — is posited as epistemically continuous with spatial and temporal perception: fallible, refinable, but genuinely tracking features of reality. The capacity to perceive the speed of an approaching object and the capacity to perceive the beauty of a living form are, on this account, exercises of the same fundamental epistemic faculty operating across different registers of the real.
