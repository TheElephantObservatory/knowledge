# Defining Culture through Behavioral Repertoires and Justification Systems

The silent repertoire and the spoken law

---

> The word 'culture' secretly names two different things: the learned behavioral habits animals share, and the uniquely human practice of giving reasons and arguing about norms. Untangling these two solves a 150-year definitional crisis.

One of the deepest sources of confusion in defining culture — a problem that has plagued scholars for over 150 years, from Tylor's 1871 'complex whole' to the 160+ competing definitions catalogued in 1952, to Gustaf Yoda's recent conclusion that the concept is indispensable yet fundamentally undefinable — is that the word 'culture' is actually doing double duty across two ontologically distinct phenomena that get tangled together. There is what we might call little-c culture: shared, learned behavioral repertoires that coordinate group behavior. This is something animals demonstrably have. Chimpanzees, cetaceans, and birds transmit behavioral patterns across generations through social learning. Then there is capital-C Culture: the dimension of justification — the self-reflective, reason-giving, account-giving dialogue that emerges specifically among language-using persons. This is the plane of existence where systems of knowledge, norms, values, and rules are not merely enacted but explicitly articulated, debated, and transmitted through propositional language. The failure to distinguish these two frequencies of behavior — the mental-animal dimension of behavioral repertoire and the culture-person plane of justification — is precisely why scholars keep producing definitions that are either too broad (including animal behavior) or too narrow (missing the embodied, procedural dimension). The Tree of Knowledge framework offers a resolution: by mapping behavior onto distinct ontological layers — biophysical ecology, technological artifacts, behavioral repertoire, and systems of justification — we can finally give culture a precise, non-circular definition rather than retreating to common-sense intuition.

## Apprentice

The word 'culture' has been causing arguments among scholars for over 150 years, and one reason is surprisingly simple: it is actually being used to describe two very different things at once. The first is the kind of culture animals have — learned habits and behaviors that get passed down through groups by watching and imitating. Chimpanzees, dolphins, and birds all do this. The second is something distinctly human — the ability to talk about behavior, argue over rules, justify norms, and transmit ideas through language and explicit reasoning.

Because scholars keep mixing these two things together under one word, every definition they produce ends up either too wide (accidentally including animals) or too narrow (missing the habitual, embodied side of human life). The result has been over 160 competing definitions and no consensus.

The resolution proposed here is to treat these as genuinely separate layers of reality. Behavioral repertoires — the habits and routines groups share — form one layer. Systems of justification — the explicit, language-based world of values, norms, and knowledge — form another. Giving each its own name and place in a broader framework finally allows culture to be defined precisely, without circularity or endless qualification.

## Adept

Since Tylor's foundational 1871 definition of culture as a 'complex whole,' the concept has resisted consensus. By 1952, Kroeber and Kluckhohn had catalogued over 160 competing definitions, and the problem has not resolved since. The core difficulty, this analysis argues, is not merely semantic imprecision but a genuine ontological conflation: the single term 'culture' is being applied across two categorically distinct phenomena operating at different levels of behavioral organization.

The first phenomenon — here called little-c culture — refers to shared, socially learned behavioral repertoires that coordinate group life. This is demonstrably not unique to humans; chimpanzees, cetaceans, and passerine birds all exhibit transgenerational transmission of behavioral patterns through social learning. The second phenomenon — capital-C Culture — refers to the plane of justification: the self-reflective, propositionally structured dialogue through which language-using persons explicitly articulate, contest, and transmit norms, values, and systems of knowledge. The distinction is not merely one of complexity but of ontological kind — the difference between enacted behavior and reasoned account-giving.

The Tree of Knowledge framework offers a principled resolution by stratifying behavior across discrete ontological layers: biophysical ecology, technological artifacts, behavioral repertoire, and systems of justification. Mapping 'culture' onto this architecture dissolves the definitional impasse. Little-c culture finds its home in the behavioral repertoire layer, shared with other social animals. Capital-C Culture belongs to the justification layer, emergent specifically with language-using persons. The result is a non-circular, ontologically grounded definition that neither over-extends to all social learning nor under-describes the full texture of human cultural life.

## Magus

The intractability of the culture concept — from Tylor's syncretic 1871 formulation through the Kroeber-Kluckhohn survey of 1952 to Gustaf Yoda's recent verdict of indispensable-yet-undefinable — reflects not merely the polysemy endemic to humanistic vocabulary but a deep ontological equivocation built into the term's ordinary use. The argument advanced here is that 'culture' functions as a false homonym, silently indexing two phenomena that differ not in degree but in kind: socially transmitted behavioral repertoires on one hand, and the propositionally structured plane of justification on the other.

The first phenomenon — behavioral repertoire, or little-c culture — is phylogenetically widespread. Comparative ethology and cultural primatology have established beyond reasonable dispute that chimpanzees, cetaceans, and corvids transmit locally specific behavioral variants across generations via social learning mechanisms. This is culture in the sense of coordinated, learned, group-differentiating practice. The second phenomenon — capital-C Culture — is constitutively tied to language-using personhood. It names the dimension of existence in which norms, values, and epistemic commitments are not merely enacted but explicitly thematized, contested, and reproduced through propositional discourse. The distinction maps roughly onto Sellars' contrast between the space of causes and the space of reasons, or Habermas' differentiation of strategic and communicative action, though the ontological framing here is more granular.

The Tree of Knowledge framework operationalizes this distinction by positing discrete ontological strata — biophysical ecology, artifact-technological systems, behavioral repertoire, and justificatory systems — each with its own emergent properties and irreducible to the layer below. Assigning little-c culture to the behavioral stratum and capital-C Culture to the justificatory stratum yields definitions that are non-circular, cross-disciplinarily tractable, and capable of accommodating both the embodied-procedural dimension that cognitivist definitions routinely elide and the normative-discursive dimension that behaviorist accounts cannot capture. The payoff is not merely taxonomic tidiness but a resolution to the explanatory deadlock that has made 'culture' simultaneously indispensable and theoretically ungovernable.
