# Building Coordination Through Shared Value Stories

The intimate architecture of civilizational survival

---

> Every global crisis is ultimately a coordination problem, and coordination requires shared values. Without a living, common story of what matters, civilizational cooperation becomes structurally impossible — making the renewal of shared meaning the most critical intervention available.

The thread from global coordination back to shared story of value is not metaphorical — it is a causal chain. Coordination, at the scale required to address any existential risk, cannot happen without resonance between the parties involved. But resonance cannot emerge without coherence, and coherence cannot be achieved without intimacy. And intimacy — whether between two people in a relationship or between civilizations — is always grounded in shared value. If there is no shared story of value, there is no basis for intimacy, which means coherence cannot form, resonance cannot arise, and coordination becomes impossible. Since every existential risk is structurally a global coordination problem, the absence of a shared story of value is not merely a cultural deficiency — it is a direct civilizational threat. This is why the work of enacting a new story of value is not peripheral to the metacrisis response; it is, in a very precise sense, the most load-bearing intervention available. Infrastructure improvements and regulatory reforms are necessary but insufficient. What is required is something in the register of superstructure — a living, participatory story of value that can hold our diversity in a context of genuine coherence. This is not a faith-based claim but a radically empirical one, emerging from both interior and exterior sciences. The think tank is not, at its core, an intellectual enterprise. It is a kind of religious-activist, world-philosophy project — a time-between-worlds, time-between-stories endeavor — madly intent on downloading a new story of value into culture before the coordination failures of the metacrisis become irreversible.

## Apprentice

Most people working on big global problems — climate change, pandemics, nuclear risk — focus on policy, technology, or institutions. But there is a deeper problem underneath all of these: people and nations cannot coordinate at the scale required unless they genuinely resonate with one another. And that kind of resonance doesn't just happen. It requires trust, and trust requires real closeness, and closeness requires that people share some fundamental sense of what matters and why.

This argument traces a precise chain: no shared story of value means no genuine intimacy, no intimacy means no coherence, no coherence means no resonance, and no resonance means coordination fails. Since every major existential risk — from ecological collapse to AI misalignment — is at its core a coordination problem, the absence of shared meaning isn't just a cultural sadness. It is a direct threat to survival.

The implication is striking: fixing infrastructure or passing better laws, while necessary, cannot be sufficient. What is actually needed is something more like a new shared story — not a religion imposed from above, but a living, participatory account of value that can hold human diversity together. This is presented not as a spiritual hope but as a practical, empirically grounded conclusion.

## Adept

The argument advanced here treats the metacrisis not as a collection of discrete systemic failures but as a single structural problem: the collapse of the conditions necessary for global coordination. Drawing on both systems thinking and depth psychology, it proposes a causal chain running from shared story of value through intimacy, coherence, and resonance to coordination capacity. Each link is load-bearing. Remove the foundation — shared value — and the entire architecture of collective response collapses.

This reframes the conventional hierarchy of interventions. Infrastructure investment and regulatory reform operate at the level of base structure. But base structure changes require prior agreement on what is worth protecting and why — which is a superstructural question. The insight here is that superstructure is not epiphenomenal to material conditions; in a coordination crisis, it is causally prior. A civilization that cannot tell a coherent shared story of value cannot mobilize around any threat, however technically well-understood.

The claim is explicitly presented as empirical rather than faith-based, drawing on both exterior sciences (complexity theory, coordination economics, political ecology) and interior sciences (contemplative traditions, developmental psychology, phenomenology). The practical conclusion is that the most leveraged intervention in the metacrisis is the cultural and philosophical work of enacting a new story of value — participatory, pluralistic, and alive — before coordination failures become irreversible. This is described as work in the register of world-philosophy and civilizational renewal rather than conventional policy or activism.

## Magus

The argument constructs a strict deductive chain within the ontology of coordination theory: existential risk mitigation is structurally isomorphic with large-scale coordination problems; coordination at civilizational scale is contingent on resonance between agents; resonance is a function of coherence in shared meaning-structures; coherence is itself downstream of intimacy — understood here not sentimentally but as the condition of genuine intersubjective transparency; and intimacy, at every scale from dyadic to inter-civilizational, is grounded in shared axiological narrative. The logical corollary is that the absence of a living, participatory story of value is not merely a cultural deficit but a direct causal antecedent to coordination failure and, by extension, to existential catastrophe.

This positions the argument in productive tension with both Marxist base-superstructure orthodoxy and Habermasian communicative rationality. Against the former, it insists that superstructure — specifically, the axiological narratives through which value is enacted and transmitted — is not reducible to or derivable from material conditions, but is in coordination crises causally prior. Against the latter, it suggests that procedural rationality alone cannot generate the resonance necessary for genuine coordination; what is required is something thicker — a shared story with affective, aesthetic, and ontological dimensions, not merely propositional consensus.

The epistemological grounding is deliberately integrative, invoking both third-person exterior sciences (complexity theory, evolutionary game theory, political ecology) and first-person interior sciences (phenomenology, contemplative epistemology, depth psychology). The resulting project is characterized as neither purely intellectual nor conventionally activist, but as inhabiting the liminal register of world-philosophy — a civilizational intervention aimed at precipitating a new story of value into cultural circulation before the metacrisis exhausts the remaining degrees of freedom for collective response.
