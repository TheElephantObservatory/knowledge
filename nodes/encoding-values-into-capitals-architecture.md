# Encoding Values Into Capital's Architecture

When we talk about how to navigate the psychedelic space — or really any space where important innovations are trying to get into the world — there's a false binary that often gets presented: either you blow up the entire macroeconomic system and start from scratch, or you sell out to extractive capitalism. But there's a third path, which is software patches on the existing system. You don't need a tabula rasa. You work with what you've got, but you bake different values into the architecture of the organization at inception. The clearest example of this is the Benefit Corporation model — where instead of pure bottom-line shareholder fiduciary responsibility, you embed into your operating agreements, your shareholder agreements, your governance structures, a commitment to people, planet, and profit. But there's an even more interesting innovation happening at the capital level. Greylock Ventures articulated what they call the 1 to 3x return space. Most venture funds need 10x returns because nine out of ten of their investments die — so the one that survives has to cover the whole portfolio. That structure systematically underfunds pro-social and important innovations that don't have moonshot economics. The 1 to 3x model says: we want to give you your money back, we want to become self-perpetuating rather than a leaky-bucket nonprofit that needs a black-tie gala every year to stay alive, and we cap returns at 3x because this is a pro-social mission and we don't want to over-harvest. That's the key insight — you can design the terms of capital itself to encode your values, and in doing so, you change what the organization is structurally capable of becoming.

## Apprentice

There's a common assumption that anyone trying to do good in the world faces an impossible choice: either tear down the whole economic system or compromise your values to survive inside it. But there's a third option that often gets overlooked — patching the existing system from within, rather than replacing it or surrendering to it. The idea is simple: you don't need to start from a blank slate. Instead, you build your values directly into the rules of your organization from the very beginning. One practical example is the Benefit Corporation, a legal structure that requires a company to consider people and the environment alongside profit — not as an afterthought, but as a legal obligation. Even more interesting is a new approach to investment funds that caps financial returns at three times the original investment. Traditional venture funds need enormous returns because most of their bets fail. But that structure quietly excludes important projects that help people without becoming billion-dollar businesses. A capped-return fund says: give investors a fair return, keep the fund self-sustaining, and don't extract more than is fair. The deeper insight is that the rules governing money and organizations are not fixed laws of nature — they are design choices, and those choices can be made differently.

## Adept

A persistent false binary haunts mission-driven enterprise: the choice between systemic overthrow and capitulation to extractive capitalism. This framing obscures a more tractable path — the deliberate re-engineering of institutional architecture within existing legal and financial infrastructure. Rather than awaiting a tabula rasa, this approach encodes values at the level of organizational inception, treating governance documents, operating agreements, and capital structures as programmable surfaces. The Benefit Corporation is the clearest instantiation of this logic. By embedding fiduciary duties to stakeholders — people, planet, and profit — directly into corporate charter and shareholder agreements, it transforms legal obligation rather than merely appealing to goodwill. But the more consequential innovation operates at the capital formation layer. Conventional venture capital is structurally incompatible with pro-social enterprise: the power-law return requirement, driven by high portfolio mortality rates, systematically excludes businesses whose value is real but whose economics are not moonshot-scale. The 1-to-3x return model, articulated by Greylock Ventures, addresses this structural exclusion directly. By capping returns at three times invested capital, the fund signals that it is optimizing for mission sustainability rather than maximum extraction. The fund becomes self-perpetuating — returning and redeploying capital — rather than dependent on philanthropic subsidy. The conceptual core here is that capital terms are not neutral instruments; they are value-laden design decisions that shape what organizations can structurally become. Changing the terms changes the selection pressure on which innovations survive.

## Magus

The dominant discourse around mission-capital alignment tends to oscillate between two inadequate poles: utopian institutional rupture and pragmatic assimilation into extractive financial logics. What this perspective proposes instead is a form of immanent institutional critique — working within existing legal and financial grammars while reprogramming their generative rules at the point of inception. The key theoretical move is to treat organizational documents and capital instruments not as neutral containers but as constitutive architectures that encode and reproduce particular value systems. This is, in effect, a Latourian insight applied to corporate law: the non-human actors — the operating agreement, the shareholder covenant, the fund prospectus — are not passive reflections of intent but active participants that stabilize or destabilize particular organizational trajectories over time. The Benefit Corporation operationalizes this by expanding the fiduciary surface, legally obligating directors to internalize externalities that shareholder primacy doctrine systematically excludes. More theoretically rich is the intervention at the level of return structure. Standard venture capital's power-law dependency — the necessity of 10x returns to compensate for portfolio mortality — functions as a hidden filter, a structural bias that reproduces the dominance of scalable, extractive business models while rendering pro-social innovations economically illegible. The 1-to-3x return architecture dissolves this filter by decoupling capital sustainability from hypergrowth imperatives. The fund becomes an enduring, self-regenerating institution rather than a time-bounded extraction vehicle. Crucially, the return cap is not merely a concession — it is a signal that redefines the optimization target and, in doing so, alters the entire selection environment for what kinds of enterprises can be born and survive within it.
