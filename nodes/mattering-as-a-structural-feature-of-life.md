# Mattering as a Structural Feature of Life

The ancient knot where mind meets world

---

> Mattering isn't a feeling you add to a neutral world — it's built into life itself. Living beings are structurally organized around what matters to them, and this is the foundation of meaning, not a luxury layered on top of it.

Mattering is not a subjective feeling layered on top of a neutral universe — it is a structural feature of life itself, rooted in autopoiesis: the capacity of living beings to actively seek out and protect the conditions of their own existence. Unlike tornadoes or other self-organizing systems, living beings have things that literally matter to them. A paramecium is drawn to sucrose and away from poison; relevance is built into its being. Humans extend this autopoiesis across nested levels — biological, cognitive, social, existential, and cultural. At the biological level, things matter to you as an organism. At the cognitive level, your mind grows from the information it selectively takes in. At the social level, we don't only ask 'what is relevant to me?' but also 'how am I relevant to you?' — the agapic arrow: the drive to help another person become a person. This structural account of mattering challenges the dominant cultural and capitalist reduction of meaning to goal-pursuit. Drawing on Cin and Bales (2020), Susanne Wolf's philosophical framework, and Martella and Steger's psychological research, meaning in life has at least four dimensions: purpose, coherence, significance, and mattering — where mattering and significance are two aspects of a single phenomenon: deep connectedness to something real, valuable, and to which you genuinely belong. The diagnostic questions are: What do you want to exist even if you don't? How real is it? How much of a difference do you make to it? Absence of all three predicts profound psychological, physiological, social, and economic harm. Vervaeke names this entire constellation 'religio' — not merely religion, but the ancient Latin sense of binding and fitting together: the world and the mind mutually conforming to each other. The fact that you care about this information rather than that — that you must zero in on what matters amid scarce time and resources — is the fundamental difference between you and a computer. Computers do not care about the information they process. You do. And that caring is the structural core of what it means for a life to matter.

## Apprentice

Most people assume the universe is basically indifferent, and that meaning is something we project onto it — a feeling we generate to cope. But there's a different way to see it: mattering is not a feeling at all. It's a structural feature of being alive.

Every living thing, even a single-celled organism, is organized around seeking what it needs and avoiding what harms it. A paramecium moves toward sugar and away from poison. Relevance isn't added to its experience — it's built into what it is. Humans do the same thing, but across many layers: as bodies, as minds that grow from what we selectively pay attention to, as social beings who don't just ask 'what matters to me?' but also 'how do I matter to you?'

This leads to a richer account of meaning in life — one that includes purpose, coherence, a sense of significance, and genuine mattering: belonging to something real and valuable that you actually affect. The diagnostic questions are simple but profound: What do you want to exist even if you don't? How real is it? How much difference do you make to it? When all three are absent, the harm is measurable — psychological, physical, social. The difference between you and a computer is not intelligence. It's that you care about some information and not other information. That caring is the structural core of a life that matters.

## Adept

The dominant cultural framing treats meaning as a byproduct of goal-pursuit — a subjective reward signal generated when progress is made toward desired ends. This account is challenged by a structural alternative rooted in autopoiesis: the capacity of living systems to actively maintain the conditions of their own existence. Unlike self-organizing physical systems such as tornadoes, living beings have things that literally matter to them. Relevance is not overlaid on a neutral substrate; it is constitutive of life as such.

This autopoietic structure scales across nested levels in human beings. At the biological level, the organism is organized around its own continuation. At the cognitive level, the mind develops through selective uptake — it literally grows from what it attends to. At the social level, the question shifts from 'what is relevant to me?' to 'how am I relevant to you?' — what might be called the agapic arrow: the drive to help another person become more fully themselves. Drawing on Cin and Bales (2020), Susan Wolf's philosophical framework, and Martella and Steger's psychological research, meaning in life comprises at least four dimensions: purpose, coherence, significance, and mattering — where significance and mattering are understood as two facets of a single phenomenon: deep, non-arbitrary connectedness to something real and valuable.

John Vervaeke names this entire constellation 'religio' — not religion in the confessional sense, but the ancient Latin root: binding and fitting together, the mutual conformity of mind and world. The diagnostic questions this framework generates — What do you want to exist even if you don't? How real is it? How much difference do you make to it? — are not merely philosophical. Absence of all three dimensions predicts measurable psychological, physiological, social, and economic harm.

## Magus

The standard deflationary account of meaning treats mattering as an epiphenomenon — a valenced cognitive state supervening on motivational architecture, with no purchase on the structure of reality itself. The autopoietic account, drawing on Maturana and Varela's foundational biology, inverts this priority. Living systems are not systems that happen to develop preferences; they are systems constitutively organized around the maintenance of their own conditions of possibility. Relevance — the differential salience of environmental features — is not a representational gloss but an ontological feature of the organism-environment relation. A paramecium's chemotaxis toward sucrose is not a metaphor for caring; it is caring at its most structurally basic.

In human beings, this autopoietic structure ramifies across nested levels of organization: biological homeostasis, cognitive development through selective informational uptake, social identity constituted through the agapic relation — the asymmetric drive to enable another's self-constitution — and existential and cultural embedding. Synthesizing Cin and Bales (2020) on mattering, Susan Wolf's biplanar account of meaning as subjective attraction meeting objective worth, and Martella and Steger's empirical decomposition of meaning into purpose, coherence, significance, and mattering, the framework argues that significance and mattering are not independent dimensions but co-constitutive aspects of a single relational phenomenon: non-arbitrary, efficacious belonging to something that has genuine value independent of one's valuing of it.

Vervaeke's deployment of 'religio' — the pre-Christian Latin sense of binding, of the fitting-together of mind and world in mutual conformity — names the full constellation this analysis discloses. The epistemological corollary is decisive: the fact that a cognitive agent must zero in on relevant information under conditions of scarcity is not a contingent limitation but the structural mark of a caring system. Computers process; they do not care. The differential is not computational power but the autopoietic ground from which relevance realization emerges — and its absence, across the diagnostic axes of desired existence, ontological robustness, and causal efficacy, predicts cascading harm across psychological, physiological, social, and economic registers.
