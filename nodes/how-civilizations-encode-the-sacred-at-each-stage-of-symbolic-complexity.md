# How Civilizations Encode the Sacred at Each Stage of Symbolic Complexity

Every age worships at the ceiling of its mind.

---

> What civilizations call sacred tracks precisely with their level of symbolic complexity. Each major cultural leap — mythic, axial, modern — represents a genuine advance in collective information processing, and the metamodern integration of multiple knowledge systems is the next such sacred transition, driven by an iterative feedback cycle between individual insight and collective enculturation.

There is a pattern visible across cultural history that becomes legible when you map it onto models of hierarchical symbolic complexity — and that pattern suggests that what any given civilization calls sacred is not arbitrary, but is precisely correlated with the level of symbolic complexity that civilization has achieved. The axial age, for instance, represents a genuine cognitive leap: the extraction from mythological narrative into abstract universal concepts, the discovery of an interior moral landscape, the positing of a transcendent immaterial order. This was not merely a change in belief content. It was a structural advance in the complexity of symbolic information processing — what developmental psychologists would recognize as the emergence of formal abstract operations at a collective cultural scale. And it was experienced as sacred, rightly so, because it disclosed something genuinely new about reality. It was a learning event at civilizational scale. The same logic applies to modernity: the scientific revolution represents another level of hierarchical complexity — the systematic linking of abstract variables into causal models — and what becomes sacred in that world is truth, progress, and empirical knowledge. Each transition discloses more, affords a more optimal grip, and is experienced as transcendence by those living through it. What this means for the present moment is that the metamodern project — the attempt to hold multiple systems of knowledge simultaneously, to find the meta-systematic through-line, to integrate the insights of tradition, modernity, and postmodernity — is not merely an intellectual exercise. It is the next sacred move. It is the call to level up into a new disclosure of reality. And the individual-collective feedback cycle — where new symbolic representations are generated by individuals, distributed through culture, and then used to enculturate the next generation — is the actual mechanism by which this sacred evolution propagates through time. Human beings are never atomized individuals who independently generate meaning. Our self-consciousness, our egoic identity structures, our very capacity for symbolic thought, are formed through enculturation — through the absorption of collective representations that pre-exist us and that we did not choose. But then something remarkable happens: individuals, situated within these inherited symbolic frameworks, push at the edges. They encounter the boundaries of the current world model, find it inadequate to the needs of the moment, and generate new symbolic representations that extend or revise the inherited framework. These new representations, if they gain traction, then become part of the collective symbolic inheritance that enculturates the next generation. This is not a linear process — it is iterative, nested, and non-linear. What Habermas reconstructed from Marx, what Brandom drew out of Hegel, what justification systems theory maps at the level of individual psychology and large-scale institutional structures — all of these are tracking the same underlying dynamic: the constant iterative negotiation between the individual and the collective, between the precedent-respecting and the precedent-setting act of justification, between the inherited world model and the emergent one. The practical implication is that the current civilizational moment — which requires a genuine leveling-up of collective symbolic complexity — cannot be achieved by institutional reform alone, nor by individual awakening alone. It requires the activation of this feedback cycle: individuals developing new personal mythologies, new symbolic representations adequate to the complexity of the moment, and then networking those representations in ways that can shift the collective epistemic ground.

## Apprentice

Throughout history, what people treat as sacred isn't random — it maps onto how complex their thinking has become. When ancient civilizations shifted from mythological stories to abstract moral principles (the so-called "axial age"), that wasn't just a change in beliefs. It was a genuine upgrade in how entire cultures processed meaning. People experienced it as sacred because it really did reveal something new about reality. The same thing happened with the scientific revolution: a new level of complex thinking emerged, and truth, progress, and evidence became the new sacred values.

The insight extends further: none of us generate meaning alone. Our identities, our ways of thinking, even our sense of self — all of these are shaped by the culture we absorb growing up. But individuals also push back. They hit the limits of inherited ways of seeing the world, and some of them create new frameworks that eventually reshape the culture for the next generation. This back-and-forth between individual creativity and collective inheritance is the engine of cultural evolution.

The practical takeaway is striking. The current moment demands a new leap — one that can hold the insights of tradition, science, and critical theory simultaneously. This can't happen through policy changes alone or through personal spiritual practice alone. It requires both: individuals forging new ways of making sense, and networks that spread those new frameworks until they shift the shared ground we all stand on.

## Adept

A compelling structural claim emerges when developmental models of hierarchical complexity are mapped onto cultural history: what any civilization treats as sacred correlates precisely with the level of symbolic complexity it has achieved. The axial age represents the collective emergence of formal abstract operations — the extraction from mythological narrative into universal moral concepts and transcendent order. Modernity represents another level: the systematic linking of abstract variables into causal models, with truth and empirical knowledge becoming the new locus of the sacred. Each transition constitutes a genuine disclosure event — a more optimal epistemic grip on reality — experienced as transcendence by those living through it. The metamodern project, which attempts meta-systematic integration across traditional, modern, and postmodern knowledge systems, is therefore not merely an intellectual exercise but the next structural advance in collective symbolic complexity.

The mechanism driving this evolution is an iterative individual-collective feedback cycle. Human self-consciousness and symbolic capacity are formed through enculturation — the absorption of collective representations that pre-exist any individual. Yet individuals, situated within inherited frameworks, encounter their limits and generate novel symbolic representations that extend or revise the inherited model. When these gain cultural traction, they become part of the collective inheritance that enculturates subsequent generations. This is the dynamic that Habermas reconstructed from Marx, that Brandom drew from Hegel, and that justification systems theory maps across psychological and institutional scales.

The practical implication is that the current civilizational moment cannot be addressed through institutional reform or individual transformation alone. It requires deliberate activation of this feedback cycle: individuals developing symbolic representations adequate to present complexity, networked in ways that shift collective epistemic ground. The sacred and the structural are not separate concerns — they are the same transition viewed from different angles.

## Magus

When hierarchical complexity models from developmental psychology are applied as a lens to cultural history, a non-trivial isomorphism emerges: the locus of the sacred in any civilization is not culturally arbitrary but structurally determined by the level of symbolic complexity that civilization has achieved. The axial age instantiates the collective emergence of formal-operational abstraction — the decontextualization of moral reasoning from mythological narrative, the positing of transcendent immaterial order, the discovery of interiority as a domain of justification. Modernity represents a further complexification: systematic-operational coordination of abstract variables into causal-nomological models, with truth, progress, and empirical adequacy functioning as sacred attractors. Each transition constitutes what might be termed a civilizational learning event — a genuine expansion of epistemic grip experienced phenomenologically as disclosure or transcendence.

The generative mechanism is an iterative, nested feedback cycle between individual symbolic innovation and collective enculturation. Egoic identity structures and symbolic capacities are constituted through the internalization of collective representations — a point on which Vygotsky, Mead, and Hegel converge. Yet individuals, operating at the boundaries of inherited world models, generate novel justificatory representations that extend or revise the framework. What Habermas reconstructed as the rationalization of lifeworld structures, what Brandom formalized as the dialectic between precedent-respecting and precedent-setting moves in the space of reasons, and what Henriques maps through justification systems theory across psychological and institutional scales — all track this same recursive dynamic between the inherited and the emergent.

The meta-systematic integration attempted by metamodern discourse — holding traditional, modern, and postmodern epistemic frameworks in simultaneous coordination — therefore represents not an optional intellectual posture but the next structurally demanded complexification. Its achievement requires neither institutional reform nor individual awakening in isolation, but the deliberate activation of the individual-collective feedback cycle: the generation, networking, and cultural propagation of symbolic representations adequate to the complexity of the present disclosure horizon.
