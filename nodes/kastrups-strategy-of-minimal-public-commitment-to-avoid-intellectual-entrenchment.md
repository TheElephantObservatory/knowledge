# Kastrup's Strategy of Minimal Public Commitment to Avoid Intellectual Entrenchment

Hold the line where you can afford to lose it.

---

> Bernardo Kastrup deliberately keeps his public philosophical commitments minimal — only the core of analytic idealism — to avoid cognitive entrenchment, preserving the freedom to evolve his thinking without fighting against his own published record.

To guard against cognitive entrenchment, Bernardo Kastrup deliberately limits his public commitments to the most basic, minimal version of his position. The strategy is this: stick to the core claim — mind is all there is, the world outside is made of mental states, the division between inside and outside is a dissociative boundary — and resist the pull to elaborate publicly into specifics, tangents, and implications, even when privately those elaborations are very much alive.

The reason is psychological and structural. The moment you become publicly committed to a detailed view full of specifics, you acquire a profound motivation not to change your mind. Saving face, protecting legacy, avoiding the labor of publicly retracting — these forces are powerful enough to stop intellectual evolution entirely. By keeping public commitments minimal and generic, you preserve the freedom to evolve privately without fighting against your own public identity. The cognitive bias of entrenchment does its greatest damage to the person themselves, not to their audience. What goes on the record should be only those things you have contemplated thoroughly, confronted with many kinds of tests, over long enough periods, that you are reasonably confident they will not need to be disowned two years later. Publishing working drafts — ideas still in flux — does not serve the cultural dialogue. It creates noise, erodes trust, and most damagingly, locks the thinker into defending positions they have not yet earned the right to hold.

## Apprentice

Bernardo Kastrup follows a deliberate strategy in how much of his thinking he shares publicly. He commits only to the most basic version of his philosophical position: that reality is fundamentally mental, that the physical world consists of mental states, and that the boundary between inner and outer experience is a kind of dissociation. Beyond that core, he resists the temptation to publish every elaboration, implication, or tangent — even when his private thinking is rich with them.

The reason is surprisingly practical. Once you go on record with a detailed, specific position, you develop a powerful motivation not to change your mind. Saving face, protecting your reputation, avoiding the exhausting work of public retraction — these pressures can quietly kill honest intellectual growth. By keeping his public commitments lean, Kastrup preserves room to change course without battling his own legacy.

This approach also reflects a view about what responsible public thinking looks like. Publishing ideas still in flux — working drafts that haven't survived serious testing — creates noise rather than clarity. It erodes trust and, worst of all, traps the thinker into defending positions they haven't yet earned confidence in. Only ideas that have been deeply contemplated and stress-tested over time deserve the weight of public commitment.

## Adept

Kastrup's epistemic strategy is a deliberate prophylactic against cognitive entrenchment — the well-documented tendency for public commitment to calcify belief. His method: restrict published positions to the irreducible core of analytic idealism (mind-at-large as ontological primitive, the physical as extrinsic appearance of mental states, dissociation as the mechanism of individuation) and withhold the richer elaborations, speculative extensions, and downstream implications that constitute his active private inquiry.

The logic is rooted in the psychology of commitment and identity. Once a thinker becomes publicly identified with a detailed, specific intellectual architecture, the costs of revision become enormous — reputational, psychological, and practical. Confirmation bias, motivated reasoning, and identity-protective cognition conspire to make the thinker their own worst obstacle. Kastrup recognizes that entrenchment damages the thinker more than the audience; the audience can simply move on, but the thinker must live inside the fortress they've built. Minimizing the surface area of public commitment is therefore not intellectual timidity but a structural safeguard for ongoing rational freedom.

This stance carries a normative dimension as well. It implies that the cultural dialogue is better served by fewer, more durable contributions than by a steady stream of provisional positions. Publishing working drafts — ideas that have not yet survived sustained adversarial scrutiny — generates epistemic noise, degrades the signal-to-noise ratio of public discourse, and creates perverse incentive structures where defending a premature position takes precedence over discovering whether it is true. The threshold for public commitment, on this view, should be high: only claims contemplated long enough and tested rigorously enough to warrant reasonable confidence in their longevity.

## Magus

Kastrup's methodological minimalism in public commitment constitutes a second-order epistemic strategy — a meta-cognitive intervention against the dynamics of belief perseverance and identity-constitutive cognition. The operative principle is that the surface area of publicly defended claims should be inversely proportional to their susceptibility to future revision. Thus only the axiomatic core of analytic idealism — the ontological primitivity of mentation, the reduction of physicality to extrinsic appearance, dissociation as the individuation mechanism — receives full public endorsement, while the inferential superstructure, however elaborate in private deliberation, remains strategically unpublished.

The justification draws on a sophisticated understanding of how commitment mechanisms distort rationality. Public identification with a detailed theoretical edifice activates a constellation of biases — motivated reasoning, sunk-cost escalation, identity-protective cognition — that function not merely as epistemic friction but as structural impediments to belief revision. Critically, Kastrup locates the primary locus of damage in the thinker rather than the audience: entrenchment is an autoimmune disorder of the intellect, wherein the defense mechanisms meant to protect coherence instead attack the capacity for growth. The asymmetry between the cost of private revision (negligible) and public retraction (enormous) makes this a rational optimization problem with a clear solution: minimize irrevocable public commitments.

The normative corollary is a critique of the contemporary epistemic economy's incentive to publish prolifically and provisionally. On this view, premature publication is not merely imprudent but epistemically corrosive — it degrades discourse through noise, erodes the credibility of the contributor, and most perniciously, transforms the thinker from an inquirer into an advocate. The threshold for public assertion becomes a function of temporal robustness: only those positions that have survived sustained internal adversarial testing across sufficient timescales merit the permanence of publication.
