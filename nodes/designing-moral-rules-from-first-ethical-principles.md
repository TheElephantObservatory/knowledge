# Designing Moral Rules from First Ethical Principles

Guarding the channel where meaning meets the world

---

> Ethics and morality aren't the same thing: morality is the rules, ethics is the deeper logic those rules are trying to serve. As the world changes, the rules must change too — but the underlying principles don't.

There's a fundamental distinction between ethics and morality that most people collapse together, but they operate at entirely different levels of abstraction. Morality is essentially a set of rules — specific, context-dependent prescriptions like 'don't type in all caps on a forum' or 'thou shalt not kill.' These rules are translations of something deeper into the particular conditions of a given world. Ethics, by contrast, is the domain of principles — the general, underlying heuristics from which rules are derived and against which they must be checked for validity. The key insight is that as the world changes, the rules must be renegotiated, but the principles remain stable. What Forrest is pointing at is something like an information-theoretic foundation for ethics: the deepest ethical principles are about the conditions that make communication — in the broadest sense, all interaction between self and world — possible at all. For instance, if pulling out a gun and shooting someone ends a conversation, that's not just a moral prohibition, it's a violation of the structural conditions that make any exchange of meaning possible. Reciprocity, symmetry, the preservation of the channel itself — these are the deep grammar of ethical life. The practical implication is significant: in a rapidly changing technological world, we can't simply inherit old rules. We have to go back to first principles and ask whether our inherited codes still serve the underlying communicative function they were always meant to serve. This reframes ethics not as a set of inherited commandments but as an ongoing engineering problem — how do we design rules that actually instantiate the principles in the conditions we now inhabit?

## Apprentice

Most people use the words 'ethics' and 'morality' interchangeably, but there's a meaningful difference worth preserving. Morality is the layer of specific rules — things like 'don't shout at people' or 'don't steal.' These rules make sense in a particular time and place, for a particular kind of world. Ethics, by contrast, is the layer beneath: the general principles that explain why those rules exist and whether they're any good.

The key insight is that rules need to be updated as the world changes, but principles stay stable. A rule about not interrupting someone mid-sentence and a rule about not shooting them both point at the same deeper principle: keep the conversation alive, preserve the conditions that make exchange possible at all. When technology reshapes how we interact, old rules may no longer serve their original purpose — and only by returning to the underlying principles can we figure out what new rules are actually needed.

This reframes ethics as an active, practical discipline rather than a museum of inherited commandments. The question isn't 'what did people used to believe was right?' but 'what rules, right now, best serve the principles that make meaningful human interaction possible?' That's a design problem as much as a philosophical one.

## Adept

A persistent conflation in popular moral discourse treats ethics and morality as synonyms, but the distinction between them is structurally significant. Morality operates at the level of rules — context-specific, world-dependent prescriptions that encode appropriate behavior for a given set of conditions. Ethics operates at the level of principles — the generative heuristics from which rules are derived and against which their validity is measured. Rules are implementations; ethics is the specification.

What makes this framework particularly generative is its information-theoretic dimension. The deepest ethical principles, on this account, aren't arbitrary cultural preferences but structural conditions for the possibility of meaningful exchange between agents. Reciprocity, symmetry, and the preservation of the communicative channel itself function as something like a deep grammar underlying all interaction. Violence doesn't merely violate a moral prohibition — it destroys the medium through which any further moral negotiation could occur. This grounds ethics in something more foundational than convention.

The practical stakes are high in an era of rapid technological change. Inherited moral codes were calibrated for worlds that no longer exist, and the temptation is either to discard them wholesale or to apply them mechanically to conditions they weren't designed for. The more rigorous approach is to treat rule-generation as an ongoing engineering problem: return to first principles, assess whether existing rules still instantiate those principles under current conditions, and redesign where they don't. Ethics, on this view, is less a body of doctrine than a methodology for deriving doctrine.

## Magus

The ethics/morality distinction being drawn here maps onto a familiar but underappreciated logical hierarchy: morality as the domain of object-level prescriptions, ethics as the meta-level discourse governing their derivation and revision. Rules are world-indexed — their validity conditions include facts about the social, technological, and communicative environment in which they operate. Principles, by contrast, are intended to be invariant across such indexing, functioning as the axioms from which context-sensitive rules are generated through something like a process of reflective equilibrium under empirical constraint.

The more original contribution is the proposed grounding of those invariant principles in communication theory broadly construed. Rather than anchoring ethics in welfare, autonomy, or virtue — the familiar triumvirate — this framework locates the deepest ethical commitments in the structural preconditions for any semiotic exchange between self and world. Reciprocity and symmetry aren't merely desirable features of interaction; they are constitutive of the channel through which meaning can be transmitted at all. On this reading, a violation like lethal violence is not simply a harm but an act of channel destruction — it forecloses the very medium through which moral repair, negotiation, or response could occur. This gives the prohibition a transcendental rather than merely consequentialist character.

The methodological upshot is significant for applied ethics in technologically volatile contexts. If moral rules are implementations of ethical principles, then periods of rapid environmental change — in which the conditions of interaction are being restructured at the infrastructure level — demand not incremental rule revision but principled re-derivation from first axioms. The question becomes formally analogous to a compiler problem: given a stable specification and a changed target architecture, generate new code that faithfully instantiates the original intent. Ethics, on this account, is a form of normative engineering, and its practitioners need both the principles and the empirical literacy to translate them.
