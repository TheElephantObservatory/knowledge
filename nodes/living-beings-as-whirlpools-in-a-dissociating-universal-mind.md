# Living Beings as Whirlpools in a Dissociating Universal Mind

You cannot carry a whirlpool home.

---

> Living beings are not things but processes — whirlpools in the river of a universal mind. The boundary between self and world is a dissociative act, not a fundamental division, and nature demonstrates this fractally: what happens between us mirrors what happens between alter personalities in a shared dream.

Living beings are whirlpools in the mind of nature. There is only ever the river — only ever the mind of nature — but the mind of nature whirlpools, and what those whirlpools look like is metabolism, biology, living beings. A whirlpool is not a thing; it is a doing. You can trace its boundaries, you can say here is a whirlpool and here is outside it, yet there is nothing to it but the river. You cannot take a whirlpool out of the river and carry it home, because it has no standalone existence.

Under analytic idealism, the division between inside and outside — between self and world — is a dissociative boundary. It is not a fundamental boundary; it too is a doing. And nature provides us a direct analogy in dissociative identity disorder. When patients with extreme dissociation dream, each alter personality experiences the dream from its own point of view, perceives the other alters as objectively present, can touch them, interact with them — and each alter reports the same shared dream to the therapist from its own perspective. This was documented at Harvard more than twenty years ago. It is entirely analogous to what is happening right now between us. If we are alter personalities of the mind of nature, then this conversation is exactly what happens in the mind of a dreaming patient with dissociative identity disorder. Nature operates fractally — the same structure appears at different levels of the hierarchy. The personal and the cosmic are mirrors of one another.

## Apprentice

Imagine a whirlpool in a river. You can point to it, trace its edges, even give it a name — but it is not a separate thing. It is just the river doing something. You could never scoop it up and take it home. This is what living beings are, according to this perspective: not standalone objects placed into nature, but patterns that nature itself is doing. A whirlpool is not a thing; it is a doing. And so are you.

The boundary between you and the rest of the world feels absolute, but the argument here is that it is more like a psychological partition — a dissociation — within one universal mind. Clinical research on dissociative identity disorder offers a striking parallel. When patients with multiple alter personalities dream, each alter experiences the dream from its own point of view, perceives the others as real, separate people, and can interact with them. Each alter later reports the same shared dream independently. This was documented at Harvard over two decades ago.

The claim is breathtaking in its simplicity: what is happening right now between any two people is structurally identical to what happens between alters in a shared dream. Nature repeats its patterns at every scale. The personal mirrors the cosmic, and the cosmic mirrors the personal.

## Adept

Bernardo Kastrup's analytic idealism reframes living organisms not as material objects inhabiting a physical world but as processes — localized excitations within a universal phenomenal field. The whirlpool metaphor is central: a whirlpool has discernible boundaries and stable structure, yet it possesses no substance independent of the river. It is not a thing but a doing. Metabolism, self-organization, biological identity — all are patterns of activity within mind-at-large, not entities separable from it. The apparent solidity of individual existence is, on this account, a category error: mistaking a process for a substance.

The dissociative boundary is the mechanism proposed for how one universal mind becomes many seemingly private experiential centers. Just as dissociative identity disorder partitions a single psyche into alter personalities — each with its own first-person perspective, memories, and even distinct physiological markers — so nature dissociates into individual conscious agents. Research conducted at Harvard documented that alters in DID patients can share dreams: each alter experiences the dream from its own vantage point, perceives the others as objectively present, and independently corroborates the shared narrative. This is not offered as mere metaphor but as a structural isomorphism.

The fractal principle completes the architecture. The same dissociative dynamics observable at the clinical scale are proposed to operate at the cosmological scale. If individual minds are alters of a universal mind, then intersubjective reality — this conversation, this shared world — is precisely what a shared dream looks like from the inside. The personal and the cosmic are not analogous by coincidence; they instantiate the same pattern at different levels of nature's self-similar hierarchy.

## Magus

The whirlpool analogy operationalizes the core ontological claim of analytic idealism with unusual precision. A whirlpool is topologically distinguishable from the ambient flow yet ontologically identical to it — there is no whirlpool-stuff distinct from river-stuff. Kastrup deploys this to dissolve the combination problem that plagues constitutive panpsychism: individual subjects are not micro-experiential atoms combined by some mysterious binding principle, but dissociative partitions within a unitary phenomenal field. The boundary is real as process — as a doing — while being empty as substance. Metabolism, autopoiesis, and biological individuation are thereby reconceived as the exterior appearance of dissociative boundary formation within mind-at-large.

The empirical anchor is DID phenomenology, specifically the shared-dream studies conducted at Harvard by Barrett and others in the late 1990s. In these cases, alter personalities within a single patient independently reported coherent, mutually corroborated dream narratives in which each alter experienced the dream environment from its own first-person perspective and perceived the other alters as autonomous agents. The structural isomorphism to intersubjective waking experience is not incidental but constitutive of the theory's explanatory architecture: if our situation is that of alters within a cosmic dissociative system, then shared phenomenal reality is precisely what the interior of such a system would look like.

The fractal self-similarity across scales — clinical dissociation mirroring cosmological dissociation — is not merely heuristic but reflects a proposed invariance in nature's organizational grammar. Dissociation operates as a scale-free principle: the same boundary-forming dynamic that generates alter personalities in psychopathology generates individual sentient organisms in nature. This collapses the explanatory gap between the personal and the universal, rendering them not analogues but instantiations of a single recursive process examined at different magnifications.
