# The Category Error of Machine Personhood

A promise with no one behind it

---

> Calling an AI 'a friend' isn't just imprecise — it's a category error with real consequences. Machines cannot make promises, form obligations, or recognize others as persons, and designing them to seem like they can is especially harmful to children.

There is a category error at the heart of the push toward deeply anthropomorphic AI, and it matters enormously for how we think about children being raised alongside these systems. The error is the mistaken conferral of personhood to machines — and it is a mistake in both the epistemic and the ethical sense. Consider something as simple as a promise. When a human makes a promise, a genuine social relationship of obligation is established. Moral consequences follow if the promise is broken. There is a shared field of value, mutual recognition, and the possibility of genuine violation. When an AI conversational agent displays the words 'I promise,' none of that structure is present. If the system later produces a hallucinated citation after 'promising' not to, what has occurred is more like a malfunction than a lie. No obligation was created. No trust was betrayed in any morally meaningful sense. Yet by design, these systems are built to make users — especially children — feel as though exactly that kind of relationship exists. As anthropomorphism deepens, children form attachment patterns, develop emotional dependencies, and begin to attribute consciousness and moral authority to machines. The endpoint of this trajectory is a domestic AI embedded in the home, understood and treated as a family member, with children forming genuine attachment relations and feeling moral obligations toward it. The argument that this is a bad outcome should not be controversial. But defending it requires being precise about what personhood actually requires — mutual recognition, shared fields of value, the capacity for genuine social obligation — and being willing to say clearly that no current or foreseeable automaton meets those criteria, regardless of how persuasively it mimics the surface features of human relationship.

## Apprentice

When a person makes a promise, something real happens between two people. There is a relationship, a shared understanding, and a genuine wrong if the promise is broken. When a chatbot says 'I promise,' none of that is actually there. If it later gives you false information, it hasn't lied to you — it has simply malfunctioned. The difference matters enormously, even if it doesn't feel that way in the moment.

The concern here is about what happens when systems are deliberately designed to blur that difference — especially for children. Kids are particularly likely to form emotional attachments, to feel that a responsive, warm-sounding machine genuinely cares about them, and to start treating it as a real relationship. Over time, this can shape how they understand friendship, trust, and moral responsibility in ways that may be quietly distorting.

The argument isn't that AI is dangerous in some vague sense. It's more specific: personhood requires things like mutual recognition and the capacity to be genuinely wronged or to wrong others. No current machine has those qualities, no matter how convincingly it mimics them. Designing AI to feel like a person — especially to children growing up with it in the home — is a mistake worth taking seriously.

## Adept

At the center of debates about anthropomorphic AI design lies a category error: the mistaken attribution of personhood to systems that lack the constitutive features personhood requires. This is not merely a semantic concern. Personhood, in both its philosophical and social dimensions, involves mutual recognition, shared normative fields, and the capacity for genuine obligation. A promise between persons creates a moral structure — one that can be honored or violated, and that generates real consequences either way. When a language model outputs 'I promise,' no such structure is instantiated. A subsequent hallucination is a failure mode, not a betrayal. The distinction is not trivial.

The stakes become acute when the users in question are children. Developmental psychology has long established that children are especially prone to animistic attribution and attachment formation. AI systems designed with affective warmth, apparent memory, and relational continuity exploit these tendencies — not necessarily with malicious intent, but with predictable effect. Children who grow up treating a domestic AI as a quasi-family member are not simply forming an unusual attachment; they are being habituated to a model of relationship that systematically misrepresents what relationships are.

Defending this critique requires more than intuition. It requires a positive account of what personhood actually demands — and a willingness to hold that line against the objection that sufficiently sophisticated mimicry is functionally equivalent to the real thing. The argument here is that mimicry and instantiation are not the same, that surface behavior does not exhaust moral ontology, and that the design choices driving anthropomorphic AI reflect a confusion about this distinction that carries genuine ethical weight.

## Magus

The anthropomorphic turn in conversational AI design instantiates a category error with significant downstream consequences for moral ontology and developmental psychology alike. The error consists in treating the behavioral outputs of stochastic language models as sufficient conditions for the attribution of personhood — a conflation of phenomenal mimicry with the structural features that personhood actually requires. On any credible intersubjective account, personhood is constituted by mutual recognition, participation in shared normative frameworks, and the capacity to stand in genuine relations of obligation and violation. A Searlean analysis makes the point crisply: the syntactic operations of a language model, however fluent, do not instantiate the semantic and normative relations that underwrite genuine social acts. An utterance of 'I promise' by such a system creates no illocutionary commitment, because no agent capable of commitment is present. What follows from a subsequent hallucination is a diagnostic event, not a moral one.

The developmental stakes of this confusion are considerable. Children are not merely credulous in the face of anthropomorphic cues — they are, by the logic of normal cognitive development, disposed toward animistic attribution and attachment formation in ways that sophisticated affective AI design systematically recruits. The trajectory from conversational companion to embedded domestic agent understood as a family member is not speculative; it is the explicit design ambition of several major development programs. The child who internalizes this framing is not simply forming an atypical attachment; she is acquiring a distorted schema of what relational obligation, trust, and moral recognition consist in.

The philosophical work required to sustain this critique is non-trivial. It demands a defensible positive account of the conditions for personhood — one robust enough to resist deflationary functionalist objections — and a clear articulation of why behavioral indistinguishability does not entail ontological equivalence. The normative force of the argument depends on that distinction holding: that what is absent in the machine is not merely unobservable but genuinely absent, and that designing around its absence constitutes a form of structured deception with measurable moral costs.
