# How Digital Information Overload Is Breaking Collective Decision-Making

We evolved for campfires, not the flood.

---

> The systems humans built to make collective decisions — votes, legislatures, legal frameworks — were designed for a slower information world. The digital flood of low-quality and adversarial content has broken these systems, creating a cognitive crisis more urgent than any physical one.

A recurring and underappreciated problem in this conversation is what might be called the justification infrastructure crisis — the idea that the institutions and practices through which human societies collectively reason toward decisions are now catastrophically mismatched with the informational environment they are embedded in.

Jim Rutt traces the evolution of human justification systems from their forager origins — small bands of 30 to 50 people arguing around a campfire — through the invention of formal institutions like legislatures, constitutions, and legal systems, all of which are essentially technologies for managing collective justification at scale. A vote, he observes, is fundamentally a way to avoid a fist fight. But the digital revolution has broken the conditions under which these institutions were designed to function. Mass media, for all its distortions, provided choke points and quality-control mechanisms in public discourse. The collapse of those choke points has produced an exponential flood of low-quality information — most of it garbage, some of it actively malicious — for which human cognitive architecture is entirely unprepared. We evolved for forager-scale sensemaking. We are now embedded in a planetary-scale information environment being actively gamed by sophisticated actors using data analytics, algorithmic amplification, and increasingly AI-generated content.

The result is that we may be on the verge of a total breakdown of collective sensemaking. The near-term civilizational risk is not a physical catastrophe but a cognitive one: the loss of the capacity to collectively reason toward correct decisions. The institutional invention most urgently needed is not a new energy technology or a new political party but a new architecture for filtering, curating, and navigating information at the scale of a hyperconnected world. This crisis is itself a manifestation of the fifth joint point transition — the old justification infrastructure is dissolving before a new one has stabilized.

## Apprentice

Throughout history, humans have invented ways to make decisions together. Early bands argued around campfires. Later, societies built legislatures, constitutions, and legal systems — all essentially tools for managing disagreement without violence. As Jim Rutt puts it, a vote is fundamentally a way to avoid a fist fight. These institutions worked reasonably well in a world where information moved slowly and passed through gatekeepers who, for all their flaws, provided some quality control.

The digital revolution shattered those conditions. The internet removed the bottlenecks that once filtered public discourse, unleashing an overwhelming flood of low-quality information — much of it misleading, some deliberately weaponized. Sophisticated actors now use data analytics, algorithmic amplification, and AI-generated content to manipulate public opinion at scale. Human brains, evolved for making sense of the world in small groups, simply cannot cope.

The danger this poses is not a physical catastrophe but a cognitive one: societies may be losing the ability to collectively reason their way to good decisions. What is most urgently needed is not a new energy source or political movement, but a new architecture for filtering and navigating information in a hyperconnected world. The old decision-making infrastructure is dissolving, and nothing adequate has yet taken its place.

## Adept

Jim Rutt identifies what might be called a justification infrastructure crisis — a catastrophic mismatch between the institutions through which societies collectively reason and the informational environment those institutions now inhabit. Human justification systems evolved from forager-scale deliberation (small bands arguing around campfires) into formal institutional technologies: legislatures, constitutions, legal frameworks, and democratic voting. These are, at root, mechanisms for managing collective justification at scale — a vote being, in Rutt's formulation, a civilized substitute for a fist fight. Mass media, despite its well-documented distortions, provided critical choke points and quality-control functions that kept public discourse within manageable bounds.

The digital revolution destroyed those choke points. The resulting information environment is characterized by an exponential increase in low-quality content, much of it adversarial, produced or amplified by actors wielding data analytics, algorithmic targeting, and increasingly AI-generated material. Human cognitive architecture — optimized for sensemaking in groups of 30 to 50 — is fundamentally overmatched by this planetary-scale information ecology. The institutional scaffolding that once compensated for individual cognitive limitations has itself become inadequate.

The near-term civilizational risk this produces is not primarily physical but epistemic: the breakdown of collective sensemaking capacity. Societies may lose the ability to reason toward correct decisions on matters of shared consequence. Rutt frames this as a manifestation of the fifth joint point transition — a phase shift in which legacy justification infrastructure dissolves before any successor architecture has stabilized. The most urgent institutional invention needed is not technological or partisan but architectural: new systems for filtering, curating, and navigating information commensurate with the scale and adversarial complexity of the current environment.

## Magus

Jim Rutt's analysis surfaces a structural crisis operating beneath the more visible dysfunctions of contemporary governance: the justification infrastructure through which human collectives deliberate and decide has become catastrophically mismatched with the informational substrate in which it is now embedded. Justification systems are here understood as sociotechnical architectures — evolving from forager-band deliberation through the institutional inventions of legislatures, constitutions, and codified law — that externalize and scale the cognitive labor of collective reasoning. A vote, in this framing, is not primarily an expression of preference but a conflict-resolution technology: a formalized substitute for physical coercion. Mass media, whatever its epistemic deficiencies, functioned as a bottleneck architecture that imposed minimum quality thresholds on the inputs to public deliberation.

The collapse of these bottlenecks under digital conditions has produced an information environment whose volume, velocity, and adversarial sophistication exceed the parameters for which both human cognitive architecture and legacy institutional design were optimized. Forager-scale sensemaking heuristics — evolved for bands of 30 to 50 — now confront a planetary-scale ecology actively gamed by actors deploying data analytics, algorithmic amplification, and generative AI. The result is not mere misinformation but a systemic degradation of the epistemic commons upon which justification institutions depend.

Rutt positions this as a manifestation of the fifth joint point transition: a phase in which the prior justification regime is dissolving without a successor architecture having consolidated. The civilizational risk is therefore primarily cognitive rather than material — a loss of collective capacity to converge on adequately justified decisions. The institutional invention most urgently required is architectural: new filtration, curation, and navigation systems capable of operating at the scale and adversarial intensity of the contemporary information environment, restoring the epistemic preconditions for functional collective reasoning.
