# Why Power Structures Select for Psychopathy and Defection

Evil was always on the org chart.

---

> Jim Rutt argues that evil is not a metaphysical mystery but a predictable outcome of game theory: the structural tension between individual and group fitness breeds free-riding and deception, while modern institutions systematically elevate the small percentage of humans born without empathy into positions of power.

Jim Rutt locates the origin of evil not in metaphysics but in game theory — specifically in the irreducible tension between individual and group fitness. In any social species, the optimal strategy for the group requires cooperation and sacrifice, but the optimal strategy for the individual is to free-ride: let others bear the costs, capture the benefits. This tension cannot be eliminated; it is structural. In a forager battle, the group wins if everyone fights bravely, but each individual's best move is to hang back. The evolutionary response was the development of sneakiness — the capacity to defect while appearing to cooperate, to exploit group solidarity without triggering punishment. This is one deep root of what we call evil. The second root is genetic: roughly one to two percent of people are born without empathy or concern for others — psychopathy as a natural variation. What makes this particularly alarming is that modern institutions, especially corporate hierarchies, actively select for psychopathic traits. Where forager societies had robust mechanisms to detect and neutralize dominators — what Christopher Boehm calls 'reverse dominance hierarchies' — Game A civilization does the opposite. C-suite executives show psychopathy rates of ten to thirty percent in some industries, compared to one percent in the general population. The levers of power attract and reward exactly the traits that forager social operating systems were designed to suppress. Evil, in this account, is not a mystery — it is a predictable output of game-theoretic dynamics and institutional selection pressures operating on a primate with a history of dominance.

## Apprentice

Jim Rutt offers a surprisingly clear explanation for why evil exists. In any group of social creatures, what's best for the group — cooperation, sacrifice, fighting together — conflicts with what's best for any single individual, who benefits most by letting others take the risks. This tension can never be fully resolved. Over evolutionary time, it produced a specific adaptation: the ability to cheat while looking cooperative, to smile while defecting. That capacity for sneakiness is one deep root of what we call evil.

The second root is biological. Roughly one to two percent of people are born without the ability to feel empathy — a condition known as psychopathy. In small-scale forager societies, these individuals were identified and kept in check by the group. But modern civilization has flipped the script. Corporate hierarchies and political systems don't suppress dominators — they promote them. Studies suggest that executives in some industries show psychopathy rates ten to thirty times higher than the general population.

Evil, in this view, isn't some dark spiritual force. It's the predictable result of a mismatch: game-theoretic pressures that reward selfishness, a small but real population of people wired to exploit others, and institutions that hand those people the keys to power.

## Adept

Jim Rutt reframes the problem of evil as an emergent property of game-theoretic dynamics rather than a metaphysical puzzle. The core tension is irreducible: in any social species, group-optimal strategies demand cooperation and costly sacrifice, while individually-optimal strategies favor free-riding — capturing the benefits of collective action without bearing its costs. In the ancestral environment of forager warfare, the group needs every member to fight bravely, but each individual's fitness is maximized by hanging back. Natural selection's response was not to resolve this tension but to equip agents with the capacity for strategic defection — sneakiness, the ability to exploit group solidarity while evading detection and punishment. This is a structural origin of evil, not a moral failing but a fitness strategy.

The second vector is genetic variance in empathy. Psychopathy — the near-total absence of concern for others — appears in roughly one to two percent of the population as a natural variation. Forager societies developed what Christopher Boehm identified as "reverse dominance hierarchies": collective mechanisms to detect, constrain, and if necessary eliminate individuals exhibiting dominator behavior. These social operating systems were remarkably effective at suppressing the expression of psychopathic traits in leadership.

Modern institutional architecture — what Rutt's framework calls "Game A" — inverts this selection pressure. Corporate hierarchies, political systems, and financial markets actively reward traits associated with psychopathy: ruthlessness, superficial charm, low empathy, risk tolerance. The result is concentration: C-suite psychopathy rates of ten to thirty percent in some industries. Evil becomes not mysterious but mechanistically predictable — a convergence of unresolvable game-theoretic tension, natural psychological variation, and institutional selection that elevates precisely the traits our ancestral systems evolved to suppress.

## Magus

Jim Rutt's account of evil constitutes a synthesis of evolutionary game theory, behavioral genetics, and institutional selection theory that dissolves the traditional metaphysical framing entirely. The foundational claim is that the tension between individual and group fitness in social species is not merely persistent but irreducible — a structural feature of any system where cooperation produces surplus value capturable by defectors. The Nash equilibrium for the individual diverges from the Pareto optimum for the group, and no mechanism can permanently close this gap. What evolution produced in response was not resolution but escalation: the cognitive architecture for strategic defection under the appearance of cooperation. Sneakiness — the capacity to simulate prosociality while extracting rents from collective action — is thus not a corruption of human nature but one of its core adaptive outputs.

The second axis is the population-level distribution of empathic capacity. Psychopathy, understood as heritable variation yielding near-zero affective empathy, persists at roughly one to two percent — a frequency consistent with frequency-dependent selection maintaining a defector morph within a predominantly cooperative population. Christopher Boehm's ethnographic work on reverse dominance hierarchies documents the forager counterstrategy: coalitional enforcement mechanisms that imposed severe fitness costs on would-be dominators, effectively suppressing phenotypic expression of exploitative strategies in leadership contexts.

The critical move in Rutt's analysis is identifying the institutional inversion. Game A civilizational structures — corporate hierarchies, financialized markets, political systems optimizing for competitive dominance — constitute selection environments that systematically favor psychopathic phenotypes. Empirical data showing C-suite psychopathy rates an order of magnitude above base rates represent not anomaly but predicted outcome. Evil, on this account, is the convergence of an unresolvable cooperative dilemma, a persistent genetic polymorphism, and institutional architectures that have dismantled the ancestral suppression mechanisms while amplifying the payoffs to defection at scale.
