# Constraining Chemical Simulation to Molecules That Can Actually Be Made

The map that finally fits the territory

---

> Most AI-designed molecules cannot actually be made, rendering them fictions rather than discoveries. Chemistry needs a simulator constrained to synthesizable molecules — and the convergence of robotic synthesis, formal programming languages, and assembly theory is bringing that threshold within reach.

The deepest problem with AI-generated chemistry — and with most computational chemistry tools today — is that they can propose molecules that cannot actually be made. This is not a technical limitation but a categorical failure: if there is no known synthetic route to a simulated molecule, the result is a fiction, not a scientific finding. What is needed is a definition of 'computational chemical space' constrained to molecules that can actually be synthesized — shifting the question from 'what molecules are theoretically possible' to 'what molecules are accessible given known chemistry and available hardware.' Assembly theory contributes by placing limits on what counts as a real molecule: not just a stable arrangement of atoms, but one that can be made in sufficient quantity to detect. The convergence of a formally complete programming language, physical robotic synthesis, and assembly-theoretic constraints on molecular identity creates, for the first time, a simulator grounded in physical reality. This is the analogue of what happened in semiconductor design — where simulation became trustworthy enough to replace physical prototyping for most of the design cycle. Chemistry is approaching that threshold, and when it crosses it, the rate of molecular discovery will undergo a phase transition.

## Apprentice

There is a deep problem hiding inside the excitement about using AI to discover new molecules: many of the molecules that computers propose simply cannot be built. They look good on screen, but no one knows how to actually make them in a lab. A molecule that exists only in a simulation is not a discovery — it is a fantasy.

The real breakthrough comes from flipping the question. Instead of asking "what molecules could theoretically exist?" the more useful question is "what molecules can we actually make with the tools and knowledge we have right now?" This means building a kind of chemistry simulator that only works within the boundaries of real, physical possibility — molecules that can be synthesized, in quantities large enough to detect and study.

Several pieces are converging to make this possible: robots that can physically carry out chemical reactions, programming languages that can describe chemistry completely, and a theoretical framework called assembly theory that helps define what counts as a real molecule versus a hypothetical one. Together, these create something chemistry has never had — a simulator you can trust the way semiconductor engineers trust their chip design software. If chemistry crosses that threshold, the pace of molecular discovery could accelerate dramatically.

## Adept

The central failure of most computational chemistry and AI-driven molecular generation is not imprecision but irrelevance: proposed molecules for which no synthetic route exists are not approximate answers — they are non-answers. The field has largely conflated theoretical chemical space (all thermodynamically stable arrangements of atoms) with accessible chemical space (molecules that can actually be synthesized given known reactions and available instrumentation). This conflation renders vast swaths of computational output scientifically meaningless.

The corrective requires constraining computational exploration to synthesizable molecules. Assembly theory provides a principled basis for this constraint by defining molecular identity not merely as a stable configuration but as an object that can be assembled through a sequence of known operations and produced in detectable quantities. When combined with a formally complete chemical programming language capable of expressing any known reaction and with physical robotic platforms that execute those programs, the result is a closed loop: simulation proposes, synthesis validates, and the space of exploration is bounded by physical reality rather than combinatorial fantasy.

This convergence mirrors what occurred in semiconductor design, where electronic design automation matured to the point that simulation replaced most physical prototyping. Chemistry is approaching an analogous threshold. The moment a chemistry simulator becomes trustworthy enough that its outputs reliably correspond to makeable molecules, the discovery cycle compresses from years to days. The rate of molecular discovery would not merely increase — it would undergo a phase transition, fundamentally altering the economics and epistemology of chemical research.

## Magus

The epistemic status of computationally proposed molecules deserves far more scrutiny than it typically receives. A molecule for which no synthetic pathway exists is not an approximation or a lead — it is ontologically vacant, a node in a graph with no physical referent. The standard framing of "chemical space" as the set of all thermodynamically permissible molecular configurations is a category error when applied to discovery, because it elides the distinction between mathematical possibility and synthetic accessibility. The relevant space is not what is stable but what is constructible.

Assembly theory offers a rigorous reframing by grounding molecular identity in constructibility: a molecule is real insofar as it can be assembled through a finite sequence of known bond-forming operations and produced in sufficient copy number to be empirically detected. This is not a pragmatic heuristic but a definitional constraint — it relocates the boundary of chemical space from the phase diagram to the assembly index. When this theoretical framework is coupled with a formally complete chemical programming language (one capable of expressing the full repertoire of known synthetic transformations) and with robotic platforms that physically execute those programs, the result is unprecedented: a simulator whose output space is coextensive with physically accessible chemistry.

The historical parallel is electronic design automation in semiconductor engineering, where the maturation of simulation fidelity eliminated most physical prototyping from the design cycle. Chemistry stands at the cusp of an equivalent transition. The convergence of assembly-theoretic constraints, programmable synthesis, and closed-loop robotic validation creates the preconditions for a phase transition in discovery rate — not a linear acceleration but a discontinuity, as the bottleneck shifts from synthesis to imagination.
