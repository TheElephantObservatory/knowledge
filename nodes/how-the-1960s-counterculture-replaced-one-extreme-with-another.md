# How the 1960s Counterculture Replaced One Extreme With Another

They won the revolution and lost the future.

---

> Oliver Griebel argues that the postwar counterculture was right to rebel against bourgeois conformity but fatally wrong in offering liberation without formation — producing a generation that won its freedom yet cannot sustain community, age wisely, or live together.

Oliver Griebel offers a quietly devastating autopsy of the postwar Western counterculture — not as a conservative critique, but as a disillusioned insider's reckoning. His argument is that the counterculture of the 1960s through 1980s was a genuine and necessary revolt against a suffocating, conformist, and often morally empty bourgeois order. The rebellion was real, the energy was real, the music was real. But the solution it proposed — hedonism, sexual liberation, anti-institutionalism, radical individualism — was structurally a mirror image of what it opposed. It replaced one extreme with its opposite rather than transcending the polarity. And now, Griebel observes, the generation that carried that revolution is aging into its 60s, 70s, and 80s, and the results are visible: difficulty sustaining community, difficulty aging with wisdom, difficulty building the relational infrastructure that a good life in its later stages requires. The communal living experiments among older Germans that he mentions are failing — not because the people are bad, but because decades of individualist formation make genuine interdependence extraordinarily hard. What Griebel is pointing at is a civilizational hangover: a generation that wanted liberation and got it, and is now discovering that liberation without formation, without ethics, without the capacity for self-transcendence, does not produce flourishing. It produces sophisticated, articulate, well-traveled people who cannot quite live together. The tragedy is not that the counterculture was wrong to rebel — it was right to rebel. The tragedy is that it had no adequate vision of what to build.

## Apprentice

Oliver Griebel makes a striking argument about the counterculture that swept through the West from the 1960s onward. He doesn't dismiss it — he sees it as a genuine and necessary revolt against a stifling, conformist society that had lost its moral vitality. The music was real, the energy was real, the need to break free was real.

But the solution the counterculture offered — radical individualism, hedonism, sexual liberation, hostility toward institutions — was simply the mirror image of what it opposed. It swapped one extreme for another rather than finding something genuinely new. Griebel observes that the generation who carried this revolution is now aging into their 60s, 70s, and 80s, and the consequences are becoming painfully visible.

Communal living experiments among older Germans are failing — not because the people involved are selfish or bad, but because decades of individualist habits make real interdependence incredibly difficult. The result is a generation of sophisticated, well-traveled, articulate people who struggle to actually live together. The tragedy, as Griebel frames it, is not that the counterculture was wrong to rebel. It was right. The tragedy is that it never developed a vision of what to build in place of what it tore down.

## Adept

Oliver Griebel presents what amounts to a disillusioned insider's autopsy of the postwar Western counterculture. His critique is distinctive because it refuses the conservative framing: the rebellion against bourgeois conformity was legitimate, even necessary. The suffocating moral emptiness of the postwar order demanded a response. What Griebel interrogates is not the impulse but the architecture of the alternative. The counterculture's proposed solution — hedonism, radical individualism, anti-institutionalism, sexual liberation — was structurally an inversion rather than a transcendence. It replaced one polarity with its opposite while remaining trapped within the same axis of tension.

The empirical weight of this argument lands in Griebel's observations about aging. The counterculture generation is now entering its final decades, and the civilizational consequences of liberation-without-formation are becoming legible. Communal living experiments among older Germans — attempts to construct late-life interdependence — are collapsing not from ill will but from a deep incapacity for sustained relational commitment, cultivated over decades of individualist practice.

What emerges is the portrait of a civilizational hangover. A generation that achieved liberation discovers that freedom without ethical formation, without practices of self-transcendence, without the discipline of genuine community, does not produce flourishing. It produces articulate, cosmopolitan isolation. Griebel's insight is ultimately tragic in the classical sense: the counterculture's error was not its rebellion but its lack of a constructive telos — a vision adequate to the freedom it won.

## Magus

Griebel's analysis operates as an immanent critique of the postwar counterculture — one that refuses both nostalgic celebration and reactionary dismissal. The rebellion against bourgeois conformism is granted its full legitimacy: the postwar order's moral bankruptcy, its affective sterility, its institutional rigidity genuinely demanded rupture. What Griebel excavates is the structural logic of the alternative that emerged. The counterculture's core propositions — radical autonomy, hedonic liberation, anti-institutionalism — constituted not a dialectical sublation of bourgeois repression but its enantiodromic inversion. The polarity flipped; the axis remained intact. Liberation was conceived purely as negation, never as formation toward a substantive good.

The phenomenological evidence Griebel marshals is drawn from the present tense of this generation's aging. Communal living experiments among elderly Germans — late attempts to construct the relational infrastructure that individualist decades failed to build — are disintegrating under the weight of habituated autonomy. The incapacity is not volitional but formational: decades of practice in self-sovereignty produce subjects for whom genuine interdependence registers as threat rather than fulfillment. The Aristotelian resonance is unmistakable — hexis shapes possibility, and the counterculture's hexis was systematically oriented away from the virtues communal life demands.

What Griebel identifies is a civilizational aporia: liberation achieved without concurrent cultivation of the capacities for self-transcendence, ethical commitment, and sustained relational fidelity yields not flourishing but a sophisticated desolation. The tragedy carries a classical structure — hamartia residing not in the rejection of the old order but in the absence of a constructive telos adequate to the freedom won. The counterculture's deepest failure was not moral but architectural: it dismantled without blueprints.
