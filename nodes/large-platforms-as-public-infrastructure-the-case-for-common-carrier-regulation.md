# Large Platforms as Public Infrastructure: The Case for Common Carrier Regulation

When the company town owns the square

---

> Once a private platform grows large enough that network effects make meaningful alternatives impossible, it functions as a public square and should be regulated as a common carrier — with enforceable requirements for viewpoint neutrality, transparency, and due process.

At a certain scale, private platforms cease to be private in any morally or legally meaningful sense, and should be regulated as common carriers. The argument draws on Metcalfe's Law — the value of a network grows exponentially with its members — which means that beyond a certain threshold, the network effects become so powerful that meaningful competition becomes practically impossible. You cannot simply 'go elsewhere' when everywhere else is orders of magnitude smaller. This is the same logic that led courts to rule that company towns must provide due process, and that shopping malls must permit petition-gathering — private property that has become the de facto public square takes on public square obligations. The phone company analogy is clarifying: if your telephone provider started silencing calls whenever you mentioned certain topics, we would immediately recognize that as an intolerable violation. The same principle should apply to platforms above a certain scale — perhaps five million unique monthly visitors as a rough threshold. The regulatory framework that should follow includes three core requirements: viewpoint neutrality, meaning platforms cannot suppress speech based on its political or ideological content; transparency, meaning users must be told specifically what rule they violated and what content triggered the violation; and due process, meaning there must be an appealable mechanism with the possibility of reversal. The current situation — where bans are issued as unreviewable death penalties and then quietly reversed under social pressure with no acknowledgment and no apology — is the architecture of Kafkaesque authoritarian power, not the architecture of a public commons.

## Apprentice

When a digital platform becomes so large that practically everyone uses it, something fundamental changes. You can't just "go somewhere else" when the alternative has a tiny fraction of the audience. This is similar to how courts have ruled that company towns must respect constitutional rights, or how shopping malls must allow petition-gathering — when private property becomes the place where public life happens, it takes on public responsibilities.

The phone company comparison makes this clear: if your phone provider started cutting off calls whenever you discussed certain topics, everyone would recognize that as outrageous. Yet major platforms routinely silence users without explaining which specific rule was broken, with no real appeals process, and sometimes quietly reverse their decisions under public pressure without ever admitting the mistake.

The proposed solution has three pillars. First, viewpoint neutrality — platforms above a certain size cannot suppress speech based on political or ideological content. Second, transparency — users must be told exactly what they said that violated which rule. Third, due process — there must be a genuine appeals mechanism that can actually reverse decisions. Without these safeguards, platform governance resembles arbitrary authoritarian power rather than the fair administration of a shared public space.

## Adept

This argument applies the common carrier doctrine to digital platforms, contending that beyond a critical scale threshold — roughly five million unique monthly visitors — network effects governed by Metcalfe's Law render meaningful competition structurally impossible. The value of a network grows exponentially with its user base, which means incumbents enjoy compounding advantages that no startup can realistically overcome. At this point, the libertarian injunction to "build your own platform" becomes as hollow as telling someone to "build your own phone company" or "build your own town."

The constitutional analogy is drawn from cases like Marsh v. Alabama, where the Supreme Court held that a company town must observe First Amendment obligations, and PruneYard Shopping Center v. Robins, which extended petition rights into privately owned commercial spaces. The principle is consistent: when private infrastructure becomes the de facto venue for public discourse, it inherits public obligations. The telephone provider thought experiment sharpens the point — content-based call termination would be immediately recognized as intolerable, yet functionally identical behavior by platforms is normalized.

The regulatory framework proposed has three structural requirements: viewpoint neutrality, prohibiting suppression based on political or ideological content; transparency, requiring specific identification of the violated rule and triggering content; and due process, mandating an appealable mechanism with genuine possibility of reversal. The current regime — where bans function as unreviewable sentences, sometimes silently reversed under social pressure without acknowledgment — is characterized as Kafkaesque authoritarian architecture. The insight is that the absence of these procedural safeguards is not a bug in platform governance but a feature of unchecked private power operating at public scale.

## Magus

The argument proceeds from a network-theoretic premise to a juridical conclusion: Metcalfe's Law dictates that network utility scales as the square of its nodes, generating winner-take-all dynamics that, past a critical adoption threshold, produce monopolistic lock-in indistinguishable in its practical effects from state-granted exclusivity. The threshold proposed — approximately five million unique monthly visitors — marks the point at which exit costs become prohibitive and the platform transitions from private service to quasi-public infrastructure. The "build your own" rejoinder collapses under the same logic that made common carrier regulation inevitable for railroads and telecommunications: the infrastructure is the market, and exclusion from it constitutes exclusion from participation itself.

The doctrinal scaffolding rests on the public function exception articulated in Marsh v. Alabama and extended in PruneYard — the principle that private property exercising traditionally public functions assumes constitutional obligations commensurate with that function. The telephone analogy serves as a reductio: content-based call termination would trigger immediate regulatory intervention precisely because we recognize telecommunications as common carriage. The argument insists this recognition must extend to platforms whose network effects have made them equally indispensable conduits of public discourse.

The tripartite regulatory framework — viewpoint neutrality, transparency, and procedural due process — maps directly onto established common carrier obligations and administrative law principles. What makes the current regime particularly pernicious is its combination of sovereign-scale power with sub-contractual accountability: bans issued without specification, appeals without adjudication, reversals without acknowledgment. This architecture — where enforcement is opaque, arbitrary, and unreviewable — replicates what Kafka anatomized as bureaucratic terror: power that cannot be questioned because it refuses to articulate its own rules. The claim is that this is not governance failure but governance absence, and that the remedy requires treating platform scale as a juridically significant fact.
