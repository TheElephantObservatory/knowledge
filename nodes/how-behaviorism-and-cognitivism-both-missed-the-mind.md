# How Behaviorism and Cognitivism Both Missed the Mind

Two blindnesses do not make a sight

---

> Behaviorism and cognitivism were not opposite theories of mind — they were two ways of missing the same thing. Both split mind from behavior. 4E cognitive science began correcting this, but what it actually points toward is mindedness: the whole animal in dynamic relation to its field.

The debate between behaviorism and cognitivism that dominated twentieth-century psychology was not really a debate about mind at all — it was two different ways of being blind to mindedness. Behaviorism said mind is a bad concept and did science on stimulus-response relations; cognitivism replied that there is an information-processing system inside the head that causes behavior. For forty years behaviorism won in America, then the cognitive revolution of the 1950s shifted the balance. But both camps shared the same error: they treated mind and behavior as separate entities. Behaviorists excluded mind; cognitivists put mind inside the skull and asked how it causes behavior. Neither grasped the dynamic unity of the two. 4E cognitive science — embodied, enacted, embedded, extended — eventually mounted the most serious critique, showing that mind is not a computational processor in a box but a complex adaptive behavioral system in relationship to an environment. That is exactly what mindedness says. The sensory-motor loop is not inside the animal and not reducible to overt movements — it is the animal as a whole in dynamic relation to its field, changing over time. Just as life is not inside a single cell but is an entire ecological complex adaptive system, mindedness is a behavioral structure: an entity in relationship to a field. The term '4E cognitive science' is actually pointing at mindedness while using the more ambiguous word 'cognitive.' What it should be called is 4E mindedness science — because mindedness is the property the four E's are circling around without quite naming. Naming it precisely is what allows us to draw the boundary clearly: between bio-cognitive intelligence in biological space, neuro-cognitive processing in nervous systems, and the full sensory-motor minded behavior of animals navigating behavioral space as a whole.

## Apprentice

For most of the twentieth century, psychology was caught between two camps. Behaviorists said we should forget about the mind entirely and just study observable actions. Cognitivists pushed back, arguing there's a kind of computer inside the head that processes information and produces behavior. These two schools fought for decades, but they actually shared the same blind spot: both treated mind and behavior as separate things. Behaviorists threw mind out; cognitivists locked it inside the skull.

More recently, a movement called 4E cognitive science — emphasizing that thinking is embodied, enacted, embedded, and extended — began to challenge this split. It showed that the mind isn't a processor sealed in a box. Instead, it's something that happens in the ongoing relationship between a living creature and its surroundings.

But even this movement hasn't quite named what it's circling around. The concept it's really pointing at is mindedness — the idea that a whole animal, navigating its world through sensing and moving, is itself the minded system. Mindedness isn't hidden inside the brain and it isn't just visible movements. It's the living creature as a dynamic, changing structure in relationship to everything around it, much like life itself isn't inside a single cell but is the whole ecological system.

## Adept

The behaviorism-cognitivism debate that structured twentieth-century psychology presented itself as a fundamental disagreement about mind, but both positions shared a deeper error. Behaviorism eliminated mind as a scientific concept and reduced psychology to stimulus-response contingencies. Cognitivism reinstated mind but localized it as an information-processing system inside the skull, then asked how this internal mechanism causes behavior. In both cases, mind and behavior were treated as ontologically separate — one camp discarded the first term, the other enclosed it within the organism and tried to build causal bridges to the second.

4E cognitive science — embodied, enacted, embedded, extended — mounted the most substantive critique of this shared assumption by demonstrating that cognition is not computation in a neural container but a complex adaptive process distributed across brain, body, and environment. The sensory-motor loop that constitutes minded activity is neither reducible to internal neural states nor to externally observable movements. It is the whole organism in dynamic relation to its field, changing over developmental and evolutionary time.

The argument here is that 4E cognitive science is actually converging on the concept of mindedness without having named it precisely. What the four E's collectively describe is mindedness — behavioral structure as a property of an entity in relationship to a field, analogous to how life is not contained in a single cell but is an entire ecological complex adaptive system. Naming this property explicitly enables a clearer taxonomy: bio-cognitive intelligence operating in biological space, neuro-cognitive processing within nervous systems, and full sensory-motor minded behavior — the animal as a whole navigating behavioral space.

## Magus

The twentieth-century contest between behaviorism and cognitivism was not, as commonly narrated, a debate about whether mind exists but rather two symmetrical failures to grasp mindedness as such. Behaviorism performed an eliminative move, excising mentalistic vocabulary and collapsing psychology into functional relations between stimuli and responses. The cognitive revolution of the 1950s appeared to restore interiority, but it did so by installing a computational architecture inside the skull and framing the central question as how internal information processing causes overt behavior. Both positions presupposed the same ontological cleavage between mind and behavior — differing only in whether to discard the first term or to sequester it within the cranium and then construct causal explanations outward.

4E cognitive science — embodied, enacted, embedded, extended — represents the most rigorous destabilization of this shared presupposition, relocating cognitive processes across the brain-body-environment system and characterizing the sensory-motor loop as irreducible to either internal computation or external movement. Yet the 4E framework retains the term 'cognitive,' inheriting precisely the ambiguity it seeks to overcome. The claim advanced here is that what the four E's collectively circumscribe, without fully naming, is mindedness: the property of being a whole organism constituted as a dynamic behavioral structure in relation to a field. This parallels the insight that life is not a substance inside a cell but an ecological complex adaptive system — mindedness is similarly a relational, temporally extended, structurally organized phenomenon.

Precise nomination of mindedness as the target property permits a tripartite distinction otherwise unavailable: bio-cognitive intelligence as the adaptive competence of biological systems, neuro-cognitive processing as the contribution of nervous system architectures, and minded behavior as the full sensory-motor engagement of an animal navigating behavioral space. Without this terminological precision, 4E research continues to orbit its own central concept without achieving conceptual closure.
