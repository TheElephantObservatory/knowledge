# The Enlightenment Gap: Why Psychology Has No Unified Paradigm

Lost at the crossroads of mind and method

---

> Psychology lacks a unified paradigm because it sits at the intersection of two unresolved Enlightenment-era failures: we have no agreed-upon account of how mind relates to matter, and no agreed-upon method for integrating scientific knowledge with subjective and social knowing.

The Enlightenment Gap is the deepest root problem underlying psychology, psychotherapy, and the crisis of meaning. It has two distinct dimensions: an ontological one and an epistemological one. The ontological dimension is the absence of any consensually agreed-upon framework for placing mind in relation to matter — what is consciousness, what is mind, and how do they fit into the physical universe? The epistemological dimension is the absence of any agreed-upon framework for placing scientific knowledge in relation to subjective and social knowledge — the three vectors of knowing that UTOK calls objective, subjective, and intersubjective. These two confusions are not the same thing, and conflating them is itself a symptom of the gap. Psychology is the first discipline where paradigmatic consensus collapses, and the reason is that it sits precisely at the intersection of both unresolved sides of the Enlightenment Gap: it is trying to be a science of mind, and we have no agreed-upon ontology of mind and no agreed-upon epistemology for how to study it scientifically. This is not a peripheral problem — it is the structural reason why psychology has no unified paradigm, why psychotherapy has no coherent scientific foundation, and why the crisis of meaning and mental health remains so intractable.

## Apprentice

There is a deep, largely unrecognized problem at the heart of modern thought called the Enlightenment Gap. It comes in two parts. First, we still have no shared understanding of how the mind fits into the physical world — what consciousness actually is and how it relates to matter. Second, we have no shared understanding of how scientific knowledge relates to what we know through personal experience or through culture and shared meaning.

These two problems are different from each other, but they tend to get lumped together, which only makes things worse. Psychology is the field that suffers most, because it is trying to be a rigorous science of the very thing — the mind — that we cannot yet place in our picture of reality. It is also trying to study something deeply personal and social using methods designed for the physical world.

This is not a minor academic debate. It is the structural reason why psychology has never achieved the kind of consensus that physics or biology enjoys, why psychotherapy lacks a coherent scientific foundation, and why the modern crisis of meaning and mental health has proven so stubbornly difficult to address. Until both dimensions of the Enlightenment Gap are confronted directly, these problems will persist.

## Adept

The concept of the Enlightenment Gap identifies the deepest structural failure underlying psychology, psychotherapy, and the broader crisis of meaning. It has two distinct dimensions that must not be conflated. The ontological dimension concerns the absence of any consensually agreed-upon framework for placing mind in relation to matter — the hard problem of consciousness and the mind-body problem remain unresolved at the level of paradigmatic consensus. The epistemological dimension concerns the absence of any agreed-upon framework for integrating scientific knowledge with subjective and intersubjective knowledge — what Gregg Henriques's Unified Theory of Knowledge (UTOK) identifies as the three vectors of knowing: objective, subjective, and intersubjective.

Psychology is the first discipline in the tree of knowledge where paradigmatic consensus collapses, and this is not accidental. It sits precisely at the intersection of both unresolved dimensions. It attempts to be a natural science of mind, yet there is no shared ontology of mind and no shared epistemology for how subjective and relational phenomena can be studied scientifically. The conflation of these two distinct gaps — treating the ontological confusion as if it were merely an epistemological one, or vice versa — is itself a symptom of the problem.

This analysis reframes psychology's fragmentation not as a sign of disciplinary immaturity but as a predictable consequence of foundational philosophical incoherence inherited from the Enlightenment. It also explains why psychotherapy lacks a unified scientific foundation and why the crisis of meaning remains intractable: these are downstream effects of a gap that no amount of empirical data alone can close.

## Magus

The Enlightenment Gap, as articulated within Gregg Henriques's UTOK framework, designates the dual philosophical failure that constitutes the deepest root problem for psychology, psychotherapy, and the contemporary crisis of meaning. Its ontological dimension is the absence of paradigmatic consensus on the place of mind in nature — the unresolved status of consciousness, subjective experience, and mental causation within a physicalist or post-physicalist ontology. Its epistemological dimension is the absence of consensus on how scientific (objective-empirical) knowledge relates to subjective (first-person, phenomenological) and intersubjective (hermeneutic, socially constituted) knowledge. These are structurally distinct problems, and their persistent conflation — treating the mind-body problem as reducible to a methodological question, or treating epistemological pluralism as entailing ontological dualism — is itself diagnostic of the gap's depth.

Psychology occupies a unique and unenviable position: it is the first node in the knowledge system where paradigmatic coherence breaks down, precisely because it must simultaneously navigate both unresolved dimensions. It aspires to scientific status while its object of study — mind — has no consensual ontological address, and its phenomena — subjective experience, meaning, relational process — have no consensual epistemological warrant within the received scientific framework. The result is not mere theoretical pluralism but foundational incoherence: competing schools operating from incompatible assumptions about what they are studying and how knowledge of it is possible.

This analysis carries significant implications. Psychotherapy's lack of a unified scientific foundation is not a failure of clinical research but a downstream consequence of unresolved metatheoretical architecture. The crisis of meaning is not merely sociological but philosophical — it reflects the inability of post-Enlightenment culture to coherently integrate objective knowledge with lived subjective and intersubjective reality. Resolution requires addressing both dimensions of the gap explicitly and independently.
