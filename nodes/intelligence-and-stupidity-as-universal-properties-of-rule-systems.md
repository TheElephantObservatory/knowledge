# Intelligence and Stupidity as Universal Properties of Rule Systems

The cube that never solves itself.

---

> Intelligence and stupidity are not human traits measured by IQ — they are universal properties of any system solving a problem. Intelligence means applying rules that reliably produce correct outputs; stupidity means applying rules guaranteed to produce wrong ones. This distinction scales from bacteria to economies.

Intelligence and stupidity are not about IQ — they are universal, scale-free properties of any system solving a computational problem. Intelligence is making hard problems easy: establishing a rule or mechanism that efficiently and reliably produces correct outputs from given inputs. Stupidity is not ignorance — ignorance is merely insufficient data, which is recoverable. Stupidity is the application of a rule system that is guaranteed, in principle, to produce wrong answers. The Rubik's Cube is the perfect illustration: the God algorithm solves any configuration in 20 moves or fewer, while a person who only rotates one face will never solve a disordered cube in the lifetime of the universe. This framework is genuinely universal. You can ask how well a bacterium follows a chemical gradient, how well a fish shoal coordinates, how well an economy clears a market — and in each case assess whether the operative rule system is provably optimal or provably self-defeating. Human culture is riddled with institutionalized stupidity: rule systems that produce worse-than-random outcomes, from certain investment strategies to one-dimensional metrics like IQ itself. The tragedy is that our anthropomorphism has confined the concept of intelligence to one species and one metric, when in fact intelligence and stupidity are properties of the entire evolutionary and cultural history of information processing in the universe.

## Apprentice

Intelligence isn't about being smart in the way we usually think. It's a property that belongs to any system — a bacterium, a flock of birds, an economy — that faces a problem and applies rules to solve it. If those rules reliably produce good answers, the system is intelligent. If they reliably produce wrong answers, the system is stupid. Ignorance, by contrast, is just missing information, and that can be fixed.

The Rubik's Cube makes this vivid. Mathematicians proved there's an algorithm that solves any scrambled cube in 20 moves or fewer. That's intelligence: a rule system that efficiently cracks the problem. Now imagine someone who only ever turns one face. They could twist that cube until the sun burns out and never solve it. That's not ignorance — they're applying a rule. It's just a rule that's guaranteed to fail.

This framework reveals something uncomfortable about human culture. Many of our institutions run on rule systems that produce worse-than-random outcomes — certain financial strategies, rigid metrics like IQ itself. The deeper tragedy is that by treating intelligence as a uniquely human quality measured by a single number, we've blinded ourselves to a phenomenon that spans all of nature and all of history.

## Adept

This framework redefines intelligence and stupidity as scale-free, substrate-independent properties of any computational system. Intelligence is the deployment of a rule system that efficiently maps inputs to correct outputs — minimizing computational cost relative to the problem's inherent complexity. Stupidity is not the absence of knowledge but the application of a rule system that is provably self-defeating: one guaranteed in principle to produce wrong answers regardless of how long it runs. Ignorance — mere data insufficiency — is categorically distinct because it is recoverable.

The Rubik's Cube serves as a canonical illustration. The "God's number" result proves that every configuration is solvable in 20 moves or fewer, establishing a theoretical ceiling on optimal performance. A solver restricted to rotating a single face, however, occupies a region of the solution space from which the goal state is unreachable — not practically difficult but formally impossible under that rule set. The distinction between these two approaches is not one of degree but of kind.

The universality claim is the most provocative element. Bacterial chemotaxis, swarm coordination in fish shoals, market-clearing mechanisms in economies — each can be evaluated by whether its operative algorithm approaches provable optimality or guarantees failure. Human culture, on this account, is saturated with institutionalized stupidity: rule systems that yield worse-than-random outcomes entrenched by tradition, authority, or misplaced confidence. The reductive anthropomorphism that confines intelligence to Homo sapiens and collapses it into a single scalar metric like IQ is itself an instance of the phenomenon it fails to recognize.

## Magus

The proposition advanced here is that intelligence and stupidity constitute dual, scale-free properties of any system engaged in computational problem-solving, wholly independent of substrate, phylogeny, or cognitive architecture. Intelligence is formally characterizable as the instantiation of a rule system whose mapping from input configurations to output states approaches provable optimality — minimizing path length through solution space relative to the problem's intrinsic complexity. Stupidity is its precise complement: not epistemic deficit but the application of an algorithm that is demonstrably closed under failure — one whose reachable state space excludes the solution set by construction. The distinction from ignorance is categorical; ignorance is a boundary condition on available data, recoverable in principle, whereas stupidity is a structural property of the operative transformation rules themselves.

The Rubik's Cube furnishes a formally exact illustration. Rokicki et al.'s proof that God's number is 20 establishes the diameter of the Cayley graph for the cube group under the half-turn metric. Any algorithm achieving solutions within this bound instantiates intelligence relative to the problem. An agent restricted to single-face rotations generates a cyclic subgroup of order four — a vanishingly small, non-transitive fragment of the full group. The goal state is not merely distant; it is structurally inaccessible. The failure is not stochastic but algebraic.

Generalized across domains — gradient-following in prokaryotic chemotaxis, distributed consensus in collective animal motion, price discovery in market microstructure — the framework permits rigorous comparative assessment of operative algorithms against theoretical bounds. Human institutional life, under this lens, is dense with entrenched rule systems producing outcomes worse than random sampling would yield. The confinement of "intelligence" to a single species and its compression into a unidimensional psychometric scalar is itself a paradigmatic instance of institutionalized stupidity: a category error elevated to orthodoxy.
