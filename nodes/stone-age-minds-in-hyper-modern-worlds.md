# Stone-Age Minds in Hyper-Modern Worlds

Culture is upstream of politics, but biology is upstream of them both. We've been on this multi-century project, really accelerating since Descartes and the Enlightenment, to separate ourselves from our animal natures — to insist that it's all about cogitation, that we have infinite agency and choice, that we've somehow transcended our heritage as, in Jamie's words, 'monkeys with clothes.' But when you actually look at how we operate — the tribalism, the role of oxytocin not just as a trust molecule but as an ethnocentric bonding agent, the mate selection dynamics, the in-group/out-group biases — the honest conclusion is staggering: remarkably little of human behavior is uniquely and distinctly 'human' in the way we flatter ourselves to believe. Most of it is governed by predictable primate encoding. And this matters enormously for how we think about our civilizational predicament. We don't primarily face an environmental problem or an energy problem or an economic problem — we face a mismatch between stone-age minds and a hyper-modern culture. The levers of politics and policy operate downstream of culture, and culture itself operates downstream of deep biological imperatives around status, belonging, reward, and threat. Until we reckon honestly with that layering — biology first, then culture, then politics — our interventions will keep missing the actual root system of the problems we're trying to solve.

## Apprentice

Most people assume that if we want to change society, we need better laws or better leaders. Some go deeper and say we need to change the culture first. But this argument pushes further back still: before culture, there is biology. Human beings spent hundreds of thousands of years evolving as social primates, and the brain we carry into modern life is essentially the same one that navigated small tribal groups on the African savanna. The tribalism we see in politics, the instinctive distrust of outsiders, the hunger for status and belonging — these are not bugs in the system or failures of education. They are ancient, deeply wired features of primate social life. The Enlightenment project tried to convince us that reason is in charge, that we are fundamentally thinking creatures who can choose our way to better outcomes. But the honest look at human behavior suggests otherwise: most of what drives us operates below the level of conscious choice. This creates a serious problem. Our political and cultural tools are designed as if people were rational agents, but they are being applied to minds shaped by stone-age pressures. Until that mismatch is honestly acknowledged, the most well-intentioned interventions will keep failing to reach the actual roots of our collective problems.

## Adept

The familiar claim that culture is upstream of politics gets an important extension here: biology is upstream of culture itself. This argument draws on evolutionary psychology and behavioral biology to challenge the Enlightenment assumption of radical human agency. Since Descartes, Western thought has progressively bracketed the animal substrate of human cognition, treating reason as the primary driver of individual and collective behavior. The evidence from primatology and neuroscience complicates this flattering self-portrait considerably. Oxytocin, for instance, is widely celebrated as a prosocial bonding molecule, but research reveals it functions as an ethnocentric agent — strengthening in-group cohesion while amplifying out-group suspicion. Mate selection, status hierarchies, threat detection, and reward-seeking all follow patterns that are recognizable across primate species and largely impervious to ideological override. The implication is that the civilizational crises typically framed as environmental, economic, or political are better understood as expressions of a deeper mismatch: Paleolithic cognitive architecture operating inside hyper-modern institutional and technological environments. Political interventions operate at the surface layer. Cultural interventions go one level deeper. But neither reaches the biological substrate where the actual behavioral defaults are encoded. This layered model — biology first, culture second, politics third — suggests that durable solutions require engaging with evolved human nature directly, rather than assuming it away through appeals to rational self-interest or moral progress.

## Magus

This argument advances a hierarchical ontology of human behavior that subordinates both cultural and political causation to evolutionary biological imperatives — a position with significant debts to sociobiology, Tinbergian ethology, and the more recent synthesis of evolutionary psychology with affective neuroscience. The Cartesian settlement, which elevated res cogitans above the animal substrate and bequeathed to Enlightenment liberalism its foundational assumption of sovereign rational agency, is here treated not merely as philosophically contestable but as empirically falsified. The neuroendocrinological case is instructive: oxytocin's role as a parochial bonding agent rather than a generalized prosocial facilitator — documented in work by Carsten De Dreu and colleagues — exemplifies how mechanisms selected for small-group cohesion generate predictable in-group favoritism and out-group derogation at scale, irrespective of ideological commitment. The broader claim is that the behavioral phenotype of Homo sapiens remains substantially continuous with ancestral primate social organization: dominance hierarchies, coalitional psychology, hyperactive agency detection, and zero-sum status competition are not cultural artifacts amenable to institutional redesign but phylogenetically conserved adaptations running beneath the threshold of deliberate cognition. The civilizational diagnostic that follows is pointed: the polycrisis — ecological, economic, political — is reframed as a single underlying problem of evolutionary mismatch, the collision between an EEA-calibrated mind and a VUCA environment of its own making. The policy implication is epistemically humbling. Interventions that operate solely at the level of incentive structures or normative discourse are engaging epiphenomena. Durable leverage requires either working with evolved behavioral tendencies rather than against them, or confronting the far harder question of whether and how the biological substrate itself might be modified — a question that opens directly onto the most contested terrain in contemporary bioethics.
