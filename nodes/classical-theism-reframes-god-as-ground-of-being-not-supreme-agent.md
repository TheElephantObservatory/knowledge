# Classical Theism Reframes God as Ground of Being, Not Supreme Agent

The argument was always about the wrong god

---

> The popular God-versus-atheism debate is a category error: both sides argue about a super-being that classical theology itself rejects. What the deeper tradition points to is an inexhaustible ground of being — encountered not through propositions but through learned ignorance, where sacredness arises as a real, transjective relationship between knower and known.

A profound reframing emerges from the distinction between classical theism and what might be called 'common theism.' Common theism — the dominant popular conception — treats God as the greatest being, a super-agent describable in anthropomorphic terms. But the classical tradition, running through Plotinus, Dionysus, Maximus, Aquinas, and Eriugena, points to something categorically different: God not as a supreme being among beings, but as the ground of being itself — the inexhaustible source of intelligibility that is both the terminus of emanation downward and the attractor of emergence upward. Karen Armstrong captures this when she writes that God is 'that which you move toward as you experience transcendence.' And Eastern Christianity formalized it through the doctrine of epektasis: God is not a resting place but the ground that supports continual self-transcendence — an infinite field through which beings perpetually exceed themselves. This means the standard debate between common theists and atheists is a category error. Both sides are arguing about a super-being, a conception that classical theology itself would reject. What extended naturalism recovers is not theism in the common sense, but something closer to non-theism: the sacred as the being-side of the experience of sacredness — not a projection, not a psychological comfort, but a real conformity between the spirit-self-soul side of the knower and the emanation-emergence polarity of what is known. Sacredness, on this account, is transjective — like meaning and relevance, it cannot be located purely in the subject or purely in the object, but arises in the right relationship between them. This transjective structure is further illuminated by the concept of learned ignorance: when we push to the very limits of intelligibility — in physics, in mystical traditions, in mathematics — we encounter paradox not as a failure of thought but as a signal of something real. Complementarity in quantum mechanics tells us something is both particle and wave, which cannot be, and yet is. The mystics of every tradition — Nicholas of Cusa, Eriugena, the Sufi masters, Nishitani in Zen — arrive at similar paradoxes: the essence of fire is not fire, because fire cannot burn itself. These are not pseudo-scientific claims. They are demonstrations of the incapacity of propositional knowing to capture reality in a complete and consistent formal system — and as Gödel showed, no such system exists. The appropriate epistemic stance toward this inexhaustible ground is what Cusa called learned ignorance: not the absence of knowledge, but the state that most allows genuine apprehension of what cannot be fully comprehended. There is a shining-in and a withdrawal simultaneously — a sense of more-real combined with a recognition that full comprehension is impossible. This is not mystical evasion. It is the phenomenologically honest response to an encounter with something that is genuinely infinite, and a structural argument for why the sacred cannot be merely subjective.

## Apprentice

Most arguments about whether God exists share a hidden assumption: that "God" means a supreme being — the biggest, most powerful agent in the universe. But an older tradition, running from ancient Greek philosophy through medieval Christian and Islamic thought, says something radically different. On this view, God isn't a being at all, but the ground from which all beings emerge — more like the source of intelligibility itself than a cosmic person.

This reframing dissolves the usual debate. If God isn't a super-being, then both the common believer defending one and the atheist denying one are arguing about the wrong thing. What remains is something harder to dismiss: the experience of sacredness. This isn't just a feeling projected onto the world. It arises in the relationship between a person reaching toward meaning and a reality that genuinely offers it — neither purely inside the mind nor purely outside it.

When we push knowledge to its limits — in physics, mathematics, or contemplative practice — we hit paradoxes. Light behaves as both wave and particle. Mystics across traditions describe encountering something overwhelmingly real yet impossible to fully grasp. The philosopher Nicholas of Cusa called the right response "learned ignorance": not giving up on understanding, but recognizing that some realities are genuinely inexhaustible. This isn't an escape from reason. It's what honest reasoning looks like when it meets something infinite.

## Adept

A critical distinction separates classical theism from what might be called common theism. Common theism treats God as the supreme being — an omnipotent agent describable in anthropomorphic terms. Classical theism, articulated through Plotinus, Pseudo-Dionysius, Aquinas, Eriugena, and formalized in Eastern Christianity's doctrine of epektasis, identifies God not as a being among beings but as the ground of being itself: the inexhaustible source of intelligibility that functions simultaneously as the terminus of emanation downward and the attractor of emergence upward. Karen Armstrong's formulation is precise: God is "that which you move toward as you experience transcendence." The standard theist-atheist debate, then, constitutes a category error — both parties contesting a super-being that classical theology itself would reject.

What extended naturalism recovers from this tradition is better described as non-theism: sacredness understood as the being-side of the experience of the sacred. This is neither projection nor psychological comfort but a real conformity between the participatory capacity of the knower and the emanation-emergence polarity of what is known. Sacredness, like meaning and relevance, is transjective — it cannot be located purely in subject or object but arises in the right relational coupling between them.

This transjective structure finds epistemic support in the concept of learned ignorance. At the limits of intelligibility — quantum complementarity, Gödelian incompleteness, the apophatic paradoxes of Cusa, Eriugena, and Nishitani — propositional knowing encounters not failure but signal. The essence of fire is not fire, because fire cannot burn itself. These paradoxes demonstrate the structural incapacity of any complete and consistent formal system to capture reality. The appropriate response is Cusa's docta ignorantia: a simultaneous shining-in and withdrawal, where heightened apprehension coexists with the recognition that full comprehension is impossible — a phenomenologically honest encounter with the genuinely infinite.

## Magus

The distinction between common theism and classical theism constitutes not a mere taxonomic refinement but a categorial rupture. Common theism posits God as ens supremum — a super-agent within the order of beings, differing from finite entities in degree rather than kind. The classical trajectory — Plotinus's One beyond being, Pseudo-Dionysius's hyperousia, Eriugena's natura quae creat et non creatur collapsing into natura quae nec creat nec creatur, Aquinas's ipsum esse subsistens — identifies the divine as the ground of intelligibility itself, functioning as both the arche of emanative descent and the telos of emergent return. Eastern Christianity's epektasis formalizes this as perpetual self-transcendence: the divine is not a terminal state but an infinite field through which beings continually exceed themselves. The popular theism-atheism debate thus operates as a shared category error, contesting a conception that the tradition's deepest resources would themselves negate.

Extended naturalism retrieves from this lineage not theism but something closer to non-theism: sacredness as the ontological correlate of the experience of transcendence — a real conformity between the participatory, spirit-self-soul dimension of the knower and the emanation-emergence polarity structuring the known. This conformity is transjective in the precise sense that meaning and relevance are transjective: irreducible to either subjective projection or objective property, emergent only within the relational coupling that constitutes genuine knowing.

The epistemology adequate to this ground is Cusa's docta ignorantia, illuminated by convergent limit-phenomena across domains. Gödelian incompleteness demonstrates the structural impossibility of a complete and consistent formal capture of arithmetical truth. Quantum complementarity forces propositional thought into irreducible paradox. The apophatic traditions — Cusa's coincidentia oppositorum, Eriugena's theophanic dialectic, Nishitani's śūnyatā as the self-overcoming of nihility — arrive at analogous structures: a simultaneous disclosure and withdrawal marking the encounter with inexhaustible reality. This is not mystical obscurantism but the phenomenologically rigorous recognition that the infinite cannot be totalized, and that the shining-in of the more-real is structurally inseparable from the impossibility of its complete comprehension.
