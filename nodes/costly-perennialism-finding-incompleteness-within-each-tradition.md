# Costly Perennialism: Finding Incompleteness Within Each Tradition

The map that knows it isn't the territory

---

> There is a crucial difference between claiming all traditions secretly agree and doing the rigorous internal work within each tradition to find where its own logic makes genuine room for otherness. The first is a platitude; the second is a civilization-level intellectual project.

The concept of 'costly perennialism' versus 'cheap perennialism' is one of the most practically important distinctions in the project of navigating global pluralism. Cheap perennialism is the assertion that all religions, all worldviews, all traditions are ultimately saying the same thing — that beneath the surface differences lies a common truth. This is appealing, and it fails. It fails because it is not experienced as given by the people inside those traditions; it waters down the very specificity that makes a tradition capable of grounding a person. You cannot stabilize someone existentially with a belief system that has been flattened into a vague universal. Costly perennialism is something entirely different. It requires going into the internal consistency of each worldview — not from the outside, not to deconstruct it, but from within — and finding what Garner calls the 'Gödel point': the precise place where the map reveals itself as not the territory, where the tradition's own internal logic opens a space for otherness without betraying itself. This is not an assertion. It is a Manhattan Project-level undertaking. It would require scholars and practitioners deeply embedded in Christianity, Islam, Buddhism, secular humanism, and every other major framework to do the painstaking work of finding, from within, the incompleteness that makes space for the other. The difference between asserting that all maps point to the same territory and actually demonstrating, from inside each map, why the map is not the territory — that difference is the difference between a platitude and a civilization-level intellectual project.

## Apprentice

A common move in conversations about religious and cultural diversity is to say that all traditions are really pointing at the same truth. This sounds generous, but it doesn't hold up. People inside these traditions don't experience them as interchangeable, and flattening their differences strips away the very specificity that makes a tradition meaningful to someone's life. A vague universal can't anchor anyone.

The alternative is far harder. Instead of asserting sameness from the outside, the proposal is to go deep inside each tradition — Christianity, Islam, Buddhism, secular humanism, and others — and find the precise point where the tradition's own internal logic acknowledges its own limits. Not where an outsider says it falls short, but where the system itself opens a crack, a space that honestly admits it doesn't have the whole picture.

This distinction matters enormously. One approach is a feel-good shortcut that collapses under scrutiny. The other is a massive, painstaking intellectual project requiring scholars and practitioners who are genuinely embedded in their traditions to do the work from within. The payoff would be a form of pluralism that doesn't ask anyone to water down what they believe, but instead finds, within each belief system, an authentic reason to make room for others.

## Adept

The distinction between 'cheap perennialism' and 'costly perennialism' names one of the most consequential fault lines in pluralist thought. Cheap perennialism — the assertion that all traditions converge on a shared underlying truth — is a familiar move in interfaith and cross-cultural discourse. Its appeal is obvious, and its failure is structural: it imposes unity from the outside, overriding the internal phenomenology of traditions whose existential grounding depends precisely on their specificity. A belief system flattened into a vague universal loses its capacity to stabilize anyone. The move is generous in intent but corrosive in practice.

Costly perennialism, as articulated by Garner, demands something fundamentally different. It requires entering the internal consistency of each worldview — not to deconstruct it from a privileged external vantage, but to locate what Garner terms the 'Gödel point': the juncture where a tradition's own logic discloses its incompleteness, where the map reveals itself as not the territory. This is not relativism. It is the discovery, from within, of an authentic opening toward otherness that does not betray the tradition's core commitments.

The practical implications are staggering. This would require deep collaboration among scholars and practitioners embedded in Christianity, Islam, Buddhism, secular humanism, and other major frameworks, each doing the painstaking work of finding their tradition's internal grounds for epistemic humility. The difference between asserting convergence and demonstrating, from inside each system, why convergence cannot be asserted but space for the other nonetheless exists — that difference separates a platitude from what Garner frames as a Manhattan Project-level civilizational undertaking.

## Magus

The cheap-versus-costly perennialism distinction isolates a structural problem in pluralist epistemology that most interfaith and comparative philosophy simply elides. Cheap perennialism performs the familiar reductive gesture: positing a transcendent convergence beneath surface doctrinal variation. Its genealogy runs through Huxley, Schuon, and popularized forms of Traditionalism, and its failure is not merely empirical but formal. It violates the first-person phenomenology of tradition-embedded practitioners, for whom specificity is not ornamental but load-bearing — the very mechanism by which a worldview achieves existential traction. A universalism imposed extrinsically cannot do the stabilizing work that traditions do precisely because of their particularity.

Garner's 'costly perennialism' reframes the task as one of internal incompleteness discovery. The operative metaphor is the Gödel point: the location within a tradition's own axiomatic structure where self-referential consistency forces an acknowledgment of what the system cannot close over. This is not apophatic theology repurposed, nor is it the deconstructive move of exposing hidden contradictions. It is the identification, using each tradition's own inferential resources, of the precise juncture where internal coherence necessitates an opening — where the map's fidelity to its own logic compels recognition that it is not the territory.

The scale of the undertaking is deliberately invoked as civilizational. It would require practitioners with genuine intrasystemic authority — not comparativists operating across traditions but scholars working from within Christianity, Islam, Buddhism, secular humanism, and other frameworks — to produce tradition-specific demonstrations of principled incompleteness. The asymmetry between asserting that all maps reference the same territory and rigorously showing, from inside each cartographic system, why no map can totalize its referent — that asymmetry marks the difference between a diplomatic platitude and a foundational contribution to the architecture of pluralist coexistence.
