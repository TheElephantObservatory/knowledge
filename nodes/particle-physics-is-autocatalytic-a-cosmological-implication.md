# Particle Physics Is Autocatalytic: A Cosmological Implication

The universe that keeps making itself

---

> The standard model of particle physics turns out to contain hundreds of autocatalytic motifs — cycles where particles regenerate one another through known transformations. This raises a testable question: could the universe's fundamental constants be tuned not for life, but for autocatalysis itself?

A remarkable and largely unnoticed fact about the standard model of particle physics is that it is collectively autocatalytic. Autocatalysis, in its most general form, requires only that the structure of transformations among entities forms cycles in which the products of reactions are also the reactants that drive further reactions. When this analysis is applied to the first thirteen particles of the standard model and their approximately twenty-four known transformation pathways, the result is that there are already 486 distinct autocatalytic motifs among them. The particle physics of our universe is not merely capable of sustaining reactions; it is structured in a way that systematically regenerates its own components. This raises a question that physics has not seriously asked: could the capacity for autocatalysis be relevant to cosmogenesis itself? If the values of the fundamental constants in our universe are particularly good at constructing autocatalytic networks — better than nearby values in the space of possible constants, or better than other mathematical structures in the octonion algebra from which our symmetry groups appear to derive — then this is a testable prediction. It would represent a form of cosmic natural selection operating not on black hole fecundity as Lee Smolin proposed, but on the generative capacity of the laws themselves.

## Apprentice

Most people think of particle physics as a story about tiny building blocks smashing into each other. But there is a hidden pattern in how these particles transform. When one particle decays or interacts, it produces other particles — which can themselves react to regenerate the original. These self-sustaining loops, called autocatalytic cycles, are well known in chemistry and biology. What is remarkable is that the thirteen basic particles of the standard model, connected by roughly two dozen known transformation pathways, already contain 486 distinct versions of these loops.

This means the physics of our universe is not just a set of rules that allows things to happen — it is structured to keep regenerating its own ingredients. The universe's particle physics is, in a sense, self-feeding.

This observation opens a striking question: what if the fundamental constants of nature are the way they are precisely because they maximize this self-regenerating capacity? Rather than asking why the universe is fine-tuned for life, the deeper question may be why it is fine-tuned for autocatalysis. This would be a form of cosmic natural selection — not based on black holes reproducing as Lee Smolin once proposed, but on the generative power of physical law itself. And unlike many fine-tuning arguments, this one could actually be tested by comparing autocatalytic capacity across different possible values of the constants.

## Adept

When the transformation pathways among the first thirteen particles of the standard model are analyzed as a reaction network, a striking structural property emerges: the system is collectively autocatalytic. Autocatalysis requires only that products of transformations serve as reactants driving further transformations, forming closed regenerative cycles. Applied to the approximately twenty-four known particle interaction channels, this analysis reveals 486 distinct autocatalytic motifs. The particle content of our universe does not merely permit reactions — it is organized to systematically regenerate its own constituents.

This finding reframes the fine-tuning problem. The conventional anthropic question asks why the constants permit complex chemistry or stellar nucleosynthesis. The deeper question may be why the standard model's symmetry structure — SU(3) × SU(2) × U(1) — produces a transformation network so rich in autocatalytic closure. If nearby values in the space of fundamental constants, or alternative symmetry groups derivable from the octonion algebra, yield significantly fewer autocatalytic motifs, then autocatalytic capacity becomes a selection criterion on physical law itself.

This constitutes a distinct alternative to Lee Smolin's cosmological natural selection, which posited selection pressure on black hole fecundity across a landscape of universes. Here, the selection criterion is not reproductive output but generative capacity — the ability of a set of physical laws to sustain self-regenerating transformation networks. Crucially, this is testable: one can compute the autocatalytic richness of the standard model under systematic variation of coupling constants and compare it against the landscape of mathematically possible gauge theories. If our universe sits at or near a maximum, the implication is profound.

## Magus

A graph-theoretic analysis of the standard model's particle transformation network — thirteen particles connected by approximately twenty-four interaction channels — reveals 486 distinct autocatalytic motifs: closed subnetworks in which every product is also a catalyst or reactant for further transformations within the set. Collective autocatalysis, formalized by Kauffman and extended by Hordijk and Steel through RAF (reflexively autocatalytic and food-generated) set theory, has been treated as a property of prebiotic chemistry. Its appearance at the level of fundamental particle physics is both unexpected and structurally significant. The standard model is not merely a catalogue of fields and couplings; it is a self-regenerating transformation algebra.

The implications for cosmogenesis are immediate. If the autocatalytic richness of a particle physics is a function of its symmetry group and coupling constants, then one can define a fitness landscape over the space of possible physical laws — parameterized, for instance, by the division algebras (reals, complexes, quaternions, octonions) from which gauge symmetries may derive, following the program of Furey and others. The conjecture is that SU(3) × SU(2) × U(1) with its observed couplings occupies a local or global maximum of autocatalytic capacity in this landscape.

This reframes cosmic natural selection. Smolin's original proposal selected on black hole production rates across a multiverse with heritable constants. The autocatalytic alternative selects on the generative closure of the transformation network itself — a more fundamental criterion, since autocatalytic structure is prerequisite to the sustained complexity from which black holes, chemistry, and observers all eventually emerge. The prediction is falsifiable: systematic computation across the constant space and across alternative algebraic structures should reveal whether our physics is anomalously autocatalytic. If so, the fine-tuning problem acquires a dynamical, non-anthropic resolution.
