# Colonialism and Climate Change Share an Ontological Root

The wound beneath the wound.

---

> Climate change is not a problem with colonial dimensions — it is the direct product of colonialism's core move: severing the relationship between humans and the rest of nature. Until that relational rupture is addressed, every climate solution risks reproducing the violence it claims to fix.

The connection between colonialism and climate change is not primarily a historical or moral argument — it is an ontological one, and it has immediate practical consequences for how we respond to ecological crisis. The foundational move of colonialism was the imposition of a sense of separation between humans and the rest of nature. This separation is what made it possible to treat land as property, to construct hierarchies between species, cultures, and peoples, and to extract without limit. Climate change is not a separate problem that happens to have colonial dimensions — it is the culmination of that same ontological architecture. Indigenous scholar Kyle Whyte frames this precisely: the ecological tipping points that the global north is now desperately trying to avoid were passed centuries ago for indigenous communities, and they are still being deepened. But more importantly, he argues that if we focus only on ecological tipping points without addressing the relational tipping points — the ruptures in how humans relate to each other and to the more-than-human world — then every intervention we devise to address climate change will reproduce the same violence toward people and other-than-human beings that caused it. Geoengineering, resource extraction justified by green transition, the removal of indigenous land protections to fund climate solutions — these are all examples of addressing the ecological symptom while deepening the relational wound. The implication is that the relational work is not supplementary to climate action. It is the precondition for climate action that does not simply extend the colonial project under ecological cover.

## Apprentice

Colonialism didn't just steal land and exploit people — it installed a way of seeing the world. At its foundation was the idea that humans are separate from nature, which made it possible to treat land as a commodity, rank entire peoples as inferior, and extract resources without limit. Climate change isn't a different problem that happens to share some history with colonialism. It is the direct result of that same worldview, still operating.

Indigenous scholar Kyle Whyte points out something striking: the ecological catastrophes that wealthy nations are now racing to prevent already happened to indigenous communities centuries ago — and are still happening. But his deeper argument is that ecological tipping points are not the only ones that matter. There are also relational tipping points — breakdowns in how people relate to each other and to the living world around them. If those relational ruptures go unaddressed, our climate solutions will keep causing harm.

This is already visible. Green energy projects displace communities. Geoengineering schemes treat the atmosphere as another thing to manage and control. Indigenous land protections get rolled back in the name of transition. Each of these fixes the symptom while deepening the underlying wound. The insight here is that repairing relationships — between peoples, and between humans and the more-than-human world — is not a nice addition to climate policy. It is the necessary foundation for climate action that doesn't simply continue colonialism under a new name.

## Adept

The link between colonialism and climate change is typically framed as historical or moral — a matter of responsibility and debt. But the more fundamental claim is ontological: colonialism's foundational gesture was the imposition of a human-nature separation that made land into property, species into resources, and peoples into hierarchies of worth. Climate change is not a distinct crisis with colonial echoes; it is the ecological terminus of that same ontological architecture. The extractive logic that drives emissions is inseparable from the relational logic that enabled colonization.

Kyle Whyte's work sharpens this considerably. He argues that the ecological tipping points now alarming the global north were crossed for indigenous communities centuries ago through dispossession, forced removal, and environmental destruction — processes that continue today. More critically, Whyte distinguishes between ecological and relational tipping points. The latter concern the ruptures in kinship, reciprocity, and accountability between human communities and between humans and the more-than-human world. His contention is that interventions targeting ecological thresholds while ignoring relational ones will inevitably reproduce colonial patterns of harm.

The evidence is already accumulating. Geoengineering proposals concentrate decision-making power in the same institutions that drove the crisis. Green transition projects justify new extractivism. Indigenous land protections are weakened to accelerate decarbonization. Each instance addresses the ecological symptom while deepening the relational wound. The implication reframes the entire hierarchy of climate response: relational repair is not an ethical supplement to technical and policy action — it is the precondition for any climate intervention that does not extend the colonial project under ecological justification.

## Magus

The standard framing positions colonialism as a historical antecedent to climate change — a causal chain mediated by industrialization, capital accumulation, and uneven development. The more radical and precise claim is that the connection is ontological rather than merely genealogical. Colonialism's constitutive operation was the installation of a metaphysics of separation — human from nonhuman, culture from nature, subject from object — that rendered land alienable, ecosystems fungible, and relational obligations to the more-than-human world unintelligible within dominant governance frameworks. Climate change is not downstream of this ontological infrastructure; it is its fullest expression.

Kyle Whyte's intervention is decisive here. By demonstrating that indigenous communities have already experienced the ecological and social disruptions that climate science now projects as future catastrophe, he collapses the temporal distancing that allows the global north to treat climate change as an unprecedented emergency rather than an ongoing colonial process. But his most consequential move is the distinction between ecological and relational tipping points. The former concern biogeophysical thresholds; the latter concern the integrity of kinship systems, reciprocal obligations, and modes of consent and accountability that structure relations among human and other-than-human persons. Whyte's argument is that crossing relational tipping points degrades the very capacity to respond to ecological ones without reproducing domination.

This has non-trivial implications for climate governance. Geoengineering, green extractivism, and the suspension of indigenous sovereignty for decarbonization objectives all instantiate a pattern: ecological remediation pursued through relational intensification of the colonial wound. The logical structure is self-defeating — the means reproduce the conditions that necessitate further intervention. Relational repair thus occupies a position not of ethical supplement but of epistemic and practical priority: it is the condition of possibility for climate action that does not merely extend the colonial project under biospheric alibi.
