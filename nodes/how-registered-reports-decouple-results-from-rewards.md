# How Registered Reports Decouple Results from Rewards

The invisible hand that bends the lens

---

> Science's reward system pays for exciting results, not accurate ones — creating quiet pressure to cut corners. Registered Reports fix this with a structural trick: peer review happens before data collection, so publication no longer depends on what you find.

The core dysfunction in science is a misalignment between values and incentives: researchers universally agree that science should be transparent, rigorous, and reproducible, yet the reward system — jobs, tenure, grants, prestige — is built entirely around getting exciting results published, not around getting things right. This creates a subtle but powerful pressure toward motivated reasoning. Most researchers don't consciously decide to cut corners; they rationalize decisions that happen to benefit their career as the scientifically correct decisions to make. The problem compounds as careers mature — it's no longer just your reputation at stake, it's your lab, your graduate students, your legacy. The insidious part is that this corruption happens largely below the threshold of conscious awareness. The solution is not to moralize at researchers, but to realign the infrastructure, norms, and incentives so that the behaviors science already values become the path of least resistance. Everyone already agrees open science is good; the barrier is purely structural. A powerful example of this structural approach is Registered Reports, which attack the root cause of publication bias rather than its symptoms. In the conventional model, peer review and editorial selection operate on outcomes — whether findings are exciting — creating enormous pressure to produce positive, surprising, or clean results. Registered Reports invert the sequence: researchers submit their question, theoretical motivation, and methodology before collecting data; peer review happens at that stage; and if the question is important and the methodology is sound, the journal grants in-principle acceptance — a commitment to publish regardless of outcome. This single structural change severs the link between 'getting an interesting result' and 'getting published,' allowing null results, replications, and messy findings to enter the scientific record on equal footing. It also forces researchers to commit to their analytical approach before seeing the data, eliminating the post-hoc rationalization that produces p-hacking. The incentive — publication — remains exactly the same; what changes is the moment in the process at which the reward is granted. That's the elegance: you don't need to change what scientists want, only the structural conditions under which they pursue it.

## Apprentice

Most scientists genuinely want to do rigorous, honest work. But the system that decides who gets jobs, funding, and recognition rewards something slightly different: producing exciting, positive results. This gap between what scientists value and what the system rewards creates a slow, mostly unconscious pressure to shade decisions in career-friendly directions. Nobody decides to cheat; they just keep making small judgment calls that happen to work out well for them.

The insight here is that moralizing at individual researchers misses the point entirely. The problem is structural, and so the solution has to be structural too. Registered Reports are a compelling example of this logic in action. Instead of reviewing a study after the results are in — when editors can favor exciting findings — journals review the question and method before any data is collected. If the plan is sound, the journal commits to publishing the results no matter what they turn out to be.

This single change severs the link between 'getting an interesting result' and 'getting published.' Null results, replications, and messy findings can now enter the scientific record on equal footing. Crucially, the incentive — publication — stays exactly the same. What changes is only the moment at which it is granted. Scientists don't need to want different things; the path to what they already want just runs through honesty instead of around it.

## Adept

The replication crisis is frequently framed as a problem of individual misconduct, but the more penetrating diagnosis locates it in a principal-agent misalignment at the institutional level. Researchers internalize the norms of open, rigorous science, yet the reward structure — tenure decisions, grant allocations, journal prestige — selects almost exclusively for novel, statistically significant findings. The result is a systematic incentive toward motivated reasoning: not fabrication, but the accumulated weight of researcher degrees of freedom exercised in career-favorable directions, rationalized post hoc as legitimate scientific judgment.

Registered Reports represent a structurally elegant intervention because they attack publication bias at its generative mechanism rather than its downstream symptoms. In the conventional pipeline, peer review and editorial selection operate on outcomes, which means the entire research process is conducted in the shadow of a filter that rewards positive results. Registered Reports invert the temporal logic: theoretical motivation, hypotheses, and analytical protocol are submitted and reviewed prior to data collection; in-principle acceptance is granted on the basis of question importance and methodological rigor alone, with the journal committing to publish irrespective of outcome.

The practical consequences are significant. Pre-registration of the analytical plan forecloses the p-hacking and outcome-switching that inflate false-positive rates. Null results and direct replications become publishable on equal epistemic footing as novel findings. And the mechanism requires no change in researcher motivation — publication remains the target incentive — only a displacement of the reward to an earlier, pre-outcome stage of the process. The elegance is precisely this: the intervention works with existing incentive structures rather than against them.

## Magus

The pathology underlying the replication crisis is best understood not as a failure of scientific virtue but as a predictable consequence of incentive misalignment operating below the threshold of deliberate agency. The formal norms of science — transparency, pre-specification, reproducibility — are widely endorsed at the level of explicit belief, yet the institutional reward function selects on a largely orthogonal criterion: the production of novel, statistically significant, narratively clean findings. This divergence generates systematic pressure toward the exploitation of researcher degrees of freedom, a phenomenon Simmons, Nelson, and Simonsohn formalized as 'p-hacking' but which more broadly encompasses the entire space of undisclosed analytic flexibility through which the prior probability of a publishable result is inflated. The corruption is insidious precisely because it operates through rationalization rather than intention — each local decision appears defensible; only the aggregate distribution of outcomes betrays the underlying bias.

Registered Reports constitute a theoretically principled structural intervention because they sever the causal pathway through which outcome-contingent publication selection distorts the research process. By relocating peer review and editorial commitment to the pre-data-collection phase — evaluating theoretical motivation and methodological adequacy in the absence of results — the format eliminates the incentive gradient that makes positive outcomes worth pursuing at the cost of analytic integrity. In-principle acceptance decouples publication probability from result valence, rendering null findings, direct replications, and high-variance outcomes institutionally equivalent to confirmatory novel results.

The deeper theoretical point is about the architecture of behavioral change. The intervention does not demand that researchers update their preferences or internalize new values; it restructures the choice environment so that existing preferences — career advancement through publication — are satisfied via a path that is structurally constrained to reward pre-specification and methodological transparency. This is mechanism design applied to epistemic institutions: the incentive is preserved, the conditionality is altered, and the emergent behavior aligns with the formal norms science already professes.
