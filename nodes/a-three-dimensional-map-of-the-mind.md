# A Three-Dimensional Map of the Mind

The distance between the script and the screen

---

> The Map of Mind uses three independent axes — evolutionary depth, inside-versus-outside, and the direction of knowing — to precisely locate any claim about the mind and explain why consciousness resists scientific capture.

The Map of Mind organizes human mindedness along three orthogonal axes, and understanding all three is essential to understanding what the map is actually doing. The first axis is vertical — it tracks the layers of evolutionary and ontological complexity from the bio-physiological layer through the mind-animal plane into the culture-person plane. This gives us Mind 1, Mind 2, and Mind 3. The second axis is spatial — it distinguishes what is happening between the animal and its environment from what is happening within the animal. This gives us the A and B subdivisions: Mind 1b is the overt minded behavior visible in the animal-environment interaction, while Mind 1a is the hidden neurocognitive processing inside. The third axis is epistemological — it tracks the direction of the knowing vector. Mind 2, subjective consciousness, is constituted by an interior epistemic position. It arises from the inside out and is not directly available to exterior behavioral observation. This is not merely a practical limitation of current science; it is a structural feature of what subjective experience is. Science as an institution is built on systematic empiricism, which commits it to a third-person behavioral vantage point. The distance between that vantage point and the interior first-person perspectival knowing of Mind 2 is what generates the hard problem and the knowledge argument. John Vervaeke's distinction between propositional and perspectival knowledge is directly relevant here: knowing everything propositionally about a movie is categorically different from seeing it. The Map of Mind gives us the conceptual architecture to hold all three axes simultaneously, so we can be precise about which domain we are studying, which epistemological vector we are operating from, and which layer of complexity we are addressing.

## Apprentice

Most arguments about the mind talk past each other because people are secretly discussing different things. The Map of Mind solves this by organizing everything about human mindedness along three separate dimensions at once. The first tracks how complex a layer of mind we are dealing with — from basic biology, through animal awareness, up to the fully human world of culture and selfhood. The second asks whether we are looking at what an animal does in its environment or what is happening invisibly inside it. The third, and most important, asks which direction the knowing is pointing.

That third dimension is what makes consciousness so hard to study scientifically. Science works by observing things from the outside. But subjective experience — the felt quality of seeing red or tasting coffee — only exists from the inside. No amount of brain scanning closes that gap, because the gap is not a technical problem; it is built into what experience actually is. Knowing every fact about a film is simply not the same as watching it.

The Map of Mind gives thinkers a shared coordinate system so they can be precise: which layer are we studying, are we looking inward or outward, and which direction does the knowing run? With those three axes in hand, classic puzzles like the hard problem of consciousness stop being mysterious paradoxes and start being predictable consequences of the map's geometry.

## Adept

The Map of Mind proposes a three-axis framework for situating any claim about human mentality. The vertical axis tracks ontological and evolutionary complexity, distinguishing the bio-physiological substrate (Mind 1) from phenomenal animal consciousness (Mind 2) and the culturally constituted person (Mind 3). The spatial axis subdivides each layer into an exterior behavioral register — the animal-environment interaction visible to a third-party observer — and an interior neurocognitive or experiential register hidden from direct view. This yields the A/B notation: Mind 1b is overt minded behavior; Mind 1a is the covert processing that underlies it.

The third axis is epistemological and carries the framework's most consequential claim. Mind 2, subjective consciousness, is not merely difficult to observe from the outside — it is constituted by an interior, first-person epistemic vector. It arises from the inside out. Science as an institution is methodologically committed to systematic third-person empiricism, which means its vantage point is structurally misaligned with the domain it is trying to study when it turns to phenomenal experience. This misalignment is precisely what generates the hard problem of consciousness and Frank Jackson's knowledge argument. No accumulation of propositional, third-person data bridges the gap to perspectival, first-person knowing — a distinction John Vervaeke draws sharply when he notes that knowing all the facts about a film is categorically different from the experience of watching it.

The framework's value is coordinative: it allows researchers and philosophers to specify simultaneously which layer of complexity is under discussion, which spatial register is being probed, and which epistemological vector is operative. Confusions that have persisted for decades in philosophy of mind often dissolve once the relevant axes are made explicit.

## Magus

The Map of Mind instantiates a triaxial coordinate system for the domain of human mindedness, each axis orthogonal to the others and each doing irreducible theoretical work. The vertical axis is ontogenetic and phylogenetic, stratifying mind into bio-physiological, phenomenal-animal, and culture-personal planes — Mind 1, 2, and 3 — in a sequence that tracks both evolutionary emergence and increasing ontological complexity. The spatial axis introduces an interior/exterior distinction within each stratum, separating the neurocognitive processing hidden inside the organism (the 'a' register) from the overt behavioral expression legible in the animal-environment coupling (the 'b' register). This bifurcation maps onto, without being reducible to, the classic distinction between neural correlates of cognition and their behavioral indices.

The epistemological axis is where the framework makes its most philosophically loaded move. Mind 2 — phenomenal, subjective consciousness — is held to be constituted by an interior epistemic vector: it is knowing from the inside out, not a property available to exterior triangulation. This is not an epistemic limitation awaiting technological remedy; it is a structural feature of what perspectival, first-person givenness is. The hard problem of consciousness and Jackson's Mary thought-experiment are, on this reading, not anomalies but predictable consequences of applying a third-person empiricist methodology to a domain whose defining characteristic is its irreducible first-person interiority. Vervaeke's taxonomy of knowing modes — propositional, procedural, perspectival, participatory — provides the finer-grained vocabulary needed here: propositional saturation of a domain does not and cannot exhaust perspectival acquaintance with it.

The Map of Mind thus functions as a meta-theoretical instrument: it does not itself resolve the mind-body problem but provides the conceptual architecture within which competing research programs can be precisely located, their domain assumptions made explicit, and their cross-talk diagnosed as category error rather than genuine empirical disagreement.
