# How Identity Is Layered Across Scales of Belonging

Who is the 'we' that speaks in you?

---

> The social dimension of knowing isn't a single layer but a nested architecture — from intimate relationships to cultural worldviews — and recognizing which layer is speaking in any moment of confusion can fundamentally change how people navigate knowledge, disagreement, and identity.

The intersubjective vector of knowing is not a single flat layer — it has a nested, fractal architecture that shapes identity from the most intimate to the most expansive scale. Drawing on Bronfenbrenner's ecological model, UTOK maps this as a series of concentric justification systems. At the micro level are the actual relational fields a person inhabits: the people they talk to, their family, their workplace, their school. These are the immediate I-thou connections that social psychology studies. Nested around those are meso-level communities — neighborhoods, organizations, extended institutions — which carry their own norms and legitimizing structures. And nested around those are macro-level cultural and political identities: national citizenship, partisan affiliation, civilizational worldview. What unifies all of these layers is that they are systems of propositional justification — verbal, symbolic networks that tell a group what is real and what ought to be, and that bind individuals into a shared identity. To be inside one of these systems is to speak in 'we' statements: we believe, we the people. The tensions that arise within a person — between personal experience, cultural inheritance, and scientific knowledge — are often tensions between these different layers of the intersubjective vector colliding with the subjective and objective ones. Knowing that these are distinct systems with distinct epistemic structures gives a person a kind of navigational clarity: they can ask, in any moment of confusion or conflict, which vector is speaking — is this my phenomenology, my cultural identity, or a scientific claim? That question alone, if widely taught, could transform how people relate to knowledge, disagreement, and their own sense of self.

## Apprentice

Much of what any person "knows" comes not from direct experience or scientific evidence but from the groups they belong to. These groups exist at different scales: the close relationships in daily life, the broader communities and organizations one participates in, and the large-scale cultural or political identities one inherits or adopts. Each of these layers carries its own set of shared beliefs — stories about what is true, what matters, and who "we" are.

The key insight is that these layers are not all the same thing. A belief absorbed from family dinner conversations operates differently from a conviction tied to national identity, even though both feel like "what I know." When people experience inner conflict or get into heated disagreements, it is often because these different layers are clashing — or because a group-level belief is colliding with personal experience or scientific evidence.

Simply learning to ask "which layer is talking right now?" can be remarkably clarifying. Is this something I feel from my own lived experience? Is it something my culture taught me? Is it a claim backed by systematic evidence? That single question, practiced regularly, offers a way to untangle confusion and engage more honestly with both oneself and others.

## Adept

Gregg Henriques' Unified Theory of Knowledge (UTOK) maps the intersubjective dimension of knowing — the domain of shared belief, language, and group identity — as a nested, fractal architecture rather than a monolithic social layer. Drawing explicitly on Bronfenbrenner's ecological systems model, UTOK identifies concentric justification systems operating at micro, meso, and macro scales. At the micro level sit the immediate relational fields: family, close peers, workplace dynamics — the I-thou encounters studied by social psychology. Meso-level structures include neighborhoods, organizations, and institutions, each carrying distinct normative frameworks. Macro-level systems encompass national, partisan, and civilizational identities — the broadest "we" narratives that shape collective meaning-making.

What unifies these layers is their shared epistemic function: each operates as a propositional justification system, a symbolic network that defines what is real and what ought to be for its members. To inhabit any of these systems is to speak in collective voice — "we believe," "we the people" — and to have one's sense of self partially constituted by that membership. The fractal nesting means that individuals simultaneously occupy multiple justification layers, which may reinforce or contradict one another.

This architecture becomes especially powerful as a diagnostic tool. Many experiences of inner conflict or interpersonal disagreement arise from collisions between these intersubjective layers, or between the intersubjective vector and the subjective (phenomenological) or objective (empirical) vectors of knowing. The capacity to ask "which vector and which layer is operative here?" provides genuine epistemic navigation — a meta-cognitive skill that, if widely cultivated, could reshape how individuals and communities relate to knowledge claims, identity, and disagreement.

## Magus

Within the UTOK framework, the intersubjective vector of knowing is theorized not as a homogeneous social field but as a recursively nested hierarchy of justification systems — a fractal architecture whose formal structure maps onto Bronfenbrenner's ecological model while receiving its epistemic substance from the concept of propositional justification networks. At each scale — micro-relational, meso-institutional, macro-cultural — distinct symbolic systems operate to legitimize ontological and normative claims, binding individuals into collective identity through shared linguistic-propositional structures. The "we" voice is the phenomenological signature of inhabitation within any such system.

The analytical power of this mapping lies in its capacity to disambiguate epistemic sources that ordinary cognition conflates. When an individual experiences cognitive dissonance or engages in intractable disagreement, the underlying dynamics frequently involve collisions between justification layers operating at different scales within the intersubjective vector, or between the intersubjective vector and the subjective (first-person phenomenological) or objective (third-person empirical) dimensions. Henriques' contribution is to render these collisions structurally legible rather than leaving them as undifferentiated "disagreement" or "confusion." Each layer possesses its own legitimizing logic, its own criteria for inclusion and deviance, and its own relationship to evidence.

The normative implication is substantial: cultivating the meta-cognitive capacity to identify which justification system is operative in any given epistemic moment constitutes a foundational skill for navigating pluralistic knowledge ecologies. This is not relativism — it does not flatten the epistemic authority of different vectors — but rather a form of structural literacy that permits individuals to hold their cultural inheritance, personal experience, and scientific understanding as distinct yet interacting domains. The question "which vector is speaking?" functions as a minimal intervention with potentially maximal downstream effects on identity coherence, dialogical competence, and epistemic humility.
