# Choosing a Spiritual Path While Knowing You Could Choose Otherwise

Surrender is still an act of will.

---

> Genuine spiritual commitment in a pluralistic age requires choosing a path with full awareness you could have chosen otherwise — not naive faith, not permanent noncommitment, but an act of existential courage that earlier religious eras never demanded.

There is a profound difference between being spiritual-but-not-religious as a kind of permanent flirtation with possibilities, and what might be called a metamodern spiritual commitment — one that involves full awareness of all the contingencies, contested metaphysics, and alternative paths, and yet still chooses to commit wholeheartedly to one. The danger in the former is what might be called a stealth narcissism: the sense that you are simply too interesting, too well-read, too widely traveled to submit yourself to any single tradition. You can critique everything, explain everything away, and in doing so you never actually surrender. But the act of surrender — and this is the paradox — is itself an act of will. To say 'I surrender' is a choice. And that makes it something qualitatively different from the naive, coerced, or inherited faith of earlier cultural moments. What metamodern spirituality requires is a self that is non-naive, non-coerced, fully aware that it could have chosen otherwise — and yet commits anyway. Not despite that awareness, but through it. This is not fundamentalism. It is a kind of existential courage that earlier forms of religion did not need to cultivate, because the choice was never so visible.

## Apprentice

Many people today describe themselves as "spiritual but not religious," sampling from various traditions without fully committing to any one. There's a hidden trap in this stance: it can become a way of protecting yourself from ever having to surrender. If you're always the one evaluating, comparing, and keeping your options open, you never actually give yourself over to anything. It can feel like sophistication, but it may quietly be a form of self-protection — or even self-importance.

The alternative isn't going back to blind faith. Instead, it involves making a genuine commitment to a spiritual path while knowing full well that other paths exist, that your metaphysics could be wrong, and that your choice is exactly that — a choice. This is what makes it different from the faith of earlier centuries, where most people simply inherited their religion and never saw alternatives. Today, choosing to commit requires a kind of courage that wasn't previously necessary.

The paradox is that surrender itself is a decision. Saying "I give myself to this" is an act of will. But that doesn't make it fake — it makes it something new. It's faith that has passed through doubt and awareness and come out the other side, not as certainty, but as a deliberate, eyes-open devotion.

## Adept

The distinction drawn here is between two postures that superficially resemble each other but differ in existential depth. The "spiritual but not religious" orientation, at its worst, functions as a permanent deferral of commitment — a stance that mistakes eclecticism for wisdom and critique for transcendence. The insight is that this posture can harbor a stealth narcissism: the implicit claim that one's consciousness is simply too expansive, too informed, to be contained by any single tradition. Everything can be explained, contextualized, deconstructed — and in the process, nothing is ever submitted to.

What is proposed as an alternative is a distinctly metamodern form of spiritual commitment. This is not pre-critical faith, nor postmodern ironic distance, but a third movement: full awareness of contingency, contested metaphysics, and the plurality of viable paths, combined with a wholehearted choice to commit to one. The act of surrender becomes paradoxical — it is volitional, which means it can never be the same as naive or coerced belief. And yet it is precisely this volitional quality that gives it its distinctive character and weight.

This reframes spiritual commitment as a form of existential courage specific to late modernity. Earlier religious formations did not require this courage because the choice was structurally invisible — tradition, community, and authority made the path appear necessary rather than contingent. In a world where alternatives are perpetually visible, choosing to commit anyway — not despite awareness but through it — becomes a qualitatively new spiritual act, irreducible to either fundamentalism or relativism.

## Magus

This insight identifies a structural transformation in the phenomenology of religious commitment under conditions of radical pluralism. The "spiritual but not religious" disposition is diagnosed not merely as indecision but as a particular failure mode of reflexive modernity: a stance in which the hermeneutics of suspicion, applied universally, becomes indistinguishable from the refusal of surrender. The critique is precise — what presents as epistemic humility may function as narcissistic self-enclosure, the subject positioning itself as permanently exterior to all traditions, too capacious for containment. Deconstruction, endlessly iterated, becomes its own form of closure.

The counterposition articulates what might be termed a post-critical commitment structure. Drawing on the logic of metamodernism's oscillation between sincerity and irony, the argument is that authentic spiritual commitment now requires the full internalization of contingency as a condition of faith rather than its negation. The act of surrender is irreducibly paradoxical: it is chosen, therefore it cannot replicate pre-modern submission; yet it is genuine surrender, therefore it exceeds postmodern performativity. The volitional character of the act — "I surrender" as speech act and existential decision simultaneously — constitutes a novel phenomenological category, distinct from both inherited belief and its ironized refusal.

What emerges is a genealogy of spiritual courage indexed to epistemic conditions. Pre-modern faith operated within what Charles Taylor calls the "enchanted" social imaginary, where commitment was structurally invisible as choice. The metamodern situation demands what earlier formations never needed to cultivate: a capacity to hold the visibility of alternatives in consciousness while nonetheless enacting wholehearted participation. This is commitment that has metabolized its own contingency — neither fundamentalist foreclosure nor relativist paralysis, but a third posture requiring sustained existential nerve.
