# The No-Self Argument Only Defeats Descartes, Not the Self

The motte was always empty.

---

> The 'no-self' argument secretly depends on a Cartesian standard of realness we abandoned for everything else centuries ago. When matter, life, and atoms failed to meet that standard, we revised the model — we didn't eliminate the referent. The self deserves the same treatment.

There is a profound double standard operating in the self/no-self debate that almost nobody names explicitly. Both sides share a common presupposition: a standard of realness that is never explicated or justified. When you examine that standard, the 'no-self' position is essentially arguing that the self is not real because it is not a Cartesian substance — an immaterial, unified, enduring thing in which all experiences inhere. But we abandoned the Cartesian notion of substance as the standard of reality for virtually everything else centuries ago, and we did not thereby conclude those things were unreal. Matter is not a Cartesian substance — it is a dynamic system of atoms, electromagnetic fields, lattice functions, gravitational interactions. We do not therefore say matter does not exist. Life turned out not to contain the élan vital. We did not therefore conclude life is an illusion. The atom turned out to be cuttable — the very word 'atom' means indivisible, and it was wrong. We did not therefore say atoms do not exist. In every single one of these cases, we discovered our particular model was pointing at something real, made false claims about what we were pointing at, and then revised the claims rather than eliminating the referent. That is the move I am proposing for the self. The 'no-self' argument sounds radical — but when you unpack it, what it actually establishes is: there is no Cartesian soul. It is a motte-and-bailey: the provocative castle of 'no self' retreats, when pressed, to the much more defensible but far less interesting bailey of 'no Cartesian substance.' This is further undermined historically: Nicholas Stang's work on the divine double shows that in the third century — in Gnosticism, Hermeticism, early Christianity, and Neoplatonism — the dominant model of the self was not a monadic Cartesian substance at all, but a self that is inherently relational, dialogical, and constituted by a spark of the divine in constant developmental movement toward reunion with its divine double. The Cartesian soul is a historically and culturally located model, not a universal feature of human self-modeling. The no-self argument needs the Cartesian content of the self-model to be universal in order to make a universal claim that the self is an illusion. If it is not universal, what the argument actually establishes is that a particular cultural-historical model turned out to be inaccurate — a very different and much smaller claim. The motte shrinks further: we are not saying there is no self, we are not even saying the self-model universally refers to a Cartesian substance, we are saying that when it does refer to a Cartesian substance, that reference fails. That is important, but it is not the radical universal claim that was advertised.

## Apprentice

There is a hidden double standard in the debate about whether the self is real. Those who argue there is no self are essentially saying: the self isn't real because it isn't an immaterial, unchanging soul-substance — the kind of thing Descartes imagined. But we stopped using that standard of realness for everything else long ago. Matter turned out to be a swirling system of particles and fields, not a solid substance. Life turned out not to contain any magical vital force. The atom turned out to be divisible, despite its name literally meaning "uncuttable." In none of these cases did we conclude the thing didn't exist. We just fixed our description of it.

So why treat the self differently? When someone says "there is no self," it sounds radical. But pressed for details, the claim usually retreats to something much smaller: there is no Cartesian soul. That's true, but it's far less interesting than advertised.

The argument gets even weaker when you look at history. The Cartesian soul isn't how humans have always understood themselves. In the third century, across Gnostic, Hermetic, and Neoplatonic traditions, the self was understood as something relational and evolving — not a fixed, isolated substance at all. If the no-self argument only works against one culturally specific model, it can't make the universal claim that selfhood itself is an illusion. What it actually shows is that one particular description was wrong — and that's a reason to revise, not to eliminate.

## Adept

The no-self position in philosophy of mind and contemplative traditions operates with an unexamined presupposition: it measures the self against a Cartesian standard of substance — an immaterial, unified, enduring thing in which experiences inhere — and, finding nothing that meets that standard, declares the self illusory. But this is a standard we abandoned for virtually every other domain of inquiry centuries ago. Matter is not a Cartesian substance; it is a dynamic system of fields and interactions. Life contains no élan vital. The atom is divisible. In each case, the referent survived the failure of the model. The rational move was revision, not elimination. The no-self argument is thus a motte-and-bailey: the provocative claim that there is no self retreats, under scrutiny, to the defensible but far less radical claim that there is no Cartesian soul.

This critique deepens when the historical contingency of the Cartesian self-model is made explicit. Nicholas Stang's work on the "divine double" demonstrates that in third-century Gnostic, Hermetic, and Neoplatonic traditions, the dominant self-model was inherently relational, dialogical, and developmental — a spark of the divine in movement toward reunion with its transcendent counterpart. The Cartesian monadic soul is not a universal feature of human self-understanding; it is a culturally and historically located construction.

This matters because the no-self argument requires the Cartesian content of the self-model to be universal in order to generate a universal conclusion. If different traditions have modeled the self in fundamentally non-Cartesian ways, then the failure of the Cartesian model establishes only that one particular cultural description was inaccurate — not that selfhood as such is illusory. The motte shrinks to a historically local observation, not the sweeping metaphysical negation that was advertised.

## Magus

The self/no-self debate is structurally parasitic on an unexplicated criterion of realness that neither side interrogates. The eliminativist position tacitly imports a Cartesian substance ontology — demanding that the self, to qualify as real, must be an immaterial, unified, temporally enduring substratum of experience — then announces its nonexistence when nothing meets that specification. Yet this is precisely the move we refused to make everywhere else in the history of ontological revision. The dissolution of Cartesian substance as the criterion of material reality yielded field theory, not matter-eliminativism. The failure of vitalism yielded molecular biology, not life-eliminativism. The fissibility of the atom yielded nuclear physics, not atom-eliminativism. In every analogous case, the rational response to model failure was referent-preserving revision. The no-self argument is thus a motte-and-bailey: the motte of "there is no self" collapses, under dialectical pressure, into the bailey of "there is no Cartesian soul" — a claim that is true, well-established, and philosophically uninteresting.

The argument's universalist pretensions are further undermined by the historical work of Nicholas Stang on the divine double in third-century metaphysics. Across Gnostic, Hermetic, early Christian, and Neoplatonic frameworks, the operative self-model was not monadic substance but relational process: a divine spark in dialogical, developmental movement toward its transcendent counterpart. The Cartesian soul is a historically contingent artifact, not a transcultural invariant of self-modeling.

This contingency is fatal to the no-self argument's scope. The eliminativist conclusion — selfhood as such is illusory — requires that the Cartesian content be universally presupposed by the self-concept, such that its failure entails the failure of all possible self-reference. If pre-Cartesian and non-Cartesian self-models were already non-substantialist, the argument establishes only the local failure of a particular model. What remains is not elimination but the philosophical task the argument foreclosed: specifying what the self positively is, once freed from the Cartesian constraint.
