# Symmathesy: How Living Systems Continuously Learn Together

The crooked tree was never broken

---

> Symmathesy names the mutual learning happening continuously among all parts of a living system. Every organism learns something different from the same conditions — and any model that ignores this ongoing, asymmetric co-learning is already obsolete.

Symmathesy — a word coined from the Greek sym, meaning together, and mathesi, meaning learning — names something that systems theory kept missing. Most models of complex systems were static: here are the agents, here are the relationships, here is the structure. But what is actually happening is that all of the agents, all of the contexts, all of the organisms are continuously learning to be together — or apart, which is also a form of learning. It is mutual learning, but crucially, mutual does not mean identical. Every organism is learning something different in response to the same conditions. Look at a crooked tree. You could ask how to make it straight. Or you could ask: how is this tree learning to be in its world? Immediately you have to expand your attention into the shadows cast by neighboring trees, the location of water, the soil nutrients, the ant colonies, the human foot traffic. Everything is shifting in relation to the tree, and the tree is shifting in relation to everything. This same logic applies to addiction, to childhood learning, to the COVID pandemic — where every household, every culture, every country processed the same information and arrived at completely different responses. That was not a failure of communication. That is how life works. The mechanical dream that you could input uniform information and receive uniform responses was always delusional. And if you add time to any static model, the model becomes obsolete, because everything is learning all the time. Symmathesy is an invitation to enter these questions differently — not to fix the child, or the school, or the economy in isolation, but to ask how the child is learning to be in their world across all of those contexts simultaneously, and to tend to the mutual learning that is already happening whether we acknowledge it or not.

## Apprentice

Most ways of understanding complex situations treat them like machines: identify the parts, map the connections, describe the structure. But living systems are not static arrangements. Everything in them — every person, creature, context — is constantly learning in response to everything else. Symmathesy is a word coined to name this process: mutual learning in living systems.

Consider a crooked tree. Instead of asking how to straighten it, ask how the tree is learning to exist in its world — responding to shade from neighboring trees, the location of water, ant colonies, foot traffic. The tree shifts in relation to its surroundings, and its surroundings shift in relation to it. This is not a flaw. This is how life works.

The same logic applies to a child struggling in school, a family navigating addiction, or entire countries responding differently to a pandemic. The dream that uniform information produces uniform responses was always false. Symmathesy invites a different kind of question — not how to fix one piece in isolation, but how all the pieces are already learning together, and what that mutual learning is producing.

## Adept

Systems theory made enormous progress by shifting attention from isolated objects to relationships and feedback loops. But most systems models remained fundamentally structural — mapping agents, connections, and architectures as if they could be frozen in time. Symmathesy, a term coined by Nora Bateson from the Greek sym (together) and mathesi (learning), names what these models kept missing: the continuous, mutual learning occurring among all components of a living system. It reframes the unit of analysis from structure to process — specifically, the process of co-learning across difference.

Critically, mutual does not mean symmetrical. Every participant in a symmathesy learns something distinct from the same conditions. A crooked tree is not a failed straight tree; it is an organism learning to exist within a particular ecology of shade, moisture, soil chemistry, and disturbance. The COVID pandemic demonstrated this at civilizational scale: identical information entered radically different cultural and household contexts and produced divergent responses. This was not a communication failure but an expression of how living systems actually process information — contextually, relationally, and asymmetrically.

This reframing carries practical consequences. Interventions aimed at isolated variables — the child, the curriculum, the policy — miss the relational learning already underway across all contexts simultaneously. Symmathesy suggests that any static model becomes obsolete the moment time is reintroduced, because everything is learning all the time. The task shifts from fixing parts to tending the quality of mutual learning that is already happening, whether or not it is acknowledged.

## Magus

Nora Bateson's coinage of symmathesy — from Greek sym (together) and mathesi (learning) — constitutes a precise ontological intervention into systems theory's persistent structural bias. Classical cybernetics and its successors advanced relational thinking enormously, yet their models remained largely synchronic: agents, feedback architectures, and coupling topologies described as if suspendable in analytical time. Symmathesy names the diachronic remainder — the continuous, asymmetric co-learning among all participants in a living system that renders any structural snapshot immediately obsolescent. It is not a property of systems but the processual ground from which systemic patterns emerge and dissolve.

The asymmetry is essential. Mutual learning does not imply convergence. Every organism calibrates differently to shared conditions, producing irreducible heterogeneity of response — what mechanistic epistemology persistently misreads as noise or failure. The crooked tree indexes an entire relational ecology: phototropic negotiation with canopy neighbors, hydrotropism, edaphic gradients, myrmecological disturbance, anthropogenic compaction. The pandemic made this visible at population scale: identical epidemiological information refracted through divergent cultural, economic, and affective contexts yielded incommensurable behavioral landscapes. The expectation of uniform output from uniform input — the core fantasy of informational determinism — was exposed as categorically inapplicable to living systems.

Symmathesy thus repositions intervention itself. Isolated causal targeting — fix the child, reform the school, adjust the policy lever — misapprehends the unit of vitality. What is actually operative is transcontextual learning: the child learning simultaneously across family, institution, peer ecology, and somatic experience. Tending to mutual learning requires attending to the relational interstices rather than the nodes, and accepting that the system's intelligence is distributed across its ongoing, never-completed process of learning to be together.
