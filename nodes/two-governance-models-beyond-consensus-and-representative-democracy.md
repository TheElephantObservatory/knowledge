# Two Governance Models Beyond Consensus and Representative Democracy

Power that dissolves before it corrupts

---

> Two governance designs — Forrest Landry's consensus-to-executive model with an instant-recall 'red button,' and liquid democracy with domain-specific proxy delegation — both achieve continuous accountability without periodic elections, and both deserve small-scale experimental testing before wider adoption.

Two governance architectures are worth taking seriously as alternatives to both pure consensus and pure representative democracy, and both are best tested at the proto-B scale before any wider application. The first, proposed by Forrest Landry, works in two phases: the entire community enters a consensus process with one goal — defining the structure and personnel of an executive function. Once consensus is reached, the consensus process dissolves and the executive operates with real authority. This is disciplined by a 'red button': at any time, a majority can instantly dissolve the executive and reconstitute a new one. The elegance is that it avoids chronic inefficiency of consensus governance at scale while preserving ultimate democratic sovereignty. The second architecture, liquid democracy or delegated proxy voting, resolves the tension between direct and representative democracy differently. Everyone retains their vote but may delegate it — and crucially, delegate it differently across issue domains. You might proxy your health care vote to your doctor, your environmental vote to the Sierra Club, your defense vote to a trusted retired officer. Delegations can be overridden on any specific issue, revoked entirely, and reassigned at any time. There are no fixed elections — the system constantly reconfigures, eliminating periodic partisan hysteria. Delegation can go to organizations as well as individuals; a delegate holding ten thousand votes has a real incentive to read the legislation. Both systems share a key property: accountability is continuous rather than periodic, with no manufactured election crises and no screaming matches over who holds power. Both may have unanticipated failure modes, which is exactly why small-scale proto-B experimentation is the right place to discover them.

## Apprentice

Most people assume governance must be either pure consensus, where everyone agrees on everything, or representative democracy, where you pick leaders every few years and hope for the best. Both have deep problems: consensus doesn't scale, and elections create cycles of partisan hysteria with long stretches of unaccountability in between. Two alternative designs deserve serious attention.

The first, developed by Forrest Landry, uses consensus for exactly one purpose: choosing who gets executive authority and how that authority is structured. Once that's settled, the consensus process ends and the executive actually governs. The catch — and the genius — is a "red button": at any moment, a simple majority can dissolve the executive and start over. Leaders have real power but live under constant accountability.

The second is liquid democracy. Instead of voting once every few years for a single representative, you keep your vote on every issue but can hand it to someone you trust — and different people for different topics. Your doctor votes on healthcare for you, an environmental group votes on climate policy, and you can take your vote back on any issue at any time. Both systems replace the drama of periodic elections with quiet, continuous accountability. Both probably have failure modes nobody has predicted yet, which is exactly why they should be tested in small communities first.

## Adept

Two governance architectures offer structurally distinct escapes from the well-known failure modes of both pure consensus and periodic representative democracy. Forrest Landry's model bifurcates governance into two phases: a consensus process whose sole function is to define the structure and personnel of an executive body, followed by genuine executive authority once consensus is achieved. The consensus layer then dissolves. Accountability is maintained through an instant-recall mechanism — a "red button" allowing a simple majority to dissolve the executive at any time and reconstitute a new one. This preserves democratic sovereignty without subjecting operational governance to the chronic inefficiency of ongoing consensus.

Liquid democracy, or delegated proxy voting, resolves the direct-versus-representative tension through a different mechanism. Every participant retains their vote permanently but may delegate it on a domain-specific basis — healthcare decisions to a medical expert, environmental policy to an advocacy organization, defense to a trusted specialist. Delegations are revocable at any time and can be overridden on individual votes. Because there are no fixed election cycles, the system reconfigures continuously, eliminating the manufactured crises and partisan mobilization that characterize periodic elections. A delegate accumulating thousands of proxies acquires both real influence and a genuine incentive to engage substantively with legislation.

The shared structural innovation is continuous rather than periodic accountability. Neither system requires citizens to compress all political preferences into a single candidate choice at arbitrary intervals. Both likely harbor unanticipated failure modes — emergent power concentration in liquid democracy, strategic manipulation of the red-button mechanism in Landry's model — which argues strongly for small-scale experimental deployment in proto-B communities before any attempt at wider adoption.

## Magus

Forrest Landry's bifurcated governance architecture and liquid democracy represent two formally distinct solutions to a shared problem: the incompatibility between the legitimacy conditions of consensus and the operational demands of executive authority at scale. Landry's insight is that consensus need not be a continuous governance mode but can function as a constitutional moment — a bounded process whose output is the specification of executive structure and personnel. Once instantiated, the executive governs with genuine decisional authority, unconstrained by ongoing deliberative requirements. The disciplining mechanism is a standing recall power: any simple majority can dissolve the executive instantaneously, triggering reconstitution. This creates a game-theoretic equilibrium in which executive actors internalize accountability without the periodic discontinuities of electoral cycles.

Liquid democracy attacks the aggregation problem differently, decomposing the unitary representative relationship into domain-specific proxy delegations. The formal structure permits transitive delegation — proxies can themselves delegate — while preserving override and revocation rights at every node. The elimination of fixed electoral periods means the representational graph reconfigures continuously in response to performance signals rather than campaign dynamics. Organizations as well as individuals can serve as delegation targets, creating institutional accountability structures that emerge endogenously rather than through constitutional design.

Both architectures share the property of continuous accountability — a non-trivial structural achievement given that most democratic theory implicitly treats accountability as necessarily periodic. Both also share vulnerability to unexplored failure modes: cascade effects in transitive delegation chains, strategic weaponization of instant recall, oligarchic convergence in proxy accumulation. These are precisely the kinds of emergent pathologies that resist theoretical prediction and require empirical discovery under controlled conditions, making small-scale proto-B experimentation not merely prudent but epistemically necessary.
