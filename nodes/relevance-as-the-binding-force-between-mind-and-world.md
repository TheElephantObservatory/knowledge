# Relevance as the Binding Force Between Mind and World

You cannot step outside the belonging.

---

> Intelligence works not by processing everything but by ignoring almost everything — zeroing in on what's relevant. But relevance is neither purely subjective nor objective; it lives in a deeper space between self and world, and losing contact with that space is the central crisis of modern knowing.

There is a profound meta-problem at the heart of all knowing: before you can solve any problem, you must first make sense of the situation, and this requires attention and memory operating under combinatorial explosion. The number of patterns you could attend to, the connections you could draw from memory, the possible futures you could consider — these are all astronomically vast. A chess game alone has more possible pathways than atomic particles in the universe. You cannot check all of them. And here is the crucial insight: you are intelligent precisely because you ignore almost all of that information almost instantaneously, zeroing in on what is relevant. Now pause on the word 'relevant.' Relevance is not cold calculation. It is how you care. It is how you commit your limited and precious resources. And notice: is relevance purely subjective? No — you can be wrong about it. I thought she was angry; it turns out she was afraid. Is it purely objective? No — nothing is relevant at all times in all contexts. Relevance is what William Desmond calls transjective — it belongs to the metaxu, the betweening, that which is before, beneath, between, and beyond all the horizontal dichotomies that beset modernity. This is the binding. This is religio. And this is what people are actually pointing at when they speak of meaning in life — not a proposition they hold, but a sense of connectedness that is deeper than subjective experience and deeper than objective attribution, yet binds them together. Truth and relevance are in a kind of Heisenberg complementarity: relevance constrains what we consider true, and truth corrects what we consider relevant. You can never step outside both at once. The view from nowhere is logically impossible — Gödel showed it, Einstein showed it, Heisenberg showed it. What we call myth is the imaginal apprehension of this metaxu — the betweening that fits the world and us together so we can fundamentally belong. And we have lost the sense of needing it, because we replaced mythic metaxu with a universal method — Descartes' dream that I need not commit, need not bind, need not transform myself for truth to be disclosed. That was the great severance.

## Apprentice

Before you can solve any problem, you first have to figure out what matters in the situation. But the number of things you could pay attention to is impossibly vast — a single chess game has more possible moves than there are atoms in the universe. You are intelligent precisely because you instantly ignore almost all of that and focus on what's relevant. But what does "relevant" actually mean?

Relevance isn't just personal opinion — you can be wrong about it. You thought someone was angry when they were actually afraid. But it's also not some fixed fact about the world — what matters shifts depending on the situation. Relevance lives in the connection between you and the world, not cleanly on either side.

This connecting space is what people are actually pointing at when they talk about meaning in life. It's not a belief you hold but a sense of deep belonging — of being fitted to the world and the world fitted to you. The modern scientific method promised we could find truth without personal commitment or transformation, without needing to care in any particular way. That promise severed us from the very thing that makes knowing possible: the living bond between what is true and what matters.

## Adept

At the foundation of all cognition lies what John Vervaeke identifies as a meta-problem: the problem of relevance realization. Before any problem can be solved, the situation must be framed — and framing requires selecting from a combinatorially explosive space of possible patterns, memories, and projections. Intelligence is not comprehensive processing; it is radical, skillful ignoring. The question then becomes: what governs this selection? Relevance is neither purely subjective nor purely objective. You can be mistaken about it, which rules out mere subjectivity. Yet nothing is relevant in all contexts, which rules out pure objectivity. Drawing on William Desmond's concept of the "transjective" and the metaxu — the between-space — this insight locates relevance in a dimension that is before, beneath, between, and beyond the subject-object dichotomy.

Truth and relevance exist in something like Heisenberg complementarity: relevance constrains what we take to be true, while truth corrects what we take to be relevant. There is no stepping outside both simultaneously. The "view from nowhere" is not merely difficult but logically impossible — a point converged upon by Gödel's incompleteness, Einstein's relativity, and Heisenberg's uncertainty.

What traditional cultures called myth was the imaginal apprehension of this metaxu — the participatory fitting-together of self and world that enables fundamental belonging. This is the original meaning of religio: binding, commitment. The Cartesian revolution promised a universal method that required no such binding — no personal transformation, no existential commitment for truth to be disclosed. That severance from the transjective is the deep source of the modern meaning crisis, and recovering it requires not nostalgia for pre-modern cosmologies but a post-critical reintegration of relevance, truth, and participatory knowing.

## Magus

The meta-problem underlying all cognitive activity is relevance realization operating under combinatorial explosion. Prior to any propositional engagement with a problem, an agent must frame the situation — selecting from astronomically vast spaces of possible attentional configurations, mnemonic retrievals, and anticipatory projections. Intelligence just is the capacity for radical, context-sensitive compression of this space. The governing principle of that compression — relevance — resists assimilation to either pole of the subject-object dichotomy. It admits of error (contra pure subjectivity) yet is irreducibly contextual (contra pure objectivity). Following Desmond's metaxological philosophy, relevance is transjective: it inhabits the metaxu, the between that is ontologically prior to and constitutive of the horizontal distinctions — subjective/objective, inner/outer, fact/value — that structure post-Cartesian epistemology.

Truth and relevance stand in a relation analogous to conjugate variables in quantum mechanics: each constrains the determination of the other, and no epistemic position can simultaneously maximize both. The aspiration to a "view from nowhere" is not an unrealized ideal but a formal impossibility — convergently demonstrated by Gödelian incompleteness, relativistic frame-dependence, and the uncertainty principle. This is not epistemic defeatism but a disclosure of the participatory structure of knowing itself.

What is called myth, properly understood, is the imaginal — not imaginary — apprehension of this transjective metaxu: the primordial fitting-together (harmonia) of agent and arena that constitutes belonging. This is religio as binding-before-belief. The Cartesian project replaced this participatory bond with a universal method predicated on radical non-commitment — the assumption that truth can be disclosed without existential transformation or attentional binding. That substitution is the great severance: not merely an epistemological error but an ontological wound, a loss of the very medium through which relevance, meaning, and truth co-arise. Recovery demands not methodological reform but metaxological reorientation — a return to the between as the generative ground of intelligibility.
