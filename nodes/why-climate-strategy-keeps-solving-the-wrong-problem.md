# Why Climate Strategy Keeps Solving the Wrong Problem

You cannot see the tree you are camouflaged into.

---

> Ecological and civilizational crises are not mechanical problems but living, overlapping processes. Nora Bateson argues that until we address the deeply embedded cultural assumptions shaping what feels normal and desirable, strategic interventions merely answer the wrong question.

One of the deepest problems with how we approach ecological and civilizational crises is that we treat them as mechanical problems — like fixing a truck — when they are actually living, multi-contextual processes that resist any single plane of description. Nora Bateson introduces the concept of 'trans-contextual' thinking as a corrective: life doesn't exist in disciplines, it exists in overlapping contexts, and when those contexts overlap, flat diagrams stop making sense. The stick bug camouflaged in the tree is her central image — if you are the stick bug and the culture is the tree, what does 'systems change' even mean? You cannot see the context you are camouflaged into. This is why directly targeting climate change does not necessarily produce culture change. The shape of the response has to meet the shape of the trouble — and the trouble is a deep, swirling, gaseous, fluid complexity. Matching it with linear strategy, policy targets, or five-step action plans doesn't meet that shape; it just generates a brilliant answer to the wrong question. The real work is down at the level of the Christmas potatoes — the deeply embedded, transgenerational, culturally marinated expectations about what is normal, desirable, and real. Nothing is 'just that and nothing more.' Every jacket bought at a fast-fashion store, every dinner table argument about mashed versus boiled potatoes, every assumption about what counts as success — all of it is a node in a vast web of contextual processes that are actually driving extraction and exploitation. Until we go there, we are rearranging deck chairs.

## Apprentice

When we face big problems like climate change, we tend to treat them like a broken machine — find the faulty part, replace it, done. But Nora Bateson argues this fundamentally misunderstands what we're dealing with. Life doesn't sort itself into neat categories. It exists in overlapping contexts — economic, emotional, cultural, ecological — all tangled together. You can't pull one thread without tugging the whole web.

Her vivid image is a stick bug camouflaged in a tree: if you are the stick bug and your culture is the tree, how would you even see what needs changing? You're embedded in it. This is why policy targets and five-step action plans often fail — they're crisp answers to the wrong question. The real shape of the trouble is fluid, swirling, and deeply layered.

The actual drivers of extraction and ecological harm live in places we rarely look — in what Bateson calls the "Christmas potatoes" level of life. The transgenerational habits, the unquestioned assumptions about what success looks like, the dinner-table arguments about how things should be done. Every fast-fashion purchase, every unexamined expectation about normalcy, is a node in the vast web that sustains the crisis. Until we engage at that depth, we're rearranging deck chairs.

## Adept

Nora Bateson's concept of trans-contextual thinking challenges the dominant approach to ecological and civilizational crises — one that treats them as discrete, mechanically solvable problems. The core argument is that life does not exist within disciplines or single planes of description; it exists in overlapping, mutually constituting contexts. When those contexts overlap, flat causal diagrams and linear intervention strategies lose their explanatory and transformative power. The shape of the response must meet the shape of the trouble, and the trouble is not linear — it is fluid, gaseous, multi-layered complexity.

The stick bug camouflaged in the tree serves as Bateson's central epistemological image. If the organism is indistinguishable from its context, then "systems change" becomes a paradox: the very perceptual apparatus through which one would identify leverage points is itself a product of the system in question. This is why directly targeting climate change does not necessarily produce culture change — the intervention operates on a surface that is produced by deeper, invisible contextual processes.

Those deeper processes are what Bateson locates at the level of transgenerational, culturally embedded expectations — the "Christmas potatoes." Every mundane assumption about what is normal, desirable, and real functions as a node in a vast relational web that drives extraction and exploitation. A fast-fashion jacket, a dinner-table preference, a definition of success — none of these is "just that and nothing more." Each is a condensation of multiple overlapping contexts. Policy targets and strategic action plans, however well-intentioned, generate precise answers to the wrong question. The real work requires engaging the contextual depth where culture actually reproduces itself.

## Magus

Nora Bateson's trans-contextual framework constitutes a fundamental epistemological challenge to the mechanistic treatment of ecological and civilizational crises. The prevailing interventionist paradigm — policy targets, strategic roadmaps, disciplinary decomposition — presupposes that complex living processes can be adequately described on a single plane and acted upon through linear causation. Bateson's corrective insists that life is irreducibly multi-contextual: contexts do not merely coexist but mutually constitute one another, generating emergent properties that resist capture by any flat diagrammatic representation. The demand is that the morphology of response match the morphology of the problem, and the problem is not a mechanism awaiting repair but a deep, swirling, fluid complexity.

The stick bug camouflaged in the tree functions as more than metaphor — it is an epistemological limit case. If the perceiving organism is itself a product of the context it would need to perceive, then the very notion of "systems change" encounters a recursive paradox. One cannot achieve an external vantage on a context into which one is camouflaged. This explains the persistent decoupling between climate intervention and culture change: surface-level targeting operates on phenomena whose generative grammar lies in contextual strata that such targeting cannot, by definition, access.

Bateson locates these generative strata in what she evocatively terms the "Christmas potatoes" — the transgenerational, culturally marinated sediment of assumptions about normalcy, desirability, and the real. Every commodity exchange, every domestic negotiation over preference, every tacit criterion of success is a condensation point within a vast relational topology that sustains extraction and exploitation. Nothing is "just that and nothing more"; each node is overdetermined by multiple intersecting contextual processes. Linear strategy, applied to such a topology, produces not transformation but the elegant resolution of a misidentified problem — brilliance in the service of the wrong question.
