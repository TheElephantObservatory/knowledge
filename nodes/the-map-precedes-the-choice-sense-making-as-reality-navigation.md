# The Map Precedes the Choice: Sense-Making as Reality Navigation

Sense-making is not merely an intellectual exercise — it is the foundational act of orienting oneself in reality in order to make meaningful choices. What draws us to this question is something very deep: we have the experience of having choice, and we are trying to figure out how to orient that choice. Sense-making, then, is about exploring what is real, because what is meaningful is bound to what is real. If you don't have an accurate map of reality, your choices — no matter how well-intentioned — will be systematically misaligned with what you actually care about. This is why sense-making is not just important but central. It is the prerequisite to everything else. Before you can act wisely, before you can build anything that works, before you can solve any problem that actually matters, you have to be able to answer: What is real? What is my relationship to reality? And what is fundamentally meaningful as a basis for how I orient my choices? Without that foundation, all planning, all strategy, all collective effort is built on sand. And this is precisely why the question of sense-making together is ultimately a question of how do I make better choices — and how do I and you become a we who make better choices — in a way that is truly responsive to the environment we actually find ourselves in.

## Apprentice

Before you can make good decisions, you need to understand what is actually going on. This sounds obvious, but it runs deeper than most people realize. Every choice you make is based on some picture of reality — some map of how things work, what matters, and what is true. If that map is wrong, your choices will go wrong too, even if your intentions are good. You can plan carefully, work hard, and still end up somewhere you never wanted to be, simply because you were navigating by a faulty picture of the world. This is why figuring out what is real is not just one task among many — it is the first task, the one everything else depends on. And it becomes even more important when people try to act together. For a group to make good choices, its members need to build a shared understanding of the situation they are actually in. Sense-making, then, is the foundational act of orienting yourself — and orienting with others — so that your choices can actually connect with what you care about and with the world as it genuinely is.

## Adept

Sense-making is positioned here not as a cognitive luxury but as the constitutive prerequisite to effective agency. The argument runs as follows: human beings are choice-making creatures, and the quality of those choices is structurally dependent on the accuracy of the model of reality from which they are made. A miscalibrated map of the world does not merely produce occasional errors — it produces systematic misalignment between intention and outcome, because every downstream decision inherits the distortions of its epistemic foundation. This reframes sense-making as an ontological and practical priority rather than a purely intellectual one. The question 'What is real?' is not abstract philosophy; it is the load-bearing question beneath all planning, strategy, and coordinated action. The insight extends naturally into the collective dimension. Individual sense-making is already difficult; shared sense-making — the construction of a sufficiently accurate and sufficiently common model of reality among a group of agents — is harder still, yet it is precisely what is required for collective action to be genuinely responsive to actual conditions rather than to inherited assumptions or social consensus. The move from 'how do I make better choices' to 'how do we make better choices together' is therefore not merely a scaling of the individual problem. It introduces new challenges of epistemic coordination, trust, and the negotiation of divergent interpretations of a shared environment.

## Magus

The claim advanced here is that sense-making occupies a foundational, lexically prior position in any coherent account of rational agency. Drawing implicitly on the tradition that runs from Peirce's pragmatic theory of inquiry through Weick's organizational sense-making and into contemporary epistemology of agency, the argument holds that the intentional structure of choice is not self-grounding — it is parasitic on a prior orientation to reality. To choose is always to choose under a description, and that description is furnished by one's operative model of what is the case. The critical implication is that epistemic error is not merely a cognitive misfortune but a practical catastrophe: systematic miscalibration of one's world-model produces not random but directionally consistent failures, because the error propagates through every act of practical reasoning that takes the model as input. This has consequences for how we understand the relationship between epistemology and ethics. Meaningfulness, on this account, is not a free-floating evaluative property but is constitutively bound to the real — what genuinely matters is indexed to what genuinely is, which means that ethical orientation without epistemic grounding is structurally unstable. The collective extension of this problem is where the argument becomes most demanding. Shared agency requires not merely aggregated individual models but something closer to intersubjective epistemic alignment — a jointly held, dynamically updated, and sufficiently accurate representation of the environment of action. This is a problem of both epistemology and social ontology: how a plurality of distinct knowers can constitute a 'we' whose collective intentionality is genuinely responsive to the world rather than to the internal dynamics of the group's own self-reproduction.
