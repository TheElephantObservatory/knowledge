# Why Innovation Gets Swallowed by the Status Quo: The Three Horizons Model

Plus ça change, plus c'est la même chose.

---

> Bill Sharpe's Three Horizons model reveals why innovation so often fails to produce real change: without a compelling vision of a fundamentally different future, new solutions get absorbed back into the status quo, creating the illusion of transformation while preserving the underlying logic.

The Three Horizons model, developed by Bill Sharpe, offers a map not of time but of attitudes toward the future — and understanding it reveals why so much well-intentioned effort fails to produce transformation. The First Horizon is the managerial disposition: contending with events as they arise, keeping the status quo functioning. The Second Horizon is entrepreneurial: the space of innovation, investment, and bringing new things into being. The Third Horizon is visionary: oriented toward a fundamentally different future, a different underlying operating logic. The problem is not that these horizons exist, but that two mechanisms keep us trapped. The first is what might be called First Horizon gossip — the enormous proportion of collective energy spent on near-term political drama that rarely changes anything structural. The second is the H2-minus Vortex: the tendency for Second Horizon innovations to be co-opted back into the First Horizon, preserving the status quo under the appearance of change. Green energy that simply enables greater total consumption through Jevons' Paradox is a classic example. The French saying 'the more things change, the more they stay the same' is not a truism but a precise description of this dynamic. Without a genuine Third Horizon attractor — a compelling vision of what we are actually moving toward — all our innovation gets swallowed by the vortex. The model's deepest insight is that transformation requires not just better solutions within the existing logic, but a different attractor altogether: a north star that gives direction to everything else.

## Apprentice

Most people assume that enough good ideas and hard work will eventually fix our biggest problems. Bill Sharpe's Three Horizons model suggests otherwise. It maps three different attitudes toward the future: managing what exists now (Horizon 1), innovating and building new things (Horizon 2), and envisioning a fundamentally different world (Horizon 3). The trouble is that without a clear vision of where we're actually headed, innovations get pulled back into serving the old system.

Consider green energy. It sounds like transformation, but if cheaper energy simply leads people to consume more overall, the net effect is zero or worse — a pattern known as Jevons' Paradox. The French saying "the more things change, the more they stay the same" captures this perfectly. Meanwhile, enormous collective energy gets burned on short-term political drama that changes nothing structural.

The model's key lesson is that real transformation doesn't come from better solutions alone. It requires a genuine north star — a compelling picture of a different kind of future — that prevents every good idea from being swallowed by the system it was meant to replace.

## Adept

Bill Sharpe's Three Horizons framework is often misread as a timeline — near, medium, and long term — but its real power lies in mapping dispositional orientations toward the future. The First Horizon represents the managerial disposition: maintaining existing systems, responding to events within the current operating logic. The Second Horizon is entrepreneurial: the space where innovation, investment, and novel approaches emerge. The Third Horizon is visionary: oriented toward a fundamentally different underlying logic for how things work. These are not sequential phases but coexisting attitudes in perpetual tension.

Two mechanisms keep systems trapped. First, what might be called First Horizon gossip — the disproportionate share of collective attention consumed by near-term political drama that rarely alters structural conditions. Second, and more insidious, is the H2-minus Vortex: the gravitational pull by which Second Horizon innovations are co-opted into First Horizon service, preserving the status quo under the appearance of progress. Green energy enabling greater total consumption through Jevons' Paradox is the canonical example. "Plus ça change, plus c'est la même chose" is not cynicism but a precise description of this dynamic.

The framework's deepest contribution is clarifying that transformation is not a function of better solutions within the existing logic but of a different attractor altogether. Without a genuine Third Horizon vision — a north star that reorients the meaning and direction of innovation — the vortex reliably captures every promising development. The implication is stark: the quality of our vision matters more than the quantity of our solutions.

## Magus

Sharpe's Three Horizons framework, properly understood, is not a temporal heuristic but a topology of dispositional orientations toward futurity. H1 encodes the managerial disposition — system maintenance, event-response, the reproduction of existing operating logic. H2 marks the entrepreneurial space — innovation, capital formation, the introduction of novelty. H3 represents the visionary attractor — a fundamentally different logic of organization, value, and purpose. The critical analytical move is recognizing these as simultaneously present orientational stances, not developmental stages.

The framework identifies two capture mechanisms that explain the persistence of structural stasis beneath apparent dynamism. The first is the attentional economy of H1 gossip: the overwhelming allocation of collective cognitive and affective resources to near-term political theatrics that operate entirely within existing structural parameters. The second, more consequential mechanism is the H2-minus Vortex — the systematic reabsorption of Second Horizon innovations into First Horizon service. Jevons' Paradox operating through green energy transitions exemplifies this perfectly: efficiency gains are metabolized by the existing consumption logic, yielding net-zero or net-negative structural change. The French aphorism becomes not folk wisdom but a precise phenomenological description of vortex dynamics.

The model's most rigorous implication concerns the function of attractors in complex system transformation. Without a genuine H3 attractor — an alternative operating logic with sufficient coherence and gravitational pull to resist H1 capture — innovation necessarily serves reproduction rather than transformation. This reframes the central challenge: it is not a deficit of solutions but a deficit of attractor formation. The quality and communicability of the Third Horizon vision becomes the rate-limiting variable in any transformative project, subordinating technical and entrepreneurial capacity to the prior question of directional coherence.
