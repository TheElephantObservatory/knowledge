# How Objective Science Grew From Subjective and Cultural Knowing

The world split into three, and forgot.

---

> Subjective experience, shared culture, and objective science emerged in that evolutionary order — yet modern thought treats science as if the other two don't exist. Recognizing all three as irreducible vectors of knowing, and mapping how they relate, is the key to closing the Enlightenment Gap.

There is a deep historical and evolutionary sequence to the three vectors of knowing that is often overlooked. Subjective phenomenological experience came first — it arose with animals, with mammals opening their eyes and encountering the world as subjects. Then, as primates began using language, the intersubjective vector emerged: propositional networks that yoke people together into shared cultural identity, binding them around what is and what ought to be. Only out of that intersubjective cultural substrate — through the refinement of mathematics, the Socratic demand for justification, and the development of experimentation — did modern empirical natural science emerge as the objective vector. This means science is not independent of the intersubjective; it is enabled by it, even as it transcends it to achieve claims that are meant to be independent of any particular perspective or value system. The tragedy of the Enlightenment is that in successfully factoring out the subject to achieve objective knowledge, science came to act as though the subject did not exist at all. The result is what UTOK calls the Enlightenment Gap: a fracture between three genuinely irreducible modes of empirical knowing — subjective phenomenology, intersubjective culture, and objective science — with no coherent picture of how they relate. Davidson named the puzzle precisely: we need an overall picture that not only accommodates all three modes but makes their relations to one another clear. Without it, we should be deeply puzzled that the same world is known to us in three such different ways. UTOK's three core pillars — the Tree of Knowledge, the iQuad coin, and the Garden — are proposed as exactly that picture: a way to hold science, phenomenology, and culture in coherent relation without collapsing any of them into the others.

## Apprentice

There are three fundamentally different ways human beings come to know the world. First, there is raw personal experience — what it feels like to see, to hurt, to wonder. This is the oldest form of knowing, stretching back to the first animals that opened their eyes. Second, as language developed, people began building shared stories, norms, and beliefs — a cultural layer of knowledge that binds communities together. Third, and most recently, out of that cultural soil grew modern science: a disciplined method for producing knowledge meant to be true regardless of who you are or what you believe.

The trouble is that when science succeeded in setting aside personal perspective to achieve objectivity, it gradually acted as though personal experience and cultural meaning didn't matter at all — or weren't real forms of knowledge. This created a deep fracture, sometimes called the Enlightenment Gap, where we have three genuine ways of knowing the world but no clear picture of how they fit together.

The philosopher Donald Davidson pointed out that we should be deeply puzzled by this situation: the same world is known to us in three very different ways, and we need an account that holds all three together without pretending any one of them is the whole story. Gregg Henriques's UTOK framework — through the Tree of Knowledge system, the iQuad coin, and the Garden — offers exactly that: a coherent map that respects subjective experience, cultural meaning, and scientific objectivity as irreducible dimensions of a single reality.

## Adept

A crucial but underappreciated insight concerns the evolutionary and historical sequence of the three vectors of empirical knowing. Subjective phenomenological experience is the most ancient: it arose with sentient animals encountering the world as subjects. The intersubjective vector emerged later, as language-using primates built propositional networks — shared beliefs, norms, and narratives — that constituted cultural identity. Only from within that intersubjective cultural substrate, through the refinement of mathematics, Socratic demands for justification, and experimental method, did the objective vector of modern natural science crystallize. Science is thus not independent of culture; it is enabled by it, even as it achieves claims intended to transcend any particular perspective.

The Enlightenment's great success was factoring out the subject to produce objective knowledge. Its great failure — what Henriques terms the Enlightenment Gap — was allowing that move to metastasize into the assumption that subjectivity and intersubjectivity are epistemically negligible or reducible to objective description. The result is a fractured intellectual landscape in which three genuinely irreducible modes of knowing coexist without a coherent metatheoretical picture relating them.

Donald Davidson articulated the puzzle with precision: any adequate philosophy must accommodate subjective experience, intersubjective agreement, and objective reality, making their relations to one another transparent. Without such a picture, the fact that one world yields three radically different epistemic modes should strike us as deeply mysterious. ## Magus

The genealogy of the three vectors of knowing reveals a nested dependency that standard epistemology obscures. Subjective phenomenological acquaintance — first-person experiential contact with the world — is phylogenetically primitive, coextensive with the emergence of sentient animal life. The intersubjective vector is a later evolutionary achievement: propositional-linguistic networks that bind conspecifics into normatively structured cultural wholes, yoking deontic and alethic commitments into shared identity. The objective vector — modern empirical natural science — is the most recent and most derived, crystallizing only when intersubjective culture generated the requisite institutional scaffolding: formal mathematics, the Socratic demand for dialectical justification, and controlled experimentation. Science is therefore not epistemically autonomous; it is a culturally enabled practice that achieves perspective-independent claims precisely by disciplining, not eliminating, its intersubjective substrate.

The Enlightenment's constitutive move — methodologically factoring out the subject to secure objectivity — produced an epistemic topology in which the very conditions of its own possibility were rendered invisible. Henriques identifies this as the Enlightenment Gap: a systematic failure to hold subjective phenomenology, intersubjective normativity, and objective natural science in coherent metatheoretical relation. Each mode is empirically irreducible — none can be derived from or collapsed into the others without explanatory remainder — yet no dominant philosophical framework adequately maps their mutual entailments and boundary conditions.

Davidson's triangulation argument names the desideratum precisely: an overall picture accommodating all three vertices while rendering their relations perspicuous. UTOK's tripartite architecture — the Tree of Knowledge System (mapping emergent complexity), the iQuad coin (integrating first-, second-, and third-person epistemics), and the Garden (grounding normative coherence) — constitutes a candidate solution: a metatheoretical framework that preserves the irreducibility of each vector while specifying the generative and constraining relations among them.
