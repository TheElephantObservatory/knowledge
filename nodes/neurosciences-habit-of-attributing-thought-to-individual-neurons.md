# Neuroscience's Habit of Attributing Thought to Individual Neurons

Wings don't fly. Birds do.

---

> Neuroscience routinely commits the mereological fallacy — attributing psychological properties like understanding or intention to individual neurons or circuits, when such properties belong only to whole organisms. This creates an illusion of explanation where the same word bridges two levels without actually connecting them.

The mereological fallacy — attributing a property of the whole to a part — is one of the most pervasive and underappreciated errors in neuroscience. Wings don't fly, birds do. Alveoli don't breathe, you do. A single renal cell is not excreting. And yet neuroscience routinely attributes psychological properties — understanding, intention, recognition — to individual neurons or circuits. The mirror neuron story is the canonical example: the claim that a neuron that fires both when a monkey performs an action and when it observes another monkey performing that action therefore 'understands the intention' of the other monkey. This is nonsense. The neuron is a part. Intention, if it exists at all as a meaningful psychological category, belongs to the whole organism embedded in a social and physical environment. The mereological fallacy is compounded by premature psychologizing — assigning a rich cognitive role to a neural element before exhausting simpler explanations — and by the reductionist assumption that the right level of explanation is always the neural circuit. What makes this particularly insidious is that the language of psychology and the language of neuroscience are used interchangeably, with the same word — 'representation' being the most egregious example — doing double duty at both levels simultaneously. This creates the illusion of explanation: you have used the same term at the neural and psychological levels, so it looks like you have connected them, when in fact you have simply borrowed the meaning of one to inflate the apparent significance of the other. The result is pseudo-explanation that satisfies the intuition without actually doing the explanatory work.

## Apprentice

There's a basic logical error that runs through much of modern brain science: taking something that only makes sense when said about a whole person and saying it about a tiny piece of the brain instead. A wing doesn't fly — a bird flies. A single lung cell doesn't breathe — you breathe. Yet neuroscientists regularly claim that a neuron "recognizes" a face or "understands" an intention. The famous mirror neuron story is a perfect case: a brain cell fires both when a monkey acts and when it watches another monkey act, and researchers leap to saying the neuron "understands" what the other monkey intends.

This mistake is made worse by a subtle language trick. Words like "representation" get used to describe both what happens in neural circuits and what happens in a person's mind. Because the same word appears at both levels, it feels like an explanation — as if naming the neural activity with a psychological term actually tells you how the brain produces the mind. But it doesn't. It just borrows the richness of the psychological meaning and pastes it onto a firing pattern.

The result is a kind of fake understanding that feels satisfying but doesn't actually explain anything. Simpler accounts of what the neuron is doing get skipped over in favor of dramatic psychological stories, and the real hard work of connecting brain activity to lived experience never gets done.

## Adept

The mereological fallacy — ascribing properties of a whole to one of its parts — is among the most pervasive yet underappreciated errors in contemporary neuroscience. Psychological predicates like intention, recognition, and understanding are properties of whole organisms situated in environments, not of neurons or circuits. Yet the field routinely attributes such capacities to neural elements. The mirror neuron literature is the canonical instance: a neuron that fires during both action execution and action observation is said to "understand" the observed agent's intention. This is a category error. The neuron is a component; intention, if it is a coherent psychological category at all, belongs to the organism embedded in its social and physical context.

The fallacy is compounded by two reinforcing habits. First, premature psychologizing: assigning rich cognitive roles to neural elements before exhausting simpler mechanistic or statistical explanations of their activity. Second, a systematic equivocation in key theoretical terms — "representation" being the most damaging example. The same word is deployed at both the neural and psychological levels of description, creating the appearance of a bridge between them. In reality, no explanatory work has been done; the psychological meaning has simply been imported to inflate the significance of the neural finding.

This produces what might be called pseudo-explanation: accounts that satisfy intuitive demands for understanding while leaving the actual explanatory gap — how neural processes give rise to psychological phenomena — entirely untouched. The interchangeable use of psychological and neuroscientific vocabulary obscures the fact that the hard problem of connecting levels of description has been sidestepped rather than solved.

## Magus

The mereological fallacy in neuroscience — the systematic misattribution of whole-organism psychological predicates to sub-personal neural components — represents not merely a recurring methodological lapse but a deep structural pathology in the discipline's explanatory logic. The predicate "understands intention" is grammatically and conceptually well-formed only when its subject is an organism embedded in a normative, social, and physical environment. To say that a mirror neuron "understands" the intention behind an observed action is to commit precisely the error of attributing to a part what is predicable only of the whole — no different in kind from claiming that an alveolus breathes or a wing flies. That this attribution has been treated as a major theoretical advance reveals how thoroughly the fallacy has been naturalized within the field.

The error is sustained by a compounding equivocation in theoretical vocabulary. Terms like "representation," "recognition," and "prediction" operate as covert bridges between levels of description, performing double duty at the neural and psychological levels simultaneously. This lexical ambiguity generates the phenomenology of explanation — the sense that something has been accounted for — without any actual deductive, mechanistic, or constitutive connection having been established. The psychological connotation inflates the neural finding; the neural instantiation lends spurious concreteness to the psychological term. The result is a closed loop of mutual legitimation that forecloses genuine inquiry into the relationship between levels.

What makes the mereological fallacy particularly insidious is its interaction with reductionist defaults and premature psychologizing. Simpler characterizations of neural activity — statistical regularities, tuning properties, dynamical descriptions — are bypassed in favor of psychologically saturated interpretations that appear to dissolve the explanatory gap between brain and mind. The gap is not dissolved; it is papered over with homonymy. The explanatory work remains entirely undone, concealed beneath a vocabulary that systematically conflates description with explanation.
