# Cells, Societies, and the Co-Creation of the Divine

They are making it as they worship it.

---

> The cells in your body sacrifice their autonomy for an organism they can never perceive — structurally mirroring how human religious communities generate and sustain a greater whole that, in turn, sustains them. Divinity may not be a pre-existing creator but an ongoing achievement of cooperative becoming.

The analogy between cellular collectives and human religiosity opens an unexpected window onto questions of sacrifice, wholeness, and the relationship between parts and wholes across scales. Consider that the cells composing a human body are engaged in something structurally analogous to a devotional practice: they sacrifice their individual autonomy for the sake of an entity — the organism — that they cannot perceive, cannot model, and cannot know. Yet that entity is not separate from them; it is constituted by their cooperative activity. The organism is both the condition of their existence and the product of their devotion. Extending this analogy, human religious practice may function similarly: communities of individuals, through ritual and collective meaning-making, generate and sustain something larger than themselves — call it God, society, or the world soul — that in turn provides the conditions for their individual existence. Durkheim saw this and concluded that God simply is society, a reductive move that loses something important. But the process-relational framing suggests a more interesting reading: the whole is genuinely real at its own level, genuinely dependent on the parts that constitute it, and genuinely capable of exerting downward causal influence on those parts. The parts are not merely worshiping something that already exists independently; they are making it as they worship it. This is neither pure bottom-up emergence nor pure top-down creation but a co-constitutive relationship in which the whole and its parts are mutually generative. The theological implication is significant: divinity, on this account, is not a pre-existing creator but an ongoing achievement of cooperative becoming — something the universe is always in the process of becoming through the creative activity of its constituent parts.

## Apprentice

Every cell in your body has given up its independence. It works, divides, and eventually dies in service of something — you — that it will never see, never understand, and never directly experience. Yet that something isn't separate from the cells; it exists because they cooperate. The organism depends on the cells, and the cells depend on the organism. Neither comes first.

Human religious life may work the same way. When communities gather in ritual, prayer, or shared meaning-making, they generate something larger than any individual — whether we call it God, the sacred, or collective spirit. That larger reality isn't just an illusion or a metaphor for society. It genuinely shapes the lives of the people who participate in it, giving them purpose, identity, and structure.

The key insight is that this is a two-way street. The whole doesn't exist before the parts create it, and the parts don't simply obey something that was already there. They make each other, continuously. If this analogy holds, then divinity isn't a finished thing sitting outside the world waiting to be discovered. It's something always being built through the cooperative activity of living beings — an ongoing process rather than a settled fact.

## Adept

A striking structural analogy connects cellular cooperation within organisms to human religious practice. Cells sacrifice individual autonomy for an entity — the organism — they cannot perceive or model, yet which is entirely constituted by their collective activity. The organism is simultaneously the product of cellular devotion and the condition of cellular existence. This recursive, co-constitutive relationship maps onto the dynamics of religious community: through ritual and collective meaning-making, individuals generate and sustain a transpersonal reality that in turn provides the framework for their individual lives.

Durkheim recognized this loop but collapsed it reductively, identifying God with society and treating the higher-level entity as merely epiphenomenal shorthand for collective behavior. A process-relational framework resists this flattening. Drawing on Whiteheadian metaphysics and its theological extensions, the argument holds that wholes are genuinely real at their own level of description — ontologically dependent on their parts yet irreducible to them, and capable of exercising genuine downward causation. The parts do not worship something that pre-exists them; they are constituting it through the very act of worship.

The theological implications cut deep. Divinity, on this reading, is not a pre-existing transcendent creator but an emergent-and-constitutive reality — something the universe is perpetually becoming through the creative cooperation of its constituents. This is neither classical theism's top-down sovereignty nor reductive naturalism's bottom-up dismissal. It is a co-constitutive ontology in which parts and wholes are mutually generative across scales, from cellular collectives to human communities to whatever transpersonal realities those communities bring into being and sustain.

## Magus

The isomorphism between multicellular cooperation and religious devotion exposes a deep structural principle operative across ontological scales. Cells composing a metazoan sacrifice reproductive autonomy for an organismic whole they cannot represent — an entity that is at once their emergent product and their existential condition. This recursive dependency is not merely analogical when mapped onto human religiosity; it identifies a shared formal structure of part-whole co-constitution that process-relational metaphysics is uniquely equipped to articulate. Whitehead's account of societies of actual occasions, in which higher-order societies exert real but non-coercive influence on their members while depending on those members for their own concrescing actuality, provides the ontological grammar.

Durkheim's identification of God with society grasped the constitutive direction — the sacred as generated by collective effervescence — but performed a mereological reduction that eliminated the genuine causal powers of the emergent whole. The process-relational correction preserves Durkheim's sociological insight while granting ontological thickness to the higher-level entity. Downward causation here is not mysterious vitalism but the constraining and enabling influence that an actual pattern of relational organization exerts on its constituent processes — what Terrence Deacon might call an "absential" constraint and what Michael Levin's work on bioelectric networks demonstrates empirically at the cellular level.

The theological yield is a conception of divinity as ongoing achievement rather than antecedent fact — neither ens realissimum nor social projection but the perpetually incomplete actualization of cooperative becoming across nested scales. The parts are not worshiping what already exists; they are enacting it into reality through their coordinated self-offering. This co-constitutive theology dissolves the classical opposition between transcendence and immanence: the divine is transcendent precisely insofar as it is irreducible to any given part, and immanent precisely insofar as it exists nowhere else than in the relational activity of those parts.
