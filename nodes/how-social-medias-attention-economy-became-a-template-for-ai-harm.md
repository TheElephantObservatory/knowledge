# How Social Media's Attention Economy Became a Template for AI Harm

The infrastructure was quietly repurposed.

---

> Social media began as a connectivity tool but became an attention extraction system that empowers extreme actors. AI will not create new perverse incentives — it will supercharge the existing ones, making the choice to leave current incentive structures unchanged an active decision to accelerate toward outcomes already identified as harmful.

The original optimism about social networking was not irrational — it was based on a real pattern in human history. Every time connectivity improves, you get jumps in information sharing, invention, and productivity. The early internet fulfilled that promise. But something changed when algorithms, newsfeeds, and performance metrics were layered on top of connection. The system stopped being about connecting people and became about performing at people — optimizing for content that generates the most views, likes, and engagement. What had been a communication infrastructure became an attention extraction system. And once that shift happened, the law of decay took over: any open system will be colonized by the actors most motivated to exploit it. The dynamics have systematically empowered the most extreme actors: far-left, far-right, foreign intelligence operations, and coordinated trolling campaigns. The rest of the population is left bewildered, asking what happened to the world they thought they understood. The answer is that the infrastructure was quietly repurposed — and AI does not introduce new perverse incentives, it finds every possible path to the ones that already exist with exponentially greater efficiency. The immersive games pulling boys out of the real world will become more immersive. AI companions substituting for human relationship will become more responsive and eventually embodied. Synthetic content will become indistinguishable from real. And the political dysfunction social media accelerated will be amplified by tools that generate persuasive content at industrial scale. The implication is not that AI is inherently catastrophic — it is that the incentive structures must be changed before the power is multiplied. If the outcome we are currently producing is a more addicted, distracted, lonely, and radicalized society, then supercharging the incentives that produce that outcome with AI is not a neutral act. It is a choice to accelerate toward a destination we have already identified as harmful.

## Apprentice

The early excitement about social media wasn't foolish. Throughout history, whenever people gained new ways to connect, good things followed — more sharing of ideas, more invention, more progress. The early internet delivered on that promise. But something shifted when platforms introduced algorithms, newsfeeds, and engagement metrics. The goal stopped being connection and became performance. Instead of talking with each other, people started performing at each other, chasing likes and views. The system was quietly redesigned to capture attention, not to foster communication.

Once that shift happened, the loudest and most extreme voices gained the most power. Political extremists, foreign propaganda operations, and organized trolling campaigns thrived, while ordinary people were left confused about why the world seemed to be falling apart. The infrastructure had been repurposed beneath their feet.

Now AI enters this landscape — not as a new source of problems, but as a multiplier of the problems that already exist. Immersive games will become more addictive. AI companions will replace more human relationships. Fake content will become impossible to detect. Political manipulation will scale to industrial levels. The core argument is straightforward: if the current system is already producing a more addicted, lonely, and radicalized society, then amplifying those same incentives with AI is not a neutral technological step. It is a deliberate choice to accelerate toward a destination we already know is harmful. The incentive structures must change before the power is multiplied.

## Adept

The initial optimism surrounding social networking reflected a well-grounded historical pattern: improvements in connectivity have reliably produced gains in information sharing, innovation, and economic productivity. The early internet validated this expectation. The critical inflection point came with the introduction of algorithmic curation, engagement metrics, and performance-driven newsfeeds. These layers transformed what had been a communication infrastructure into an attention extraction system — one optimized not for connection but for content that maximizes views, likes, and time-on-platform. This architectural shift triggered a predictable dynamic: any open system will be colonized by the actors most motivated to exploit it. The result has been the systematic empowerment of extreme political actors, foreign intelligence operations, and coordinated influence campaigns, while the median user experiences growing disorientation.

The argument extends to artificial intelligence not as a novel threat vector but as an accelerant of existing perverse incentive structures. AI does not introduce fundamentally new failure modes — it discovers and exploits every available path to the incentives already embedded in the system, but with exponentially greater efficiency. Immersive gaming becomes more captivating, AI companions more responsive and eventually embodied, synthetic content indistinguishable from authentic material, and persuasive political content producible at industrial scale.

The implication is precise and consequential: AI is not inherently catastrophic, but deploying it atop unreformed incentive structures constitutes an active choice rather than a passive technological development. If the current configuration of digital platforms is producing measurably more addicted, isolated, and radicalized populations, then multiplying the power of those same configurations is not neutral. It is an acceleration toward a destination already identified as harmful. The intervention point is the incentive architecture itself, and it must be addressed before the amplification occurs.

## Magus

The genealogy of techno-optimism around social networking is not a story of naïveté but of pattern recognition applied to an incomplete system. Historically, connectivity gains — from printing to telegraphy to the early internet — have reliably generated positive-sum dynamics in information diffusion and productive capacity. The discontinuity emerged not with the network itself but with its monetization layer: the imposition of algorithmic curation, engagement metrics, and feed architectures that reoriented the system's objective function from facilitating communication to maximizing attention extraction. This constitutes a classic case of Goodhart's Law operating at civilizational scale — the metric of engagement became the target, and in doing so ceased to measure what it was originally meant to proxy.

The subsequent colonization dynamics follow directly. An open system optimized for engagement will be dominated by actors with the highest motivation-to-exploit ratio: ideological extremists, state-sponsored information operations, and coordinated inauthentic behavior networks. The median participant becomes a substrate rather than a beneficiary — bewildered by an epistemic environment that has been adversarially restructured beneath the threshold of conscious perception. This is not conspiracy but emergent exploitation of misaligned incentive gradients.

Artificial intelligence enters this landscape not as an exogenous shock but as a gradient-descent optimizer applied to pre-existing objective functions. It will not generate novel perverse incentives; it will saturate the exploitation surface of those already operative. Immersive environments become more absorptive, parasocial substitutes more convincing, synthetic content epistemically indistinguishable from authentic testimony, and persuasion architectures scalable to industrial throughput. The critical insight is that deploying transformative capability atop unreformed incentive structures is not a technologically determined outcome — it is a policy choice with identifiable consequences. The intervention surface is the incentive architecture itself, and the window for structural reform precedes, not follows, the capability multiplication.
