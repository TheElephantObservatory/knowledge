# How the Brain Builds a Unified Field of Conscious Experience

The plasma ball at the center of the self

---

> Conscious experience can be understood as a layered field: a core of pleasure-and-pain feeling that binds perception to motivation, overlaid by a cortical broadcast that models the world across time, with an attentional spotlight that brings hereness and nowness to whatever it touches.

A useful model for understanding the architecture of Mind 2 — subjective consciousness — is the plasma ball metaphor. Evolution produced two fundamental steps in the development of conscious experience. The first was a binding event: the convergence of exterior perception, interior motivational state, and energized emotion into a unified field. Pleasure and pain are the earliest expressions of this binding — valence qualia that tie sensory input to motivational states and generate learning by tracking whether an organism's engagement with the world is going well or badly. This produces a core affective field, a kind of semantic subjective ground that enacts the body rather than modeling it. The second step was the layering of a cortex over this core. The cortex models mindedness from within that core field — integrating sensory and perceptual information in the posterior regions, and manipulating representations across time through working memory and executive control in the frontal regions. Working memory itself has a dual phenomenological structure: a visuospatial sketchpad for imagery and a phonological loop for inner speech, coordinated by a central executive around an episodic buffer. The result is a virtual field — Mind 2 — that models Mind 1 through a binding structure that affords learning, and then extends that into a broadcast perspectival awareness across time. Conscious contents come in three kinds: core valence qualia that give the structure its affective flavor; adjectival qualia that are the sensory properties of objects and experiences; and an adverbial attender — the beam of conscious attention — that brings hereness, nowness, and togetherness to whatever it indexes. This is not a theater model or a stream model, but a field model with a core, a broadcast function, and an attentional spotlight operating within it.

## Apprentice

One way to picture conscious experience is as a kind of energy field with a hot center and an expanding glow — like a plasma ball. At the center is something very old in evolutionary terms: the raw feeling of whether things are going well or badly. This isn't a thought or an image — it's a bodily sense of pleasure or pain that ties what you're sensing to what you need. It's the most basic form of "what it's like" to be alive.

Layered over that core is the more recently evolved cortex, which does something remarkable: it builds a model of the world and of your own mind from within that feeling. The back of the cortex processes what you see, hear, and touch. The front holds things in mind over time, plans, and coordinates — using a kind of inner sketchpad for images and an inner voice for words, all managed by a central coordinator.

The result is a three-part structure of conscious experience. There are core feelings that color everything with good-or-bad tone. There are the sensory qualities of things — redness, loudness, warmth. And there is the beam of attention itself, which brings the sense of "here, now, together" to whatever it lands on. This isn't consciousness as a movie screen or a flowing river — it's a living field with a center, a broadcast, and a searchlight.

## Adept

The plasma ball metaphor offers a structural model of subjective consciousness — Mind 2 — built on two evolutionary innovations. The first is a binding event: the convergence of exteroceptive perception, interoceptive motivational state, and energized emotion into a unified core affective field. Pleasure and pain are the primordial expressions of this binding — valence qualia that couple sensory input to motivational relevance and generate learning by tracking whether organismic engagement is going well or badly. Crucially, this core field enacts the body rather than representing it; it is a semantic subjective ground, not a model.

The second innovation is cortical layering over this affective core. Posterior cortex integrates sensory and perceptual information, while frontal regions support working memory and executive control — the manipulation of representations across time. Working memory itself has a dual phenomenological architecture: a visuospatial sketchpad and a phonological loop, coordinated by a central executive around an episodic buffer. The cortex thus models mindedness from within the core field, extending the binding structure into a broadcast perspectival awareness.

This yields a taxonomy of conscious contents. Core valence qualia provide affective flavor — the good-or-bad tone saturating experience. Adjectival qualia are the sensory properties of objects and scenes — color, texture, pitch. The adverbial attender is the beam of conscious attention itself, conferring hereness, nowness, and togetherness upon whatever it indexes. The resulting architecture is neither a Cartesian theater nor a Jamesian stream but a field model: a core, a broadcast function, and an attentional spotlight operating within a unified phenomenal space.

## Magus

This model proposes that Mind 2 — the architecture of subjective consciousness — is best understood as a layered field rather than a theater or stream. Its evolutionary foundation is a binding event that fuses exteroceptive perception, interoceptive motivational state, and affective arousal into a core phenomenal field. Valence qualia — pleasure and pain — are the earliest and most fundamental products of this binding, functioning as learning signals that track the organism's relational success or failure. The core field is enactive rather than representational: it does not model the body but constitutes a semantic subjective ground from which all further conscious elaboration proceeds.

Cortical evolution layers a modeling capacity over this affective core. Posterior regions integrate sensory-perceptual content; frontal regions sustain working memory and executive control, enabling the temporal manipulation of representations. Working memory's phenomenological structure — Baddeley's visuospatial sketchpad and phonological loop, coordinated by a central executive around an episodic buffer — provides the machinery through which Mind 2 models Mind 1 and extends binding into broadcast perspectival awareness. The cortex thus operates from within the core field, not above it; representation is always saturated by valence.

Conscious contents are taxonomized into three kinds. Core valence qualia provide the affective coloring — the hedonic and motivational tone pervading experience. Adjectival qualia are the sensory properties predicated of objects and events. The adverbial attender — the phenomenal beam of attention — confers indexical presence: hereness, nowness, and the binding-into-togetherness of attended contents. This tripartite structure constitutes a field model with a generative core, a cortical broadcast function, and an attentional spotlight — an architecture that sidesteps both homuncular theater models and overly deflationary process accounts while preserving the explanatory traction of each.
