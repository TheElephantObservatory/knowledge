# Academic Science's Refusal to Divide Labor Wastes Scientific Talent

Everyone must be the whole orchestra.

---

> Academic science forces every researcher into one role — the PI who does everything — wasting vast talent. If science adopted specialization the way business did centuries ago, and rewarded diverse contributions, it could fundamentally restructure itself as a collective enterprise.

Academic science is organized around a pathological assumption: that the only legitimate career endpoint is the PI — the CEO of a vertically integrated research unit who must personally conceive the question, secure funding, design the study, collect data, analyze it, and write the paper. This model wastes enormous talent by excluding everyone who cannot or does not want to perform all of those functions simultaneously. It also caps the scale of any research program at what one graduate student can accomplish in one dissertation. The business world solved this problem centuries ago through specialization and coordination — marketing, operations, engineering, and management are distinct roles aligned toward a common goal. Science could do the same: a data specialist who generates high-quality datasets but hates writing papers, a machine learning expert who builds algorithms others use, a methodologist embedded in a core facility — all of these could be legitimate, credited, career-sustaining contributions if the reward system recognized them. The emerging infrastructure of open science — data citation standards, preprint repositories, shared code repositories, core operating facilities — is quietly building the horizontal coordination layer that would make this possible. The bottleneck is cultural and incentive-based: as long as the only thing that counts is the PI-authored journal article, specialization remains invisible and unrewarded. Changing what counts as a scholarly contribution could fundamentally restructure how science is organized as a collective enterprise.

## Apprentice

In most of the working world, people specialize. One person handles design, another manages logistics, someone else runs the numbers. But academic science still operates as if every researcher must do everything: come up with the question, get the money, run the experiments, crunch the data, and write the paper. The only recognized career destination is the Principal Investigator — essentially a one-person show expected to master every phase of research.

This wastes an extraordinary amount of talent. Someone brilliant at generating clean, reliable datasets but uninterested in writing journal articles has no clear career path. A machine learning expert who builds tools other scientists depend on gets little formal credit. The system treats these contributions as invisible because the only currency that matters is the traditional research paper with a PI's name on it.

The tools to fix this are already emerging. Open data standards, shared code repositories, and preprint servers are creating new ways to share and credit work. The real barrier is cultural: science needs to expand what counts as a legitimate scholarly contribution. If it did, research could scale far beyond what any single person can accomplish alone.

## Adept

The dominant organizational model of academic science rests on a peculiar assumption: that the Principal Investigator must function as a vertically integrated unit, personally responsible for ideation, grant acquisition, study design, data collection, analysis, and publication. This model effectively caps the scale of any research program at what one trainee can accomplish in a single dissertation cycle, and it systematically excludes talented individuals whose strengths lie in only some of these functions. The business world abandoned vertical integration centuries ago in favor of specialization and horizontal coordination — distinct roles aligned toward shared objectives. Science has not.

The consequence is a massive misallocation of human capital. A data engineer who produces high-quality, reusable datasets, a methodologist embedded in a core facility, a computational scientist who builds analytical pipelines — none of these roles map onto the PI career track, so they remain structurally precarious and professionally invisible. The incentive architecture of academia, organized almost entirely around PI-authored journal articles, actively discourages the specialization that would allow research to operate at greater scale and reliability.

Critically, the infrastructure for an alternative is already materializing. Data citation standards, preprint repositories, shared code platforms, and core operating facilities constitute an emerging horizontal coordination layer. What remains is the harder problem: reforming the reward system so that diverse contributions — datasets, software, methodological innovations, replication efforts — carry genuine career currency. Changing what counts as a scholarly contribution is not merely an administrative adjustment; it is a prerequisite for reorganizing science as a genuinely collective enterprise.

## Magus

The PI model represents academic science's most consequential structural pathology: an implicit insistence that legitimate knowledge production requires vertical integration within a single investigator who must simultaneously function as intellectual architect, fundraiser, project manager, bench scientist, analyst, and author. This assumption is so deeply naturalized that its extraordinary inefficiency goes largely unexamined. It constrains the scale of any research program to the bandwidth of one dissertation, systematically selects against individuals whose comparative advantage lies in any single phase of the research pipeline, and produces a tournament economy in which the vast majority of trained scientists are discarded rather than deployed.

The analogy to pre-industrial craft production is instructive. The business world's transition to specialized, horizontally coordinated labor — with distinct functions for strategy, operations, engineering, and distribution — did not merely increase efficiency; it made possible entirely new categories of enterprise. Academic science remains stuck in the artisanal phase, not for lack of technical infrastructure but because its reward system recognizes exactly one output: the PI-authored journal article. Datasets, software, methodological innovations, replication studies, and curatorial labor remain what economists would call non-rival goods produced under conditions of zero formal compensation.

The emerging open science stack — DOI-based data citation, preprint servers, version-controlled code repositories, core facilities operating as shared research utilities — is quietly assembling the coordination layer that specialization requires. But infrastructure without incentive reform is inert. The bottleneck is fundamentally cultural and credentialing-based: until tenure committees, funding agencies, and disciplinary gatekeepers expand what counts as a scholarly contribution, the division of cognitive labor will remain illegible to the institutions that govern scientific careers. The stakes are not incremental; this is the difference between science as a collection of artisan workshops and science as a coordinated collective enterprise.
