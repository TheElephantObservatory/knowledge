# American Polarization Is a Systems Problem, Not a People Problem

The fever lives in the pipes.

---

> Most Americans are not deeply polarized — research identifies an 'exhausted majority' of moderate citizens. The real problem is that social media, cable news, and congressional incentives amplify extremes, meaning the fever is in the infrastructure, not the people.

Most Americans are not as polarized as the system makes them appear. Research by the organization More in Common identifies what they call the 'exhausted majority' — a large group of Americans across four distinct clusters who are actually quite reasonable and moderate. The problem is not the average citizen; it is the architecture of the system itself. Social media, cable news, and the incentive structures of Congress give disproportionate megaphones to those pursuing commercial or ideological interests at the extremes. The danger is not that Americans have become irredeemably tribal — most haven't. The danger is that the network through which they communicate and make collective decisions has been optimized for outrage, not for truth-seeking or compromise. This means the solution is not to despair about human nature but to redesign the system. The fever is in the infrastructure, not primarily in the people.

## Apprentice

American politics feels hopelessly divided, but research by the organization More in Common suggests something surprising: most Americans are actually pretty reasonable. They identified what they call the "exhausted majority" — a large group spanning four distinct clusters who hold moderate views, are open to compromise, and are tired of the fighting. The loud extremes on both sides get most of the attention, but they don't represent most people.

So why does everything feel so broken? The answer lies in the systems we use to communicate and govern. Social media platforms are designed to maximize engagement, which means amplifying outrage. Cable news chases ratings by spotlighting conflict. Politicians in Congress face incentive structures that reward partisan performance over quiet problem-solving. These systems hand megaphones to the most extreme voices while the moderate majority goes unheard.

This reframing matters because it shifts the diagnosis — and therefore the cure. If the problem were human nature, there would be little hope. But if the problem is the architecture of communication and decision-making, then it can be redesigned. The fever is in the infrastructure, not in the people themselves, and that is actually grounds for cautious optimism.

## Adept

Research by More in Common has mapped the American electorate into distinct attitudinal clusters and found that roughly two-thirds of Americans fall into what they term the "exhausted majority" — four groups that, despite real differences, share a fatigue with polarization, a willingness to engage in good-faith disagreement, and broadly moderate instincts. The politically vocal extremes — the "progressive activists" and "devoted conservatives" — together constitute a minority, yet they dominate public discourse. This finding challenges the prevailing narrative that the country is split into two irreconcilable tribes.

The critical insight is structural rather than psychological. The apparent polarization is an emergent property of the communication and governance architecture: algorithmic amplification on social media platforms optimized for engagement, cable news business models dependent on affective arousal, and congressional incentive structures — from gerrymandered districts to primary election dynamics — that reward ideological purity over legislative compromise. These systems function as selection mechanisms, systematically elevating extreme voices and suppressing moderate ones.

This reframing has significant implications for reform strategy. If mass polarization were primarily a bottom-up phenomenon rooted in genuine attitude divergence, institutional redesign would be insufficient. But if the system is manufacturing the appearance of polarization by distorting signal transmission — giving disproportionate megaphones to commercial and ideological entrepreneurs at the tails — then the intervention point is the infrastructure itself. Electoral reform, platform regulation, media ecosystem redesign, and changes to congressional procedure become not peripheral but central. The fever is in the network, not the nodes.

## Magus

More in Common's "Hidden Tribes" research operationalizes a segmentation of the American electorate that reveals a striking disjunction between the distribution of actual citizen attitudes and the distribution of discursive salience. Their "exhausted majority" — comprising Traditional Liberals, Passive Liberals, the Politically Disengaged, and Moderates — constitutes roughly 67% of the population, yet exercises negligible influence on the tone and content of public debate. The affective polarization literature (Iyengar et al.) has documented rising out-group animosity, but this finding suggests that much of that animosity may be an artifact of exposure to curated extremity rather than a reflection of deeply held dispositional hostility.

The explanatory mechanism is fundamentally architectural. Algorithmic content curation on platforms optimizes for engagement metrics that correlate with emotional arousal — particularly moral outrage — creating what Tobias Rose-Stockwell has called an "attention economy" structurally biased toward polarizing content. Cable news operates under analogous commercial logics. Meanwhile, congressional incentive structures — shaped by closed primaries, safe districts produced by partisan gerrymandering, and small-dollar fundraising dynamics that reward performative extremism — select for representatives whose behavior diverges systematically from their median constituents' preferences. The system functions as a distortion filter, not a mirror.

This analysis carries a precise normative implication: the locus of intervention must be infrastructural rather than attitudinal. Efforts aimed at changing hearts and minds misidentify the causal pathway. The relevant variables are platform architecture, media market structure, electoral system design, and legislative procedural rules — the transmission channels through which collective preferences are aggregated and expressed. The pathology is in the network topology and its incentive gradients, not primarily in the nodes. Reform, not moral exhortation, is the indicated prescription.
