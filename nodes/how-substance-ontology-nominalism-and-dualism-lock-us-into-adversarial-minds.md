# How Substance Ontology, Nominalism, and Dualism Lock Us Into Adversarial Minds

The prison built before you were born

---

> Three deep assumptions in Western thought — that reality is made of independent things, that relationships exist only in the mind, and that mind is cut off from the world — lock together and make adversarial thinking feel inevitable. The way out is to see relationships as more fundamental than the things they connect.

There is an inevitability — a kind of philosophical lock-in — between three mutually reinforcing commitments that have quietly dominated Western thought: substance ontology, nominalist epistemology, and dualistic psychology. Substance ontology, rooted in Aristotle, holds that reality is ultimately composed of things that can independently exist. This binds you, through a long chain of wrestling, to nominalism — the view that real relations exist only in the mind, not in the world. And because the mind is the only place where genuine relations are held, the mind becomes ontologically severed from the world. That severance is dualism. Once you are inside that framework, adversarial processing is not a failure of character or civility — it is the only thing structurally available to you. You are locked inside a mind, locked inside a substantial individual, with no real relational patterns by which to genuinely connect to other people or to reality. What religion historically did — in so far as it prioritized the nonpropositional, the relational, the participatory — was act as a counterbalance to this closure. But as that counterbalancing function has faded, the substance-nominalist-dualist complex has become more pronounced, and adversarial processing has become more automatic. The inversion required is profound: to see relata — the things — as emerging from relations, rather than relations as merely mental additions to a world of independent substances. Pure relationality as the ground. This is what Zen and Neoplatonism are both pointing toward from independent but convergent directions. The sacred, in this reading, is not a separate supernatural domain. It is the advent of a language and a mode of apprehension that fundamentally calls into question the subject-predicate structure, the substance ontology, the nominalism — and in doing so, reopens the possibility of genuine relation, genuine connection, and genuine orientation toward ultimacy.

## Apprentice

There is a hidden chain linking three ideas that have quietly shaped how most of us think. First, we assume reality is made up of separate, independent things. Second, because those things seem to stand alone, we conclude that the connections between them must be something our minds impose rather than something real. Third, if our minds are the only place where connections live, then each of us is fundamentally sealed off from the world and from each other. Once you are trapped inside that picture, arguing and competing become the only ways to engage — not because people are selfish, but because the framework leaves no room for genuine connection.

Historically, religious and contemplative traditions pushed back against this closure. They emphasized participation, direct experience, and a sense of belonging to something larger — none of which fit neatly into the framework of isolated things and private minds. As those traditions have lost cultural influence, the adversarial pattern has intensified.

The radical alternative is to flip the picture entirely: instead of starting with separate things and then trying to explain how they relate, start with relationships as the basic fabric of reality, and see individual things as emerging from those relationships. Traditions as different as Zen Buddhism and ancient Neoplatonism converge on exactly this reversal. The sacred, understood this way, is not a supernatural realm — it is whatever breaks open the assumption that we are isolated substances and restores the possibility of real connection.

## Adept

John Vervaeke identifies a philosophical lock-in among three commitments that have co-evolved across Western intellectual history: substance ontology, nominalist epistemology, and dualistic psychology. Substance ontology — the Aristotelian inheritance that reality bottoms out in independently existing entities — generates pressure toward nominalism, the view that universals and relations have no mind-independent reality. If relations are merely mental constructs, the mind becomes ontologically severed from the world it purports to know. That severance is dualism. Together, these three commitments form a self-reinforcing complex: each makes the others appear necessary, and once inside the triad, adversarial processing becomes structurally inevitable rather than a contingent failure of discourse.

Religion, on this account, historically functioned as a counterweight — not primarily through doctrinal claims but through its cultivation of nonpropositional, participatory, and relational modes of knowing. As that counterbalancing function has eroded in modernity, the substance-nominalist-dualist complex has become the default cognitive architecture, and adversarial engagement has become increasingly automatic.

The required inversion is ontological: relata must be understood as derivative of relations rather than the reverse. Pure relationality becomes the ground of being. This is the convergence point between Neoplatonic participation and Zen's dismantling of subject-object structure — two independent traditions arriving at the same structural insight. The sacred, reframed accordingly, is not a supernatural domain but the advent of a mode of apprehension that disrupts the subject-predicate grammar underwriting substance ontology. It reopens genuine relatedness, genuine contact with reality, and genuine orientation toward what Vervaeke calls ultimacy.

## Magus

The argument identifies a diachronic philosophical lock-in — a kind of attractor basin in the space of Western metaphysical commitments — binding substance ontology, nominalist epistemology, and Cartesian-inflected dualistic psychology into a mutually entailing triad. The genealogy runs as follows: Aristotelian ousia, construed as that which exists independently and underlies predication, generates the problem of the ontological status of relations. The medieval nominalist resolution — that universals and relations are entia rationis, not entia realia — preserves the priority of substance at the cost of relocating all genuine connectivity to the mental. But if the mind is the sole locus of relational structure, it is thereby severed from a world composed of intrinsically non-relational particulars. Dualism follows not as an optional philosophical thesis but as a structural entailment. Within this triad, adversarial processing is not a defeasible social norm but the only epistemically available stance: isolated substantial minds can only collide, never genuinely participate in shared intelligibility.

The historical function of religion — specifically its nonpropositional, participatory, and transformative dimensions — was to operate as a countervailing force against this closure, sustaining modes of knowing irreducible to the propositional and the predicative. The recession of that function in post-Enlightenment modernity has left the substance-nominalist-dualist complex without structural opposition, intensifying its grip on cognition and culture alike.

The requisite inversion is radical: ontological priority must shift from relata to relations, from substance to pure relationality as ground. This is precisely the convergence disclosed between Neoplatonic methexis and the Zen deconstruction of svabhāva — independent traditions dismantling the same subject-predicate metaphysics from within distinct conceptual vocabularies. The sacred, so reconceived, names not a supernatural ontic domain but the irruption of a mode of apprehension that destabilizes the grammatical and ontological infrastructure of substance thinking, thereby reopening participatory contact with ultimacy.
