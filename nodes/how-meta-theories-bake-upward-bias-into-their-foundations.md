# How Meta-Theories Bake Upward Bias Into Their Foundations

The higher you climb, the less ground you touch.

---

> Most big-picture theories of human development assume that 'up' is the only direction that matters — more complex, more abstract, more elevated. Mark Edwards argues this altitudinal bias systematically excludes grounding, relational, and horizontal ways of knowing, and that the absences it produces can transform the entire framework.

One of the most underexamined problems in meta-theoretical work is what Mark Edwards calls the 'altitudinal bias' — the systematic privileging of ascending, hierarchical lenses over descending, grounding, or horizontal ones. In Western philosophy and spirituality, the directional metaphor is almost always upward: waking up, growing up, cleaning up, turning up. But this cultural assumption gets quietly baked into the architecture of meta-theoretical frameworks themselves, so that the frameworks don't merely describe development — they enact a particular directional prejudice about what development means. Edwards points to Wilber's integral model as a clear example: the entire structure is organized around altitude, around climbing a hierarchy of complexity. But this raises a serious empirical and philosophical question: where is the descending lens? Where is the lens of groundedness, of rootedness, of what indigenous Australian knowledge systems carry that cannot be placed at the bottom of any developmental scale without distorting the data? Edwards is not making a merely pluralist or politically correct argument here — he's making a methodological one. When you build altitude into the foundational architecture of your meta-theory, you don't just describe the world from a particular angle; you systematically exclude entire categories of insight that don't fit the upward trajectory. The result, he argues, is what he calls 'towers of Babel' — ever more abstract, ever more elevated theoretical structures that lose contact with the ground they were meant to illuminate. This altitudinal bias is further illuminated by what Edwards calls 'productive absence': after years of deep engagement with Wilber's integral theory, he encountered the work of Lev Vygotsky — the Soviet developmental psychologist whose concept of mediated learning and the zone of proximal development represents one of the most important contributions to developmental theory of the twentieth century. Vygotsky is completely absent from Wilber. Not marginalized, not underemphasized — absent. And that absence, Edwards argues, is not accidental. It reflects a structural feature of Wilber's framework: its individualist, interiorist, and altitude-focused architecture simply has no natural place for Vygotsky's fundamentally social, mediational, and horizontal understanding of how minds develop through relationship and cultural tools. The absence didn't just tell Edwards that Wilber had missed something — it told him that the entire architecture of the framework needed to be reconsidered. This is what Roy Bhaskar calls 'negative transfiguration' — the process by which identifying an absence doesn't just add to a framework but transforms it, producing genuinely new theoretical possibilities that could not have been reached by additive synthesis alone. The corrective is not to abandon hierarchy altogether, but to treat the altitudinal lens as one lens among many, subject to the same critical scrutiny as any other — and to actively seek out the lenses that Western meta-theory has structurally absented.

## Apprentice

When we talk about human growth, the metaphors almost always point upward — waking up, growing up, reaching higher stages. Mark Edwards notices that this isn't just a figure of speech; it's a deep assumption baked into the very structure of major theories about how people and cultures develop. The most influential of these, Ken Wilber's integral model, is organized entirely around "altitude" — climbing a ladder of increasing complexity. But what about the insights that don't fit on a ladder?

Edwards points out that some of the most important knowledge traditions — including indigenous Australian ways of knowing — emphasize rootedness, groundedness, and connection rather than ascent. When a framework is built around climbing, these perspectives get pushed to the bottom or left out entirely. He gives a striking example: Lev Vygotsky, one of the twentieth century's most important thinkers about how minds develop through relationships and cultural tools, is completely absent from Wilber's work. Not downplayed — missing.

That absence isn't a small oversight. Edwards argues it reveals something structural: the framework simply has no natural place for ideas about development that move sideways, through connection, rather than upward through stages. The fix isn't to throw out hierarchy, but to recognize it as just one lens among many — and to actively look for the perspectives that an upward-only view has made invisible.

## Adept

Mark Edwards identifies what he calls 'altitudinal bias' as one of the most consequential yet underexamined problems in meta-theoretical work. Western philosophy and developmental theory overwhelmingly privilege ascending, hierarchical metaphors — stages, levels, altitude — and this directional prejudice doesn't remain at the level of rhetoric. It gets structurally embedded in the architecture of frameworks like Wilber's integral model, where the entire organizational logic revolves around climbing a hierarchy of complexity. The result is not neutral description but systematic exclusion: perspectives grounded in rootedness, horizontal relationality, or indigenous knowledge systems that resist vertical ordering are either distorted to fit the schema or rendered invisible.

Edwards illustrates this through what he calls 'productive absence.' After years of deep engagement with Wilber's work, he encountered Vygotsky's developmental psychology — the zone of proximal development, mediated learning, the fundamentally social and tool-mediated character of cognitive growth. Vygotsky is entirely absent from Wilber's corpus. Edwards argues this is not an oversight but a structural consequence: an individualist, interiorist, altitude-organized framework has no natural affordance for Vygotsky's horizontal, mediational account of development. Drawing on Roy Bhaskar's concept of 'negative transfiguration,' Edwards contends that recognizing such absences doesn't merely supplement a framework — it transforms it, opening theoretical possibilities unreachable through additive synthesis.

The corrective Edwards proposes is methodological rather than merely pluralist. Hierarchy is not abandoned but repositioned as one lens among many, subject to the same critical scrutiny applied to any other. The task becomes actively seeking out the lenses that altitude-centered meta-theory has structurally absented — descending, grounding, and horizontal perspectives that carry their own irreducible epistemic contributions.

## Magus

Mark Edwards's critique of 'altitudinal bias' targets a foundational but largely uninterrogated structural feature of Western meta-theoretical architecture: the systematic privileging of ascending, hierarchical lenses over descending, grounding, and horizontal ones. The directional metaphorics of development — waking up, growing up, cleaning up — are not merely heuristic conveniences but ontological commitments that get sedimented into framework design. In Wilber's integral model, altitude functions as the master organizing principle, rendering the entire developmental landscape legible only along a vertical axis of increasing complexity. Edwards's argument is methodological, not pluralist: when verticality is architecturally foundational rather than one lens among many, entire categories of insight are not merely underweighted but structurally precluded. The frameworks become what he calls 'towers of Babel' — gaining abstraction while losing contact with the ground they purport to illuminate.

The concept of 'productive absence' sharpens this critique. Edwards's encounter with Vygotsky — whose mediational epistemology, zone of proximal development, and fundamentally social-relational account of cognitive genesis represent a major twentieth-century contribution to developmental theory — revealed not a gap in Wilber's scholarship but a structural impossibility within the framework's individualist, interiorist, altitude-organized architecture. Vygotsky's horizontal, tool-mediated, culturally embedded developmentalism has no natural locus within such a system. This connects directly to Bhaskar's 'negative transfiguration': the identification of a determinate absence that does not supplement but transforms the theoretical field, generating possibilities inaccessible through additive synthesis alone.

The implication is architecturally radical. The corrective demands not the abandonment of hierarchy but its demotion from foundational principle to one perspectival lens among many — subjected to the same metatheoretical scrutiny as any other, and counterbalanced by active recovery of the descending, grounding, and relational lenses that altitude-centered frameworks have rendered structurally absent.
