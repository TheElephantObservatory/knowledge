# Conditioning, Not Nature: The Cultivable Human

When we look at human nature and try to understand our biological orientations, we run into a fundamental problem: evolutionary psychology is a deeply riddled field because we study humans who have been ubiquitously conditioned — mostly within what you might call the WEIRD model (Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic) — and then we take that conditioned behavior to be human nature itself. We then use reductionism to find genes that correlate with those behaviors and declare the case closed. But this misses something crucial. If you look across different cultures over long periods of time, you see radically different medians and distributions for important qualities — levels of violence, peacefulness, the value placed on education and attention and nuance. These aren't just outlier individuals; they represent genuinely different developmental trajectories at the population level. The Jewish cultural example is instructive here: even embedded in hostile environments with low access to public education and persistent persecution, certain value systems around attention, learning, and nuance remained remarkably high relative to the surrounding culture. What this tells us is that human nature is not the inexorable driver of our problems. Rather, what we're dealing with is a failure of human conditioning — a failure to develop the capacities that our nature makes possible but does not guarantee. The distinction matters enormously for how we think about solutions. If our destructive behaviors were hardwired, we'd be stuck. But if they're the product of conditioning environments that failed to cultivate our latent capacities, then redesigning those environments — the education systems, the cultural value structures, the developmental contexts — becomes one of the most important levers we have.

## Apprentice

A common assumption is that human beings are naturally violent, selfish, or short-sighted — that our worst behaviors are simply baked into our biology. But this assumption may rest on a serious mistake. Most of what we study when we study human behavior comes from people raised in a narrow slice of human experience: modern Western societies with particular economic and educational conditions. When we observe those people and conclude we've found human nature, we're actually finding the product of a specific kind of upbringing. Look across history and cultures, and the picture changes dramatically. Some societies have sustained remarkably high levels of peacefulness, intellectual curiosity, or careful attention to nuance — not as rare exceptions, but as population-wide norms. This suggests that human beings have a much wider range of possible development than we typically assume. The destructive patterns we worry about aren't inevitable features of our species. They're more likely the result of environments that failed to draw out better capacities — capacities that are genuinely available to us but that require the right conditions to develop. This reframes the problem entirely. Instead of managing an unchangeable nature, the real challenge becomes designing better conditions for human development.

## Adept

Evolutionary psychology faces a foundational sampling problem. The behaviors it most often studies are drawn from WEIRD populations — Western, Educated, Industrialized, Rich, Democratic — and these behaviors are then naturalized as species-typical through reductionist genetic correlation. The circularity is significant: conditioned outputs are treated as biological baselines, and molecular correlates of those outputs are taken as causal confirmation. Cross-cultural and historical data complicate this picture considerably. Distributions of traits like interpersonal violence, epistemic patience, and valuation of learning vary not merely at the individual level but at the population level across time and context — suggesting genuine developmental plasticity rather than fixed biological expression. The persistence of certain value orientations within diaspora communities under conditions of material deprivation and social hostility offers a particularly striking case: it implies that cultural transmission systems can maintain and reproduce high-capacity developmental norms even when structural supports are absent. The theoretical implication is that what presents as human nature may largely be the residue of conditioning environments — specifically, environments that have systematically under-cultivated available human capacities. This distinction carries real consequences. A hardwired account of destructive behavior forecloses intervention at the level of cause. A conditioning account opens the possibility of redesigning developmental environments — educational structures, cultural value systems, early-life contexts — as primary levers for shifting population-level behavioral distributions.

## Magus

The epistemological problem at the core of much evolutionary psychology is one of confounded naturalization: behavioral phenotypes produced under historically contingent and culturally specific developmental regimes are submitted to quantitative genetic analysis, and the resulting heritability estimates and gene-behavior correlations are interpreted as evidence of species-typical biological architecture. The WEIRD sampling critique, while now widely acknowledged, has not fully dislodged the deeper methodological habit of treating modal Western behavior as the null hypothesis for human nature. What cross-cultural ethnography and historical demography suggest, however, is that the variance in morally and cognitively significant traits — tolerance for ambiguity, capacity for sustained attention, orientation toward epistemic humility, propensity for intra- and inter-group violence — is not adequately explained by genetic population differences. The distributions themselves shift, at the population level, as a function of developmental ecology. The persistence of particular intellectual and ethical orientations within historically persecuted diaspora communities, maintained across generations without access to formal institutional supports, points toward the robustness of cultural transmission as a developmental mechanism capable of sustaining high-complexity human capacities under adverse conditions. The theoretical reorientation this demands is substantial. If destructive behavioral patterns are not expressions of fixed biological imperatives but are instead the downstream products of developmental environments that failed to actualize latent human capacities, then the locus of causal intervention shifts from the genome to the conditioning architecture itself — the pedagogical, cultural, and institutional structures that shape the developmental trajectory of the organism. This is not a soft constructivism that denies biological constraint; it is a claim about where within the gene-environment interaction the most tractable leverage points lie.
