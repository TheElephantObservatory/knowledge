# Free Will, Decision, and Choice Are Not the Same Thing

The menu was never the whole meal.

---

> Free will, decision, and choice are three distinct concepts routinely conflated in Western thought. 'Free will' is incoherent, 'decision' is mere elimination among given options, but 'choice' — the capacity to imagine options not yet on the menu — is real, irreducibly first-person, and always present-tense.

The concepts of free will, decision, and choice are routinely treated as interchangeable in Western culture — but they represent three genuinely distinct things, and conflating them has caused enormous philosophical confusion. 'Free will' is an incoherent concept: 'freedom' as the absence of deterministic constraint is a notion borrowed from physics and mathematics, where logic is perfectly specified all the way down to zero — but causation is not perfectly specified in that way. To speak of freedom from determinism already presupposes a deterministic universe, which most serious philosophers reject. And 'will' as a kind of subjective dominance over objective reality fails because the relationship between subject and object is more fundamental than either — you cannot have the subject conditioning the object when the relationship itself is primary. 'Decision,' by contrast, is about cutting away — the root is the same as 'incision' or 'genocide' — selecting among presented options by elimination. 'Choice,' however, is something richer: it is the capacity to imagine options that were not on the menu, to create a fourth path where only three were offered. Choice is real — not in the sense that science uses 'exist,' but in the deeper sense that it partakes simultaneously of change, causation, and selection. Critically, choice is only ever first-person and present-tense. You cannot make choices yesterday or tomorrow. The now is always the locus of choice. This is why no third-person scientific description will ever capture what choice is — two-thirds of what is real is not observable, and the subjective immediacy of choosing is precisely in that unobservable domain.

## Apprentice

Most people use 'free will,' 'decision,' and 'choice' as if they mean the same thing. This conflation has created centuries of confused debate. When we untangle them, something surprising emerges: free will doesn't actually make sense as a concept, decision is more limited than we think, and choice is far more powerful — and stranger — than either.

'Free will' borrows the idea of 'freedom' from physics and math, where it means the absence of deterministic constraint. But this already assumes the universe is fully deterministic — something most serious thinkers reject. And 'will' implies a subject forcing its desires onto reality, which falls apart once you realize the relationship between subject and object is more basic than either one alone. 'Decision,' meanwhile, literally means cutting away — like incision. It's the process of eliminating options from a set someone else has already defined for you.

'Choice' is something else entirely. It's the ability to imagine a fourth option when only three were offered — to create possibilities, not just select among them. And here's the key: choice only ever happens now, in the first person. You can't choose yesterday. You can't choose tomorrow. No outside observer can watch you choose, because the lived immediacy of choosing belongs to the part of reality that isn't observable. This is why science, which works in the third person, will never fully capture what choice is.

## Adept

The Western philosophical tradition has generated enormous confusion by treating free will, decision, and choice as synonyms. This analysis argues they are three genuinely distinct concepts, and that disentangling them dissolves several classical impasses. 'Free will' is diagnosed as incoherent on two fronts. First, 'freedom' as the absence of deterministic constraint is a notion imported from formal systems — logic, mathematics, physics — where specification runs all the way down to zero. Causation, however, is not perfectly specified in this way; to speak of freedom from determinism already presupposes a deterministic framework, which is precisely what is in question. Second, 'will' as subjective dominance over the objective fails because the subject-object relation is ontologically prior to either relatum — the relation cannot be derived from one of its own terms.

'Decision' fares better but remains limited. Its etymology — sharing roots with incision, excision, genocide — reveals its logic: cutting away, eliminating from a pre-given set. Decision operates within a closed option space defined externally. 'Choice,' by contrast, names the capacity to generate options not previously available — to introduce a fourth possibility where only three existed. Choice is creative rather than eliminative.

Critically, choice is irreducibly first-person and present-tense. It cannot be exercised retrospectively or prospectively; the now is its only locus. This is why no third-person scientific description can capture it. The insight here is that roughly two-thirds of what is real — encompassing change, causation, and selection simultaneously — is not observable from the outside. The subjective immediacy of choosing inhabits precisely that unobservable domain, making choice real in a sense deeper than empirical existence, yet permanently inaccessible to objectifying inquiry.

## Magus

The persistent conflation of free will, decision, and choice in Western discourse is not merely a terminological imprecision but a category error with cascading philosophical consequences. This tripartite distinction proceeds by dismantling each term's ontological credentials independently. 'Free will' collapses under dual critique: 'freedom' as negation of deterministic constraint imports its semantics from formal domains — mathematics, logic, physics — where specification is complete to zero. Causation, however, lacks this perfect specification; invoking freedom from determinism performatively presupposes the deterministic closure it claims to escape. 'Will' as subjective conditioning of the objective fares no better, since the subject-object relation is ontologically primitive — prior to and constitutive of its relata. One cannot coherently derive the relation from the dominance of one term over the other without vicious circularity.

'Decision' is etymologically and structurally eliminative — cognate with incision, excision, the -cide of genocide — operating within a closed, externally furnished option space. It is selection by negation. 'Choice,' however, names a generative capacity: the introduction of possibilities absent from the presented set, the creation of a fourth path where three were given. Choice thus partakes simultaneously of change, causation, and selection — the three constituents of the real — but in a mode irreducible to any one of them.

The deepest claim is that choice is exclusively first-person and present-tense. It cannot be displaced into past or future without ceasing to be choice. The now is its sole locus of operation. This entails that no third-person descriptive framework — scientific or otherwise — can capture choice, because approximately two-thirds of what is real resides in domains inaccessible to observation. The subjective immediacy of choosing occupies precisely this unobservable territory, rendering choice real in a sense that transcends empirical existence while remaining permanently opaque to objectifying methodologies.
