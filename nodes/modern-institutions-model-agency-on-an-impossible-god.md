# Modern Institutions Model Agency on an Impossible God

When you can't be omniscient, nothing seems possible.

---

> Modern institutions model agency on a hidden theology: first achieve godlike knowledge, then exert godlike control. When that model fails, people conclude nothing can be done — because they defined power as something only God could wield. A relational, improvisational alternative already exists and has a better historical track record.

There is a theological structure hidden inside the modern idea of agency that almost nobody names explicitly. The large agencies of modernity — states, international institutions, major organizations — have been operating with a model of action that mirrors a particular way of imagining what it would be like to be God. Step one: omniscience. You seek to know the world from above, to map it completely, to have a total picture. Step two: omnipotence. From that position of total knowledge, you act on the world to produce intended outcomes. James C. Scott traced some of the histories of this in Seeing Like a State. The problem is that when this model of agency breaks down — when it becomes clear that you cannot know the world from above and cannot guarantee outcomes — you don't automatically arrive at a better model. If you lose faith in that kind of agency but haven't questioned the theology implicit in it, you end up making yourself powerless by the very way you've described what power would look like. You've defined agency in a way that only God could exercise it, and then concluded that nothing can be done. The alternative — creating conditions of possibility — is a genuinely different model. It doesn't seek to know in advance, doesn't seek to control outcomes. It comes from tai chi teachers, from theater improvisation, from anthropology, from many sources. What they share is an understanding of agency as relational, responsive, and generative rather than omniscient and controlling. Karl Polanyi's account of the double movement in The Great Transformation illuminates this further: the counter-movement that eventually produced some of the better achievements of the twentieth century was not a planned political alternative placed alongside the dominant order on the same plane. It was something improvised from below, by people who did not share a political ideology, responding to the chaos of the top-down project being perpetrated on them. The counter-movement didn't need to win the argument first. It needed to exist. This is not a counsel of passivity. It is a more honest account of how change actually happens — and a clue to what kinds of strategy might make sense now.

## Apprentice

There is a hidden religious structure inside the way modern states and large organizations think about getting things done. The pattern goes: first, try to see everything from above — map the whole situation, gather total knowledge. Then, from that godlike vantage point, act decisively to produce the result you want. This is essentially trying to be omniscient and omnipotent. James C. Scott explored how states have operated this way in his book Seeing Like a State.

The trouble comes when this approach fails — as it inevitably does, because no one can actually know everything or control outcomes perfectly. If you've defined real agency as requiring total knowledge and total control, then losing confidence in that model doesn't free you. It paralyzes you. You conclude that nothing meaningful can be done, because you've set the bar at godhood.

But there is another way of thinking about action. Tai chi teachers, theater improvisers, and anthropologists all point toward it: agency understood not as commanding outcomes from above, but as creating conditions where good things become possible. Karl Polanyi showed that the great social achievements of the twentieth century emerged not from a master plan but from improvised responses by ordinary people reacting to the damage of top-down schemes. This isn't passivity — it's a more honest description of how change actually works.

## Adept

A theological architecture lies concealed within the dominant modern conception of agency. Large-scale actors — states, international bodies, major institutions — have operated according to a two-step model that recapitulates divine attributes: first omniscience (total surveillance, comprehensive mapping, synoptic knowledge), then omnipotence (decisive intervention to produce predetermined outcomes). James C. Scott's analysis in Seeing Like a State documents the catastrophic consequences of this high-modernist logic. But the deeper problem is structural: when this model of agency collapses, it does not automatically yield a better one. If the implicit theology has not been named and questioned, the failure of godlike agency produces not humility but despair — the conclusion that nothing can be done, because agency itself has been defined in terms only a deity could satisfy.

The alternative is not a scaled-down version of the same model but a genuinely different paradigm: agency as the creation of conditions of possibility. This understanding surfaces across disparate traditions — somatic practices like tai chi, theatrical improvisation, relational anthropology — all of which treat action as responsive, situated, and generative rather than predictive and controlling.

Karl Polanyi's account of the double movement in The Great Transformation provides historical grounding. The counter-movement that eventually produced welfare states, labor protections, and social insurance was not a coherent political program placed alongside laissez-faire on the same ideological plane. It was improvised from below by actors who shared no common ideology, responding to the lived destruction wrought by the top-down project. The counter-movement did not need to win the argument first; it needed to exist. This reframes strategic thinking: not planning the alternative in advance, but cultivating the relational and institutional soil from which alternatives can emerge.

## Magus

The modern concept of agency harbors an unexamined theology whose two moments — omniscience and omnipotence — reproduce the classical divine attributes as operational premises for statecraft and institutional action. The synoptic ambition Scott diagnosed in high modernism is not merely an epistemological error but a soteriological one: the state seeks redemption through total legibility, then acts as demiurge upon the flattened world it has constructed. When this project fails, the theological infrastructure remains intact, generating not alternative praxis but its precise negation — impotence as the shadow of omnipotence. Agency defined sub specie aeternitatis admits no middle term; one is either God or nothing.

To name this structure is already to begin dissolving it. The alternative model — agency as the cultivation of conditions of possibility — draws on traditions that never shared the Cartesian-theological premises: the yielding responsiveness of tai chi's ting jin, the radical availability of improvisational theater, the participant-observation stance of anthropological fieldwork. These are not metaphors for political action but epistemological and somatic disciplines that instantiate a relational ontology of agency, one in which the actor is always already embedded in the field being acted upon.

Polanyi's double movement illuminates the historical efficacy of this alternative. The counter-movement he traces in The Great Transformation was not a counter-program but an emergent phenomenon — heterogeneous, ideologically incoherent, improvised by actors whose shared condition was exposure to the catastrophe of imposed market society. Its achievements were not designed but precipitated. This distinction between designing outcomes and precipitating emergence marks the fault line between the two theologies of action. The strategic implication is precise: what is needed is not a better plan but a denser ecology of responsive, relationally constituted actors capable of generating possibilities that cannot be specified in advance.
