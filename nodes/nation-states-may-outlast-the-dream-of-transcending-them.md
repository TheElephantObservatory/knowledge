# Nation-States May Outlast the Dream of Transcending Them

The cage that holds may also shelter.

---

> The dream of voluntary, post-national belonging may be losing a race against a far older force: the nation-state reasserting itself as the only container that can hold societies together when everything else fractures — and AI-era authoritarianism may arrive not by choice but by default.

There is a profound tension between the impulse toward planetary, post-national identity and the very real possibility that the nation-state is not dissolving but merely convulsing — and that what follows the current turbulence may not be a flowering of affinity-based voluntary communities but rather a doubling down on the nation-state as the only available container for collective survival. The instinct to transcend the nation-state may itself be a luxury of a particular historical moment, and what we are actually entering is a period of retrenchment — where nations that invested heavily in state coherence, like China, begin to look like they are 'working' while more open, experimental arrangements look fragile. The danger is that instead of people freely finding their affinity groups, we return to what our ancestors did: forcing affinity on people who happen to share a military, economic, and bureaucratic region. The public outcry against authoritarianism may be, homeopathically, a harbinger of exactly what is coming — an AI-supercharged authoritarian consolidation that arrives not because anyone chose it but because the alternatives failed to cohere in time. El Salvador under Bukele offers an unusually legible case study in this dynamic: a country with eight violent conflicts in two hundred years, profound class stratification, and the intergenerational trauma of civil war and gang rule does not naturally produce spontaneous fellow-feeling — yet Bukele has mythically woven together a collective imaginal in which a Palestinian-Salvadoran philosopher-king restores order and dignity to a people who had been the murder capital of Latin America, and that story is working not because people are naive but because the alternative was so viscerally unbearable. The lesson this offers to anyone thinking about civilizational transition is uncomfortable: the shared narrative that binds a people does not need to be historically accurate, philosophically sophisticated, or ideologically pure — it needs to be emotionally true to the wound it is healing. The danger is that the same mechanism that heals can calcify into something that excludes, and the prison industrial complex, the state of exception, the seventy thousand people detained without due process, are the shadow side of the same mythic consolidation. This is not a comfortable thesis for those whose long-term impulses run toward voluntary, networked, post-national forms of belonging — it suggests that the metamodern project of building new containers may be racing against a much older and more powerful gravitational field pulling civilization back toward enforced coherence.

## Apprentice

There is a growing hope that humanity is moving beyond nations toward something more fluid — communities people choose rather than borders they inherit. But this idea may be a luxury of a relatively stable era. As global systems shake, the nation-state isn't disappearing; it may be tightening its grip. Countries with strong centralized control, like China, start to look functional while more open, experimental societies look fragile. The pull isn't toward freedom — it's toward enforced togetherness under a single story.

El Salvador offers a vivid example. After centuries of violence, civil war, and gang rule, President Bukele built a powerful national myth: a leader who restored order and dignity to a broken people. That story works not because citizens are gullible, but because the previous reality — being the murder capital of the world — was unbearable. People will accept a binding narrative when the alternative is chaos.

The uncomfortable lesson is this: the stories that hold societies together don't need to be accurate or sophisticated — they need to speak to the pain people actually feel. But the same force that heals can harden into something dangerous. Bukele's seventy thousand detained without trial are the dark side of the same myth. For anyone hoping humanity will organize itself through voluntary networks and shared values rather than state power, the warning is stark: that gentler vision may simply not cohere fast enough before the older, harder pattern reasserts itself.

## Adept

A fundamental tension is emerging between post-national aspirations — the metamodern impulse toward voluntary, affinity-based belonging — and the gravitational reassertion of the nation-state as the only viable container for collective survival under stress. The instinct to transcend national borders may itself be historically contingent, a product of decades of relative stability now ending. As systemic turbulence intensifies, states that invested in centralized coherence appear to be "working," while more open, networked arrangements look increasingly fragile. The risk is not that authoritarianism is chosen but that it arrives by default, supercharged by AI-enabled surveillance and governance, because decentralized alternatives failed to cohere in time.

El Salvador under Nayib Bukele provides an unusually legible case study. A country scarred by eight violent conflicts in two centuries, deep class stratification, and the intergenerational trauma of civil war and gang dominance does not spontaneously generate fellow-feeling. Bukele's achievement is mythic consolidation: weaving a collective imaginal in which a Palestinian-Salvadoran philosopher-king restores dignity to a people who had been the murder capital of the world. This narrative succeeds not through naivety but through emotional truth — it speaks directly to the wound. The binding story need not be historically rigorous or ideologically pure; it must be felt as real by those it addresses.

The shadow is inseparable from the light. The state of exception, the prison-industrial apparatus, seventy thousand detained without due process — these are not aberrations but the structural underside of the same mythic force. This thesis is deeply uncomfortable for those oriented toward networked, voluntary, post-national futures: it suggests the project of building new containers is racing against a civilizational undertow far older and more powerful than any emergent alternative has yet managed to match.

## Magus

The dialectic between post-national aspiration and nation-state retrenchment may be resolving not in synthesis but in gravitational capture. The metamodern wager — that voluntary, affinity-based communities can replace inherited territorial containers — presupposes a surplus of systemic stability that is rapidly evaporating. What appears as the convulsion of the nation-state may be misread as its death throes when it is in fact its immune response. States that maintained coherence through centralized narrative and bureaucratic density now exhibit a functional advantage that the more porous, experimentally governed polities cannot match under conditions of cascading polycrisis. The homeopathic principle applies: the very intensity of public outcry against authoritarianism may be the prodromal signal of its consolidation, arriving not through ideological conquest but through the failure of alternatives to achieve sufficient binding force.

Bukele's El Salvador crystallizes this dynamic with unusual clarity. Eight violent conflicts across two centuries, profound class stratification rooted in colonial extraction, the intergenerational somatic trauma of civil war and pandilla rule — none of this generates spontaneous Durkheimian solidarity. What Bukele accomplished is the construction of a collective imaginal: a mythic narrative in which a Palestinian-Salvadoran philosopher-king restores order and dignity to a people for whom the alternative was viscerally unbearable. The operative mechanism is not rational persuasion but emotional veridiction — the story is true to the wound, and that is sufficient for social binding. Its philosophical sophistication or historical accuracy is irrelevant to its efficacy.

The shadow cannot be disaggregated from the consolidation. The state of exception, the carceral apparatus holding seventy thousand without due process, the suspension of juridical norms — these are not deviations from the mythic project but its necessary complement. The uncomfortable implication for civilizational transition theory is structural: the project of building post-national containers may be operating on a timescale incompatible with the speed at which enforced coherence reconstitutes itself, and the older gravitational field may simply be stronger than anything yet assembled to resist it.
