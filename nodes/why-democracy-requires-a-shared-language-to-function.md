# Why Democracy Requires a Shared Language to Function

The cosmopolitan dream, quietly hollowing itself out

---

> Oliver Griebel argues that democracy functionally requires a shared language community — not out of nationalism, but because citizens who cannot follow political discourse in the governing language lose the capacity to hold power accountable, making post-national institutions like the European Parliament democratically hollow.

Oliver Griebel articulates a sobering but precise diagnosis of why liberal democracy is structurally fragile in multilingual, multi-ethnic societies. His core argument is that democratic participation requires a shared linguistic substrate — not as a cultural preference, but as a functional prerequisite. For citizens to form genuine political judgments, to evaluate what their governments are doing, to hold power accountable, they must be able to understand the discourse in which power is exercised. When large portions of a population cannot adequately parse the language of political life — whether because they are recent immigrants, or because the governing language is not their mother tongue — democracy does not merely become less efficient; it begins to lose its legitimating basis entirely. This is why Griebel is skeptical of the European Parliament as a genuinely democratic institution: even citizens who are engaged and educated struggle to follow what is debated in Strasbourg, and most don't try. The nation-state, for all its historical baggage, maps onto something real — a language community capable of collective self-governance. The implication is uncomfortable: the cosmopolitan dream of post-national governance may be beautiful in theory but corrosive in practice, because it severs the link between the governed and their capacity to understand, contest, and shape what governs them. Griebel is not a nationalist — he is a cosmopolitan who has thought carefully enough about democracy's preconditions to recognize that scaling governance beyond linguistic comprehension is not progress but erosion.

## Apprentice

Oliver Griebel makes a sharp but uncomfortable argument: democracy only works when citizens can actually understand what their government is saying and doing. This isn't about cultural pride or national identity — it's about something more basic. If you can't follow the debates, read the laws, or grasp the arguments politicians are making, you can't really participate in governing yourself. Your vote becomes a guess rather than a judgment.

This leads Griebel to a pointed critique of institutions like the European Parliament. Even well-educated, politically engaged citizens rarely follow what happens in Strasbourg, because the debates happen across dozens of languages and layers of translation. The result is a parliament that looks democratic on paper but lacks the real-world connection between rulers and ruled that makes democracy meaningful.

The deeper implication is that the nation-state, whatever its flaws, exists for a reason: it roughly corresponds to a group of people who share a language and can therefore genuinely govern themselves together. Griebel isn't celebrating nationalism — he's a cosmopolitan thinker who has reluctantly concluded that stretching governance beyond the boundaries of mutual comprehension doesn't expand democracy but quietly dismantles it.

## Adept

Oliver Griebel advances a structuralist critique of post-national governance grounded in what might be called the linguistic preconditions of democratic legitimacy. His central claim is that meaningful democratic participation — not mere electoral ritual, but the substantive capacity of citizens to evaluate, contest, and shape political decisions — depends on a shared linguistic substrate. Without it, the feedback loop between governed and governing breaks down, and democratic institutions retain their procedural form while losing their legitimating substance.

The European Parliament serves as Griebel's primary case study. Despite its formal democratic architecture — elections, debates, committees — it operates in a communicative environment so fragmented that even motivated citizens cannot meaningfully track its deliberations. Translation infrastructure does not solve the problem; it merely masks it. The epistemic gap between European citizens and European governance is not a bug to be patched but a structural feature of supranational institutions that outrun the linguistic communities they claim to represent.

What makes Griebel's position distinctive is its provenance: this is not ethno-nationalist nostalgia for the nation-state but a cosmopolitan's reluctant recognition that the nation-state maps onto something functionally indispensable — a language community capable of collective self-governance. The implication cuts against both right-wing nationalism and left-liberal internationalism. The nation-state is defended not as an identity project but as the largest viable unit of genuine democratic accountability. Scaling governance beyond linguistic comprehension, Griebel suggests, is not democratic progress but democratic erosion dressed in universalist rhetoric.

## Magus

Oliver Griebel's intervention operates at the intersection of democratic theory, philosophy of language, and the legitimation crisis of supranational governance. His thesis is deceptively simple in formulation but structurally devastating: democratic legitimacy presupposes a communicative community, and communicative community presupposes linguistic mutual intelligibility. Where the governed cannot parse the discourse in which power is exercised — cannot follow its arguments, detect its evasions, or contest its premises — the democratic mandate degenerates into a procedural fiction. Legitimation without comprehension is not thin democracy; it is the simulation of democracy.

Griebel's critique of the European Parliament crystallizes the argument. The institution satisfies every formal criterion of representative democracy yet fails the substantive test: almost no European citizenry can follow, in real time and with genuine discursive competence, the deliberations conducted in its name. Translation regimes create an illusion of accessibility while introducing irreducible epistemic friction — nuance lost, rhetorical force flattened, accountability diffused across linguistic layers. The Parliament thus exemplifies what might be termed governance beyond the horizon of comprehension, where the scale of political authority systematically exceeds the scale of political understanding.

The philosophical weight of Griebel's position lies in its refusal of both poles of the standard debate. He neither romanticizes the nation-state as Herderian Volksgemeinschaft nor dismisses it as a contingent historical artifact to be transcended. Instead, he identifies it as the approximate institutional correlate of a language community — the largest unit within which the epistemic conditions for democratic self-governance can be reliably sustained. The cosmopolitan project, on this account, faces a dilemma it has largely declined to confront: that universalizing governance without universalizing comprehension does not democratize power but insulates it from the only form of accountability that gives democracy its normative force.
