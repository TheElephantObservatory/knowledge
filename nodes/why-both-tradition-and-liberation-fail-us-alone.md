# Why Both Tradition and Liberation Fail Us Alone

The quilted self, and the terror of choosing everything.

---

> Every society is pulled between inherited structures that stabilize identity and the freedoms that dissolve them. Both are necessary and both are destructive — and no one has yet found a way to hold them together without collapse.

Philip Rief's central insight, which Daniel Garner builds his entire 'Belonging Again' framework upon, is that society is perpetually navigating a tension between two forces: 'givens' and 'releases.' Givens are the unquestioned, axiomatic structures of life — the things you simply find yourself always already inside of, like Christianity in a pre-modern village. They are not experienced as constraints until you are already in the mode of seeking release from them. Releases are the freedoms from those givens, won in the name of individuality, equality, and progress. The tragedy is that both are simultaneously necessary and destructive. Givens create existential stability — the psyche feels 'quilted together' — but they are also the precise conditions for what Hannah Arendt called the 'banality of evil': the everydayness of atrocity, where oppression is simply what one does without question. Releases from givens represent genuine ethical advancement and expanded freedom, but they produce a new crisis: when everything must be chosen rather than inherited, nothing feels authoritative. The chosen thing feels contingent, and contingency cannot ground the self. The person freed from all givens becomes existentially overwhelmed — and that overwhelm is precisely what makes strongmen, totalitarianism, and regressive belonging so appealing. They promise to restore the feeling of givenness. So the conservative instinct toward tradition and the liberal instinct toward liberation are both correct, and both catastrophically incomplete. We have not yet found a way to hold both simultaneously — not as a compromise between them, but as what Aristotle would call a 'fitting mean.'

## Apprentice

There are things in life you never chose but which hold your world together — your culture, your religion, the roles you were born into. Philip Rieff called these "givens." They make life feel solid and meaningful, but they also make cruelty invisible. When oppression is just "the way things are," no one questions it. That's the dark side of stability.

So people fight free of those inherited structures, and rightly so. Every expansion of rights and individual freedom is a "release" from some given. But here's the problem: when everything in your life becomes a matter of personal choice rather than inheritance, nothing feels truly authoritative anymore. Every commitment feels like it could just as easily have been something else. That sense of contingency is quietly devastating.

Daniel Garner, building on Rieff's framework, argues this is the defining tension of modern life. The person liberated from all givens doesn't feel free — they feel overwhelmed. And that overwhelm is exactly what makes authoritarian leaders so seductive: they promise to restore the feeling that the world is solid again. Both the conservative love of tradition and the progressive drive toward liberation are getting at something real, but neither alone is enough. The challenge is holding both together — not as a compromise, but as something genuinely new.

## Adept

Philip Rieff's dialectic of "givens" and "releases" identifies the structural tension at the heart of every social order. Givens are the axiomatic, pre-reflective frameworks — religious, cultural, institutional — that individuals find themselves always already inside. They are not experienced as constraints until the subject has already entered the posture of seeking liberation from them. They provide what might be called existential quilting: the psyche is anchored, identity feels non-negotiable, and the social world appears as natural fact. But this same quality of unquestioned givenness is precisely what Hannah Arendt diagnosed as the condition for the banality of evil — atrocity normalized as routine.

Releases are the hard-won freedoms from those structures, achieved in the name of individuality, equality, and moral progress. They represent genuine ethical advancement. Yet they generate their own pathology: when all commitments must be chosen rather than inherited, nothing carries the weight of authority. The chosen thing feels contingent, and contingency cannot ground the self. Daniel Garner's "Belonging Again" framework takes this as its central problem — the modern subject, freed from all givens, does not experience liberation but existential overwhelm.

This overwhelm is the precise psychological condition that makes authoritarian movements so appealing. Strongmen and totalitarian ideologies promise to restore the feeling of givenness — to make the world feel solid and unchosen again. The conservative instinct toward tradition and the liberal instinct toward liberation are thus both correct and both catastrophically incomplete. What remains missing is what Aristotle would recognize as a fitting mean: not a compromise between givens and releases, but a way of inhabiting both simultaneously without collapse.

## Magus

Philip Rieff's analytic of "givens" and "releases" constitutes one of the most penetrating structural diagnoses of cultural order and its dissolution. Givens designate the pre-reflective, axiomatic conditions of social existence — those frameworks within which subjectivity is constituted before any act of critical distantiation becomes possible. They are ontologically prior to the subject who might reject them; one does not experience the given as constraint until one has already entered the hermeneutic posture of release. Their psychic function is stabilizing — what Rieff understood as the therapeutic integration of the self within sacred order — yet this same pre-reflective character is the condition of possibility for what Arendt theorized as radical evil's banality: the normalization of atrocity within unexamined lifeworlds.

Releases — the emancipatory negations of given structures in the name of autonomy, equality, and moral universalism — represent genuine ethical achievement. Yet they produce a distinctive modern pathology. When commitment becomes wholly elective, it is haunted by its own contingency. The voluntarist subject cannot ground itself in what it has merely chosen, because choice presupposes the very authority it seeks to establish. Daniel Garner's "Belonging Again" framework, built explicitly on Rieff's foundations, identifies this as the generative crisis of late modernity: not the absence of options but the absence of anything that could feel binding.

This existential overwhelm is precisely the libidinal substrate upon which authoritarian movements operate. They do not primarily offer ideology; they offer the phenomenology of givenness restored — the felt sense of a world that is simply so. The conservative and progressive orientations thus each grasp one pole of an antinomy neither can resolve alone. What has not yet been achieved — what Garner frames as the essential task — is an Aristotelian fitting mean: not a dialectical synthesis that sublates the tension, but a mode of dwelling that sustains both the authority of the given and the ethical imperative of release without collapsing into either.
