# How Seven Million Years of Habitat Shaped Human Cognition

Shakespeare learned from stones.

---

> Rich Blundell's Earthling Theory argues that human cognitive capacities — metaphor, narrative, imagination — were not invented by the mind but bestowed by millions of years of habitat relationships, making the continuity between nature and culture a traceable historical fact rather than a poetic suggestion.

Rich Blundell offers what he calls Earthling Theory as a narrative strategy for making the continuity between nature and human cognition inescapable rather than merely plausible. The argument runs through deep evolutionary time: beginning roughly seven million years ago, a primate lineage was drawn — again and again, across thousands of generations — out of its arboreal niche and into successive habitats of increasing complexity. Each habitat — savanna, estuary, glacier margin, coastal shelf — carried its own unique intelligence, its own relational structure, its own demands on perception, memory, and anticipation. Over eons, those habitats literally endowed that lineage with the cognitive capacities we now call distinctively human: narrative, aesthetic appreciation, metaphor, the capacity to imagine futures. The trajectory from the Oldowan stone chopper through the Acheulean hand axe to the Levallois technique is not merely a story of tool improvement — it is a story of nature teaching a species to think. Metaphor, imagination, the capacity to hold a story — these are not inventions of the human mind imposed on a passive world. They are gifts of the relationships between that species and the world it inhabited. Shakespeare is, on this view, a downstream expression of what the stones of the earth taught our ancestors. This reframing dissolves the apparent boundary between nature and culture, between the biological and the symbolic, and makes the continuity Blundell insists upon not a poetic gesture but a traceable historical fact.

## Apprentice

Rich Blundell proposes something he calls Earthling Theory, which reframes how we think about the origins of the human mind. The core idea is striking: our ability to tell stories, appreciate beauty, use metaphors, and imagine the future didn't spring from some uniquely human spark. Instead, these capacities were shaped over millions of years by the environments our ancestors moved through — savannas, estuaries, coastlines, glacier edges. Each habitat posed new challenges and offered new relationships, gradually building the cognitive toolkit we now consider distinctively human.

Consider the history of stone tools. The progression from crude choppers to elegantly crafted hand axes to the sophisticated Levallois technique isn't just about better technology. It traces a deepening conversation between a species and its world — nature literally teaching our ancestors to think in more complex ways. On this view, Shakespeare's plays are a distant downstream echo of what stone, water, and landscape taught early hominins.

This reframing matters because it dissolves the familiar wall between nature and culture. Human creativity isn't something we imposed on a passive world; it's something the world grew in us. Blundell's strategy is to make this continuity not just a nice idea but an inescapable conclusion drawn from deep evolutionary evidence.

## Adept

Rich Blundell's Earthling Theory operates as a narrative strategy designed to render the continuity between natural history and human cognition not merely plausible but inescapable. The argument traces a primate lineage across roughly seven million years of habitat transitions — from arboreal niches through savanna, estuary, glacier margin, and coastal shelf environments. Each successive habitat carried its own relational intelligence, its own demands on perception, memory, and anticipation. Blundell contends that these environments did not merely select for cognitive traits but actively endowed the lineage with capacities we now classify as distinctively human: narrative reasoning, aesthetic sensibility, metaphorical thinking, and prospective imagination.

The lithic record serves as a central thread. The trajectory from Oldowan choppers through Acheulean bifaces to the Levallois prepared-core technique is read not as a sequence of technological improvements but as evidence of deepening cognitive entanglement between organism and environment. Tool-making becomes a proxy for the world's progressive instruction of a species in relational complexity. Shakespeare, in this framework, is a late expression of capacities that originated in the dialogue between hominin bodies and the material affordances of stone, water, and terrain.

The theoretical payoff is a dissolution of the nature-culture boundary that does not rely on philosophical assertion but on traceable evolutionary history. Blundell reframes metaphor, imagination, and symbolic thought as ecological inheritances rather than cognitive inventions, positioning human culture as continuous with — rather than emergent from or imposed upon — the intelligence already present in habitat relationships. The move is deliberately anti-exceptionalist without being reductive.

## Magus

Rich Blundell's Earthling Theory constitutes a sustained argument that human symbolic and narrative capacities are ecological endowments rather than emergent properties of an autonomous cognitive architecture. The framework tracks a hominin lineage across approximately seven million years of niche transitions — arboreal canopy to open savanna, riparian and estuarine margins, periglacial zones, coastal shelves — treating each habitat not as a passive selection environment but as a relational field possessing its own intelligence, its own structure of affordances and demands on perception, memory, and anticipatory cognition. The claim is ontological, not merely metaphorical: habitats taught the lineage to think.

The lithic sequence — Oldowan, Acheulean, Levallois — functions as Blundell's evidentiary spine. Each technological transition indexes not improved instrumental rationality but a deepening entanglement between organism and material world, a progressive internalization of relational complexity that eventually yields the capacities for metaphor, narrative coherence, and counterfactual imagination. The Levallois prepared-core technique, requiring the knapper to hold an absent form in mind before striking, already instantiates the cognitive architecture that will later produce poetry and speculative thought. Shakespeare becomes legible as a downstream expression of what lithic and ecological relationships inscribed in hominin neurology across deep time.

The strategic force of Earthling Theory lies in its refusal to treat nature-culture continuity as a philosophical position requiring argumentative defense. By grounding the claim in traceable paleoanthropological and paleoecological evidence, Blundell converts what is often presented as a speculative ontological commitment into a historical demonstration. The boundary between the biological and the symbolic is not bridged but shown never to have existed — metaphor, aesthetic appreciation, and narrative are repositioned as gifts of habitat rather than inventions of mind, dissolving human cognitive exceptionalism without collapsing into eliminative reductionism.
