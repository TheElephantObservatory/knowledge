# Three Modes of Knowing the Same World: Subjective, Intersubjective, and Objective

Science forgot the eye behind the lens

---

> Humans know the world through three irreducibly different modes — subjective experience, shared culture, and objective science — yet no framework has coherently related them. UTOK argues these modes emerged in evolutionary sequence and that modern confusion stems from collapsing 'empirical' to mean only scientific measurement, erasing the subject in the process.

There is a profound puzzle at the heart of human knowledge that has never been adequately solved: we know the world through three irreducibly different modes — subjective phenomenology, intersubjective culture, and objective science — and yet they all purport to be about the same world. Donald Davidson captured this when he wrote that we need an overall picture that not only accommodates all three modes of knowing but makes their relations to one another clear. What UTOK argues is that these three vectors have a natural historical and evolutionary sequence: subjective experience came first, arising with mammals and primates who open their eyes and encounter the world as subjects. Then, as primates began using propositional language, they generated intersubjective systems of justification — shared verbal networks that yoke people together around what is and ought to be, forming the basis of culture and collective identity. And finally, out of that intersubjective cultural matrix, modern empirical natural science was born — a method for generating claims that transcend any particular perspective, translating the world into mathematics and repeatable experiment. The critical insight is that science is intersubjectively enabled but objectively transcendent: it is produced collectively but aims at truths that hold independent of any individual or group. The problem is that in achieving that objectivity, science systematically factored out the subject — and then acted as though the subject didn't exist. One of the most underappreciated confusions in modern intellectual life follows from this: the conflation of the word 'empirical' with the methods of objective natural science alone. Empiricism at its root simply means knowledge grounded in observation — but there are fundamentally different kinds of observation corresponding to the three vectors. The subjective vector is grounded in phenomenological observation — first-person qualitative empiricism in the tradition of David Hume. The objective vector is grounded in third-person measurement, building generalizable models that yield the same predicted outcomes regardless of who is observing — what David Deutsch calls 'good explanations.' Science builds general models of terrain, extracting what can be modeled and explaining everything else as error, while the subjective vantage point is always situated in the real and encounters the full empirical world including what science would call error. Recognizing that both are genuinely empirical but structurally incommensurable is the first step toward understanding why the Enlightenment Gap opened up. UTOK's three core pillars — the Tree of Knowledge for science, the iQuad coin for subjective phenomenology, and the Garden for intersubjective culture — are designed precisely to hold all three vectors in coherent relation, giving us for the first time an architecture that is true to our best science, true to the unique idiographic individual, and true to the wisdom of cultural collectives.

## Apprentice

There is a deep puzzle hiding in plain sight: we understand the world in three fundamentally different ways. First, each of us has a private, felt experience — what it's like to see red or feel grief. Second, we share language and culture, building collective agreements about what's true and what matters. Third, we have science, which aims at truths that hold no matter who you are or where you stand. These three ways of knowing are all real, all valid, and yet they don't easily fit together.

The insight here is that these three modes appeared in a natural sequence over evolutionary time. Subjective experience came first, arising with animals who opened their eyes and encountered the world. Then language enabled groups to build shared systems of meaning — culture. And out of culture, science eventually emerged as a method for reaching truths that transcend any single perspective. The problem is that science, in achieving its remarkable objectivity, quietly removed the experiencing subject from the picture and then forgot it had done so.

This forgetting created a subtle but enormous confusion: the word 'empirical' got hijacked to mean only what science measures, when it really just means knowledge grounded in observation. But there are different kinds of observation — your felt experience of the world is genuinely empirical, just as a laboratory measurement is. Recognizing that both are real but structurally different kinds of knowledge is the key to closing what UTOK calls the Enlightenment Gap.

## Magus

Davidson's tripartite challenge — to construct an account that accommodates subjective phenomenology, intersubjective justification, and objective naturalism while rendering their mutual relations transparent — remains arguably the deepest unsolved structural problem in epistemology. UTOK's response is to demonstrate that these three epistemic vectors are not coeval alternatives but bear a determinate phylogenetic and historical ordering. Phenomenological subjectivity is the originary epistemic mode, arising with the mammalian and primate capacity for qualitative encounter with the world-as-given. Propositional language then inaugurated intersubjective systems of justification — shared discursive networks that function as what Wittgenstein might call normative scaffolding, yoking persons together around claims about what is and what ought to be, thereby constituting culture as an emergent ontological domain. Modern empirical natural science subsequently crystallized out of this intersubjective matrix as a self-correcting method for generating perspective-transcendent claims, encoding phenomena in mathematical formalisms and reproducible experimental protocols.

The decisive insight concerns science's peculiar epistemic topology: it is intersubjectively constituted yet objectively transcendent, collectively produced yet oriented toward truths that obtain independent of any knower or community. In achieving this transcendence, however, science performed a systematic subtraction of the subject — and subsequently mistook that methodological abstraction for an ontological conclusion. The downstream confusion is the conflation of 'empirical' with third-person quantitative methodology exclusively. Yet Humean phenomenological observation and Deutschian good explanation represent structurally incommensurable empiricisms: science constructs nomothetic models of terrain, relegating residual variance to error, while the situated subject inhabits the full idiographic manifold, including precisely what those models discard as noise.

Grasping this incommensurability without reducing either pole is the precondition for diagnosing the Enlightenment Gap. UTOK's triadic architecture — the Tree of Knowledge mapping objective science, the iQuad coin indexing first-person phenomenology, and the Garden cultivating intersubjective wisdom — constitutes a metatheoretical framework designed to hold all three vectors in productive tension, achieving the Davidsonian desideratum without sacrificing any mode's epistemic integrity.
