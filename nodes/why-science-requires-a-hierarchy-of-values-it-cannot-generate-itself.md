# Why Science Requires a Hierarchy of Values It Cannot Generate Itself

The bludgeon was always waiting.

---

> Science tells us how to say true things about reality; religion tells us how to live in it well. Without the character and good faith that a vertical hierarchy of values provides, science collapses into ideology — as recent institutional failures vividly demonstrate.

Science and religion are not competing for the same domain — they are complementary and mutually necessary. Science is about understanding how to say true things about reality: perceiving it clearly, articulating it honestly. Religion is about how to live in reality — how to inhabit it with wholeness, vitality, and fruitfulness. These two functions obviously fit together. If you cannot understand reality well, or if you lie about it constantly, your life degrades. Equally, your capacity to do science degrades when the underlying ethos of honesty is absent — because the value of truth cannot come from science itself. It must come from something that precedes it. What we have witnessed in the corruption of scientific institutions — the capitulation to ideological pressure on questions of gender, COVID policy, and beyond — is precisely what happens when people engaged in science lack the character necessary to do it properly. Character is the thing that allows values to show up reliably under duress. Above that is good faith: the actual capacity to be in relationship with reality and with other people on the basis of what is good and true, without needing your own version of things to be dominant. Without good character and good faith, the institutional fabric of science becomes ideology, and ideology becomes a bludgeon. The lesson is not that science is wrong, but that science without the vertical dimension — without a hierarchy of values that precedes and orients it — is catastrophically incomplete.

## Apprentice

Science and religion are often treated as rivals, but this perspective argues they serve entirely different and complementary functions. Science is the discipline of perceiving reality clearly and articulating it honestly. Religion — understood broadly — is about how to actually live in reality with wholeness and vitality. One without the other is incomplete.

The key insight is that science depends on values it cannot generate on its own. The commitment to honesty, the willingness to accept uncomfortable truths, the discipline to resist social pressure — none of these come from the scientific method itself. They come from something deeper: from character, which allows a person to hold to their values even under duress, and from good faith, which is the capacity to relate to reality and to other people based on what is actually true rather than what is convenient.

When that deeper foundation is missing, science breaks down. The recent corruption visible in scientific institutions — where ideological pressure has distorted findings on sensitive topics — is not a failure of science as a method. It is a failure of the people doing science to possess the character the enterprise demands. The lesson is stark: science without a preceding hierarchy of values that orients and sustains it is not just incomplete but dangerously so.

## Adept

This argument reframes the science-religion relationship not as a territorial dispute but as a functional complementarity. Science is an epistemic discipline — the practice of perceiving reality accurately and articulating it with fidelity. Religion, in its deepest sense, is an existential discipline — the practice of inhabiting reality with coherence, vitality, and fruitfulness. These are not competing claims about the same domain; they are two necessary dimensions of a well-ordered engagement with what is.

The critical move in this reasoning is the identification of a dependency relation. Science presupposes the value of truth, but it cannot ground that value within its own framework. The commitment to honest inquiry, the refusal to subordinate findings to social or political expedience — these are moral and characterological achievements, not methodological ones. Character is defined here as the reliable expression of values under pressure, and good faith as the deeper capacity to remain in authentic relationship with reality and with others without needing to dominate the epistemic space.

The contemporary evidence is damning. Institutional science's capitulation on politically charged questions — gender ideology, pandemic policy, and adjacent domains — illustrates precisely what happens when the vertical dimension is absent. Without a hierarchy of values that precedes and orients scientific practice, the enterprise becomes vulnerable to ideological capture. The method remains formally intact while the substance is hollowed out. Science does not become wrong; it becomes something worse — a legitimating apparatus for conclusions reached on other grounds. The corrective is not anti-science sentiment but the recovery of the moral and metaphysical infrastructure that makes genuine science possible.

## Magus

The proposition advanced here dissolves the familiar science-religion antinomy by relocating each term within a functional topology. Science operates as an epistemic praxis — the disciplined apprehension and articulation of what is the case. Religion, stripped of its polemical caricature, operates as an ontological praxis — the cultivation of a mode of inhabitation adequate to reality's demands. These are not rival magisteria but orthogonal axes of a single coherent orientation. Their complementarity is not diplomatic compromise; it is structural necessity.

The deeper claim concerns the genealogy of scientific normativity. The value of truth — without which the entire scientific enterprise is motivationally inert — cannot be derived from within the scientific framework. It is a pre-scientific commitment, rooted in what might be called the vertical dimension: a hierarchy of values that is irreducible to empirical findings and that functions as the condition of possibility for honest inquiry. Character, in this analysis, is the mechanism by which such values achieve reliable instantiation under adversarial conditions. Good faith is the still deeper stratum — an orientation toward reality and intersubjectivity that is not instrumentalized by the need for epistemic dominance.

The diagnostic force of this framework becomes apparent when applied to the visible degradation of institutional science. The ideological capture of research domains — gender, epidemiology, climate — is not an exogenous corruption but an endogenous collapse, the predictable consequence of conducting science within a flattened axiological landscape. When the vertical dimension is denied or forgotten, the institutional apparatus of science does not simply err; it transmutes into ideology while retaining the formal markers of epistemic authority. The result is not ignorance but something more pernicious: systematized bad faith wearing the vestments of objectivity. Recovery demands not methodological reform but metaphysical renovation.
