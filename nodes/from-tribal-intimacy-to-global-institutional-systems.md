# From Tribal Intimacy to Global Institutional Systems

The machinery that outgrew its makers

---

> Every civilization from Rome to the present runs on the same basic operating system — hierarchy, money, and law — and that system is now hitting the limits of what it was ever capable of solving.

There is a profound and underappreciated historical arc to the crisis we now face, and it runs through what we might call the Dunbar coherence and the emergence of Game A. Somewhere around the upper paleolithic transition, human beings developed something genuinely new: culture. A form of group coherence that allowed humans to coordinate, to embody models of other people's inner states, to track the combinatorial web of relationships within a community — and this made humans extraordinarily effective. They spread across the entire globe, filled every ecological niche, became the apex predator everywhere they went. But this Dunbar coherence had a hard ceiling: it couldn't scale beyond roughly 150 people. And eventually, humans ran into other humans. Population rose beyond the carrying capacity of local environments, real scarcity emerged, and groups began competing with each other. This created an evolutionary pressure to find a new solution — a way to get people to work together effectively at scale. That solution is what we call Game A. Game A solved three problems simultaneously: how do you sustain a large group in relationship to nature, how do you prevent it from collapsing from internal defection and corruption, and how do you generate enough collective power to compete with other groups? The answer Game A arrived at was formal structure — money, law, hierarchy, semantic narratives that could be written down, taught, simplified, and repeated. These are computational actuations that reduce the complexity of human relationships into manageable inputs and outputs. Everything from the Ming Dynasty to Rome to the 21st century United States is a variation on this theme. They are all inside the possibility space of Game A. And now we are at the edge of that possibility space — facing problems that Game A, by its very architecture, cannot solve.

## Apprentice

For most of human history, small bands of people held together through genuine relationships. Everyone knew everyone else, could read their intentions, and trusted the web of connections that kept the group functioning. This worked beautifully — and it helped humans spread across the entire planet. But it had a hard limit: once a group grew past roughly 150 people, that kind of intimate coherence broke down.

When populations grew and groups began competing for scarce resources, a new solution emerged. Societies invented money, laws, hierarchies, and written stories about who we are and what we owe each other. These tools allowed millions of strangers to cooperate without needing to personally know one another. Every major civilization — ancient Rome, imperial China, the modern nation-state — is a version of this same invention.

The argument here is that this entire family of solutions shares a common architecture, and that architecture has a ceiling of its own. The problems now pressing on humanity — ecological, social, political — are precisely the kind that this system was never designed to handle. Understanding that we are not facing a policy failure but a structural one is the first step toward imagining something genuinely different.

## Adept

The concept of 'Dunbar coherence' names the form of social organization that emerged during the upper paleolithic transition, when humans developed the cognitive capacity for culture: the ability to model other minds, track complex relational webs, and coordinate behavior through shared symbolic frameworks. This capacity was extraordinarily adaptive, enabling humans to occupy every ecological niche on the planet. Its constraint is well-documented — the neocortical ceiling at roughly 150 individuals beyond which this form of coherence degrades.

The pressure of inter-group competition and resource scarcity created selection pressure for a qualitatively different coordination mechanism. What gets called 'Game A' is the entire family of solutions that civilizations converged upon: formal hierarchy, monetary abstraction, codified law, and transmissible semantic narratives. These are not merely social conventions but computational strategies — they reduce the irreducible complexity of human relationships into legible, repeatable inputs and outputs that can function at population scales far beyond Dunbar's limit. The Ming Dynasty, the Roman Empire, and contemporary liberal democracies are all variations within this same possibility space.

The critical insight is architectural rather than political. Game A did not fail through corruption or mismanagement alone — it is approaching the boundary conditions of what its structure can process. The coordination problems now facing civilization, including ecological overshoot, multipolar traps, and the collapse of institutional trust, are not bugs within Game A but emergent properties of its core logic. This framing reorients the question: not how to reform existing institutions, but whether a successor coordination architecture is possible.

## Magus

The periodization proposed here locates the originary rupture not at the Neolithic or the Axial Age but at the upper paleolithic cognitive transition, wherein homo sapiens acquired the recursive mentalizing capacity sufficient to sustain what the framework terms 'Dunbar coherence' — a culturally-mediated group epistemics grounded in direct dyadic and triadic relationship tracking, reputational memory, and affective attunement. This form of social integration proved extraordinarily fit, underwriting the rapid cladistic expansion of anatomically modern humans across every available biome. Its constraint is neurologically grounded: the neocortical processing ceiling that Dunbar's research operationalized as an upper bound of approximately 150 stable social relationships.

The transition to what is termed 'Game A' represents not a linear development but a phase shift in coordination logic, driven by the selection pressures of inter-group competition under conditions of genuine resource scarcity. The solutions that emerged — monetary abstraction, codified legal systems, formal hierarchy, and transmissible ideological narratives — constitute what might be called a lossy compression of social complexity: they sacrifice the full-bandwidth relational information of Dunbar-scale coherence in exchange for scalar reach. These mechanisms are, in an information-theoretic sense, computational actuations that render the combinatorial complexity of large-group dynamics tractable by reducing it to legible, iterable symbolic operations. The entire civilizational record from Sumer to the present is, on this reading, a vast exploration of the possibility space opened by this compression strategy.

The theoretical weight of the argument falls on the claim that this possibility space is now exhausted — not contingently, through misgovernance, but necessarily, through the structural properties of the compression itself. The pathologies characteristic of late Game A, including multipolar defection traps, Molochian optimization dynamics, and the systematic destruction of the ecological substrate, are not deviations from the system's logic but its immanent expressions. This reframes civilizational crisis as a problem of computational architecture rather than political will, and poses the question of whether any successor system can achieve Dunbar-quality coherence at civilizational scale.
