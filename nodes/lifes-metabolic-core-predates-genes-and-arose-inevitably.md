# Life's Metabolic Core Predates Genes and Arose Inevitably

Chemistry didn't stumble into life — it fell.

---

> Every known prokaryote contains self-sustaining chemical cycles made only of small molecules — no DNA, RNA, or proteins needed. This suggests molecular self-reproduction isn't a freak accident but an inevitable phase transition, and that metabolism evolved by mixing and matching modular, gene-like chemical building blocks.

A striking and underappreciated result discussed in this conversation is Joanna Xavier's computational finding that all 6,700 known prokaryotes — every bacterium and archaeon — contain collectively autocatalytic sets of small molecules, with no DNA, no RNA, and no proteins involved. This is presented as possibly the most important result in origin-of-life research in the last decade. The small molecules involved include iron-sulfur complexes and simple organic molecules, and the sets already encode the core of an energy metabolism — linking redox reactions and ATP metabolism. What makes this finding structurally interesting beyond its empirical content is what it implies about the architecture of metabolic evolution. Work by Hordijk and Steel had previously shown that large autocatalytic sets are not monolithic — they are composed of smaller, irreducible autocatalytic sets, sometimes called RAFs. Ursula Marin and Vazas then recognized that each irreducible autocatalytic set is itself a replicator — heritable and exchangeable, analogous to a gene but operating at the level of metabolism rather than sequence. This means the 6,700 distinct small-molecule autocatalytic networks found by Xavier are not independently invented — they are different combinations of a shared library of irreducible metabolic modules. Metabolism, on this view, evolved by horizontal exchange and recombination of these composable units, in a manner structurally parallel to how genetic material is exchanged. The implication is that molecular reproduction — at least at the metabolic level — is not a rare accident requiring a long improbable sequence of independent steps, but an expected phase transition: once the diversity of molecules in a chemical environment crosses a threshold, the ratio of possible reactions to molecules rises to the point where a collectively autocatalytic set almost inevitably assembles. Xavier's empirical result grounds this theoretical prediction in the actual biochemistry of every living cell on Earth.

## Apprentice

Joanna Xavier's computational analysis revealed something remarkable: all 6,700 known bacteria and archaea contain networks of small molecules that collectively sustain and reproduce themselves — without any involvement of DNA, RNA, or proteins. These networks run on simple chemistry: iron-sulfur compounds, basic organic molecules, and the energy reactions that every cell depends on. This may be the most significant finding in origin-of-life research in the past decade.

What makes this even more striking is the deeper structure it reveals. Earlier mathematical work showed that these self-sustaining chemical networks aren't single indivisible wholes — they're built from smaller, irreducible units that can be mixed and matched. Each of these units behaves like a primitive gene: it can be inherited, swapped between systems, and recombined with others. The 6,700 different networks Xavier found aren't 6,700 independent inventions — they're 6,700 different combinations drawn from a shared toolkit of chemical modules.

This reframes the origin of life. Rather than requiring an astronomically unlikely sequence of lucky accidents, self-reproducing chemistry appears to be something that emerges almost inevitably once a chemical environment becomes diverse enough. Cross a threshold of molecular variety, and the sheer number of possible reactions makes self-sustaining cycles practically guaranteed. Xavier's work shows this isn't just a theoretical prediction — it's written into the actual chemistry of every living cell on Earth.

## Adept

Joanna Xavier's computational study demonstrated that all 6,700 sequenced prokaryotes — every known bacterium and archaeon — harbor collectively autocatalytic sets composed exclusively of small molecules, with no macromolecular catalysts (DNA, RNA, or proteins) required. These sets incorporate iron-sulfur complexes and simple organics, and already encode core energy metabolism linking redox chemistry to ATP production. This result has been characterized as potentially the most consequential empirical finding in origin-of-life research in the last decade.

The structural implications run deeper than the empirical observation itself. Building on Hordijk and Steel's mathematical framework, which showed that large reflexively autocatalytic food-generated (RAF) sets decompose into smaller irreducible components, Marin and Vazas recognized that each irreducible autocatalytic set functions as a replicator — heritable, exchangeable, and composable, operating at the metabolic level rather than the sequence level. Xavier's 6,700 distinct networks are therefore not independent inventions but combinatorial assemblies drawn from a shared library of irreducible metabolic modules. Metabolism, on this account, evolved through horizontal exchange and recombination of these units, structurally paralleling lateral gene transfer.

The theoretical backbone is Kauffman's phase-transition argument: as molecular diversity in a chemical environment increases, the ratio of possible catalyzed reactions to molecular species grows supralinearly, until collective autocatalysis becomes statistically inevitable rather than improbable. Xavier's finding anchors this prediction in extant biochemistry, showing that the phase transition isn't merely plausible in prebiotic scenarios — its products persist in the metabolic core of every living prokaryotic cell. Molecular self-reproduction at the metabolic level appears to be a thermodynamic near-certainty, not a frozen accident.

## Magus

Xavier's computational result — that collectively autocatalytic sets of small molecules, free of any macromolecular catalysis, are universally present across all 6,700 characterized prokaryotic genomes — constitutes a finding whose implications for origin-of-life theory have not been adequately absorbed. The sets in question involve iron-sulfur clusters and simple organic intermediates already encoding the deep architecture of energy metabolism: redox coupling and ATP synthesis. This is not a reconstruction of a hypothetical prebiotic chemistry but a structural invariant of extant biochemistry.

The critical interpretive move comes from layering this empirical result onto the RAF (reflexively autocatalytic food-generated) formalism of Hordijk and Steel, which establishes that large autocatalytic sets are not monolithic but decompose into irreducible sub-RAFs. Marin and Vazas's recognition that each irreducible RAF constitutes a bona fide replicator — heritable, laterally transferable, and composable — transforms Xavier's 6,700 distinct networks from a catalogue into a combinatorial grammar. These networks are not convergently evolved independent solutions but permutations over a shared alphabet of metabolic replicators, exchanged horizontally in a manner formally analogous to genetic recombination. Metabolism, under this reading, possesses its own pre-genetic hereditary logic.

The phase-transition framing completes the argument. As molecular diversity in a chemical milieu increases, the hypergraph of possible catalyzed reactions densifies supralinearly relative to the node set, guaranteeing the emergence of autocatalytic closure beyond a critical threshold. Xavier's universality result empirically instantiates this theoretical prediction: collective autocatalysis is not a contingent achievement of early chemistry but an attractor in compositional space, one whose signature remains legible in the metabolic core of every prokaryote. The origin of metabolic self-reproduction is recast from frozen accident to expected criticality.
