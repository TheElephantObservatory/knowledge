# Collective Trauma as a Hidden Driver of the Metacrisis

Civilization put its head down and never looked up.

---

> The Great Acceleration was not driven by greed but by collective trauma. Edwards argues that humanity's post-war rush into production and consumption was a civilizational trauma response — and until metacrisis thinking accounts for this, its interventions will miss the deepest drivers of the crisis.

The metacrisis, Edwards argues, cannot be adequately understood without a concept that is almost entirely absent from current metacrisis discourse: collective trauma. The standard framing treats the metacrisis as a convergence of systemic failures — ecological, political, epistemic, economic. But Edwards proposes a deeper causal story. The first half of the twentieth century was a period of catastrophic global trauma: two world wars, the Spanish flu, the Chinese cultural revolution, colonial violence on an industrial scale. Tens of millions of deaths, entire civilizations shattered. And what did humanity do in response? What humans always do with trauma — it went back to work. It put its head down, secured its families, rebuilt its economies, and threw itself into the cycles of production and consumption that the postwar economic order made available. The Great Acceleration of the second half of the twentieth century — the exponential growth in energy use, resource extraction, carbon emissions, and economic output — is not, on this reading, primarily a story of greed or short-sightedness. It is a story of traumatized civilizations doing what traumatized individuals do: seeking security, stability, and control through the most available means. The implication is profound: the resistance to climate transition, the political support for figures like Trump or Putin among business communities, the inability to collectively face the scale of change required — these are not simply failures of information or political will. They are expressions of unprocessed collective fear. People do not abandon the economic systems that gave them security after catastrophic loss without enormous psychological cost. Until meta-theory and sustainability science develop frameworks adequate to collective trauma at a planetary scale, Edwards suggests, the interventions they propose will continue to miss the deepest drivers of the crisis they are trying to address.

## Apprentice

The metacrisis — the idea that today's biggest problems are all tangled together — is usually explained as a pile-up of broken systems: broken politics, broken economies, a broken relationship with nature. But Edwards argues something crucial is missing from this picture: trauma, experienced not just by individuals but by entire civilizations.

The first half of the twentieth century was catastrophic on a scale hard to fully absorb. Two world wars, a global pandemic, colonial violence, tens of millions dead. When the dust settled, humanity did what traumatized people always do — it put its head down and sought safety. The massive economic boom of the second half of the century, with its explosive growth in energy use, consumption, and extraction, looks different through this lens. It wasn't just greed or poor planning. It was billions of people clinging to whatever offered stability and security after unimaginable loss.

This reframing has a sharp practical point. If the resistance to changing our economic systems runs this deep — rooted not in ignorance but in unprocessed fear — then better data and smarter policies alone won't be enough. People don't voluntarily abandon the systems that made them feel safe after catastrophe. Until the fields trying to address the metacrisis take collective trauma seriously, Edwards suggests, they will keep proposing solutions that never quite reach the real problem.

## Adept

Edwards identifies a critical blind spot in metacrisis discourse: the near-total absence of collective trauma as an explanatory framework. The standard metacrisis narrative treats the convergence of ecological, epistemic, political, and economic crises as a product of systemic design failures — extractive capitalism, institutional decay, epistemic fragmentation. Edwards does not reject this framing but argues it is incomplete at a causal level. The deeper story begins with the first half of the twentieth century: two world wars, the Spanish flu, the Chinese Cultural Revolution, and industrialized colonial violence produced civilizational-scale trauma affecting billions. The response followed a recognizable psychological pattern — not reflection, but survival behavior.

The Great Acceleration, on this reading, is recast from a story of collective short-sightedness into a trauma response writ large. The exponential growth curves in energy consumption, resource extraction, and GDP after 1950 represent traumatized societies seeking security, predictability, and material stability through the mechanisms the postwar economic order made available. Production and consumption became civilizational coping strategies.

The implications for transition politics are significant. Resistance to climate action, the gravitational pull of authoritarian strongmen among business elites, the paralysis before necessary systemic change — these are not merely failures of information or political will. They are expressions of unprocessed collective fear. People do not relinquish the systems that restored their sense of safety after catastrophic loss without enormous psychological cost. Edwards contends that until meta-theory and sustainability science develop frameworks adequate to trauma operating at planetary scale, their proposed interventions will continue to address symptoms while leaving the deepest drivers of the metacrisis untouched.

## Magus

Edwards advances a thesis that is at once psychohistorical and metatheoretical: the metacrisis cannot be adequately apprehended without foregrounding collective trauma as a primary causal vector — a concept he finds almost entirely absent from the existing discourse. The prevailing metacrisis literature treats the polycrisis as an emergent property of interlocking systemic failures across ecological, economic, epistemic, and governance domains. Edwards does not dispute the phenomenology but challenges the causal architecture. He locates the generative conditions of the Great Acceleration not in the structural logic of capital or the failures of Enlightenment rationality per se, but in the civilizational trauma field produced by the first half of the twentieth century — a period in which two world wars, pandemic, revolution, and industrialized colonial violence killed tens of millions and shattered the psychosocial coherence of entire populations.

The postwar economic order, in this framing, functioned as a collective dissociative strategy. The exponential growth curves that define the Anthropocene — carbon emissions, resource throughput, energy consumption — are legible as the behavioral signature of traumatized civilizations pursuing security and control through the only available means: production, accumulation, and the stabilization of material conditions. The Great Acceleration was not primarily a failure of foresight; it was a survival response operating beneath the threshold of collective reflexivity.

This reframing carries pointed implications for transition theory. The tenacity of fossil-capital regimes, the affective appeal of authoritarian consolidation among economic elites, the systemic inability to metabolize the scale of transformation required — these phenomena resist adequate explanation within purely structural or informational models. They become intelligible as expressions of unmetabolized civilizational fear. Edwards's challenge to the field is direct: until metatheoretical and sustainability frameworks develop analytic and interventional capacities adequate to collective trauma at planetary scale, they will continue to misidentify the depth at which the metacrisis is constituted.
