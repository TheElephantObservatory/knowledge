# Two Selves in Conflict: How Psychotherapy Repairs the Neurotic Loop

Propositions pass through skin; the rest must be inhabited

---

> The self contains genuinely different processing systems — one experiential and affect-laden, one narrative and propositional — and most psychological suffering arises not from either system alone but from destructive loops between them. Therapy works by changing the relationship between these selves, not by narrating over the experiential one.

The clinical encounter reveals something philosophically important about the architecture of the self that theoretical models tend to obscure: there are genuinely different information-processing systems operating at different timescales, speaking different languages, and they are not simply hierarchically organized but dynamically interrelated in ways that can go into vicious or virtuous cycles. The experiential self — perspectival, participatory, affect-laden, operating in the language of image, desire, and emotion — processes experience on a fundamentally different timescale and in a fundamentally different register than the self-conscious recursive propositional narrator. These are not just different levels of the same system; they are different systems. Propositions pass through the skin without losing their form — I can describe the red object behind you and you receive exactly the propositional content I transmitted. But the experiential self cannot be transmitted that way; it must be encountered, activated, inhabited. The therapeutic implication is that the primary domain of psychopathology most amenable to psychotherapy — the large class of internalizing disorders — involves a neurotic loop in which negative feelings about negative events generate a secondary reflective reaction that either over-regulates and jams the experiential self into a closet, where it builds pressure and eventually floods, or allows it to spill out and become totalizing. The therapeutic move is not to narrate but to change the relation with the experiential self — to bring curiosity rather than critical control, open exploration rather than resistance, compassionate acceptance rather than denial. When this succeeds, what happens is a reconsolidation: the affective narrative relation is reworked, the past is re-narrated not through intellectual revision but through a genuine shift in the felt sense of self-in-relation, and the person fits differently in the interpersonal matrix going forward. The logos of narrative coherence fertilizes the phenomenological affordance to repeat the experience more meaningfully.

## Apprentice

There are at least two very different selves operating inside every person. One is the feeling self — the one that processes life through emotion, imagery, desire, and bodily sensation. The other is the narrating self — the voice in your head that reflects, explains, and tries to make sense of things using words and logic. These aren't just two layers of the same system. They speak different languages and operate on completely different timescales. You can pass a sentence to someone and they receive it intact, but you cannot transmit a felt experience that way. It has to be lived.

Most common psychological suffering — anxiety, depression, shame spirals — comes from a toxic loop between these two selves. Something painful happens, feelings arise, and then the narrating self either clamps down on those feelings (stuffing them away until they explode) or lets them take over completely. Either way, the relationship between the two selves becomes adversarial.

The therapeutic breakthrough isn't about coming up with a better story about your past. It's about changing how the narrating self relates to the feeling self — replacing judgment with curiosity, control with openness, denial with compassion. When that shift happens, something remarkable occurs: the past actually feels different, not just intellectually but in the body. The person reorganizes from the inside out, and their relationships change accordingly.

## Adept

Clinical practice exposes a structural feature of selfhood that purely theoretical models tend to flatten: the experiential self and the propositional-narrative self are not merely different levels within a single hierarchy but genuinely distinct information-processing systems. The experiential self — perspectival, participatory, saturated with affect, operating through image and desire — works on a fundamentally different timescale and in a fundamentally different register than the recursive, self-conscious narrator. Propositions are transmissible across persons without loss of form; experiential states are not. They must be activated, inhabited, encountered from within.

This architectural distinction illuminates the core mechanism of internalizing psychopathology. The neurotic loop involves a secondary reflective reaction to primary negative affect: the narrative self either over-regulates the experiential self — suppressing it until pressure builds and it floods the system — or under-regulates, allowing affective states to become totalizing. In both cases, the pathology lies not in the content of either system but in the dysfunctional dynamic between them.

The therapeutic intervention that actually works targets this inter-system relation rather than narrative content. Bringing curiosity rather than critical control, open exploration rather than resistance, compassionate acceptance rather than denial — these moves restructure the affective-narrative interface. When successful, what occurs is a genuine reconsolidation: the felt sense of self-in-relation shifts, the past is re-narrated not through intellectual revision but through transformed phenomenological ground. Narrative coherence then fertilizes the capacity to repeat and deepen this experiential reorganization, creating a virtuous cycle where logos and lived experience mutually reinforce a more integrated mode of being.

## Magus

The clinical encounter functions as a philosophical instrument, disclosing an architecture of selfhood that theoretical abstraction systematically occludes. What emerges is an irreducible plurality: the experiential self — perspectival, participatory, affect-laden, trafficking in image and desire at sub-propositional timescales — and the recursive propositional narrator are not hierarchically nested but constitute genuinely distinct processing systems in dynamic interrelation. The asymmetry is revealing: propositional content passes through the skin isomorphically, but experiential states resist transmission and demand inhabitation. This is not an epistemic limitation but an ontological feature of the experiential register.

The large class of internalizing disorders can be understood as a specific failure mode of this inter-systemic dynamic. A neurotic loop forms: primary negative affect triggers a secondary reflective operation that either hyper-regulates — compressing the experiential self into a sealed container where hydraulic pressure accumulates toward eventual flooding — or hypo-regulates, allowing affective states to totalize and colonize the narrative frame. The pathology is neither in the affect nor in the narration but in the relational topology between them, a topology that can enter vicious or virtuous cycling.

Effective psychotherapy operates precisely at this juncture, not by revising propositional content but by transforming the stance the narrative self adopts toward experiential process — from critical surveillance to curiosity, from resistance to open exploration, from denial to compassionate acceptance. The result, when achieved, is reconsolidation in the strong sense: the affective-narrative relation is reworked such that the felt sense of self-in-relation genuinely shifts. The past is not reinterpreted but re-experienced through an altered phenomenological ground. Narrative coherence — logos — then fertilizes the phenomenological affordance for repetition with deepened meaning, establishing a virtuous cycle in which propositional integration and experiential transformation become mutually constitutive.
