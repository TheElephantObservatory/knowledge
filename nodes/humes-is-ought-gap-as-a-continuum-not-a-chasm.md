# Hume's Is-Ought Gap as a Continuum, Not a Chasm

The sixteen-year-old really does think better.

---

> The supposedly unbridgeable gap between facts and values — inherited from Hume — may be far less firm than modernity assumes, and this has radical consequences for psychology: if normative claims can be genuine facts, then developmental progress is real, not merely cultural preference.

One of the things I want to put on the table as a genuine philosophical problem is whether the is-ought distinction that modernity inherited from Hume is actually as firm as we have been told. The standard Enlightenment picture holds an unbridgeable chasm between descriptive and normative claims, between facts and values. But the philosophical foundations of this picture are much shakier than they appear. Putnam's arguments, the Baskarian dialectic, Quine's dissolution of the analytic-synthetic distinction — these suggest that the is-ought gap is not a chasm but something more like a continuum, with real distinctions but not the absolute separation modernity assumed. This matters enormously for metapsychology. If you accept the strict fact-value distinction, then any normative claim in psychology — that one way of justifying beliefs is better than another, or that one stage of development is more adequate — steps outside science into mere opinion. But if the is-ought distinction is more porous, then normative facts are genuinely facts, and a psychology that ignores them is not more scientific but less adequate to its subject matter. The developmental tradition, from Baldwin through Piaget through the neo-Piagetians, was always implicitly working with this more porous picture. The child at sixteen does not merely think differently about causality than the child at four — the child at sixteen thinks better, and that betterness is not just a cultural preference but something that can be demonstrated, argued for, and built into a theory of what it means for a mind to deepen into reality.

## Apprentice

There's a long-standing idea in modern thought that you can never get from "what is" to "what ought to be" — that facts and values live in completely separate worlds. This idea, traced back to the philosopher David Hume, has shaped how we think about science, morality, and knowledge for centuries. But several important philosophical arguments suggest this wall between facts and values isn't nearly as solid as we've been taught.

This matters deeply for how we understand the mind and its development. If facts and values are strictly separate, then saying a teenager reasons "better" than a toddler is just an opinion — not something science can claim. But if the wall is more like a fence with gaps in it, then "better reasoning" can be a genuine fact about the world, something we can study and demonstrate.

The tradition of developmental psychology — studying how children's thinking grows and changes — has always quietly assumed this. A sixteen-year-old doesn't just think differently about cause and effect than a four-year-old; they think more adequately. That improvement isn't just a cultural bias. It's something real, and a psychology that refuses to acknowledge it isn't being more rigorous — it's actually missing something essential about what minds do as they develop.

## Adept

The Humean is-ought distinction — the claim that no normative conclusion can be derived from purely descriptive premises — has functioned as a foundational axiom of modern epistemology. Yet its philosophical foundations are considerably less secure than the standard Enlightenment picture suggests. Putnam's collapse of the fact-value dichotomy, Bhaskar's dialectical arguments, and Quine's dissolution of the analytic-synthetic distinction all converge on a similar conclusion: the relationship between descriptive and normative claims is better understood as a continuum with real but non-absolute distinctions, rather than an unbridgeable logical chasm.

The implications for metapsychology are profound. Under a strict fact-value separation, any normative claim within psychology — that certain forms of reasoning are more adequate, that developmental stages represent genuine cognitive progress — is automatically relegated to the domain of subjective preference, outside the scope of scientific inquiry. A more porous is-ought boundary, however, opens the possibility that normative facts are genuinely facts, amenable to demonstration and theoretical integration.

This is precisely the picture that the developmental tradition from Baldwin through Piaget to the neo-Piagetians has always implicitly presupposed. The claim that a sixteen-year-old's causal reasoning is not merely different from but superior to a four-year-old's is not a cultural imposition but a defensible normative-empirical claim. A psychology that systematically excludes such claims in the name of value-neutrality does not achieve greater scientific rigor — it produces a framework inadequate to its own subject matter. Recognizing this transforms developmental psychology from a mere catalogue of cognitive changes into a discipline that can speak meaningfully about what it means for a mind to deepen into reality.

## Magus

The Humean is-ought distinction has operated as something close to a regulative principle for post-Enlightenment thought, enforcing a categorical separation between descriptive and normative discourse that disciplines from metaethics to philosophy of science have largely taken as given. Yet the philosophical warrant for this separation has been progressively undermined from multiple directions: Putnam's entanglement arguments demonstrate that epistemic values are constitutive of factual inquiry itself; Bhaskar's dialectical critical realism shows that absences and constraints carry normative force internal to ontological structure; Quine's dismantling of the analytic-synthetic distinction removes the very logical architecture on which a clean is-ought partition depends. What emerges is not the erasure of the distinction but its reconceptualization — from an absolute logical gap to a gradient within a unified space of rational inquiry.

The metapsychological stakes are considerable. If the strict dichotomy holds, then developmental psychology's normative commitments — that formal operations represent cognitive advance, that later stages are more adequate to reality — are extra-scientific impositions, mere preferences dressed in empirical clothing. But if normative facts are genuinely facts, then a psychology that excludes them purchases methodological purity at the cost of descriptive adequacy. The developmental tradition from Baldwin through Piaget to the constructivist neo-Piagetians was always working within this more porous framework, treating the child's cognitive trajectory not as mere sequential variation but as progressive deepening — an ontogenetic movement toward greater adequacy of representation.

The sixteen-year-old's grasp of causality is not merely different from the four-year-old's; it is demonstrably better, and that betterness is articulable, defensible, and theoretically consequential. To deny this is not scientific restraint but a philosophical commitment masquerading as one — and a commitment whose own foundations have been substantially eroded.
