# Beyond Fear, Fight, Follow, Fuck: Communitas Without Collapse

When somebody comes through with charismatic authority, we tend to do one of four things — rooted in Lisa Feldman Barrett's notions of interoception and affect. If we are passively attracted, we want to follow them. If we are actively attracted, it merges into the sexual-erotic — we want to fuck them. If we are negatively passive, we fear them. If we are negatively active, we want to fight them. Fear, fight, follow, fuck — these are predictable quadrants of how most of this plays out. What genuine wisdom communities are trying to explore is something beyond all four: communitas — a sense of shared co-creative interdependence, whether that's group flow, Otto Scharmer's U-theory, or Victor Turner's communitas — where something co-creative and amazing happens without surrendering agency into unhealthy authority structures. The deeper question isn't just how to avoid cults; it's how to achieve genuine communitas without the gravitational pull of charismatic authority collapsing everything back into one of those four primitive responses.

## Apprentice

When someone walks into a room with a powerful, magnetic presence, something happens in our bodies before our minds catch up. We tend to react in one of four ways: we want to follow them, we feel sexually drawn to them, we fear them, or we want to confront them. These aren't random — they map onto something deep in how our nervous systems process attraction and threat. The problem is that all four of these reactions hand power over to the charismatic person. We either submit, desire, flee, or fight. None of these responses keep us fully ourselves. What the best communities and gatherings try to create is something genuinely different — a state where people think and create together without anyone collapsing into worship or rivalry. Researchers and thinkers have called this 'communitas': a kind of shared aliveness where the group becomes more than the sum of its parts, without anyone losing their independence. The real challenge isn't just avoiding cults or bad leaders. It's learning how to reach that rare, generative togetherness without the magnetic pull of a powerful personality dragging everyone back into one of those four primitive reactions.

## Adept

Drawing on Lisa Feldman Barrett's constructivist model of emotion — in which affect is generated through interoceptive prediction rather than hardwired circuits — this framework maps four predictable responses to charismatic authority onto a valence-arousal grid. Passive positive affect produces the follower: deferential, receptive, surrendering agency upward. Active positive affect tips into the erotic: desire that seeks merger rather than discipleship. Passive negative affect generates fear: withdrawal, appeasement, the freeze response dressed in social clothing. Active negative affect produces the antagonist: the fighter, the rival, the one who defines themselves against the charismatic figure rather than toward them. Fear, fight, follow, fuck — four quadrants, all of which represent a kind of agency collapse in the presence of concentrated social power. The insight is that genuine wisdom communities are attempting something structurally different: communitas in Victor Turner's sense, or the collective intelligence described by Otto Scharmer's U-theory and research into group flow states. These are conditions where co-creative interdependence emerges without the group's energy being organized around a single attractor. The difficulty is gravitational. Charismatic authority creates a social field that tends to pull every interaction back toward one of the four primitive responses. Designing communities that sustain communitas — that hold open the space of shared emergence — requires actively working against these default attractors, which means understanding them with precision first.

## Magus

The framework proposed here applies Barrett's theory of constructed emotion — specifically her account of interoception as predictive inference generating valenced, aroused affect — to the social phenomenology of charismatic authority. Rather than treating responses to charisma as culturally contingent or individually idiosyncratic, the model identifies four attractor states organized along orthogonal axes of valence and arousal. Low-arousal positive affect yields identificatory discipleship: the follower who cathects the charismatic figure as ego ideal in the Freudian sense, surrendering epistemic and agentive autonomy through idealization. High-arousal positive affect produces eroticized merger: libidinal investment that seeks not guidance but incorporation, collapsing the boundary between admiration and desire. Low-arousal negative affect generates the appeasement-fear complex: hypervigilance, submission under threat, the fawn response that mimics followership while organized around avoidance. High-arousal negative affect produces rivalrous antagonism: the fighter who remains defined by the charismatic field even in opposition, a negative dependency structurally isomorphic to discipleship. What unites all four is the loss of what Winnicott might call the capacity to be alone in the presence of another — genuine subjectivity dissolves into the relational field organized by the charismatic attractor. Against this, the concept of communitas — Turner's liminal intersubjectivity, Scharmer's presencing, Csikszentmihalyi's collective flow — represents an attempt to sustain what might be called distributed agency under conditions of high collective affect. The theoretical and practical problem is one of basin dynamics: charismatic authority creates deep attractor wells, and communitas requires holding the system in a metastable region that resists collapse into any of the four primitive configurations. This is less a psychological achievement than a structural and ecological one, demanding deliberate design of the conditions under which shared emergence becomes possible.
