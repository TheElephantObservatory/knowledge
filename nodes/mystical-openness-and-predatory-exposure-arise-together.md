# Mystical Openness and Predatory Exposure Arise Together

The membrane thins for everything at once.

---

> Genuine mystical openness and encounters with predation are not opposites but co-arising features of the same permeability. The thinning of the membrane between self and world that allows contact with the sacred also exposes one to what is dangerous.

A recurring structural pattern runs through Bruce Alderman's biography, identified both in his own account and in Cheryl Hsu's phenomenological observation as co-host: every period of genuine mystical opening is accompanied by, and often intertwined with, encounters with predation, danger, and violence. Cheryl names this as a dual structure — on one side, a creative innocence, an ability to be with the full vibrancy of the world; on the other, always present, a hyper-animalistic awareness of predators that keeps the child very close to the body even while playing. These are not opposites but co-arising features of the same mode of being. The child communing with nature in the bird sanctuary is also the child being hunted by a predator in the church parking lot. The young man experiencing his strongest mystical states in Varanasi is also the young man hiding on a rooftop from a gang of goons, being held to a wall by a trident, confronted on a bus with scimitars. This is not coincidence and it is not simply the texture of an adventurous life. It suggests something about the structure of liminal experience itself — that genuine openness to depth, to presence, to the sacred, involves a thinning of the ordinary protective membrane between self and world, and that this thinning is not selective. The same permeability that allows contact with living presence also allows contact with predatory presence. The shamanic literature has always known this — the capacity to move between worlds is also exposure to what moves between worlds. Bruce's biography reads as an extended lived experiment in what it costs and what it yields to remain that open.

## Apprentice

A striking pattern emerges in Bruce Alderman's life story: every period of deep spiritual opening arrives tangled with real danger. The child experiencing wonder in a bird sanctuary is also the child being stalked by a predator in a parking lot. The young man having his most powerful mystical experiences in India is also the young man hiding on a rooftop from armed men. These pairings are not coincidence.

Cheryl Hsu, observing this pattern, names it as a dual structure — on one side, a creative innocence that can fully receive the world's beauty and depth; on the other, a hyper-alert, animal awareness of threat. These two capacities don't cancel each other out. They belong together. They are two faces of the same way of being alive.

The insight is that genuine openness to the sacred involves a thinning of the usual barrier between self and world — and that thinning is not selective. The same permeability that lets in living presence also lets in predatory presence. To remain that open is not just a spiritual achievement; it is a continuous negotiation with real cost and real danger. Alderman's biography reads as a lifelong experiment in what it takes, and what it yields, to stay that permeable.

Cheryl Hsu identifies this as a dual structure — creative innocence and hyper-animalistic threat awareness co-arising as features of the same mode of being, not as opposites requiring resolution. Remaining permeable at that degree is a sustained existential negotiation — an extended lived experiment in what it costs and what it yields to refuse the re-thickening of the membrane that ordinary life continually offers as protection.

## Magus

The biographical pattern Cheryl Hsu identifies in Bruce Alderman's life constitutes a phenomenological datum of considerable structural significance: mystical opening and predatory encounter are not sequentially related but co-arising, dual aspects of a single mode of permeability. Her formulation — creative innocence on one axis, hyper-animalistic threat awareness on the other — resists dialectical resolution. These are not thesis and antithesis but simultaneous features of the same ontological thinning. The child in the bird sanctuary and the child hunted in the parking lot occupy one experiential field; the mystic in Varanasi and the man held at trident-point share a single condition of exposure.

This observation reframes liminal experience as structurally non-selective permeability. The membrane between self and world that ordinary consciousness maintains as protective barrier thins under conditions of genuine openness to depth — but that thinning cannot be directed only toward the sacred. Whatever moves between worlds moves in both directions. The shamanic traditions encode this knowledge: initiation is wounding, the healer's gift is the healer's vulnerability, the open channel is the open wound. Alderman's biography does not illustrate this principle so much as it instantiates it across decades of lived phenomenology.

What emerges is not a heroic narrative but an empirical question about the economics of permeability — what sustained openness at this degree costs somatically, psychologically, relationally, and what forms of knowledge or contact it alone makes possible. Alderman's life reads as an extended experiment in refusing the re-thickening of the protective membrane, holding open a condition that most organisms — and most contemplative traditions — eventually learn to modulate, ritualize, or close.
