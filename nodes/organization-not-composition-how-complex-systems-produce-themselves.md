# Organization, Not Composition: How Complex Systems Produce Themselves

The whole arrives first, making its own parts.

---

> Understanding a complex system means grasping how it is organized, not what it is made of. In living systems, this goes further: the whole actively generates its own parts, creating causal loops that standard models of emergence cannot yet capture.

One of the most profound reframings in complexity science is the shift from asking 'what is this system made of?' to asking 'how is this system organized?' Stuart Kauffman crystallized this for Joe Norman with the phrase 'propagation of organization.' The insight is that what's essential about a system — how it produces behaviors, how it interacts with other systems — is not located in the stuff it's made of, but in the patterns of constraint and structure on interactions. This is a direct challenge to the Western reductionist default, which treats decomposition into parts as the royal road to understanding. Complexity science says no: the organization is the phenomenon. And this has a corollary that is even stranger and more cutting-edge — in organic, growing complex systems, the whole actually synthesizes its own components. Unlike artificial systems that are assembled from pre-existing parts, a developing organism generates its functional components through the very process of its own becoming. The parts don't precede the whole; the whole is what makes the parts what they are. This is what makes 'downward causation' such a live and unresolved question — not just that higher levels constrain lower ones, but that the emerging domain actively creates the conditions for the sub-patterns within it. Joe Norman gives the example of Turing morphogenesis patterns on fish skin: yes, reaction-diffusion dynamics can account for the stripe patterns, but the medium on which those patterns form is itself being generated by the organism as it develops. There's a causal loop that standard models of emergence don't capture. We are, as Norman puts it, only at the beginning of grappling with how functional properties arise not just from a component in isolation, but from that component in irreducible relationship to the context that gave rise to it.

## Apprentice

Most of science works by breaking things into smaller pieces to understand them. But complexity science argues that the most important thing about a system isn't what it's made of — it's how it's organized. Stuart Kauffman captured this with the phrase "propagation of organization," pointing out that a system's behavior comes from the patterns and relationships between its parts, not from the parts themselves.

This gets even more surprising when you look at living, growing systems. Unlike a machine, which is assembled from pre-made components, a developing organism actually creates its own parts as it grows. The whole comes first, and the parts emerge from it — not the other way around. Think of the stripe patterns on a fish: while chemistry can explain how stripes form on a surface, the surface itself is being built by the organism at the same time. There's a circular cause-and-effect that our usual scientific tools struggle to describe.

As Joe Norman emphasizes, science is only beginning to wrestle with this puzzle. A component's function doesn't come from the component alone — it comes from the component's relationship to the larger system that brought it into existence. Understanding that relationship may require entirely new ways of thinking.

## Adept

Stuart Kauffman's phrase "propagation of organization" marks a decisive shift in complexity science: the essential character of a system lies not in its material constituents but in its patterns of constraint and relational structure. This directly challenges the reductionist program that treats decomposition into parts as the primary explanatory strategy. Organization is not an epiphenomenon layered on top of matter — it is the phenomenon. What a system does, how it couples to its environment, how it persists — all of these are properties of how interactions are structured, not of what the interacting elements happen to be made of.

The corollary is more radical still. In organic, developing complex systems, the whole synthesizes its own components. Unlike engineered artifacts assembled from pre-existing parts, a biological organism generates its functional sub-structures through the process of its own becoming. The parts do not precede the whole; the whole is what makes the parts what they are. Joe Norman illustrates this with Turing morphogenesis patterns on fish skin: reaction-diffusion dynamics explain the stripe patterns, but the medium on which those patterns form is itself being produced by the developing organism. This creates a causal loop — a form of downward causation — that standard emergence frameworks fail to accommodate.

This insight reframes downward causation not merely as higher-level constraint on lower-level dynamics, but as the active creation of the conditions under which sub-patterns exist at all. As Norman argues, functional properties arise not from components in isolation but from components in irreducible relationship to the context that generated them — a problem complexity science is only beginning to formalize.

## Magus

Kauffman's injunction to study the "propagation of organization" rather than the inventory of substrates constitutes a foundational reorientation: the explanatory target in complex systems is the relational architecture of constraint, not the material identity of constituents. This is not merely a methodological preference but an ontological claim — that organization is causally primary, that the topology of interactions is what generates the behavioral repertoire, the coupling modalities, and the persistence conditions of the system. Reductionist decomposition, on this view, systematically destroys the very thing it seeks to explain.

The deeper consequence emerges in the domain of biological development, where the relationship between whole and part inverts the logic of assembly. In organic systems, the developing totality synthesizes its own components; parts do not pre-exist their integration but are constituted through the system's process of becoming. Norman's example of Turing morphogenesis on fish skin is precisely targeted: reaction-diffusion dynamics account for pattern formation on a substrate, but the substrate itself is a product of the organism's concurrent self-construction. The explanatory regress here is genuine — the pattern depends on the medium, the medium depends on the organism, and the organism's identity is partly constituted by the pattern. Standard supervenience-based emergence frameworks, which assume a fixed lower-level base, cannot represent this circularity.

What is at stake is the status of downward causation as something stronger than constraint: the emerging organizational domain actively generates the conditions of existence for its sub-patterns. Functional properties, as Norman insists, are irreducibly relational — they belong neither to the component nor to the context but to their co-constitution. Formalizing this co-constitutive causality remains one of the central open problems in the sciences of complexity.
