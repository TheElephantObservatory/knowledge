# Encoding Values into Organizational Governance and Capital

Architects of a more gentle harvest

---

> Between revolution and sellout lies a third path: redesigning the legal and financial architecture of organizations from the start so that values are structurally encoded, not just aspirationally stated.

When we talk about how to navigate the psychedelic space — or really any space where important innovations are trying to get into the world — there's a false binary that often gets presented: either you blow up the entire macroeconomic system and start from scratch, or you sell out to extractive capitalism. But there's a third path, which is software patches on the existing system. You don't need a tabula rasa. You work with what you've got, but you bake different values into the architecture of the organization at inception. The clearest example of this is the Benefit Corporation model — where instead of pure bottom-line shareholder fiduciary responsibility, you embed into your operating agreements, your shareholder agreements, your governance structures, a commitment to people, planet, and profit. But there's an even more interesting innovation happening at the capital level. Greylock Ventures articulated what they call the 1 to 3x return space. Most venture funds need 10x returns because nine out of ten of their investments die — so the one that survives has to cover the whole portfolio. That structure systematically underfunds pro-social and important innovations that don't have moonshot economics. The 1 to 3x model says: we want to give you your money back, we want to become self-perpetuating rather than a leaky-bucket nonprofit that needs a black-tie gala every year to stay alive, and we cap returns at 3x because this is a pro-social mission and we don't want to over-harvest. That's the key insight — you can design the terms of capital itself to encode your values, and in doing so, you change what the organization is structurally capable of becoming.

## Apprentice

When people try to build something good in the world — a healthcare company, a psychedelic therapy clinic, a social enterprise — they're often told there are only two options: tear down capitalism entirely, or play by its rules and compromise your values. But there's a third option that gets overlooked. You can work inside the existing system while quietly rewiring it from the inside, starting at the moment you build your organization.

The Benefit Corporation is one example. Instead of being legally required to maximize profits for shareholders above all else, a Benefit Corporation bakes commitments to people and the planet directly into its founding documents. The law itself becomes a container for your values.

An even more radical idea is happening at the level of investment. Most venture funds need enormous returns because most of their bets fail. That math systematically ignores good ideas that will never be billion-dollar businesses. A newer model caps investor returns at three times their money — enough to be self-sustaining, not enough to over-extract. The insight is simple but powerful: if you design the terms of money itself to reflect your values, you change what your organization is structurally capable of becoming.

## Adept

The dominant framing in mission-driven enterprise presents a false dilemma: systemic overthrow or capitulation to extractive capitalism. This insight proposes a third path — iterative patches on existing legal and financial infrastructure that encode values at the architectural level rather than relying on cultural goodwill or founder intention alone. The key move is intervening at inception, before path dependencies calcify.

The Benefit Corporation structure is the clearest institutional expression of this logic. By embedding fiduciary duties to stakeholders — people, planet, and profit — directly into operating and shareholder agreements, the organization's legal DNA is altered. Governance structures become value-enforcement mechanisms rather than mere administrative formalities. This matters because culture drifts; legal architecture is stickier.

The more sophisticated innovation operates at the capital stack level. Conventional venture capital's portfolio math — requiring 10x returns to compensate for a ~90% failure rate — structurally excludes pro-social ventures with modest but reliable economics. The 1-to-3x return model, articulated by funds like Greylock Ventures, targets self-perpetuation over extraction: returning capital, remaining solvent without philanthropic subsidy, and capping upside to prevent over-harvesting. This reframes the fund itself as a values-bearing instrument. The implication is that capital terms are not neutral — they are design decisions, and designing them deliberately changes the solution space available to the organizations they fund.

## Magus

The prevailing discourse around mission-aligned enterprise tends to oscillate between revolutionary abolitionism and pragmatic accommodation — a dialectic that forecloses the more generative possibility of immanent structural transformation. This perspective rejects the tabula rasa fantasy in favor of what might be called incremental constitutional redesign: the strategic inscription of normative commitments into the legal and financial substrates of organizational life at the moment of formation, before institutional isomorphism renders deviation costly.

The Benefit Corporation represents the most legible instantiation of this logic. By modifying the fiduciary duty structure — historically the primary mechanism through which shareholder primacy is enforced — these entities achieve a kind of legal pluralism within the corporate form itself. Stakeholder obligations are not aspirational addenda but enforceable constitutional constraints, altering the decision-theoretic landscape for management and board alike. The organization's capacity for ethical drift is architecturally curtailed rather than culturally managed.

The more theoretically rich intervention occurs at the level of capital formation. Standard venture fund mechanics — predicated on power-law return distributions to offset high portfolio attrition — function as a selection filter that systematically defunds ventures whose value propositions are social rather than exponential. The 1-to-3x return thesis disrupts this by decoupling fund viability from moonshot economics: targeting capital preservation and modest appreciation sufficient for self-perpetuation, while imposing a return ceiling that operationalizes the anti-extractive commitment as a binding term rather than a stated preference. The fund becomes a normative instrument. What this reveals is that capital structure is never axiologically neutral — it is always already encoding a theory of value, and the choice is whether that encoding is deliberate or inherited.
