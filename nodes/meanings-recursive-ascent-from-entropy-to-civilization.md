# Meaning's Recursive Ascent: From Entropy to Civilization

Meaning has complexified across cosmic evolution not by replacing earlier forms but by layering new registers of semantic information upon existing substrates — each level dependent upon but irreducible to what came before. Beginning as a purely thermodynamic imperative, the most rudimentary form of meaning is simply what aids an entity in resisting entropic dissolution — what physicist Carlo Rovelli calls 'direct meaning': information with immediate causal bearing on an organism's thermodynamic viability. From this foundation, meaning deepens through biological life into goal-directed behavior — energy capture, self-maintenance, reproduction — then diversifies through the evolution of nervous systems into felt experience, normative valence, and the inner world of subjectivity. Language then performs a double transformation: it reframes meanings from all prior levels while simultaneously opening entirely new domains — self-awareness, theory of mind, philosophy, religion, science — that have no analogue below the cultural threshold. This extraordinary semantic complexity is grounded in what Rovelli calls 'indirect meaning': information relevant not because it directly sustains survival, but because it increases the probability of acquiring more directly meaningful information. Crucially, indirect meanings can themselves generate further indirect meanings, each layer more abstracted from the thermodynamic foundation yet still tethered to it through a long causal chain — a recursive dynamic that bootstraps an entire civilization of meaning from that original survival signal. The crucial philosophical move is the distinction between reduction and foundation: human meaning cannot be reduced to thermodynamic survival any more than the contents of a book can be reduced to the paper it is written on — yet without the paper, there is no book. This gives us a naturalistic grounding for meaning that is neither reductive nor mysterian: a chain of successively more complex semantic registers, each adding articulation to the last, running unbroken from physics to culture.

## Apprentice

What does it mean for something to 'mean' anything at all? One compelling answer traces meaning back to its most basic form: information that helps a living thing survive. A bacterium responding to a chemical gradient is already, in a primitive sense, interpreting its world — that chemical signal means something because it bears on whether the bacterium lives or dies. From this starting point, meaning grows more elaborate at every stage of evolution. Animals develop nervous systems that generate feelings and preferences. Humans develop language, which does something remarkable: it not only describes the world but opens up entirely new territories of meaning — self-reflection, storytelling, science, religion — that simply did not exist before. The key insight is that each new layer of meaning builds on what came before without being reducible to it. Human love, grief, or philosophical wonder cannot be explained away as mere survival chemistry — yet they would not exist without that chemistry as their foundation. Think of it like a book: the words cannot be reduced to paper and ink, but without paper and ink, there are no words. This gives us a way to understand meaning as genuinely real and rich, while still being fully part of the natural world.

## Adept

This framework proposes a stratified ontology of meaning grounded in thermodynamics and elaborated through successive evolutionary transitions. Drawing on physicist Carlo Rovelli's distinction between 'direct' and 'indirect' meaning, it argues that the most primitive semantic register is simply information with immediate causal bearing on an organism's capacity to resist entropic dissolution — a survival signal. Biological evolution then builds upon this foundation, generating goal-directed behavior, homeostatic regulation, and reproductive strategy as increasingly complex expressions of the same underlying imperative. The emergence of nervous systems introduces a qualitatively new register: felt experience, normative valence, and phenomenal subjectivity — dimensions of meaning that are causally dependent on thermodynamic viability but cannot be derived from it by any straightforward reduction. Language marks a second major discontinuity. It does not merely encode meanings from prior levels but actively reframes them while simultaneously generating domains — self-awareness, theory of mind, metaphysics, science — with no functional analogue below the cultural threshold. Indirect meanings, those relevant not because they directly sustain survival but because they increase the probability of acquiring directly meaningful information, prove recursively generative: each layer of abstraction can bootstrap further layers, producing the extraordinary semantic complexity of human civilization. The philosophical payoff is a position that avoids both eliminative reductionism and mysterianism. The relationship between levels is one of foundation rather than identity: higher-order meanings are genuinely novel and irreducible, yet they remain tethered to the thermodynamic substrate through an unbroken causal chain. The book-and-paper analogy captures this precisely — the contents of a text cannot be reduced to its physical medium, yet the medium remains an indispensable condition of possibility.

## Magus

The framework advances a hierarchical semiotics grounded in non-equilibrium thermodynamics, proposing that meaning is not a late-arriving anomaly in a physicalist universe but a property that complexifies continuously from the most primitive dissipative structures upward. Rovelli's category of 'direct meaning' — information causally efficacious with respect to an organism's thermodynamic viability — establishes the ground level: a signal means something, in this minimal sense, insofar as it differentially affects the probability of the organism's persistence as a far-from-equilibrium system. This is meaning in its Peircean indexical mode stripped to its physical minimum. Biological evolution elaborates this substrate through the emergence of teleonomic organization, where meaning becomes encoded in the formal causality of metabolic and reproductive systems — a transition that corresponds roughly to Terrence Deacon's 'absential' causation, where what is not yet present (the organism's future state) exerts organizational constraint on present dynamics. The phylogenetic elaboration of nervous systems introduces phenomenal and normative registers: valenced experience, affective salience, and the proto-intentionality of perceptual representation. These constitute genuine ontological novelty — emergent properties in the strong sense, supervening on but not derivable from lower-level descriptions. Language then performs a double semiotic operation: it re-encodes meanings from all prior registers into a symbolic medium while simultaneously enabling the recursive self-application of that medium, generating the reflexive and hypothetical domains — counterfactual reasoning, narrative identity, institutional ontology — that constitute the cultural lifeworld. Rovelli's 'indirect meaning' names the mechanism of this recursion: meanings that function instrumentally with respect to the acquisition of further meanings, enabling an open-ended bootstrapping dynamic. The resulting position occupies a precise philosophical niche: it is naturalistic without being eliminativist, emergentist without invoking strong metaphysical discontinuity, and grounds the irreducibility of higher semantic registers in the same logical structure as the irreducibility of syntactic content to its material inscription.
