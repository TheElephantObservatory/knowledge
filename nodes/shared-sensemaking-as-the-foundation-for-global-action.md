# Shared Sensemaking as the Foundation for Global Action

To solve the world, we must see it together.

---

> The world's biggest crises share hidden common roots — and the most critical of those roots is our collective inability to agree on what's actually happening and what to do about it.

Underlying all of these distinct global crises — environmental, technological, economic, geopolitical — there is something in common, and naming that common substrate is what the concept of the metacrisis is pointing at. It is not just a list of serious problems. It is the recognition that there are shared generative dynamics: patterns of human behavior, patterns of collective choice-making, and the way those patterns interact with markets, states, and technology, that both give rise to these problems and make them structurally resistant to being solved. One of the most critical of those underlying dynamics is the breakdown of shared sensemaking. If we cannot agree on what the nature of the problems are, or what good solutions look like, then some portion of the population works on what they believe is important while another portion does everything it can to prevent that from happening. And even when there is some agreement in principle — say, on nuclear disarmament, or on pricing carbon, or on not building AI weapons — if we cannot achieve international coordination, then anyone who consciously chooses to act on that principle disadvantages themselves in the geopolitical game of power relative to those who don't. So nobody can do the effective thing. You get collective action failures and races to the bottom. Sensemaking is therefore not a soft or secondary concern — it is one of the most foundational leverage points in the entire metacrisis, because without some better shared understanding of what needs to be done, there is no starting point for the coordination and collective choice-making that any real solution requires.

## Apprentice

Climate change, AI risk, economic instability, geopolitical conflict — these look like separate problems, but there may be something deeper connecting them. The concept of the metacrisis names that deeper layer: the shared patterns of human behavior and collective decision-making that generate these problems in the first place, and that also make them so hard to solve.

One of the most important of those underlying patterns is the breakdown of shared sensemaking — the erosion of our collective ability to agree on what the problems actually are and what good solutions look like. When people can't agree on basic facts or values, different groups end up working at cross-purposes, with some actively blocking what others are trying to build.

Even when there is broad agreement in principle — say, on limiting nuclear weapons or regulating AI — the inability to coordinate internationally means that anyone who acts on that principle puts themselves at a disadvantage relative to those who don't. The result is collective action failures and races to the bottom, where no one can do the right thing without being punished for it. Rebuilding shared sensemaking isn't a soft cultural concern — it may be the most foundational problem of all.

## Adept

The metacrisis framework proposes that the proliferating global crises of the 21st century — ecological overshoot, AI misalignment risk, financial instability, democratic erosion, geopolitical fragmentation — are not independent phenomena but expressions of shared generative dynamics. These dynamics include the structural incentives embedded in markets and states, the feedback loops between technology and power, and the collective choice-making failures that prevent adaptive responses. The metacrisis is therefore not a list of problems but a diagnosis of the systemic substrate from which those problems emerge and within which they resist resolution.

Among the most critical of these generative dynamics is the collapse of shared sensemaking. Sensemaking here refers to the socially distributed process by which communities construct coherent models of reality sufficient to coordinate action. When that process breaks down — through epistemic fragmentation, information ecosystem degradation, or the weaponization of narrative — populations lose the capacity to converge on problem definitions or solution criteria. The result is not merely disagreement but active interference: factions working in direct opposition, each convinced the other is the obstacle.

This dynamic compounds into structural coordination failures at the international level. Even where normative consensus exists in principle — on carbon pricing, nuclear non-proliferation, or AI governance — defection remains individually rational in the absence of enforceable coordination mechanisms. The insight here is that sensemaking is not a precondition that can be assumed and bypassed; it is itself one of the highest-leverage intervention points in the metacrisis, because without sufficient shared understanding, the political will and institutional architecture required for any systemic solution cannot be assembled.

## Magus

The metacrisis concept performs a specific theoretical operation: it reframes the apparent multiplicity of contemporary global crises as surface-level manifestations of a deeper structural pathology in the relationship between human cognitive and institutional architectures and the complex adaptive systems — ecological, technological, economic — within which they are embedded. Rather than treating each crisis domain as requiring domain-specific remediation, the metacrisis lens foregrounds the generative dynamics that are causally upstream of the crises themselves: the incentive structures of competitive state systems, the externality-blind logic of capital accumulation, and critically, the epistemological infrastructure through which collective agents model their situation and coordinate response.

The breakdown of shared sensemaking occupies a privileged position within this causal topology. Sensemaking, understood in the tradition of Weick and extended into the domain of collective epistemology, refers to the processes by which distributed social actors construct sufficiently convergent world-models to enable coordinated action under uncertainty. Its degradation — through the structural incentives of attention-economy media, the deliberate manufacture of epistemic uncertainty by vested interests, and the fragmentation of interpretive communities — produces not merely cognitive disagreement but what might be called adversarial epistemics: conditions in which the very act of problem-definition becomes a site of political contestation, foreclosing the possibility of shared solution-space exploration.

The deeper structural problem is the interaction between epistemic fragmentation and the collective action logic of multipolar competitive systems. Even where normative convergence is achieved within epistemic communities, the Westphalian architecture of sovereign states creates defection incentives that render unilateral compliance individually irrational — a dynamic visible across nuclear governance, climate finance, and emerging AI arms control. The argument, then, is that sensemaking repair is not a soft or preparatory concern but a constitutive condition of possibility for the institutional coordination that any adequate response to the metacrisis requires: without it, the political substrate for binding multilateral commitment cannot form.
