# How Animal Mindedness Disappeared from Western Thought

The cat lands on its feet; we have no word for why

---

> Modern thought lost a category Aristotle had: animal mindedness, the sensory-motor awareness that sits between dead matter and human self-reflection. Without a name for this middle ground, psychology has been trying to study the mind while missing the very thing it studies.

The disappearance of animal mindedness as a distinct ontological category is one of the most consequential blind spots in modern intellectual history. Aristotle's scales of nature recognized four levels: inanimate objects, plants, animals characterized by sensory-motor activity, and rational animals. That middle category — minded animals — had genuine conceptual standing. But when modern science and philosophy reorganized the world, the living world collapsed flora and fauna together into a single biological domain, and humans stood apart as the sole locus of mind. The result was a bipolar ontology — matter on one side, human self-reflective consciousness on the other — with animal mindedness falling through the gap. Descartes crystallized this by defining mind as the self-aware 'I think therefore I am' and matter as the mechanistic, mathematizable world, cutting out precisely what sits between those poles: the sensory-motor loop of animals navigating 3D behavioral space. Behaviorism then stripped out the mental adjective entirely, and cognitivism brought mind back only to locate it inside the skull as a computational processor, again severing it from embodied, embedded, enacted, extended animal life. The parallel to biology is exact: imagine trying to do biology without the concept of life, forced to describe a tree as a highly complex chemical unit, unable to name the category that gives the discipline its coherence. That is precisely the situation psychology and cognitive science are in with respect to mindedness. Everyone can see the difference between a dead cat falling like a stone, an anesthetized cat falling without behavioral response, and a living aroused cat that resists being pushed, rotates mid-air, lands on its feet, and runs — these are not different quantities of the same behavior but qualitatively different classes of process. Without a word that names the property, we grab pieces: subjective experience, information processing, neural computation, embodiment. Each piece is real, but each is mistaken for the whole, exactly like the blind men and the elephant. Mindedness — the functional awareness and responsiveness of the animal as a whole in 3D behavioral space, constituted by the sensory-motor loop — is the word that names the whole animal. It was there in Aristotle, visible to anyone watching ants carry food or a cat rotate mid-air. It simply got forgotten. And the cost of that forgetting is that we have been trying to understand psychology, cognition, and behavior without a word for the thing we are actually studying.

## Apprentice

Aristotle divided the natural world into four levels: lifeless objects, plants, animals with senses and movement, and rational beings. That third category — animals that perceive and respond to the world — had a real place in how people understood reality. But somewhere along the way, modern thinking collapsed plants and animals into one bucket called "biology" and reserved the word "mind" exclusively for human self-awareness. The in-between category of animal mindedness simply vanished.

Consider three versions of a cat: a dead cat falling like a rock, a sedated cat falling limp, and a fully alert cat that twists mid-air, lands on its feet, and bolts. These aren't just different degrees of the same thing — they represent fundamentally different kinds of events. The alert cat is doing something the other two are not: actively navigating the world through its senses and body working together.

The problem is that without a word for this property, researchers have been forced to grab at fragments — brain computation, subjective experience, bodily reflexes — each capturing a piece but none naming the whole. It is like trying to do biology without the concept of "life." The term "mindedness" names what was lost: the functional awareness of a whole animal moving through and responding to its environment. Aristotle saw it. Anyone watching an ant carry food can see it. It just got forgotten, and the cost has been enormous.

## Adept

Aristotle's scala naturae recognized a distinct ontological level between vegetative life and rational thought: the animal, defined by sensory-motor activity and a form of awareness irreducible to either mechanism or reason. Modern philosophy and science dismantled this category. Descartes split reality into res cogitans — mind as self-reflective consciousness — and res extensa — matter as mechanism — leaving no conceptual room for the minded-but-not-self-reflective animal. Biology subsequently merged flora and fauna into a single domain of living matter, while "mind" migrated exclusively to the human side of the ledger. The result was a bipolar ontology with animal mindedness falling through the gap.

Behaviorism compounded the erasure by stripping mentalistic vocabulary from scientific discourse entirely. Cognitivism restored "mind" but redefined it as skull-bound computation, severing it from the embodied, embedded, enacted, and extended character of animal life. Each subsequent framework — phenomenology, information processing, enactivism, 4E cognition — recovered a genuine fragment, but without a unifying category, the field recapitulated the parable of the blind men and the elephant.

The analogy to biology is precise: imagine attempting biological science without the concept of "life," forced to describe organisms as complex chemical systems with no term for the organizing principle. This is the current situation in psychology and cognitive science with respect to mindedness — the functional awareness and responsiveness of the whole animal constituted by the sensory-motor loop operating in three-dimensional behavioral space. The concept was present in Aristotle, observable in any animal navigating its environment, and its disappearance represents one of the most consequential blind spots in modern intellectual history.

## Magus

The collapse of Aristotle's tetrapartite ontology — inanimate, vegetative, animal-sensory, rational — into the Cartesian binary of res cogitans and res extensa constitutes a foundational category error whose consequences ramify through every subsequent attempt to theorize mind. Descartes's cogito located mindedness exclusively in self-reflective consciousness, while his mechanistic physics absorbed everything else into mathematizable extension. What fell through was precisely the middle term: the sensory-motor animal, whose navigational competence in three-dimensional behavioral space is neither mere mechanism nor propositional thought. The post-Cartesian settlement then biologized fauna alongside flora under a single rubric of living matter, completing the erasure of animal mindedness as a distinct ontological kind.

Behaviorism's eliminativism and cognitivism's computational internalism represent successive failures to recover the lost category. Behaviorism removed the mental predicate; cognitivism restored it only to sequester it inside the skull as symbol manipulation, reproducing the Cartesian severance of mind from body-in-world. The 4E correctives — embodied, embedded, enacted, extended — each retrieve a genuine dimension but remain partial recoveries, fragments mistaken for wholes in the absence of the integrating concept. The structural parallel to biology is exact and instructive: without "life" as a theoretical primitive, one could describe metabolic cascades and membrane dynamics indefinitely without ever naming what organizes them into an organism.

Mindedness — understood as the functional awareness-and-responsiveness of the whole animal constituted by the sensory-motor loop — names the property whose absence has rendered psychology and cognitive science disciplines without an adequate concept of their own subject matter. The qualitative discontinuity between a dead cat, an anesthetized cat, and an aroused cat navigating mid-air fall is not a gradient but an ontological boundary. Aristotle marked it; modernity erased it; the cost is a science of mind that cannot say what mind is.
