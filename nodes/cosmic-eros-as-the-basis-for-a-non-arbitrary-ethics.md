# Cosmic Eros as the Basis for a Non-Arbitrary Ethics

If value is real, so is its negation.

---

> Cosmo-erotic humanism argues that the universe's consistent movement toward greater complexity, intimacy, and emergence constitutes a real, non-arbitrary foundation for ethics — making it possible to name both genuine value and genuine evil.

Cosmo-erotic humanism proposes that the universe has first principles — deep generative values that have been operative across the entire arc of cosmic and biological evolution — and that these constitute a non-arbitrary foundation for ethics. One of the most important of these is what the book calls Eros: the movement of disparate elements coming together into higher-order wholes, generating novel capacity through emergence. This is not a metaphor — it is observable at every level of evolution, from cellular self-organization through the emergence of ecosystems to the formation of cultures. Stuart Kauffman called something like this the fifth force. The cosmological claim is that this movement has directionality — the universe has been selecting for increasing complexity, increasing intimacy, increasing entwinement — and that this selection process is itself evaluative. It is not random. The ethical implication is significant: human activities that move in the direction of greater intimacy, greater complexity, greater emergence are aligned with what reality is actually doing — and activities that move in the opposite direction are not merely suboptimal, they are anti-value. This is how the book can speak of evil without embarrassment: if value is real, its negation is real. One reason people resist making value ontologically real is precisely because it forces them to confront the reality of evil — it is more comfortable to live in a universe where nothing is truly bad because nothing is truly good. But a cosmology which cannot account for evil is not a serious cosmology. The existence of existential weaponry — technology specifically designed to end all life on Earth — is the most extreme contemporary instantiation of anti-value, and it can only be named as such if we are willing to say that value is real.

## Apprentice

There is a pattern visible across the entire history of the universe: simple things come together to form more complex things, and those complex things develop capacities that none of their parts had alone. Atoms form molecules, molecules form cells, cells form organisms, organisms form cultures. This isn't random — it has a direction. Cosmo-erotic humanism calls this driving force Eros: the movement of separate elements weaving together into richer, more intimate wholes.

The ethical claim follows directly from this observation. If the universe really does move toward greater complexity and connection, then human actions that support this movement are aligned with something deep and real — not just personal preference or cultural convention. And actions that destroy complexity, sever connection, or eliminate life altogether aren't just unfortunate choices. They work against what reality itself is doing.

This framework is willing to say something many modern philosophies avoid: that evil is real. Technologies designed to end all life on Earth represent the most extreme form of anti-value. But you can only call them that if you're willing to say value itself is real. Many people resist this precisely because it's uncomfortable — if good is real, then its opposite must be too. But a worldview that can't account for evil, the argument goes, isn't a serious worldview.

## Adept

Cosmo-erotic humanism, as articulated by Marc Gafni and Zak Stein, advances the claim that the universe operates according to discernible first principles — deep generative values that have been operative from the Big Bang through biological evolution to the emergence of culture. Chief among these is Eros, defined not as sexual desire but as the cosmological movement whereby disparate elements integrate into higher-order wholes, generating novel emergent capacities. Stuart Kauffman gestured toward something similar in describing a "fifth force" — a self-organizing tendency that exceeds what the known physical forces alone can explain. The claim is that this movement has directionality: the cosmos consistently selects for increasing complexity, increasing intimacy, increasing entwinement.

The philosophical stakes are considerable. If this directionality is real and not merely projected, then it constitutes a non-arbitrary ground for ethics. Human activities that participate in the movement toward greater emergence and intimacy are aligned with the evaluative grain of reality itself. Activities that reverse this movement — that fragment, simplify, or annihilate — are not merely suboptimal strategies but instances of genuine anti-value.

This is where the framework confronts what softer relativisms avoid: the ontological reality of evil. One reason value is so often relegated to the subjective is that making it real forces a confrontation with its negation. It is psychologically easier to inhabit a universe where nothing is truly bad because nothing is truly good. But cosmo-erotic humanism insists that a cosmology incapable of accounting for evil is not serious. Existential weaponry — technology engineered to extinguish all life — represents the most extreme contemporary instantiation of anti-value, and it can only be coherently named as such within a framework where value is ontologically real.

## Magus

Cosmo-erotic humanism advances a strong ontological thesis: that Eros — understood as the cosmological movement whereby disparate elements achieve integration into higher-order wholes with novel emergent properties — is not a metaphor imposed on nature but a first principle discernible across every register of cosmic and biological evolution. From autocatalytic molecular sets through endosymbiotic cellular organization to the stratified complexity of ecosystems and cultural formations, the pattern is consistent and directional. Kauffman's intuition of a "fifth force" — an organizational tendency irreducible to the four known physical forces — converges with this claim. The argument is that the universe's persistent selection for increasing complexity, intimacy, and entwinement is itself evaluative: reality is not axiologically neutral but generatively biased toward deepening integration.

The metaethical consequence is precise. If the directionality of cosmic evolution constitutes a non-arbitrary evaluative structure, then ethics is not grounded in convention, sentiment, or contractual agreement but in the deep grammar of existence itself. Actions that participate in the Erotic arc — generating greater complexity, deeper intimacy, more encompassing wholes — are aligned with what is ontologically operative. Actions that move counter to this arc instantiate anti-value in the strict sense: they negate what reality is selecting for.

This framework deliberately reclaims the category of evil as philosophically indispensable. The widespread reluctance to grant value ontological status is diagnosed as motivated in part by the discomfort of its corollary — that if value is real, its negation must be equally real and must be confronted as such. A cosmology that cannot account for evil is, on this view, structurally incomplete. Existential weaponry — technology whose telos is the annihilation of all emergent complexity on Earth — represents the limit case of anti-value, nameable only within a framework that has refused the comfort of axiological nihilism.
