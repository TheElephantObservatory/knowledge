# Physics Cannot Explain Why Anyone Does Physics

The universe writing equations about itself

---

> Any 'theory of everything' that explains only matter and energy is structurally incomplete — it cannot account for the existence of minds capable of formulating it. Whitehead's process metaphysics offers a recursive solution: experience goes all the way down, and consciousness is nature's elaboration of itself.

One of the deepest problems with standard physicalist accounts of the universe is that they fail to be sufficiently recursive — they don't include their own conditions of possibility. Physicists seek a 'theory of everything,' but such a theory, if it only describes the laws governing matter and energy, cannot explain how there came to be conscious biological organisms capable of seeking and finding such an explanation in the first place. The laws of physics, as we usually conceive them, give no indication that there should be life on any planet, let alone conscious intelligent life capable of writing down equations about those very laws. This isn't merely a gap in our current knowledge — it's a structural incompleteness in the framework itself. What we call a 'physical law' is already a metaphor: 'law' is a sociological concept projected onto nature. The equations are abstractions developed in human minds; they point to something real, but they are not the thing itself. A genuinely complete account of the universe must be recursive enough to explain not just what exists, but why there are entities capable of explaining it — and that requires going beyond physics into metaphysics, into asking what the nature of matter must be such that mind could emerge from it at all. Whitehead's concept of prehension is his attempt to answer exactly this question: a single metaphysical concept applicable from subatomic causation all the way to conscious perception. For Whitehead, conscious experience is not categorically different from physical causation — it is a further elaboration of it. Even in the oscillations of electromagnetic waveforms there is a tension between the actualized past and the possible future, a minimal form of aim he calls 'subjective aim.' Over billions of years, these vibrational patterns form harmonies that complexify, driven by an aim at aesthetic satisfaction. The universe, on this account, is engaged in producing more and more intense forms of beautiful experience. Life doesn't just want to survive; it wants to live better. And science itself — our curious drive to understand ourselves and our universe — is as profound an expression of purpose as one could imagine. This is highly speculative metaphysics, but it is the minimal story required to explain not just that life emerged, but that something like us, capable of asking why, emerged at all.

## Apprentice

Physics aims to explain everything in the universe, but there's a deep problem: the laws of physics, as usually understood, say nothing about why conscious beings like us should exist at all. The equations describe how matter and energy behave, but they can't explain how matter eventually organized itself into creatures capable of writing those equations. This isn't just something we haven't figured out yet — it's a blind spot built into the framework itself.

The philosopher Alfred North Whitehead proposed a radical solution. Instead of treating mind and matter as fundamentally different things, he suggested that even the simplest physical events contain a faint echo of what we call experience — a basic "feeling" of the past and a pull toward what comes next. He called this "prehension." Over billions of years, these tiny flickers of experience built on each other, growing more complex, eventually producing life, awareness, and the drive to understand.

On this view, the universe isn't a dead machine that accidentally produced consciousness. It's more like a vast creative process aiming at richer and more beautiful forms of experience. Science itself — our restless curiosity about how things work — is one of the most remarkable expressions of that cosmic drive. This is speculative philosophy, but it may be the minimum story needed to explain not just that the universe exists, but that beings capable of asking why exist within it.

## Adept

Standard physicalism faces a recursion problem: a theory of everything couched solely in terms of matter and energy cannot account for its own conditions of possibility. The laws of physics, however precisely formulated, contain no indication that conscious organisms capable of formulating those laws should arise. This is not an empirical gap awaiting future data but a structural incompleteness in the explanatory framework. Even the term "physical law" is already a metaphor — a sociological concept projected onto regularities in nature. The equations are human abstractions pointing at something real, but they are not the thing itself.

Whitehead's process philosophy addresses this incompleteness head-on through the concept of prehension — a single metaphysical category spanning subatomic causation and conscious perception. For Whitehead, every actual occasion involves a "feeling" of antecedent data and a subjective aim toward novel realization. Conscious experience is not ontologically alien to physical process; it is its most elaborate expression. Even electromagnetic oscillations exhibit a minimal tension between actualized past and possible future, a proto-experiential directedness that, over evolutionary time, complexifies into the rich interiority of sentient life.

The result is a genuinely recursive cosmology: the universe is understood as a creative advance toward increasingly intense forms of aesthetic satisfaction, and the emergence of scientific inquiry — beings asking why anything exists at all — is itself among the most profound expressions of that advance. This is admittedly speculative metaphysics, but it represents the minimal explanatory commitment required to account not merely for the existence of matter, but for the existence of minds capable of interrogating matter's nature.

## Magus

The structural incompleteness of physicalist ontology lies not in any particular empirical lacuna but in its failure of recursion: an account of reality framed exclusively in terms of efficient causation among extensional entities cannot generate the conditions under which such an account becomes formulable. Physical laws, so called, are already metaphorical — the juridical concept of "law" projected onto observed regularities, while the equations encoding them are abstractions developed within conscious experience and irreducible to the phenomena they describe. A theory of everything that omits the theorizer is not merely incomplete but self-undermining.

Whitehead's speculative scheme answers this challenge through prehension as a transcategorical primitive: the conformal feeling by which each actual occasion inherits its datum from the settled past while orienting itself via subjective aim toward determinate satisfaction. Crucially, this structure applies univocally from quantum-level occasions through biological organisms to reflective consciousness — no ontological discontinuity is introduced. What we call physical causation and what we call experience are distinguished not in kind but in degree of integrative complexity. Even the periodic oscillations of electromagnetic fields instantiate a minimal polarity between the givenness of the actual and the lure of the possible, a proto-aesthetic tension that, through evolutionary elaboration, yields the rich qualitative interiority characteristic of higher-grade organisms.

The cosmological upshot is a universe whose creative advance is intrinsically teleological — aimed at the intensification of aesthetic experience. The emergence of scientific inquiry, far from being an epiphenomenal accident in a purposeless cosmos, stands as one of the most concentrated expressions of the universe's own drive toward self-comprehension. This is maximal metaphysical speculation, yet it constitutes the minimal recursive framework capable of explaining not only that entities exist but that entities capable of demanding explanations exist among them.
