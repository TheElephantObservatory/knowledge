# How Propositional Language Created a New Plane of Human Existence

To speak is to be called to account

---

> Human language didn't just make us smarter — it created a fundamentally new plane of existence. Once sentences can be questioned and must be justified, entire systems of law, religion, science, and culture emerge to govern what counts as legitimate.

One of the most generative conceptual moves in UTOK is the claim that propositional language does not merely enhance cognition — it creates an entirely new plane of existence, ontologically discontinuous from the minded animal world that preceded it. Henriques calls this the culture-person plane.

The transition begins with what Michael Tomasello identified as the human capacity for shared intentionality — the ability to synchronize attention and intention with others in ways that exceed what other great apes can do. From this foundation, symbolic tagging of the world becomes possible, and eventually a tipping point is reached: the emergence of full propositional syntax. A proposition is not merely a label or a signal — it is a claim that can be questioned, negated, and evaluated for truth. The moment you can form a sentence, you can be asked: how do you know that? Are you sure? Could you be wrong?

This question-and-answer dynamic is the engine of an entirely new kind of complexity. Once propositions can be questioned, they must be justified. Once justification is required, networks of legitimizing structures begin to form — what Henriques calls justification systems, or Capital-C Culture. To be a person is not merely to have a mind or a self — it is to be accountable on the social stage, able to justify your actions to others using shared propositional structures. Law, religion, science, money, political ideology — all are justification systems: networks of propositions that legitimate certain actions and delegitimate others.

This is why human psychology cannot be understood purely through the lens of neuroscience or animal behavior. The human mind is not just a more complex animal mind — it is a mind recruited into a justification system, shaped by it, and made accountable to it. The science of human psychology therefore necessarily bridges into the social sciences, because persons are constituted in part by the cultural justification structures they are socialized into.

## Apprentice

Most people assume human beings are simply cleverer animals — that language gave us a better toolkit but didn't change what we fundamentally are. Gregg Henriques challenges this assumption directly. He argues that when humans developed the ability to form full sentences — claims that can be questioned, denied, or defended — something genuinely new came into existence. Not just smarter minds, but a whole new layer of reality.

Think about what happens the moment someone can say a sentence. Someone else can ask: "How do you know that?" or "Why should I believe you?" That simple exchange changes everything. Suddenly, people need reasons. They need to justify what they say and do. Over time, entire systems grow up around this need — legal codes, religious doctrines, scientific methods, economic rules. These are all, at bottom, networks of claims that tell communities what counts as acceptable and what doesn't.

This is why understanding human psychology requires more than studying the brain or watching animal behavior. A person isn't just a creature with a complex mind — a person is someone who lives inside these justification systems, shaped by them from childhood, and held accountable by them every day. Human psychology, in this view, necessarily reaches into sociology, law, philosophy, and culture.

## Adept

Gregg Henriques's Unified Theory of Knowledge (UTOK) makes a strong ontological claim: propositional language does not merely augment animal cognition — it inaugurates a genuinely new plane of existence, the culture-person plane. Building on Michael Tomasello's work on shared intentionality, Henriques traces a developmental arc from joint attention and symbolic tagging to the emergence of full propositional syntax. The critical threshold is the proposition itself — a structure that can be negated, questioned, and evaluated for truth value. Once propositions exist, the question "how do you know?" becomes possible, and with it, the demand for justification.

This demand is generative in a precise sense: it produces justification systems, which Henriques designates as Capital-C Culture. Law, religion, science, money, and political ideology are all networks of propositions that function to legitimate and delegitimate actions within social groups. To be a "person" in the UTOK framework is not merely to possess a self-conscious mind — it is to be constituted as an accountable agent on a social stage, where one's claims and actions must be defended through shared propositional structures.

The implications for psychology are far-reaching. If persons are partly constituted by the justification systems they inhabit, then human psychology cannot be reduced to neuroscience or comparative animal behavior. The minded animal and the culture-person occupy ontologically discontinuous planes. A complete science of human behavior must therefore bridge into the social sciences, treating cultural justification structures not as contextual background but as constitutive features of what it means to be human.

## Magus

Henriques's UTOK advances a thesis of ontological discontinuity: the emergence of propositional language does not represent a quantitative enhancement of primate cognition but the instantiation of a novel plane of existence — the culture-person plane — irreducible to the behavioral investments of minded animals. The genealogy runs through Tomasello's shared intentionality thesis, but the decisive move is identifying the proposition as the unit of ontological rupture. A proposition is not a signal or an indexical tag; it is a truth-evaluable structure that invites negation, interrogation, and demand for warrant. The moment "p" can be met with "why p?" a recursive justificatory dynamic is unleashed that no enhancement of associative learning could produce.

What crystallizes from this dynamic are justification systems — Capital-C Culture in Henriques's nomenclature. These are not epiphenomenal overlays on biological drives but constitutive architectures of personhood. Legal codes, theological doctrines, scientific paradigms, monetary conventions, and ideological formations all function as legitimation networks: interconnected propositional structures that distribute normative force across social fields. Personhood, on this account, is an ontological category distinct from selfhood or minded agency — it designates the condition of being recursively accountable within such networks.

The methodological consequence is severe. If the culture-person plane is ontologically discontinuous from the minded-animal plane, then no reductive program — whether neuroscientific, evolutionary-psychological, or behaviorist — can furnish a complete account of human psychology. The discipline necessarily extends into jurisprudence, sociology of knowledge, and cultural theory, not as interdisciplinary courtesy but as structural requirement. Henriques thus positions justification systems as the missing joint that articulates the natural and social sciences within a unified metatheoretical framework.
