# Assembly Theory Grounds Complexity in Physical Structure, Not Observer Labels

The world does not care how you encode it.

---

> Most measures of complexity are secretly observer-dependent — they change based on how you choose to describe a system. Assembly theory attempts to fix this by grounding complexity in physical structure and molecular abundance, making it possible to distinguish life's products from chance without importing human representational conventions.

There is a deep and underappreciated problem with how we define and measure complexity, and it has serious consequences for how we think about life detection. Most complexity measures — and there are dozens of them — are label-dependent. They depend on how you choose to encode your description of the system. They are observer-relative in a way that makes them unsuitable as physical quantities. Assembly theory attempts to solve this by grounding complexity in measurement: the assembly index is not assigned by an observer choosing a labeling scheme, but derived from the physical structure of the object itself, specifically from the minimum number of recursive construction steps required to produce it from its own parts. This is why copy number is not an afterthought but a constitutive part of the theory. A single complex object could in principle be produced by chance or by deliberate engineering. But a complex object appearing in high abundance — in the quantities required to even register in a mass spectrometer, which requires roughly ten thousand copies of a molecule — cannot be explained without an evolutionary process that keeps constructing it. This is also why randomness, as a concept, deserves scrutiny. Randomness is a mathematical structure, a product of computation theory. What actually happens in quantum mechanics is not randomness but indeterminacy — something quite different. The conflation of the two is one of many places where human cognitive architecture gets silently imported into descriptions of physical reality. Assembly theory is an attempt to build a theory of complexity that is anchored in the physical world rather than in the representational conventions of the observer.

## Apprentice

When scientists try to measure how complex something is, they run into a hidden problem: the answer usually depends on how they choose to describe the thing in the first place. Change your description scheme and you change the complexity score. This makes most complexity measures more like opinions than physical facts.

Assembly theory takes a different approach. Instead of asking how hard something is to describe, it asks how many construction steps are needed to build an object from its own basic parts. This "assembly index" comes from the object's physical structure, not from an observer's choices. But there's a crucial second ingredient: how many copies of that object exist. A single complex molecule might appear by sheer luck. But thousands of identical copies of a complex molecule — the kind of abundance you'd need just to detect it with laboratory instruments — point strongly toward some ongoing process that keeps producing it. That process, the theory argues, is evolution.

This framework also challenges casual use of the word "randomness." What happens in quantum mechanics is better described as indeterminacy — the outcome isn't determined in advance — which is different from the mathematical concept of randomness developed in computation theory. Confusing the two is one example of how human thinking habits get quietly baked into descriptions of nature. Assembly theory is an effort to measure complexity in a way that belongs to the physical world rather than to the conventions humans use to talk about it.

## Adept

A persistent and largely unacknowledged problem afflicts complexity science: the vast majority of complexity measures are label-dependent. They shift with the encoding scheme, the alphabet chosen, the level of coarse-graining applied. This makes them observer-relative quantities masquerading as objective descriptors — a serious liability when the goal is something like life detection, where the stakes demand physical grounding rather than representational convenience.

Assembly theory, developed by Lee Cronin and collaborators, proposes a resolution by anchoring complexity in measurement rather than description. The assembly index of an object is defined as the minimum number of recursive joining operations needed to construct it from its own building blocks. Crucially, this is not a property assigned through an external labeling choice but one derived from the object's internal structure. The theory then introduces copy number as a constitutive — not supplementary — variable. A single molecule with a high assembly index is ambiguous: it could be a product of chance or deliberate synthesis. But detecting that same molecule at abundances of ten thousand or more copies (the threshold for mass spectrometric detection) effectively rules out stochastic production and points toward selection — an evolutionary process capable of repeatedly generating the same complex structure.

The framework also invites a reexamination of randomness itself. In computation theory, randomness is a well-defined mathematical structure. In quantum mechanics, the relevant concept is indeterminacy — the absence of a determined outcome prior to measurement — which is conceptually distinct. The habitual conflation of these two reflects a broader tendency to import features of human cognitive architecture into physical theory without acknowledgment. Assembly theory represents a deliberate attempt to construct a complexity measure that resists this importation, remaining answerable to physical reality rather than to the conventions through which observers represent it.

## Magus

The proliferation of complexity measures across information theory, algorithmic complexity, and statistical mechanics masks a foundational deficiency: nearly all such measures are label-dependent, varying under changes of encoding, alphabet, or coarse-graining protocol. They are, in the strict sense, observer-relative quantities — a fact that renders them inadequate as candidates for physical observables, particularly in contexts like prebiotic chemistry and astrobiology where one seeks to distinguish the products of selection from those of stochastic assembly without presupposing a privileged descriptive framework.

Assembly theory addresses this by defining complexity operationally through the assembly index: the shortest pathway of recursive joining operations required to construct a given object from its elementary subunits, where the pathway is determined by the object's own compositional structure rather than by an externally imposed representation. The theory's insistence on copy number as constitutive rather than ancillary is not a pragmatic concession but a theoretical commitment. A solitary high-index molecule is epistemically underdetermined — consistent with both chance concatenation and directed synthesis. But the same molecule at copy numbers on the order of 10⁴ (the detection threshold of standard mass spectrometry) demands explanation in terms of a generative process with memory: selection operating over iterated construction. Abundance becomes the empirical signature that disambiguates complexity-from-process from complexity-from-accident.

This reorientation also exposes a conceptual conflation embedded in standard discourse: the identification of quantum indeterminacy with mathematical randomness. The latter is a product of computation theory, possessing precise formal properties; the former denotes the absence of determinate values prior to measurement, a fundamentally different ontological claim. The slippage between them exemplifies how features of human cognitive and representational architecture are silently projected onto physical descriptions. Assembly theory is, at its core, an attempt to construct a measure of complexity that is answerable to the causal structure of the physical world rather than to the representational habits of its observers.
