# Cybernetic Systems Already Outpace Human Understanding and Awareness

The wire was always a fiction.

---

> We already live inside cybernetic control systems that operate far beyond human perceptual bandwidth, yet our cultural discourse pretends otherwise. The urgent task is not to fear a future AI apocalypse but to honestly reckon with the feedback loops already running the show.

We are living in a cybernetic reality, and a significant portion of our cultural and political discourse is organized around a pleasant fiction that we are not. The word cybernetics deserves rehabilitation. It was coined to describe control systems — sense the world, make a prediction, take an action, observe the result, update, repeat. The OODA loop. The origins of modern computing were not spreadsheets and websites; they were anti-aircraft targeting systems, naval gun stabilizers, the entire project of building machines that could close feedback loops faster and more accurately than humans. That is what computing has always been about at its core. The fly-by-wire fighter jet is the paradigm case: the F-22 is aerodynamically unstable by design, and no human pilot could keep it in the air for five seconds without the flight computer. We call it fly-by-wire to give the pilot a narrative of control, but the wire is a fiction. The plane is flying itself; the human is providing high-level intent. This dynamic — where humans provide goals and machines execute the control loops — is now propagating into information systems, economic systems, and social systems at a scale and speed that exceeds human perceptual bandwidth. We process ten bits per second of conscious experience. These systems operate at orders of magnitude beyond that. The danger is not some future AGI apocalypse scenario; the danger is the more mundane and immediate one that cybernetic systems are already running ahead of human understanding, and we are collectively pretending otherwise. The only intellectually honest and morally responsible response is to make as many people as possible genuinely aware of this — not through panic, not through culture war, but through elevating the discourse to where we are actually talking about the real dynamics at play.

## Apprentice

Modern computing didn't begin with spreadsheets or websites. It began with machines designed to aim guns and shoot down planes — systems that could sense the world, predict what would happen next, act, and adjust faster than any human. That cycle of sensing, predicting, acting, and updating is what "cybernetics" originally meant, and it remains the core logic of the machines that now shape our lives.

Today, this same logic runs through the information systems, financial markets, and social platforms we depend on every day. These systems close feedback loops at speeds no human mind can follow — we consciously process roughly ten bits per second, while the machines operate at orders of magnitude beyond that. The F-22 fighter jet offers a vivid example: it is deliberately designed to be unflyable by a human alone. The pilot sets intentions; the computer keeps the plane in the air. We call it "fly-by-wire" to preserve the comforting story that a person is in charge.

The real danger isn't some dramatic future scenario where artificial intelligence turns against us. The danger is quieter and already here: cybernetic systems are outrunning human understanding right now, and most public conversation hasn't caught up. The responsible path forward is to help as many people as possible see this clearly — not through fear, but through honest, elevated conversation about how these dynamics actually work.

## Adept

The word "cybernetics" — from Wiener's original coinage describing control systems built on feedback loops (sense, predict, act, observe, update) — deserves serious rehabilitation. The genealogy of modern computing is not the spreadsheet or the browser; it is the anti-aircraft predictor, the naval gun stabilizer, the entire wartime project of closing feedback loops faster and more accurately than human operators could manage. This origin story is not incidental. It reveals the persistent core logic of computation: not data storage or communication, but real-time adaptive control.

The F-22 Raptor serves as the paradigm case. It is aerodynamically unstable by design — no human pilot could maintain controlled flight for five seconds without the flight computer continuously correcting thousands of variables per second. "Fly-by-wire" is a narrative device that preserves the fiction of human control; in practice, the human provides high-level intent while the machine executes the control loop. This architecture — human goal-setting layered atop machine-speed cybernetic execution — is now propagating into information ecosystems, financial markets, algorithmic governance, and social platforms at scales that exceed human perceptual bandwidth. Conscious human cognition processes approximately ten bits per second; these systems operate orders of magnitude beyond that threshold.

The salient risk is therefore not a speculative AGI catastrophe but something more immediate and more banal: cybernetic systems are already running ahead of collective human comprehension, and a significant portion of cultural and political discourse is organized around the pleasant fiction that they are not. The intellectually honest response is not panic or culture-war framing but a deliberate effort to raise public literacy about the feedback-loop dynamics that are already shaping outcomes at every level of society.

## Magus

The historiography of computation suffers from a persistent misattribution of origins. The foundational artifacts are not ledgers or communication networks but Wiener's anti-aircraft predictors, fire-control systems, and servomechanisms — machines engineered to close feedback loops at speeds exceeding human neuromuscular latency. Cybernetics, in its original Wienerian sense — the study of control and communication in the animal and the machine — names the operative logic that has never ceased to be computation's animating core, however thoroughly it has been obscured by consumer-facing metaphors of "tools" and "platforms."

The fly-by-wire fighter constitutes the limit case that clarifies the general principle. The F-22's relaxed static stability means the airframe is, absent continuous computational correction, divergent within fractions of a second. The pilot provides intentional vectors; the flight computer executes a high-frequency OODA loop that no biological nervous system could sustain. The phrase "fly-by-wire" functions as what might be called a control narrative — a semiotic layer that preserves the phenomenology of agency while the actual cybernetic closure occurs elsewhere. This architecture is now the template, not the exception: algorithmic market-making, recommendation engines, autonomous logistics, and platform governance all instantiate the same pattern of human intent parsed and executed by machine-speed feedback systems operating well beyond the approximately ten bits per second of conscious human bandwidth.

What follows is that the most consequential epistemic failure of the present moment is not ignorance of some future superintelligence risk but the collective maintenance of a pleasant fiction — that human deliberative agency remains the primary locus of control in systems already governed by cybernetic dynamics. Dismantling this fiction requires neither alarmism nor partisan mobilization but a disciplined elevation of discourse toward the actual feedback architectures that are, in the most literal sense, already steering.
