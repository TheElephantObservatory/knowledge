# How Modernity Discarded Value by Confusing It with Causation

We learned to do everything except ask why.

---

> Modernity confused its mastery of causation with a complete account of knowledge, discarding the category of value along with religious authority. The result is a civilization that can do almost anything but has lost the capacity to ask whether it should — and this gap is now existential.

There is a profound distinction between what we can do and what we should do — and modernity has largely collapsed this distinction by treating causation as the only valid basis for knowledge. When the Enlightenment revealed that religious institutions had poor causal knowledge about the natural world, we made a catastrophic category error: we discarded not just their causal claims, but the entire apparatus of value, meaning, and community that religion carried. We substituted abstract value — financial instruments, utilitarian calculus, effective altruism's 'greatest good for the greatest number' — for embodied, relational value. But value is never abstract. It always lands in the body. It always lands in life. No amount of understanding causation will ever tell you anything about the nature of good choice, because choice must be understood in terms of choice itself. The result of this substitution is that we now live in a civilization that is extraordinarily capable — we can do almost anything — but has lost its capacity to ask whether it should. This is not merely a philosophical problem. When we face civilizational-scale risks that require multi-generational responses, when the complexity exceeds what any election cycle or market mechanism can process, the absence of a coherent framework for 'what should we do' is not an academic gap — it is an existential one. The meaning crisis is, at its root, the crisis of having discarded the category of value itself.

## Apprentice

When the scientific revolution showed that religious institutions were often wrong about how the natural world works, something important was lost in the correction. We didn't just reject bad explanations about nature — we threw out the entire framework that helped people navigate questions of meaning, value, and what constitutes a good life. We replaced embodied, lived values with abstract substitutes: money, utilitarian calculations, optimization metrics.

But knowing how things work never tells you what you should do. Causation and value are fundamentally different categories. You can understand every mechanism in the world and still have no basis for making a wise choice, because choice has to be understood on its own terms — through lived experience, relationships, and a felt sense of what matters.

This isn't just a philosophical puzzle. We now face problems — climate change, artificial intelligence, ecological collapse — that require coordinated responses across generations. These challenges exceed what elections or markets can handle. Without a coherent way to ask "what should we do," as opposed to "what can we do," the meaning crisis isn't merely cultural malaise. It is a civilizational vulnerability at the worst possible moment.

## Adept

This perspective identifies a catastrophic category error at the heart of modernity: the collapse of the distinction between causation and value. When Enlightenment rationality exposed the poor causal models embedded in religious institutions, the corrective overreached. Rather than simply updating our causal knowledge, we discarded the entire normative and relational apparatus that religion had carried — frameworks for meaning, community, embodied practice, and the discernment of good choice. The baby went out with the bathwater on a civilizational scale.

The substitution that followed replaced embodied, relational value with abstraction: financial instruments, utilitarian calculus, effective altruism's aggregative logic. But value, the argument holds, is irreducibly embodied — it lands in the body, in life, in the particular. No amount of causal understanding generates normative knowledge, because choice must be understood in terms of choice itself. This is not an anti-science claim; it is a claim about the boundaries of what causal epistemology can deliver.

The consequences are now existential rather than merely philosophical. Civilizational-scale risks — climate destabilization, AI alignment, ecological overshoot — demand multi-generational, coherent normative responses. Yet our dominant institutions (markets, electoral cycles, technocratic governance) are optimized for causal manipulation, not value discernment. The meaning crisis, on this reading, is not a symptom of insufficient information or inadequate technology. It is the structural consequence of having collapsed the category of value into the category of causation, leaving us extraordinarily capable but normatively incoherent at precisely the moment coherence matters most.

## Magus

The core claim here is that modernity's epistemic triumph — the establishment of causal-mechanistic explanation as the gold standard of knowledge — entailed a ruinous conflation: the reduction of all valid knowledge to causal knowledge, thereby eliminating value as an autonomous epistemic category. The Enlightenment's justified critique of religious institutions' causal inadequacy metastasized into the wholesale rejection of the normative, communal, and phenomenological functions those institutions carried. What followed was not secularization but a substitution regime: embodied, relational value was displaced by abstract proxies — capital, utilitarian aggregation, consequentialist optimization — each presupposing that value can be rendered commensurable and computed rather than discerned and inhabited.

This analysis insists on an irreducible gap between causation and normativity. Choice, properly understood, is not derivable from causal models; it must be apprehended through the category of choice itself. Value always instantiates in the particular — in bodies, in relationships, in the texture of lived experience. The attempt to abstract it necessarily strips it of its constitutive features. This echoes but extends critiques from Heidegger's technological enframing, MacIntyre's diagnosis of emotivism, and Iain McGilchrist's hemispheric analysis, while grounding the problem in a sharper epistemological claim about category boundaries.

The existential stakes become legible against this backdrop. Multi-generational, civilizational-scale coordination problems — precisely the challenges now confronting us — require normative coherence that no market signal, electoral mechanism, or technocratic optimization can supply. These institutions process causal complexity; they are structurally mute on questions of the good. The meaning crisis, then, is not a failure of psychology or culture but the predictable consequence of a civilization that systematically dismantled its capacity for value discernment while exponentially amplifying its causal power. The asymmetry is the danger.
