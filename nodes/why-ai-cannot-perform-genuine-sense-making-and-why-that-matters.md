# Why AI Cannot Perform Genuine Sense-Making, and Why That Matters

The view from nowhere, made into infrastructure.

---

> AI cannot achieve genuine sense-making because it lacks embodied experience — and the real danger is not that it fails, but that treating it as an authority destroys the distributed human coordination that actually works.

The AI debate is being conducted almost entirely on the wrong terms. The question is not whether AI systems will become smarter or more capable. The question is whether they can ever be genuine participants in sense-making — and the answer, from first principles, is no. Sense-making is not computation over data. It is the activity of an embodied, embedded system drawing on its own history of experience within a context to generate constraints that allow it to distinguish signal from noise, cause from correlation, and relevant from irrelevant. AI systems do not have this architecture. They cannot hallucinate less, not because of engineering failures that will eventually be solved, but because hallucination is the structural consequence of having no experiential ground to curb confabulation. Claiming that AI will achieve human-level sense-making is not a bold technological prediction — it is closer to claiming faster-than-light travel. But there is a deeper problem beyond the technical one. If an AI system is a worse decision-maker than humans, we should not use it. If it is genuinely better, then we must do what it says — and that is authoritarianism. It is the end of free agency, of coordination through mutual experience, of the social fabric that is constituted by people figuring things out together in context. The alternative — a civilization in which every person is honored for the expertise that emerges from their place, heard within that place, and coordinating with those around them on the basis of direct experience — is not a utopia. It is simply what we are designed to do. AI, as currently conceived and deployed, is the view from nowhere made into infrastructure, and it will systematically poison the cognitive environment the way lead poisoning poisons a body.

## Apprentice

The mainstream AI debate focuses on whether machines will get smarter, but this framing misses the point entirely. The real question is whether AI can ever truly make sense of the world the way people do — and the argument here is that it fundamentally cannot. Sense-making is not just processing information. It requires a living being with a body, a history, and a place in the world. Without that grounding, AI systems have no way to tell what matters from what doesn't. Their tendency to "hallucinate" — to confidently produce nonsense — is not a bug that engineers will eventually fix. It is a built-in consequence of having no real experience to anchor their outputs.

But the problem goes even deeper than bad answers. If AI is worse than humans at making decisions, there is no reason to rely on it. If it is somehow better, then we face a chilling outcome: we must obey it, surrendering our own judgment and agency. Either way, we lose. What gets displaced is the messy, essential process of people figuring things out together based on what they actually know and have lived through.

The alternative vision is not some impossible dream. It is a world where each person's hard-won expertise — rooted in their specific place and experience — is respected and woven into collective decision-making. AI, deployed as a universal authority detached from any lived context, threatens to poison this process the way a toxin poisons a body: slowly, systemically, and from the inside out.

## Adept

This argument reframes the entire AI discourse by shifting from capability questions to architectural ones. The claim is not that AI systems lack sufficient data or compute, but that they are structurally incapable of sense-making. Sense-making, properly understood, is the activity of an embodied, embedded agent drawing on its own experiential history within a specific context to generate the constraints necessary for distinguishing signal from noise, cause from correlation, relevance from irrelevance. AI systems lack this architecture entirely. Hallucination is therefore not an engineering problem awaiting a solution — it is the inevitable structural consequence of operating without experiential ground. Comparing the expectation that AI will achieve human-level sense-making to expecting faster-than-light travel is not rhetorical excess; it reflects a category error in the discourse.

The deeper critique is political and existential. A decision-making tool that is worse than humans is useless. One that is genuinely better than humans demands obedience — and obedience to an unembodied oracle is authoritarianism by another name. It dissolves free agency, erodes coordination through mutual experience, and dismantles the social fabric constituted by people reasoning together in context. This is not a slippery-slope argument but a structural analysis: the logic of optimization-as-authority has no stable resting point short of total deference.

The constructive counterpart envisions a civilization organized around distributed, place-based expertise — where each person's knowledge, earned through their particular embeddedness, is honored and integrated through direct coordination. AI as currently conceived represents the "view from nowhere" made into infrastructure. Deployed at civilizational scale, it systematically degrades the cognitive environment, functioning as an epistemic toxin analogous to environmental lead — invisible, cumulative, and corrosive to the very capacities it claims to augment.

## Magus

The central provocation here is that the AI debate suffers from a fundamental category error: it treats sense-making as a computational problem admitting of engineering solutions, when sense-making is constitutively the activity of an embodied, historically situated agent generating contextual constraints — constraints that render the distinction between signal and noise, cause and correlation, relevance and irrelevance possible in the first place. Without experiential embeddedness, there is no ground from which to curb confabulation. Hallucination is thus not a deficiency relative to some achievable standard but the structural signature of a system that lacks the enactive architecture required for epistemic traction. The analogy to faster-than-light travel is precise: what is being asked for violates the constitutive conditions of the phenomenon, not merely its current implementation.

The political argument is equally rigorous in its logic. The deployment of AI as decision-authority generates an inescapable dilemma: inferior performance renders it unjustifiable, while superior performance demands deference — and deference to an agent without situatedness, stakes, or accountability is authoritarianism stripped of even the legitimating fiction of shared experience. What is foreclosed is not merely individual autonomy but the entire ecology of distributed cognition through which human communities coordinate: the social epistemology constituted by persons reasoning from within their particular embeddedness toward mutual intelligibility.

The normative horizon implied is not nostalgic but architectonic. A civilization that honors place-based expertise and coordinates through direct experiential encounter is not utopian aspiration but a description of the cognitive niche Homo sapiens actually occupies. AI-as-infrastructure instantiates Nagel's "view from nowhere" at civilizational scale — an epistemic toxin operating with the insidious cumulative logic of lead poisoning, degrading precisely those capacities for contextual judgment and participatory sense-making upon which any viable collective intelligence depends.
