# Science as Inter-Subjective Practice, Not Pure Objectivity

No map without a mapmaker.

---

> Science is not raw objectivity but a specialized cultural practice designed to filter out subjectivity — and this very filtering creates a blind spot, leaving no place on the map for the mapmaker who draws it.

A subtle but important thread running through this conversation concerns the relationship between the three vectors of knowing — subjective phenomenological experience, inter-subjective cultural knowledge, and objective scientific knowledge — and the question of what science actually is and where it fits within that larger map.

Henriques argues that modern empirical natural science is not simply 'objective knowledge' in the colloquial sense. It is a very specific kind of inter-subjective practice — one that has developed particular rules for factoring out subjective qualities and values in order to produce claims that can be verified across observers. Science emerges out of culture: it is a specialized form of inter-subjective knowledge production that has developed methods for tracking what Rutt calls the 'inter-objective' — the features of reality that independent observers can converge on regardless of their particular perspectives.

But this very strength is also a structural blind spot. By systematically factoring out the subjective, science creates a map of the world that has no place for the mapmaker. The unique idiographic perspective of each knowing subject — what Henriques calls the 'I' in the iquad coin — is not merely a source of error to be eliminated; it is the epistemic portal through which all knowledge, including scientific knowledge, is accessed. There is no view from nowhere. Every scientific claim is made by a particular subject, embedded in a particular cultural justification system, using instruments and methods developed within a particular historical tradition.

This does not undermine science — it contextualizes it. The unified theory of knowledge is not an attempt to subordinate science to subjectivity or culture, but to map the relationships between all three vectors accurately. The enlightenment gap, in this framing, is precisely the failure to have done this mapping: we have extraordinarily powerful scientific knowledge of the material and biological world, but no coherent shared framework for understanding how that knowledge relates to the conscious subjects who produce it and the cultural systems within which it is embedded.

## Apprentice

Science is often treated as the gold standard of objective knowledge — the way we get past personal biases and cultural assumptions to see reality as it truly is. But Gregg Henriques argues that science is actually a very specific cultural invention: a set of shared rules that communities of researchers developed over centuries to produce claims that different observers can agree on. It didn't drop from the sky. It grew out of human culture.

The problem is that in order to work so well, science deliberately strips out the personal and subjective. It asks: what can anyone verify, regardless of who they are or what they feel? That's enormously powerful, but it also means science has no built-in way to account for the conscious person doing the knowing. Every measurement is taken by someone. Every theory is proposed within a particular tradition. The knowing subject isn't a flaw in the system — it's the doorway through which all knowledge enters.

This insight doesn't weaken science. It puts it in its proper place on a larger map. The real gap in modern thought, Henriques suggests, is that we have spectacular scientific knowledge of the physical world but no shared framework for understanding how that knowledge connects to the minds that produce it and the cultures that sustain it.

## Adept

Henriques draws a critical distinction between three vectors of knowing: subjective phenomenological experience, inter-subjective cultural knowledge, and objective scientific knowledge. His key move is to reposition modern empirical science not as pure objectivity but as a highly refined inter-subjective practice — one that has evolved specific methodological rules for factoring out subjective qualities in order to produce claims verifiable across independent observers. Science tracks what might be called the inter-objective: features of reality that converge regardless of the observer's particular perspective. But it remains, at root, a cultural institution with a history, norms, and justification structures.

This reframing exposes a structural blind spot. By systematically excluding the subjective, science generates a map of the world with no place for the mapmaker. The idiographic perspective of each knowing subject — what Henriques terms the 'I' in the iQuad Coin — is not merely noise to be filtered out. It is the epistemic portal through which all knowledge, scientific knowledge included, is accessed. There is no view from nowhere; every empirical claim is made by a situated subject operating within a particular cultural justification system.

The unified theory of knowledge does not subordinate science to subjectivity or relativize it into one opinion among many. It contextualizes science within a coherent meta-framework that maps the relationships between subjective experience, cultural knowledge systems, and scientific inquiry. The "Enlightenment Gap," in this framing, names precisely the absence of such a map: extraordinary empirical power coupled with no shared understanding of how that power relates to the conscious subjects who wield it and the cultural architectures that make it possible.

## Magus

Henriques's analysis performs a decisive epistemic repositioning: modern empirical natural science is not the direct apprehension of objective reality but a specialized inter-subjective practice that has developed rigorous protocols for factoring out subjective qualities — phenomenological texture, evaluative orientation, idiographic particularity — in order to produce convergent claims across independent observers. Science tracks the inter-objective, but it does so from within culture, not from outside it. Its methods, instruments, and justification criteria are themselves products of historically situated communities of inquiry operating under particular normative commitments about what counts as evidence, replication, and explanation.

The structural consequence is a systematic occlusion of the epistemic subject. By design, science eliminates the first-person perspective as a variable, producing a cartography of the world that contains no representation of the cartographer. Yet the 'I' of the iQuad Coin — the irreducible idiographic locus of experience — is not an artifact to be controlled away. It is the transcendental condition of all empirical access. Every observation report presupposes a situated observer; every theoretical framework is proposed, evaluated, and revised by conscious agents embedded in justification systems that are themselves culturally constituted. The view from nowhere is a regulative ideal, not an achievable epistemic position.

The Enlightenment Gap names the failure to integrate these vectors into a coherent meta-epistemological framework. The unified theory of knowledge does not relativize science but rather maps its precise location within a triadic structure of knowing — subjective, inter-subjective, and objective — thereby revealing both the source of its extraordinary power and the nature of its constitutive limitation. The gap is not a deficit of scientific knowledge but a deficit of reflexive knowledge about the conditions under which scientific knowledge is produced and situated.
