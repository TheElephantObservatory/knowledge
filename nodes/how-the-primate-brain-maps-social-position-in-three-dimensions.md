# How the Primate Brain Maps Social Position in Three Dimensions

The cucumber knows where it stands.

---

> The primate mind tracks social position — rank, alliance, engagement — using the same cognitive architecture it uses to navigate physical space. These spatial mappings of social relations are not metaphorical but constitutive, forming the pre-verbal foundation for all human social cognition.

The influence matrix captures something easy to underestimate: the degree to which the primate mind is constitutively organized around tracking its position in a relational field. This is not a secondary or derived capacity — it is a core feature of social mammal cognition. What the influence matrix maps is the relational process dimension of self-other dynamics: specifically, how much an agent can move others, and how much others value that agent. This tracking happens along three axes. The first is the vertical dimension of rank, status, and power — the sense of being above or below in a dominance hierarchy. The second is cooperative allyship — the network of kin and reciprocal relationships that determine who shares resources with whom. The third is the dimension of engagement versus distance — how much active relational involvement exists between agents. What is remarkable, and what connects directly to the exaptation argument, is that the mind appears to map social space using the same basic architecture it uses to navigate physical space. The same three-dimensional spatial reasoning that orients an organism in an inanimate environment gets recruited to create a social topography. We look up to people, we look down on them, we feel close or distant — these are not metaphors that happen to use spatial language. They reflect a genuine cognitive architecture in which social relations are represented using spatial coordinates. The cucumber-grape experiment with macaque monkeys makes this vivid: the monkey who receives a cucumber after watching another receive a grape is not simply disappointed — it is registering a shift in its relational value, a downward movement in the social map, and it responds with the full affective force that such a shift warrants. This pre-verbal, embodied, relationally constituted sense of one's place in a social field is the foundation upon which all subsequent human social cognition — including the justification dynamics of propositional language — will be built.

## Apprentice

The primate brain is deeply wired to track where it stands in relation to others. This isn't a nice-to-have social skill — it's a core feature of how social mammals think. The "influence matrix" describes three dimensions along which this tracking happens: up-down (who has power over whom), side-by-side (who are your allies and kin), and close-far (how engaged or distant you are from others).

What makes this especially striking is that the mind appears to use the same mental machinery for mapping social relationships that it uses for navigating physical space. When people say they "look up to" someone or feel "close to" a friend, they aren't just borrowing spatial language for convenience. The brain genuinely represents social position using something like spatial coordinates — a kind of internal social map.

The famous cucumber-grape experiment illustrates this vividly. When a monkey watches another monkey receive a grape and then gets handed a mere cucumber, its furious reaction isn't just about wanting better food. The monkey is registering a shift in its social value — a demotion on the map — and responding with real emotional force. This embodied, pre-verbal sense of one's place among others is the deep foundation on which all later human social thinking gets built.

## Adept

The influence matrix framework identifies a constitutive feature of social mammal cognition: the continuous, pre-reflective tracking of one's position within a relational field. This is not an incidental byproduct of intelligence but a core organizing principle of the primate mind. The matrix operates along three axes — a vertical dimension of rank, status, and dominance; a horizontal dimension of cooperative allyship and resource-sharing networks; and a proximal-distal dimension of relational engagement versus withdrawal. Together, these axes define a social topography that the organism inhabits as concretely as it inhabits physical space.

The key theoretical move is the exaptation argument: the cognitive architecture originally evolved for spatial navigation — orienting the body in three-dimensional physical environments — has been recruited to represent social relations. Expressions like "looking up to" someone or feeling "close" are not post-hoc metaphors layered onto abstract social concepts. They reflect a genuine isomorphism between spatial reasoning and social cognition, where relational position is encoded in quasi-spatial coordinates.

The cucumber-grape experiment with capuchin monkeys provides a compelling behavioral illustration. The monkey receiving the inferior reward after witnessing a conspecific receive a superior one is not merely experiencing frustration about food quality. It is registering a downward shift in relational value — a recalibration of its position on the social map — and the affective response carries the full weight of that positional displacement. This pre-verbal, embodied relational sense constitutes the deep substrate upon which distinctly human capacities, including propositional justification and normative reasoning, are subsequently constructed.

## Magus

The influence matrix theorizes that the primate mind is constitutively organized around the real-time computation of relational position within a social field — not as a derived or modular capacity but as a foundational architecture of social mammal cognition. Three orthogonal dimensions structure this computation: a vertical axis encoding dominance, rank, and status differentials; a cooperative axis mapping allyship, kinship, and reciprocal exchange networks; and an engagement axis registering the degree of active relational involvement versus withdrawal. These dimensions jointly constitute a social topography that functions as the organism's primary navigational frame for conspecific interaction.

The deeper claim is exaptational: the three-dimensional spatial reasoning system that evolved to orient organisms in physical environments has been co-opted to generate social-relational representations. The pervasive spatialization of social language — verticality for status, proximity for intimacy, laterality for alliance — is not metaphorical overlay but cognitive homology. Social space is computed using genuinely spatial coordinates, making the influence matrix a case study in how phylogenetically ancient representational formats get repurposed for novel adaptive domains. The affective valence attached to positional shifts — the rage of the macaque receiving a cucumber after witnessing a grape — indexes not mere reward prediction error but a displacement in relational value coordinates, processed with the full motivational urgency that spatial disorientation would command.

This pre-linguistic, somatically encoded relational positioning constitutes the deep stratum upon which distinctly human cognitive achievements are scaffolded. Propositional justification, normative discourse, and the entire apparatus of reason-giving emerge not de novo but as elaborations of this prior architecture — the primate influence matrix exapted into the space of linguistic and cultural negotiation.
