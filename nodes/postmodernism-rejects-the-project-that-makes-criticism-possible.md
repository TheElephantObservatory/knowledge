# Postmodernism Rejects the Project That Makes Criticism Possible

Thank you. We'll get back to work.

---

> When postmodern thinkers land a valid criticism of Enlightenment reasoning, modernists treat it as useful feedback; postmodernists treat it as proof the whole project has failed. This asymmetry reveals that postmodernism isn't sharper critique — it's a rejection of the framework that makes critique meaningful.

One of the most clarifying distinctions in the entire modern-versus-postmodern debate concerns not the criticisms themselves, but what those criticisms are *for*. Stephen Hicks draws a sharp line here: the modern project is self-correcting by design. Bacon, Descartes, Locke — they built in the expectation that hypotheses would fail, that theories would be found wanting, and that criticism was the engine of improvement, not the evidence of collapse. When a postmodern thinker like Foucault or Rorty mounts a genuinely good criticism of, say, a naive version of empiricism or a particular philosophy of science, the modernist response is: 'Thank you. We'll get back to work.' The postmodern response is: 'Therefore, the Enlightenment has failed.' The criticism is identical. The philosophical stance toward the criticism is entirely different. Postmodernism is not a more rigorous version of internal critique — it is a categorical rejection of the project that makes critique meaningful in the first place. Lyotard's skepticism toward all meta-narratives, Foucault's reduction of knowledge to power regimes — these are not sharper tools for finding truth; they are arguments that the search for truth is itself a kind of power play. And that distinction matters enormously, because if you accept it, you lose the very ground on which any criticism, including postmodern criticism, could claim to be *correct*. The political genealogy of postmodernism reinforces this reading: the first two generations of postmodern thinkers were not neutral analysts who reluctantly concluded the modern project had failed. They were, from the outset, committed to a broadly left-wing, anti-capitalist, anti-industrial framework, and they encountered modernity already opposed to its institutional expressions. This prior commitment meant they were systematically motivated to amplify every negative and minimize every positive — which explains why their criticisms never seem to find anything redeemable, and why every new problem is treated as definitive proof of systemic failure rather than as a challenge to be solved. The philosophical question this raises is whether it is possible to evaluate the modern project without such a prior commitment — and whether any successor project, including the metamodern one, is immune to the same dynamic.

## Apprentice

There's a crucial difference between criticizing something to make it better and criticizing something to tear it down. The founders of modern thinking — people like Bacon, Descartes, and Locke — expected their ideas to be challenged and revised. That was the whole point. Criticism wasn't a sign of failure; it was the engine of progress. So when someone points out a real flaw in, say, how we gather evidence or build scientific theories, the modern response is essentially: "Good catch. Let's fix it."

Postmodern thinkers like Foucault and Rorty often identify the same flaws, but draw a radically different conclusion: the Enlightenment itself has failed. Knowledge isn't really about truth — it's about power. Every big story we tell about progress is just a disguise for domination. The problem is, if you accept that all claims to truth are really just power moves, then your own criticism can't claim to be true either. You've sawed off the branch you're sitting on.

Stephen Hicks adds a historical layer to this argument. The major postmodern thinkers weren't neutral observers who reluctantly concluded that modernity had failed. They arrived already opposed to capitalism, industry, and Western institutions. This prior commitment made them systematically inclined to see only what was wrong with the modern project and never what was working. The important question this raises is whether anyone — including those who come after postmodernism — can evaluate these ideas without a similar hidden bias.

## Adept

Stephen Hicks identifies what may be the single most clarifying distinction in the modernism-postmodernism debate: the difference lies not in the content of criticisms but in their intended function. The Enlightenment project was designed to be self-correcting. Bacon's empiricism, Descartes' methodological doubt, Locke's epistemology — all presupposed that hypotheses would fail and theories would require revision. Criticism was constitutive of the enterprise, not evidence against it. When a postmodern thinker mounts a genuinely incisive critique of naive empiricism or a particular philosophy of science, the modernist absorbs it as productive feedback. The postmodernist treats it as a death certificate for the entire project. The criticism is identical; the philosophical stance toward it is categorically different.

This distinction carries a devastating logical implication. Lyotard's incredulity toward meta-narratives, Foucault's genealogical reduction of knowledge to power-regimes, Rorty's pragmatist dissolution of truth — these are not refinements of the critical apparatus. They are arguments that the search for truth is itself a form of domination. But if all truth-claims are power plays, then postmodern criticism cannot exempt itself from its own diagnosis. The framework is self-undermining: it destroys the epistemic ground on which any claim, including its own, could be adjudicated as correct.

Hicks reinforces this philosophical point with a political genealogy. The first two generations of postmodern thinkers were not disinterested analysts who reluctantly concluded modernity had failed. They were committed from the outset to anti-capitalist, anti-industrial positions, encountering modernity already opposed to its institutional expressions. This prior commitment created systematic motivation to amplify negatives and suppress positives — explaining why postmodern critique never finds anything redeemable in the Enlightenment legacy. The pressing question this raises is whether any successor framework, including metamodernism, can credibly claim immunity from the same motivated reasoning.

## Magus

Hicks isolates a distinction that cuts to the architectonic level of the modernism-postmodernism dispute: the divergence concerns not the propositional content of criticisms but their meta-philosophical function. The Enlightenment project, from Bacon's novum organum through Lockean empiricism to Popperian falsificationism, was constitutively self-correcting — it internalized fallibilism as a feature, not a vulnerability. A valid critique of naive inductivism or foundationalist epistemology is, within this framework, simply the mechanism working as intended. The modernist response to such critique is iterative refinement. The postmodernist response — exemplified by Lyotard's delegitimation of meta-narratives, Foucault's power-knowledge nexus, Derrida's deconstruction of presence — is categorical: the failure of any particular instantiation is taken as evidence that the regulative ideal of truth-seeking rationality is itself incoherent or ideological.

The self-referential problem here is not merely a tu quoque. If knowledge-claims are reducible to epistemic power-regimes, and if the aspiration to objective truth is a disguised will-to-domination, then postmodern critique forfeits the normative ground from which it could claim superiority over the positions it dismantles. Rorty's attempt to finesse this through ironic redescription and Foucault's strategic refusal of normative foundations do not resolve the performative contradiction — they merely aestheticize it. The critique remains parasitic on the very epistemic norms it repudiates.

Hicks's political genealogy deepens the diagnosis. The principal postmodern theorists — Heidegger's heirs, the Frankfurt School's successors, the post-structuralists — arrived at their encounter with modernity already committed to anti-capitalist, anti-liberal priors. This motivational structure produced a systematic asymmetry: every failure of modern institutions was treated as definitive falsification, while every success was reinterpreted as mystification or co-optation. The genealogy raises an uncomfortable reflexive question for any post-postmodern synthesis: whether the evaluation of modernity's legacy can ever be disentangled from the evaluator's prior political commitments, and whether metamodern frameworks merely reproduce this dynamic under more sophisticated self-awareness.
