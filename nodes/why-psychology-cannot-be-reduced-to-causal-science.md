# Why Psychology Cannot Be Reduced to Causal Science

The mind keeps its reasons to itself.

---

> Psychology is caught between two kinds of explanation — causes and reasons — and cannot succeed by importing only the causal tools of natural science. A mature psychology must treat value and normative entailment as real features of the world, not residue to be eliminated.

Psychology occupies a uniquely strange position in the landscape of the sciences because its subject matter — the mind — sits precisely at the boundary between the space of causes and the space of reasons. In the natural sciences, explanation is causal: if you hit a ball with a given velocity, it will travel in a predictable direction. But when you explain why a person ran down the hill, you give not only causes but reasons — and reasons are inherently normative. Piaget captured this with the deliberately paradoxical phrase 'normative fact': two plus two equals four is not a causal fact but a normative entailment. You say it not because you are caused to but because it is correct. This places psychology in a fundamentally different stratum of scientific activity. The disenchantment and reductionism that proved so powerful in the natural sciences cannot be straightforwardly imported into psychology, because psychology must traffic in the normative — in value, entailment, and correctness, not just causation. This is why a purely experimental, measurement-based psychology is structurally inadequate: it attempts to answer normative questions with causal tools. A mature metapsychology must therefore expand the metaphysical commitments of science itself to include value and normative entailment as genuine features of reality, not merely as folk-psychological residue to be eliminated. Without this expansion, psychology will continue to produce what Zak Stein calls 'mongrel concepts' — folk psychological ideas reified through statistical technique, mistaken for real entities in the mind, and then industrially scaled into measurement instruments that confuse rather than illuminate the nature of persons. The concept of relevance sits precisely at this boundary between cause and reason, which is why relevance realization may be a pivotal concept for any metapsychology that hopes to bridge the two.

## Apprentice

In the natural sciences, we explain things by pointing to causes: a ball moves because a force acted on it. But when we explain why a person did something, we don't just cite causes — we give reasons. Reasons involve rightness and wrongness, better and worse. Two plus two equals four not because some force makes it so, but because it's correct. Psychology sits right at the boundary between these two kinds of explanation, and that makes it a uniquely strange science.

The problem is that mainstream psychology has tried to operate as though it were just another natural science — measuring, predicting, and reducing everything to causal mechanisms. But you can't fully explain a person's thinking without talking about whether their reasoning is good or bad, valid or mistaken. When psychology ignores this, it ends up taking everyday ideas about the mind, dressing them up in statistics, and treating them as if they were discovered natural kinds — real things in the brain — when they may be nothing of the sort.

A genuinely mature psychology would need to expand what counts as scientifically real to include values and logical relationships, not just physical causes. The concept of relevance — how we determine what matters in any given moment — may be a key to bridging these two worlds, because relevance is neither purely causal nor purely logical. It lives exactly where psychology must learn to work.

## Adept

Psychology occupies an anomalous position among the sciences because its subject matter — the mind — straddles what philosophers distinguish as the space of causes and the space of reasons. Natural-scientific explanation is causal and mechanistic, but psychological explanation necessarily traffics in reasons, which are normative: they involve correctness, validity, and value. Piaget captured this tension with the phrase 'normative fact' — the truth that two plus two equals four is not a causal regularity but a normative entailment. This dual character means that the disenchantment and reductionism that powered the natural sciences cannot be imported wholesale into psychology without distortion.

The structural consequence is that a purely experimental, measurement-based psychology is inadequate to its own domain. It attempts to resolve normative questions — what constitutes good reasoning, appropriate emotion, healthy development — using exclusively causal tools. The result, as Zak Stein has argued, is the proliferation of 'mongrel concepts': folk-psychological notions reified through factor analysis and psychometric technique, mistaken for natural kinds, and then industrially scaled into assessment instruments that obscure rather than illuminate personhood.

A viable metapsychology must therefore expand the metaphysical commitments of science itself, treating value and normative entailment as genuine features of reality rather than eliminable folk residue. The concept of relevance realization, developed in the work of John Vervaeke, sits precisely at this boundary — relevance is neither purely causal nor purely rational, but constitutively involves both. This makes it a potentially pivotal concept for any metapsychological framework that aspires to integrate causal explanation with normative understanding without collapsing one into the other.

## Magus

The peculiar epistemic predicament of psychology stems from the fact that its explanandum — minded activity — is constitutively situated at the intersection of the space of causes and the space of reasons, to use Sellarsian terminology. Natural-scientific explanation proceeds within the space of causes: nomological regularities, efficient mechanisms, predictive models. But psychological explanation cannot remain there, because to characterize a cognitive agent is already to invoke normativity — correctness conditions, rational entailment, evaluative orientation. Piaget's deliberately paradoxical notion of the 'normative fact' crystallizes this: the entailment that two plus two equals four is not a causal regularity but a normative necessity, and any science of the mind that cannot accommodate this distinction is operating with a truncated ontology.

The methodological consequence is severe. A psychology modeled exclusively on natural-scientific reductionism systematically confuses normative questions with causal ones, generating what Zak Stein identifies as 'mongrel concepts' — folk-psychological categories subjected to operationalization and factor-analytic reification, then mistaken for genuine psychological natural kinds. These pseudo-entities are subsequently industrially scaled into psychometric instruments whose apparent precision masks a fundamental category error. The measurement apparatus produces numbers, but the numbers do not track joints in nature; they track the statistical shadows of pre-theoretical assumptions.

What is required is a metapsychological expansion of scientific ontology to include value and normative entailment as irreducible features of reality — not as eliminable folk residue but as belonging to what is genuinely the case about minded beings. The concept of relevance realization, as articulated in John Vervaeke's framework, is particularly promising here because relevance is neither reducible to causal mechanism nor to formal logical entailment; it is the pre-reflective normative sensitivity through which an agent's cognitive ecology is organized. It thus sits at exactly the juncture where a non-reductive metapsychology must learn to theorize.
