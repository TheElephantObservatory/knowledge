# Meaning Descends: Teleology From Physics to Culture

If meaning arises from the mutual information correlating entities and environments, then the learning process of entities in the universe extends across all levels of complexity — from human culture down through animal cognition, biological organisms, and even into inanimate matter. To delineate these distinct levels, we can speak of four kinds of learning: Structural Learning connected to matter, Genetic Learning connected to life, Cognitive Learning connected to minds, and Symbolic Learning connected to culture. This framework reveals that meaning is not a uniquely human invention or a subjective social construct, but rather something that connects down into the very rudiments of reality. Even a whirlpool — a dissipative structure that maintains its informational organization against entropy — exists in an entity-field relationship whose correlation defines a crude but real form of meaningful knowledge. More broadly, dissipative structures like whirlpools, hurricanes, and tornadoes exhibit teleological, goal-directed behavior emerging entirely on the plane of matter, prior to any biology: they arise and persist because they more effectively dissipate free energy through the spontaneous emergence of greater order, a process Jeremy England and others call dissipative adaptation. Environmental conditions select for which structures emerge, meaning a natural pressure to minimize free energy spontaneously creates ordered structures with an objective function — purpose arising immanently from physics, not imposed from outside. This offers a promising bridge toward understanding abiogenesis, since biological organisms are clearly a subset of dissipative structures, suggesting life itself may be a continuation of a process that begins deep in the material world. Far from diminishing the concept of meaning or purpose, this continuity elevates it — demonstrating that teleology is a genuine feature of nature, not an arbitrary projection of the human mind.

## Apprentice

Most people assume that meaning and purpose are things only humans create — through language, culture, and conscious thought. But there is a compelling case that meaning runs much deeper than that, all the way down into the physical world itself. Consider a whirlpool: it is not alive, it cannot think, yet it maintains a stable, organized shape by continuously processing energy from its environment. It persists because it is good at something — dissipating energy. In a real sense, it has a relationship with its surroundings that shapes what it is. This same logic scales upward through living organisms, animal minds, and human culture. Each level involves a kind of learning — matter, genes, cognition, and symbols each encode information about the world in their own way. The physicist Jeremy England has shown that physical systems naturally tend to organize themselves when they are good at absorbing and releasing energy. This suggests that the drive toward order and purpose is not something injected into nature from outside — it is built into physics from the start. Life, then, may not be a miraculous exception to the material world, but a continuation of something that was already happening long before the first cell ever formed.

## Adept

A powerful framework proposes that meaning is not a uniquely human or even biological phenomenon, but a natural consequence of mutual information between entities and their environments. This perspective identifies four nested levels of learning — Structural, Genetic, Cognitive, and Symbolic — corresponding to matter, life, mind, and culture respectively. Each level represents a distinct mode by which systems encode and exploit correlations with their surroundings. The insight gains particular force when applied to dissipative structures: physical systems like whirlpools, hurricanes, and tornadoes that maintain organized, far-from-equilibrium states by continuously processing energy flows. These structures exhibit something genuinely teleological — they arise and persist precisely because they are effective at dissipating free energy. Jeremy England's work on dissipative adaptation formalizes this intuition, demonstrating that thermodynamic pressures can spontaneously select for increasingly ordered structures with what amounts to an objective function. Environmental conditions act as a selection pressure at the purely physical level, prior to any biology. This reframes abiogenesis not as a mysterious leap from dead matter to living purpose, but as a natural continuation of processes already operating in inanimate systems. Crucially, this does not reduce meaning to mere mechanism — it rehabilitates teleology as an objective feature of physical reality. Purpose, on this account, is immanent in the structure of the universe, not a subjective overlay applied by conscious minds. The continuity across levels suggests that human culture and symbolic meaning are the most complex expression of something that begins at the thermodynamic roots of existence.

## Magus

The proposal that meaning supervenes on mutual information between an entity and its embedding environment generates a striking corollary: if informational correlation is the substrate of meaning, then proto-semantic relationships obtain wherever such correlations exist — including in purely abiotic dissipative structures. The four-level taxonomy of learning modes (Structural, Genetic, Cognitive, Symbolic) provides a formal scaffold for tracing this continuity across ontological registers, resisting both eliminativist reduction and vitalist discontinuity. Dissipative structures occupy a theoretically pivotal position in this account. Operating far from thermodynamic equilibrium, they sustain negentropic organization through continuous energy throughput — a dynamic that constitutes a primitive but non-trivial entity-field correlation. Jeremy England's dissipative adaptation framework supplies the mechanistic grounding: statistical mechanics predicts that systems subject to periodic forcing will be thermodynamically selected for configurations that more efficiently absorb and dissipate that forcing, yielding spontaneous increases in structural order as a consequence of entropy maximization at the system boundary. The environment thus functions as a selective regime at the sub-biological level, instantiating something formally analogous to fitness landscapes without invoking replication or heredity. This has direct implications for abiogenesis: biological organisms, as a proper subset of dissipative structures, may represent a quantitative rather than qualitative transition — the crossing of a threshold within a continuous physical process rather than the emergence of an entirely novel ontological category. The philosophical yield is considerable. Teleology, long exiled from naturalistic ontology following the Newtonian settlement, is here rehabilitated not as anthropomorphic projection but as an immanent structural feature of physical law. Meaning, purpose, and goal-directedness are revealed as gradient phenomena distributed across the full complexity hierarchy of the cosmos.
