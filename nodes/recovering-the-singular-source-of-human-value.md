# Recovering the Singular Source of Human Value

The sacred ground beneath our shifting sand

---

> Modern culture confuses 'values' (plural, cultural preferences) with 'value' (singular, the deep ground of what matters). Without the latter, the former become arbitrary and manipulable — and that collapse has consequences far beyond philosophy.

There is a crucial distinction that tends to get collapsed in public discourse between value in the singular and values in the plural. When we speak of values plurally — as in 'our company values' or 'shared values' — we are typically speaking of derivative, culturally contingent preferences or commitments: things like integrity, loyalty, pleasure, or fairness. These are real, but they are downstream of something more fundamental. Value in the singular — what might be called the ground of value — is something closer to what the Dao points at, or what the great religious traditions mean by the sacred, or what philosophy has historically called the transcendentals: truth, beauty, and goodness. The profound philosophical error of modernity has been to treat the plural, derivative values as if they were the whole story, and in doing so to lose contact with the singular ground from which they derive their force. This matters enormously because when you try to campaign for values, or encode them in messaging, or build ethical frameworks on top of them, without any grounding in that deeper singular sense of value, you are building on sand. The values become arbitrary, negotiable, and ultimately manipulable — which is precisely what we observe in contemporary political and commercial life. The recovery of a serious philosophical account of value — one that recognizes it as something that can be perceived directly, not merely constructed or agreed upon — is not an academic luxury. It is a civilizational necessity.

## Apprentice

Most people use the word 'values' to mean something like a list of commitments — honesty, loyalty, fairness, kindness. These are real things, but there is a deeper question lurking beneath them: where do they get their force? Why does honesty matter? Why does beauty move us? The argument here is that there is something more fundamental than any particular value — a ground of value itself — and that modern life has largely forgotten how to talk about it.

This forgetting has a cost. When values are treated as just preferences — things a culture agrees on, or a company chooses to promote — they become negotiable. They can be rebranded, weaponized, or quietly abandoned when inconvenient. Anyone who has watched political messaging or corporate ethics initiatives will recognize the pattern: the language of values everywhere, and the substance of it nowhere.

The recovery being called for here is not nostalgic or religious in a narrow sense. It is philosophical: a return to the idea that value is something that can be genuinely perceived and recognized, not merely invented or voted on. The great wisdom traditions — from Daoism to the Western concept of truth, beauty, and goodness — were pointing at this. Getting it back into view is not a luxury. It may be one of the more urgent intellectual tasks of the moment.

## Adept

A persistent confusion in contemporary ethical and political discourse collapses the distinction between value (singular) and values (plural). The plural form — 'shared values,' 'organizational values,' 'value alignment' — refers to culturally contingent normative commitments: integrity, fairness, loyalty, and so on. These are derivative. They carry weight only insofar as they are grounded in something more fundamental: a singular sense of value that the philosophical tradition has variously approached through the transcendentals (truth, beauty, goodness), through concepts of the sacred in religious thought, or through the Daoist notion of an underlying order that cannot be fully articulated but can be oriented toward.

The diagnostic claim here is that modernity's characteristic error has been to treat the derivative as foundational — to build ethical frameworks, political philosophies, and institutional cultures on top of plural values while remaining agnostic or dismissive about the singular ground. The consequence is precisely what is observable: values become arbitrary, subject to rhetorical capture, and ultimately manipulable. When there is no shared account of what value is, there is no stable basis from which to resist the reduction of values to preferences, and preferences to power.

The corrective is not a return to any particular metaphysical tradition, but a renewed philosophical seriousness about value as something that can be perceived — apprehended directly, as one apprehends a mathematical truth or a moral horror — rather than merely constructed through consensus. This is not an academic project. The erosion of a credible account of value at the civilizational level has practical consequences that are already visible in the texture of public life.

## Magus

The distinction between value (singular, ontological) and values (plural, normative) maps onto a fault line running through the history of metaethics, but it has acquired renewed urgency as that distinction has been systematically effaced in both analytic moral philosophy and in the broader culture shaped by it. The singular sense of value — what the Scholastics approached through the transcendentalia (verum, bonum, pulchrum), what Plato's Form of the Good gestures toward, what the Daoist tradition encodes in the concept of de as the particular expression of the Dao — is not a value among values. It is the condition of intelligibility for normative discourse as such. Values in the plural are downstream articulations: culturally mediated, historically situated, and inherently revisable. Their claim on us is parasitic on a prior, non-constructed orientation toward what is genuinely real, genuinely good, genuinely beautiful.

The philosophical pathology of modernity, on this account, is not relativism per se, but a more subtle error: the substitution of the derivative for the foundational, followed by the attempt to reconstruct normativity from within the derivative register alone. The result is what Bernard Williams diagnosed as the 'peculiar institution' of morality — a domain of categorical demands floating free of any account of why they should bind — and what Alasdair MacIntyre traced in the fragmentation of moral language into incommensurable emotivist fragments. The values discourse of contemporary political and commercial life is the cultural precipitate of this failure: earnest, ubiquitous, and structurally hollow.

What is being called for is not a revival of any particular metaphysical system but a recovery of the phenomenological and contemplative registers in which value is apprehended rather than constructed — the mode of perception that Iris Murdoch associated with 'moral vision' and Simone Weil with attention. The argument is that this recovery is not a refinement of academic philosophy but a precondition for any serious ethical or political renewal.
