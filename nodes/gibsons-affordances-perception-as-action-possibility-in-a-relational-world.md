# Gibson's Affordances: Perception as Action-Possibility in a Relational World

You have to be there before you can see it.

---

> Perception isn't about building a mental picture of reality — it's about detecting possibilities for action. J.J. Gibson's ecological approach, extended by Joe Norman's work on perceptual learning, reveals that knowing and seeing are embodied, relational processes that unfold through sustained interaction with an environment.

J.J. Gibson's ecological approach to perception offers a radical reframing of what it means to see and know the world — one with deep implications for how we think about cognition, agency, and the relationship between organism and environment. The standard Western model of perception treats the brain as a modeling device: it gathers sense data and reconstructs an internal representation of the external world. Gibson's alternative is that organisms do not perceive abstract physical properties — they perceive affordances. An affordance is an action-relevant relationship: not 'that branch is approximately cylindrical' but 'that branch is graspable.' Perception is fundamentally about possibilities for action, not about building a neutral picture of reality. This means perception is irreducibly relational — it cannot be located in the organism alone or in the environment alone, but only in the relationship between the two. The size of a branch is only meaningful relative to the size of a hand that might grip it. Each species therefore inhabits a distinct Umwelt — a life-world structured by the organism's own morphology and capacities. What Joe Norman adds from his own experimental work is a temporal and dynamic dimension: perceptual learning is the process by which affordances are discovered over time through repeated embodied interaction with an environment. You cannot know what it is like to perceive something you have not yet perceived — you have to put yourself in the situation repeatedly until something snaps into place, a self-organization process that cannot be top-down directed. His homesteading experience makes this vivid: after a year of small incremental decisions about fences, gates, trees, and compost, semantic relationships between parts of the property began to emerge that he could not have designed in advance and would not have been able to see from the outside. Perception, knowledge, and design are all, at bottom, ecological processes.

## Apprentice

Most people assume that seeing works like a camera: your eyes take in data, and your brain builds an internal model of the world. J.J. Gibson proposed something very different. He argued that what we actually perceive are not abstract properties like shape or distance, but possibilities for action — what he called affordances. You don't see a branch as "cylindrical"; you see it as graspable. And what counts as graspable depends on the size of your hand, which means perception lives in the relationship between you and your surroundings, not inside your head alone.

Joe Norman extends this idea by emphasizing that affordances aren't just sitting there waiting to be noticed. They have to be discovered through repeated, hands-on interaction over time. You literally cannot perceive certain things until you've spent enough time immersed in a situation for new relationships to snap into focus — a kind of self-organizing process that can't be rushed or planned from above.

Norman draws on his own experience homesteading to make this concrete. After a year of incremental decisions about fences, trees, gates, and compost, meaningful connections between parts of the property started to emerge — relationships he could never have designed in advance or seen from the outside. The implication is striking: perception, knowledge, and even design are not top-down intellectual exercises. They are ecological processes that grow out of sustained engagement with the world.

## Adept

J.J. Gibson's ecological psychology represents a fundamental break from the representationalist model of perception that dominates cognitive science. Rather than treating the brain as a device that reconstructs an internal model from impoverished sense data, Gibson argued that organisms directly perceive affordances — action-relevant relational properties of the environment. A branch is not first perceived as cylindrical and then inferred to be graspable; graspability is what is perceived. This makes perception irreducibly relational: affordances exist only at the interface between an organism's morphology and its environment. Each species therefore inhabits a distinct Umwelt, a perceptual world structured by its own bodily capacities.

Joe Norman's contribution is to foreground the temporal and developmental dimension that Gibson's framework implies but does not fully elaborate. Drawing on his own experimental and experiential work, Norman argues that perceptual learning — the progressive differentiation of affordances — is a self-organizing process that unfolds only through repeated embodied engagement. It cannot be directed top-down or anticipated from outside the process. You cannot know what a new perceptual distinction will feel like before you have made it.

Norman's homesteading experience serves as a vivid case study. Over a year of incremental, situated decisions — about fences, gates, compost, trees — semantic relationships between elements of the property emerged that were invisible at the outset and could not have been specified in a design document. This points toward a broader claim: that perception, knowledge acquisition, and design are all fundamentally ecological processes, arising from sustained coupling between agent and environment rather than from abstract planning or internal computation.

## Magus

Gibson's ecological optics dismantles the transduction-and-reconstruction pipeline that underwrites both classical cognitivism and most Bayesian approaches to perception. The key ontological move is the replacement of stimulus properties with affordances — relational invariants specified not in the organism's neural activity nor in the physical environment per se, but in the organism-environment system taken as an irreducible unit of analysis. Perception is thus direct in a precise sense: it does not require inferential mediation because the information specifying affordances is already structured in ambient energy arrays. The corollary is that each species' Umwelt — in von Uexküll's sense — is constitutively shaped by its sensorimotor morphology, making the very idea of a species-neutral description of "the environment" incoherent at the ecological scale.

Joe Norman's extension of this framework centers on the dynamics of perceptual learning as a self-organizing, temporally extended process. His claim, grounded in both formal investigation and first-person experiential inquiry, is that affordance discovery cannot be reduced to hypothesis testing or Bayesian updating. It is better understood as a phase transition in the agent-environment coupling: a reorganization that emerges from sustained interaction and that is, in principle, opaque to the agent prior to its occurrence. One cannot represent what one has not yet learned to perceive.

Norman's homesteading work provides an existence proof. Through a year of incremental, embodied decision-making — siting fences, managing compost, pruning trees — higher-order semantic relationships between elements of the property crystallized that were neither designable ex ante nor visible to an external observer. The implication generalizes: design, epistemic development, and perceptual differentiation are isomorphic ecological processes, each requiring protracted coupling and resisting decomposition into context-free rules or top-down specification.
