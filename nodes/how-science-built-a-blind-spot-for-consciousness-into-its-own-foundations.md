# How Science Built a Blind Spot for Consciousness Into Its Own Foundations

The eye cannot see itself seeing.

---

> The hard problem of consciousness is not a puzzle science discovered but one it created — by methodologically excluding subjective experience from nature centuries ago, then forgetting it had done so. Solving it requires not a better theory within the current framework, but a fundamentally different ontology.

The hard problem of consciousness is not an accidental puzzle that science stumbled upon — it is a problem that the methodology of modern science generated by its own internal logic, and then forgot it had generated. The move begins with Galileo's methodological decision to bracket the qualitative, experiential dimensions of nature — color, warmth, feeling — and treat them as secondary properties added by the subjective organism, while reserving the title of 'real' for matter in motion, the quantitative and measurable. Descartes then elevated this methodological procedure into a metaphysical ontology: two substances, mind and matter, cleanly separated. Over the course of modernity, as Thompson, Glazer, and Frank document in The Blind Spot, this bifurcation of nature was forgotten as a choice and became invisible as an assumption. The result is that science now finds itself unable to explain the very thing that makes science possible — the first-person, embodied, phenomenological encounter with the world that is the transcendental condition for any inquiry whatsoever. You cannot explain consciousness using the tools of a methodology that was constructed by setting consciousness aside. The blind spot is structural: the eye cannot see itself seeing. What is needed is not just a better theory of consciousness within the existing framework, but a post-physicalist ontology that brings the qualitative back into nature — not as a secondary epiphenomenon sitting awkwardly atop a mechanical substrate, but as genuinely constitutive of what nature is. The redness of a sunset is as much a part of nature as the wavelengths a physicist might invoke to explain it. Science is a historical, cultural, embodied activity, and any honest philosophy of science must reckon with that.

## Apprentice

When modern science began, thinkers like Galileo made a practical decision: set aside the felt qualities of experience — color, warmth, taste — and focus only on what can be measured. Descartes then hardened this into a worldview, splitting reality into mind and matter as if they were two entirely separate things. Over time, people forgot this was ever a choice. It came to seem like the obvious truth about how things are.

The consequence is a strange trap. Science now tries to explain consciousness — our inner experience of the world — using tools that were specifically designed by leaving consciousness out of the picture. It is like trying to see your own eyes without a mirror. The philosopher-scientists behind The Blind Spot (Thompson, Glazer, and Frank) argue that this is not an accidental gap in knowledge but a structural blind spot built into the method itself.

The way forward, this line of thinking suggests, is not to keep searching for consciousness inside the machinery of physics and neuroscience as currently conceived. Instead, it means rethinking what nature itself is — bringing qualities like the redness of a sunset back into the fabric of reality, not as illusions produced by brains, but as genuine features of the world. Science is a human activity, carried out by embodied beings, and any honest account of it must start from that fact.

## Adept

The hard problem of consciousness — why and how subjective experience arises from physical processes — is typically framed as a frontier puzzle awaiting a sufficiently powerful scientific theory. This analysis reframes it as a self-generated artifact of modern science's founding methodology. Galileo's decision to bracket qualitative, experiential properties (color, warmth, felt meaning) as "secondary" and privilege quantitative, measurable properties as the sole constituents of objective reality was a methodological move, not a discovery about nature. Descartes formalized this into substance dualism, and over centuries the bifurcation of nature — to use Whitehead's phrase — hardened from a deliberate abstraction into an invisible metaphysical assumption.

Thompson, Glazer, and Frank, in The Blind Spot, trace how this forgetting operates. Science presupposes the first-person, phenomenological encounter with the world as the transcendental condition of all inquiry, yet its methodology systematically excludes that encounter from its ontology. The result is structurally analogous to an eye that cannot see itself seeing: consciousness becomes inexplicable precisely because the explanatory framework was constructed by setting it aside.

The implication is that no theory of consciousness formulated within standard physicalism can resolve the problem, because the problem is generated by physicalism's constitutive exclusions. What is called for is a post-physicalist ontology — one that reintegrates qualitative, experiential dimensions as genuinely constitutive of nature rather than treating them as epiphenomenal byproducts of a fundamentally mechanical substrate. The redness of a sunset belongs to nature no less than electromagnetic wavelengths. Any adequate philosophy of science must reckon with the fact that science is itself a historical, embodied, culturally situated practice.

## Magus

The standard construal of the hard problem positions it as an explanatory gap that a mature neuroscience or physics of information might eventually close. This genealogical critique inverts that framing: the hard problem is not a residual mystery but a necessary consequence of the constitutive exclusions through which modern natural science defined its own domain of inquiry. Galileo's methodological epoché — bracketing the qualitative-experiential as "secondary" and reserving ontological privilege for the quantitative-measurable — was radicalized by Descartes into a full substance dualism, which subsequent centuries naturalized into an unmarked metaphysical presupposition. Whitehead diagnosed this as the bifurcation of nature; Husserl identified it as the crisis of European sciences. Thompson, Glazer, and Frank's The Blind Spot extends this lineage, documenting how the founding abstraction became invisible to the tradition it founded.

The structural topology of the problem is reflexive: the first-person phenomenological encounter with the world functions as the transcendental condition of possibility for empirical inquiry, yet the ontology underwriting that inquiry has no place for it. Consciousness is not merely difficult to explain within physicalism — it is what physicalism was engineered to exclude. The explanans was constructed by excising the explanandum. This is not an epistemic gap amenable to incremental closure but a categorial incommensurability internal to the framework itself.

The prescription follows from the diagnosis: not a better theory of consciousness within the received ontology, but a post-physicalist reconstitution of nature that restores qualitative, experiential dimensions as ontologically primitive rather than epiphenomenally supervenient. The redness of a sunset is as much a determination of nature as the spectral distribution a physicist invokes. Science, as a historical and embodied praxis, cannot coherently exempt its own conditions of possibility from its account of the real.
