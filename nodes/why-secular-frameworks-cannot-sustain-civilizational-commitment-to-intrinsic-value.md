# Why Secular Frameworks Cannot Sustain Civilizational Commitment to Intrinsic Value

Things worth dying for cannot be peer-reviewed.

---

> Secular frameworks cannot generate the language of intrinsic value needed to sustain civilizational commitment. The return to religion — not vague spirituality — may be the only way to recover resources for strong moral conviction before fundamentalists claim that territory by default.

The return to religion — chosen deliberately over spirituality — is one of the most important and misunderstood trends of our moment. You cannot run a civilization on the idea that its fundamental values are arbitrary, and you cannot run a civilization on the idea that there are no fundamental values at all. Postmodernity called the bluff of modernity's self-evident truths, and now it is genuinely unclear, philosophically, why human rights are valuable. Secular humanist scientific frameworks do not provide the resources for strong commitment to intrinsic value — they do not provide the language to say there are things more important than my own survival, which is precisely the language that motivates real political will, real sacrifice, real solidarity. Jürgen Habermas argues that there are untapped semantic potentials in religious language that science cannot replace. This is not anti-science but anti-reductionism: we desperately want to protect human vulnerability, dignity, and attention, but a hodgepodge of scientific facts cannot mobilize political will when our backs are against the wall. The danger is that if the only people saying deep things about intrinsic value are fundamentalists, and the well-educated refuse to engage with anything outside scientific discourse, then fundamentalists win by default. The task is to recover what is genuinely invaluable in these traditions — the language of strong commitment, of things worth dying for, of a cosmos in which value is not arbitrary — while allowing what needs to pass to pass.

## Apprentice

There is a growing sense that modern secular society has a serious gap: it can tell us how the world works, but it cannot tell us why anything truly matters. Science explains facts about human biology and behavior, but it struggles to say why human dignity is sacred, why some things are worth sacrificing for, or why our deepest values are anything more than personal preferences. Without that deeper language, it becomes very hard to rally people around shared commitments when times get difficult.

This is why the deliberate return to religion — not a loose, pick-and-choose spirituality, but serious engagement with religious traditions — is emerging as one of the most important cultural shifts of our time. The philosopher Jürgen Habermas, himself not a theologian, has argued that religious language contains irreplaceable resources for talking about human worth and moral obligation that secular language simply cannot match.

The concern is urgent: if thoughtful, educated people refuse to engage with questions of ultimate value because they seem unscientific, the only voices left speaking about what is truly worth defending will be fundamentalists. The challenge is to recover the genuine power of religious traditions — their capacity to name what is sacred and non-negotiable — without falling into dogmatism.

## Adept

Postmodernity successfully dismantled modernity's claim that its core values — human rights, dignity, equality — were self-evident. But it offered no replacement foundation, leaving contemporary civilization in a peculiar bind: it holds values it can no longer philosophically justify. Secular humanist and scientific frameworks, for all their descriptive power, lack the normative resources to ground strong claims about intrinsic value. They cannot furnish the language that says some things matter more than survival itself — precisely the language required to mobilize genuine political will, solidarity, and sacrifice.

This is the context in which the return to religion — deliberately distinguished from spirituality — becomes philosophically significant. Drawing on Habermas's argument about the "untapped semantic potentials" in religious traditions, this perspective holds that religious language carries irreducible capacities for articulating commitment, obligation, and the sacred that scientific discourse cannot replicate. This is not an anti-scientific position but an anti-reductionist one: the claim is that a civilization cannot sustain itself on empirical facts alone when what it needs is a framework for strong normative commitment.

The political stakes are concrete. If the educated classes abandon the terrain of ultimate value to fundamentalists — treating all non-empirical discourse as intellectually beneath them — then fundamentalism wins by default, because it is the only remaining voice speaking with conviction about what is non-negotiable. The task, then, is a difficult act of recovery: to retrieve from religious traditions their genuine capacity to name what is invaluable and to ground civilizational commitment, while allowing historically contingent doctrinal elements to fall away.

## Magus

The philosophical predicament is precise: postmodernity's deconstruction of modernity's supposedly self-evident truths was successful on its own terms, but it left no viable account of why normative commitments — human rights, dignity, the inviolability of persons — should command allegiance. Secular scientific naturalism, despite its extraordinary explanatory reach, is structurally incapable of generating strong claims about intrinsic value. It can describe preference, survival advantage, and social utility, but it cannot say that there exist things more important than one's own persistence — the very assertion that underwrites real political will, sacrificial solidarity, and civilizational endurance. The gap is not epistemic but motivational and ontological: what is needed is not more information but a language adequate to the weight of what it names.

Habermas's thesis concerning the unexhausted semantic potentials of religious language becomes decisive here. Religious traditions carry resources for articulating strong commitment — for naming the sacred, the non-negotiable, the cosmically grounded ought — that resist translation into the flattened idiom of empirical description. The return to religion, deliberately chosen over the diffuse eclecticism of contemporary spirituality, represents an attempt to recover these resources with philosophical seriousness. This is anti-reductionism, not anti-rationalism: the recognition that a hodgepodge of scientific facts cannot constitute a civilizational grammar when existential stakes demand one.

The danger is a vacuum effect. If the intellectually sophisticated categorically refuse engagement with non-empirical normative discourse, the field of ultimate value claims is ceded entirely to fundamentalism — the only remaining discourse willing to speak with unconditional conviction. The task is therefore one of critical retrieval: to recover from within religious traditions the genuine capacity to ground intrinsic value and mobilize commitment, while exercising the discernment to let historically contingent formations pass. The alternative is not neutrality but abdication.
