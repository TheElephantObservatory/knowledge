# The Iatrogenic Spiral: When Solutions Become Tomorrow's Problems

There's a deep structural problem with how civilizations solve problems: most of what we call problem-solving is actually the generation of new problems that will require future problem-solving. You solve a problem that was itself caused by a previous solution, and your solution will in turn generate the next problem. The market incentive is always on creating new things, and the history books celebrate inventors — but very often the best way to solve a problem is to reverse something, to remove a cause rather than add a new layer of complexity. Authentic progress requires caution and restraint: an emphasis on maintaining what works, on not breaking things that don't need to be broken, on asking whether the solution you're proposing will itself become the next problem. Until we understand what authentic progress entails, we will keep running this loop — solving problems we created, creating problems we'll need to solve, and calling the whole cycle advancement.

## Apprentice

Most of what gets called progress is actually just one problem replacing another. A solution gets invented, celebrated, and written into the history books — and then, quietly, it becomes the source of the next crisis. The car solved the problem of slow travel and created the problem of pollution. Antibiotics solved the problem of infection and created the problem of resistant bacteria. This pattern repeats endlessly, and the troubling part is that civilization tends to reward the people who add new things, not the people who remove old mistakes. Inventors get statues; the person who quietly reverses a bad decision gets nothing. The deeper insight here is that genuine progress often requires subtraction rather than addition — taking away a cause instead of piling on another layer of complexity. It also requires restraint: asking, before any solution is deployed, whether it will itself become the next problem. Until that question becomes a standard part of how societies think about change, the cycle continues — solving yesterday's solutions, creating tomorrow's problems, and calling the whole thing advancement.

## Adept

There is a structural pathology embedded in how modern civilizations conceptualize problem-solving: the dominant mode is additive intervention, which systematically generates second-order problems that then demand further intervention. This is not merely an ironic side effect but an architectural feature of how progress is incentivized and narrated. Market systems reward novelty; historiography celebrates invention. The result is a ratchet effect in which complexity accumulates, each solution embedding new failure modes into the substrate of civilization. The insight being advanced here is that authentic progress frequently demands the inverse operation — not the addition of a new mechanism but the removal of a prior cause. This is a form of via negativa applied to civilizational design: the best intervention is often the one that isn't made, or the one that undoes what was previously done. What this perspective calls for is a reorientation of the problem-solving ethos around caution, reversibility, and maintenance — what might be termed a conservative epistemology of change, not in the political sense but in the engineering sense. Chesterton's fence is the relevant heuristic: before removing or adding anything, understand why the existing structure is the way it is. The failure to institutionalize this kind of restraint means that civilizations remain trapped in a loop of reactive complexity, mistaking the acceleration of the problem-solution cycle for genuine advancement.

## Magus

The critique advanced here operates at the level of what might be called civilizational epistemology — the implicit frameworks through which societies identify, categorize, and respond to problems. The central claim is that the dominant problem-solving paradigm is structurally autocatalytic: interventions generate the conditions for their own succession, producing a recursive loop in which each solution is simultaneously the resolution of a prior problem and the generative cause of a subsequent one. This dynamic is not incidental but is overdetermined by the incentive architecture of market economies and the commemorative logic of historical narrative, both of which systematically privilege additive novelty over subtractive correction. The philosophical tradition most relevant here is the via negativa, extended from its theological and apophatic origins into a prescriptive methodology for systemic intervention. Paired with this is the Popperian notion of piecemeal social engineering — the argument that large-scale, irreversible interventions are epistemically reckless given the limits of predictive knowledge about complex adaptive systems. What the insight demands is a revaluation of maintenance, reversibility, and restraint as primary virtues of civilizational design — a shift from a teleological model of progress as accumulation toward a homeostatic model attentive to system integrity and the asymmetric costs of irreversible error. The normative implication is not conservatism as stasis but a rigorous pre-mortem epistemology: every proposed solution must be interrogated as a potential future problem before it is instantiated as a present answer. Absent this discipline, the problem-solution cycle functions not as progress but as a complexity treadmill, mistaken for advancement precisely because its velocity is high.
