# Why Theologians Stopped Asking God to Change His Mind

The prayer that only moves the one who prays

---

> If God is all-knowing, all-good, and unchanging, asking for specific outcomes seems incoherent — a problem raised most sharply not by atheists but by theologians themselves. The most compelling responses don't defend petitionary prayer on its original terms but reframe it as self-transformation rather than divine persuasion.

The most serious philosophical challenge to petitionary prayer comes not only from skeptics but, often more incisively, from within the faithful traditions themselves. If God is omniscient, omnibenevolent, impassible, and immutable, then petitionary prayer becomes incoherent — to pray for a specific outcome presumes either that we know better than an all-knowing God, or that God is not already tending to the situation with perfect care, or that a human being can change the will of an unchanging God. Kant captured this sharply: it is an absurd and presumptuous delusion to think that by insistent prayer one might deflect God from the plan of divine wisdom. In response, religious thinkers have offered rearticulations that sidestep the problem: one reframes prayer as a primal human expression — we pray because we must, not because it makes logical sense; another repositions prayer not as an attempt to influence God but as an attempt to influence oneself — to align with one's highest values, cultivate virtue, and open a calm interior space to face difficulty with honesty and courage. The serenity prayer is the paradigmatic example: its efficacy lies entirely in the subjective transformation of the one praying. These rearticulations do not abandon prayer — they rescue it from a framework it was never designed to fit.

## Apprentice

There's a deep problem with asking God for things, and it doesn't come from skeptics — it comes from the very beliefs that religious people hold most dear. If God already knows everything, already cares perfectly, and never changes, then what exactly is a prayer request supposed to accomplish? Are we informing God of something? Persuading a being who can't be persuaded? Kant put it bluntly: it's delusional to think you can nag God into changing a perfect plan.

Rather than abandoning prayer, thoughtful religious thinkers have quietly redefined what it's for. One approach says prayer is simply something humans need to do — a cry from the heart that doesn't require logical justification. Another, more philosophically satisfying move says prayer isn't about changing God at all. It's about changing yourself. When you pray for courage, patience, or acceptance, the act of praying is itself the mechanism of change.

The Serenity Prayer is the clearest example: it asks for wisdom to accept what can't be changed and courage to change what can. Its power lies entirely in what it does to the person saying it. These reframings don't kill prayer — they rescue it from a theological framework that was always going to make it look absurd.

## Adept

The deepest challenge to petitionary prayer is endogenous to theism itself. Classical divine attributes — omniscience, omnibenevolence, impassibility, immutability — render the act of requesting specific outcomes from God logically incoherent. If God already knows the situation, already wills the best possible response, and cannot be moved or altered by external causes, then petitionary prayer either presumes superior knowledge, doubts divine goodness, or attempts the metaphysically impossible. Kant articulated this with characteristic precision: to suppose that persistent entreaty could deflect God from the plan of divine wisdom is both absurd and presumptuous.

What makes this problem philosophically interesting is not the objection itself but the sophisticated rearticulations it has generated from within religious thought. Two major strategies emerge. The first treats prayer as a primal, pre-rational human expression — something closer to a cry than a proposition, justified not by its logical coherence but by its existential necessity. The second, more philosophically robust strategy repositions the causal arrow entirely: prayer does not act upon God but upon the one who prays. It becomes a practice of self-alignment — cultivating virtue, clarifying values, and opening interior space for honest confrontation with difficulty.

The Serenity Prayer serves as the paradigmatic case. Its entire efficacy is subjective: it transforms the disposition of the speaker without requiring any metaphysical claim about divine responsiveness. These rearticulations represent not the abandonment of prayer but its philosophical rescue — extracting it from a framework of divine causation it was never designed to inhabit and relocating it within a framework of human moral and psychological transformation.

## Magus

The incoherence of petitionary prayer under classical theism constitutes one of philosophy of religion's most elegant internal contradictions. The conjunction of omniscience, omnibenevolence, impassibility, and immutability renders any request-directed-at-God either epistemically presumptuous (the petitioner claims informational advantage over an omniscient being), morally impertinent (the petitioner doubts the benevolence already operative), or metaphysically impossible (the petitioner seeks to introduce change into an immutable will). Kant's formulation remains the sharpest articulation: petitionary prayer as an "absurd and presumptuous delusion" that one might deflect God from the plan of divine wisdom. Crucially, this objection gains its force not from naturalistic premises but from theism's own highest commitments — it is the God of Anselm and Aquinas, not the God of popular devotion, who makes petition unintelligible.

The philosophical interest lies in the rearticulation strategies deployed in response. One lineage — traceable through existentialist and phenomenological theology — relocates prayer in the domain of primal expression, treating it as Urschrei rather than propositional address: we pray because the human situation demands utterance, not because the utterance admits of truth-conditions. A second, more analytically tractable strategy inverts the causal vector entirely. Prayer becomes reflexive praxis — an exercise in axiological self-alignment, virtue cultivation, and the deliberate construction of interior dispositions adequate to confronting contingency with honesty and courage.

The Serenity Prayer functions as the paradigm case: its efficacy is entirely immanent, requiring no transcendent causal mechanism. These rearticulations do not dissolve prayer but perform a decisive relocation — from a metaphysics of divine responsiveness to a phenomenology of human transformation, rescuing the practice from a theological architecture whose internal logic was always destined to render it otiose.
