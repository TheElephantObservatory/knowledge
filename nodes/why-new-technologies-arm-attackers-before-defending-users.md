# Why New Technologies Arm Attackers Before Defending Users

The sword always arrives first.

---

> New technologies are consistently weaponized against ordinary people before defenses arrive. The sword-and-shield dynamic suggests the only structural protection for AI agents is an economic model where the agent serves — and is paid by — the individual user alone.

The sword-and-shield dynamic describes a recurring pattern in technological disruption: when a powerful new capability emerges, it is almost always weaponized against ordinary people before it is deployed to protect them. Spam calls are a perfect example — the bad actors got robocalling technology and flooded our phones with it years before any meaningful filtering existed. The same pattern played out with the internet more broadly: the infrastructure that was supposed to democratize information and improve civic participation instead became a vector for algorithmic manipulation, attention harvesting, and the industrialization of misinformation. The ad-supported model was the pivotal corruption — it misaligned the incentives of every platform against the interests of its users, generating roughly two dollars a month per user in advertising revenue in exchange for systematically distorting their perception of reality. The concern with AI agents is that the same dynamic will recur: the shield — agents that are genuinely beholden only to the individual they serve — arrives after the sword has already done its damage. The only structural defense against this is ensuring that the economic model of the agent is one where the agent is hired by and accountable to the individual user, not to an advertiser or platform. Once an agent's revenue comes from selling your attention or your data, its incentives are no longer aligned with yours. The local model running on your own hardware, open source, with no external dependency, is the most robust version of this — because its continued existence depends entirely on your finding it useful.

## Apprentice

There is a recurring pattern in how powerful technologies reach ordinary people: the harmful uses arrive first, and the protections come later. Spam robocalls flooded phones for years before decent call-filtering existed. The internet was supposed to empower citizens, but instead it became a machine for harvesting attention and spreading misinformation — largely because advertising became the dominant business model, paying platforms roughly two dollars per user per month to subtly work against those users' interests.

This pattern — called the sword-and-shield dynamic — raises urgent questions about AI agents, the software that will increasingly act on our behalf. If an AI agent is funded by advertisers or by selling your data, its loyalty is divided. It may look like it is working for you, but its real job is delivering your attention to someone else. The concern is that these compromised agents will become widespread before genuinely user-aligned alternatives are available.

The proposed defense is straightforward: the agent must be hired by you, paid by you, and accountable only to you. The strongest version of this is an open-source model running on your own hardware, with no dependency on any outside company. If the only reason it keeps running is that you find it useful, then its interests and yours are the same.

## Adept

The sword-and-shield dynamic identifies a structural regularity in technological disruption: offensive applications of a new capability reach scale before defensive ones do. Robocalling technology was industrialized by bad actors years before carrier-level filtering matured. The internet's ad-supported model represents a particularly consequential instance — by monetizing user attention at roughly two dollars per user per month, it systematically misaligned platform incentives against user welfare, converting infrastructure designed for democratic participation into a vector for algorithmic manipulation and epistemic corruption.

The framework's current application is to AI agents. The risk is that agent architectures funded by advertising, data brokerage, or platform commissions will proliferate before user-sovereign alternatives achieve comparable capability and distribution. An agent whose revenue derives from selling your attention or behavioral data is structurally incentivized to optimize against your interests, regardless of its surface-level design. This is not a bug in implementation but a consequence of the economic model itself.

The proposed countermeasure is to enforce alignment through the funding mechanism: an agent must be economically accountable solely to the individual it serves. The most robust instantiation is a locally hosted, open-source model with no external dependency — because its survival is contingent entirely on user utility. This eliminates the principal-agent problem at the architectural level. The insight is that alignment is not primarily a technical challenge in model training but an economic design problem: whoever pays the agent's costs determines whose interests it ultimately serves.

## Magus

The sword-and-shield dynamic formalizes an asymmetry intrinsic to the diffusion of general-purpose technologies: offensive deployment achieves scale along lower-friction gradients than defensive deployment, because exploitation of existing vulnerabilities requires less coordination than the construction of new protective infrastructure. The ad-supported internet is the canonical case study — a two-dollar-per-user-per-month revenue model effected a wholesale inversion of platform teleology, converting systems nominally designed for epistemic democratization into apparatus for attention commodification and systematic reality distortion. The corruption was not incidental but pivotal: advertising as the dominant business model constituted a structural misalignment that propagated through every downstream design decision.

Applied to AI agents, the framework predicts that agent architectures with exogenous funding dependencies — advertising, data brokerage, platform-mediated commissions — will achieve distribution dominance before user-sovereign alternatives reach functional parity. The principal-agent problem here is not metaphorical but literal: an agent whose continued operation depends on revenue streams orthogonal to user welfare will optimize for those streams, producing a surface-level simulation of alignment masking deep structural antagonism. The insight is that alignment in deployed agent systems is underdetermined by training objectives and overdetermined by economic architecture.

The prescribed intervention targets the incentive topology directly. An agent that is hired, compensated, and dismissible exclusively by the individual user collapses the principal-agent gap by construction. The maximally robust instantiation — a locally executed, open-source model with no external runtime dependency — achieves this by making the agent's persistence contingent solely on demonstrated user utility. This is alignment through economic selection pressure rather than through objective specification, recognizing that the latter is perpetually vulnerable to Goodhart dynamics while the former, when properly structured, is self-correcting.
