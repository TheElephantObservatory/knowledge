# Nihilism as Civilizational Symptom: From Lost Purpose to Lynch Mobs

Even Egypt had its true believers.

---

> Nihilism is not a philosophical choice but a civilizational symptom: when a society loses its shared sense of forward direction, the resulting frustration crystallizes into resentment and scapegoating. The only real antidote is not counter-attack but the collective courage to choose a new direction worth striving toward.

Nihilism is not primarily a philosophical position — it is a civilizational symptom with a precise structural cause. When a society loses its sense of a felic direction — a forward and upward orientation toward something worth striving for — the psychic pressure that accumulates turns inward or outward as resentment, and resentment organizes itself into lynch mobs. Bard traces a direct line from the collapse of shared purpose to scapegoating cultures, whether targeting the heterosexual white man or the coastal elite. Both are lynch mobs; neither provides a felic direction; neither offers exology — a path out of one paradigm and into another. The deeper diagnosis is what Bard, following Nietzsche, calls the 'last man': the person who believes the current social operating system is simply given and unchangeable — the Egyptian who tells Moses to accept slavery because that is just how things are. The antidote is not to attack the lynch mobs, which only produces a third lynch mob. The antidote is heroism in the original sense: the refusal to be resentful, the willingness to sit down with others and imagine a different world, and then begin building it. Grand Project A — the promised land reframed not as a utopia but as a chosen direction of effort — is what replaces the void. Without it, the frustration of a post-Christian, post-meaning culture will continue to generate exactly the polarization and self-destruction we are witnessing.

## Apprentice

When a culture loses any shared sense of where it's heading — any feeling that there's something worth building together — people don't just shrug and move on. They get frustrated, and that frustration turns into blame. Someone must be at fault for the emptiness. This is how scapegoating cultures form: one group blames "the elites," another blames "straight white men," and both are essentially doing the same thing — forming mobs around resentment rather than purpose.

Alexander Bard argues that nihilism isn't really a philosophy people adopt. It's what happens automatically when a civilization runs out of forward momentum. Following Nietzsche, he calls the deepest expression of this the "last man" — someone who assumes the current way of doing things is simply how reality works and can never change. It's the voice that says "this is just how things are, stop dreaming."

The solution isn't to fight the mobs, because that just creates another mob. The real antidote is what Bard calls heroism: the willingness to refuse resentment, sit down with others, and start imagining a genuinely different future — then actually work toward it. Not a perfect utopia, but a chosen direction of shared effort. Without that, a post-meaning culture will keep tearing itself apart.

## Adept

Bard's diagnosis reframes nihilism not as an intellectual stance but as a structural inevitability when a civilization loses what he terms its felic direction — a collectively felt orientation toward something worth striving for. Once that forward-and-upward vector collapses, psychic pressure doesn't dissipate; it converts into resentment. Resentment, in turn, self-organizes into scapegoating formations — lynch mobs — regardless of whether they target from the political left or right. The insight is that these opposing mobs are structurally identical: neither provides a felic direction, and neither constitutes exology, a viable path from one paradigm into another.

The deeper layer of the argument draws on Nietzsche's concept of the last man, which Bard repurposes as a civilizational archetype: the person who treats the current social operating system as ontologically fixed. This is the Egyptian who tells Moses that slavery is simply the nature of things. The last man is not merely complacent — he is the structural guarantor of nihilism's persistence, because he forecloses the possibility of paradigmatic departure.

Critically, Bard insists that attacking the lynch mobs only generates a third mob. The antidote is heroism in its pre-modern sense: the refusal of resentment coupled with the constructive will to envision and build an alternative. This is what he frames as Grand Project A — not a utopian endpoint but a deliberately chosen direction of collective effort. Without such a project, post-Christian, post-meaning societies will continue to produce exactly the polarization and civilizational self-destruction now visible everywhere.

## Magus

Bard's intervention repositions nihilism from the domain of philosophical voluntarism to that of civilizational pathology with a precise etiology: the disappearance of felic direction — the collectively inhabited vector of purposive striving that orients a culture forward and upward. The claim is not merely that meaning has eroded but that the absence of directional telos generates a specific psychodynamic cascade. Accumulated purposelessness converts into resentment, and resentment, following its own organizational logic, crystallizes into scapegoating structures. These formations — whether targeting patriarchal whiteness or cosmopolitan elites — are functionally isomorphic: neither constitutes exology, neither opens a passage from the exhausted paradigm into a successor frame. They are symptoms performing the role of solutions.

The Nietzschean substrate is explicit. Bard reactivates the figure of the last man not as a moral failing but as a structural position within civilizational decline: the subject who naturalizes the reigning social operating system, rendering paradigmatic departure literally unthinkable. The archetype is the Egyptian who counsels Moses to accept enslavement as ontological bedrock. This figure is nihilism's immune system — the mechanism by which a dying paradigm defends itself against its own supersession.

The prescribed counter-move is deliberately non-dialectical. Opposing the lynch mobs merely instantiates a third mob within the same resentment topology. Bard's alternative — heroism in the archaic, pre-ressentiment sense — demands the capacity to refuse reactive affect and instead engage in collective world-projection. Grand Project A names this: not utopia as terminus but direction as such, the re-establishment of felic orientation as civilizational infrastructure. Without it, the post-Christian vacuum guarantees the indefinite reproduction of polarization as the dominant mode of cultural metabolism.
