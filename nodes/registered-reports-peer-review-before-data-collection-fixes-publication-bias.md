# Registered Reports: Peer Review Before Data Collection Fixes Publication Bias

Judge the question, not the answer.

---

> Registered Reports flip peer review to before data collection, so studies are judged on their question and method rather than their results. This single structural change corrects publication bias, improves methodology in real time, and opens a path to merging funding and publishing into one process.

Registered Reports represent a deceptively simple but structurally profound inversion of the peer review process that realigns scientific incentives at their root. In the traditional system, peer review evaluates results — was the finding exciting, novel, positive? This creates systematic pressure toward false positives, selective reporting, and publication bias, evidenced by the fact that over 90% of published results in some fields are positive. Registered Reports shift the moment of peer review to before data collection: authors submit their research question and methodology, and if accepted, receive in-principle publication acceptance regardless of outcome. This single change produces two measurable effects. First, roughly 60% of published Registered Reports yield negative results — immediately demonstrating that publication bias is being corrected. Second, early citation data suggests these papers are cited at least as often as traditional papers, undermining the editor's objection that negative results kill impact factors. But the most profound mechanism may be that pre-data peer review actually improves the science itself: reviewers who flag methodological flaws before the study runs can prompt real corrections, whereas post-hoc review only generates regret. The further implication is structural unification — since Registered Reports look functionally identical to grant applications, there's an opportunity to merge funding decisions and publication commitments into a single review process, eliminating redundancy and guaranteeing that funded science gets reported.

## Apprentice

In most of science, a study gets reviewed after the results are in. This sounds reasonable, but it creates a deep problem: reviewers and editors are drawn to exciting, positive findings, which means scientists face enormous pressure to produce them — sometimes by cherry-picking data or quietly dropping experiments that didn't work. The result is a published literature skewed toward findings that may not hold up.

Registered Reports offer a surprisingly simple fix. Researchers submit their question and planned method for peer review before collecting any data. If the plan passes review, the journal commits to publishing the final paper no matter what the results show. This one change has dramatic consequences: around 60% of Registered Reports end up reporting negative or mixed results, compared to the usual rate of under 10%. That alone suggests the traditional system was filtering out most of the truth.

But the benefits go further. Because reviewers see the methodology before the study runs, they can actually fix problems — not just point them out after it's too late. And since a Registered Report looks a lot like a grant application, there's a natural opportunity to combine funding review and publication review into a single step, ensuring that every funded study gets reported rather than buried.

## Adept

Registered Reports enact a structurally simple but consequentially profound inversion of peer review: the evaluation point shifts from post-result to pre-data-collection. Authors submit their research question, hypotheses, and full methodology for review. If accepted in principle, the journal guarantees publication regardless of outcome. This decouples editorial decisions from result valence, directly attacking the incentive architecture that drives publication bias, p-hacking, and selective reporting — pathologies well-documented across biomedical, psychological, and social sciences.

The empirical signal is already striking. In fields where over 90% of traditionally published findings are positive, approximately 60% of Registered Reports yield null or negative results. This inversion is not evidence of worse science; it is evidence that the filter has been corrected. Early citation analyses further undermine the longstanding editorial objection that null results depress journal impact factors — Registered Reports appear to be cited at comparable or higher rates than conventional papers. The mechanism likely involves methodological quality: pre-data review allows genuine correction of design flaws, statistical power issues, and analytic ambiguities before they become irreversible.

The deeper structural implication concerns institutional redundancy. A Registered Report is functionally identical to a grant application: both present a question, a rationale, and a method for prospective evaluation. This convergence opens the possibility of a unified review process in which funding bodies and journals co-evaluate proposals, simultaneously committing resources and publication space. Such integration would eliminate the well-known problem of funded studies that never reach the literature, closing the loop between investment and accountability in the research enterprise.

## Magus

Registered Reports represent a first-order intervention on the incentive topology of scientific publishing. By relocating peer review to the pre-data stage, they sever the feedback loop between result valence and publication probability — the loop that has, across decades, selected for inflated effect sizes, undisclosed analytic flexibility, and the systematic suppression of null findings. The mechanism is deceptively minimal: in-principle acceptance conditional on methodological adequacy, independent of outcomes. Yet this single constraint restructures the entire decision space available to researchers, reviewers, and editors.

The empirical consequences are already legible. The base rate of positive results in Registered Reports drops to roughly 40%, against a background exceeding 90% in conventional literatures — a shift interpretable not as degraded discovery but as the restoration of a realistic prior over hypothesis space. Citation-level data, though early, suggests no impact penalty, challenging the revealed preference of editors who have long treated null results as reputational liabilities. More fundamentally, pre-data review converts the reviewer's role from forensic critic to methodological collaborator: identified flaws in power analysis, confound structure, or analytic specification can be remediated before they are baked into irreversible datasets.

The most far-reaching implication is architectural. Registered Reports and grant applications share identical epistemic structure — prospective evaluation of question, design, and analytic plan. This isomorphism invites institutional fusion: a single review event that simultaneously allocates funding and secures publication commitment. Such unification would address the well-documented file-drawer problem at its source, guaranteeing that publicly funded research enters the public record. The result is not merely a reform of peer review but a redesign of the accountability structure linking scientific investment to scientific disclosure.
