# The Blind Superorganism of Global Economic Growth

Billions of hands on a ghost wheel

---

> The global economy isn't run by anyone — it's a self-driving system where billions of individually sensible decisions add up to a collective outcome nobody chose and nobody can easily stop.

The global economy can be understood as a blind superorganism — not controlled by billionaires or politicians, but driven by an emergent metabolism that no individual or institution directs. Every actor in the system — individuals, families, small businesses, corporations, nation-states — is making locally rational decisions to optimize for profit and accumulation. Aggregate all of those decisions and you get a system with its own momentum, its own trajectory, its own logic. GDP is historically around 99% correlated with energy use and nearly 100% correlated with material throughput — copper, lithium, plastics, concrete, wood — all growing at roughly the same rate. Monetary systems are layered on top of this physical reality as a kind of overlay, but the underlying engine is thermodynamic. The system keeps growing until something in the ecological substrate breaks, or until growth stops and you face the geopolitical and social consequences of a shrinking pie — where on average everyone has less, and the competition over who bears that loss becomes destabilizing. What makes this so difficult is precisely that it is not a conspiracy. There is no villain to remove, no lever to pull. The runaway train has no driver. It has only the collective momentum of billions of rational local decisions aggregating into a globally irrational outcome. Understanding this is the prerequisite for any serious conversation about what governance, what constraints, and what alternative coordination mechanisms might actually be capable of changing the trajectory.

## Apprentice

Most people assume that if the world is heading somewhere dangerous, someone must be steering it there — a powerful government, a shadowy group of billionaires, or a conspiracy of some kind. But there's a more unsettling possibility: the global economy is more like a runaway train with no driver. Every person, business, and country is simply doing what makes sense for them — trying to grow, earn more, and stay competitive. When you add all those decisions together, you get a system with its own direction and momentum that nobody actually chose.

The physical evidence for this is striking. Economic output has historically tracked almost perfectly with energy use and the consumption of raw materials — copper, concrete, plastic, wood. Money is real, but it's layered on top of a deeper physical reality: the economy is, at its core, a machine that processes energy and matter.

This matters because it changes what kinds of solutions are even possible. If there's no villain, removing a villain won't fix anything. The challenge is figuring out what kinds of rules, institutions, or shared agreements could actually redirect a system that emerges from the sum of everyone's individual choices — and that's a much harder problem than it first appears.

## Adept

The global economy exhibits the properties of a superorganism: a complex adaptive system whose macro-level behavior is not reducible to the intentions of any individual actor or institution. Each node in the network — households, firms, states — optimizes locally for accumulation and competitive advantage. The aggregate result is a system with emergent properties, including a growth imperative, that no participant designed and no authority governs. This is not a metaphor but a structural description of how decentralized incentive systems produce path-dependent trajectories.

The thermodynamic grounding of this claim is empirically robust. GDP correlates historically at roughly 99% with primary energy consumption, and material throughput — across commodities as varied as copper, lithium, timber, and cement — scales at approximately the same rate as economic output. Monetary systems function as a coordination overlay, but the underlying engine is biophysical. The economy is, in the most literal sense, a dissipative structure: it maintains itself by continuously processing energy and exporting entropy.

The political implication is severe. Growth-dependent systems face a binary: expand or face distributional conflict over a contracting surplus. There is no stable equilibrium at zero growth within current institutional arrangements. What this framing demands is not moral condemnation of any actor, but a clear-eyed analysis of what forms of collective coordination — regulatory, constitutional, or otherwise — could operate at the systemic level rather than the individual one. That is the only register in which the problem is actually tractable.

## Magus

The conceptual move here is to treat the global economy not as an institution amenable to governance through the adjustment of incentives at the margin, but as an autopoietic system whose growth metabolism is constitutively prior to the political and monetary structures layered upon it. Drawing on thermodynamic economics in the tradition of Georgescu-Roegen, Ayres, and Warr, the argument holds that GDP is not a measure of value creation in any abstract sense but a proxy for the rate at which a society dissipates energy gradients and processes material stocks into waste. The near-unity correlation between economic output and both primary energy consumption and aggregate material throughput is not coincidental — it is the signature of a system whose 'growth' is, at the physical level, identical with increasing entropic throughput.

The superorganism framing extends this by invoking the logic of emergent behavior in complex adaptive systems. No agent in the network intends the macro-trajectory; each pursues locally Pareto-improving strategies within the fitness landscape defined by competitive accumulation. The system's momentum is therefore not a product of coordination but of its absence — a classically Smithian dynamic that, at civilizational scale and ecological limit, inverts from generative to destructive. The absence of a directing agent is not a reassuring finding but a diagnostic one: it forecloses the class of solutions premised on identifying and constraining a responsible party.

The governance implications are correspondingly radical. Standard regulatory interventions operate within the system's logic and are subject to competitive erosion — the Jevons paradox being the canonical instance. What the analysis demands is inquiry into coordination mechanisms capable of acting on the system's boundary conditions rather than its internal incentive structure: constitutional constraints on throughput, international frameworks with genuine enforcement capacity, or institutional redesigns that alter the fitness landscape itself. The framing is, in this sense, a prerequisite for distinguishing serious proposals from category errors.
