# Educational Skill Building versus AI Prosthetic Dependency

The muscle that remains when the supplement fades

---

> There's a crucial difference between tools that build your capabilities and tools that replace them. The question for AI in education isn't whether to allow it, but at what point its introduction stops building skill and starts substituting for it.

There's a fundamental distinction between technology as enhancement versus technology as prosthetic, and it maps directly onto how we should think about AI in education. An enhancement is something that, when removed, leaves you more capable than before — like lifting weights with creatine, where the muscle remains after you stop taking the supplement. A prosthetic, by contrast, replaces a function you never developed or are now losing. GPS is the paradigmatic case: if you learn to navigate by compass, map, sun position, and wind vectors, and then use GPS on top of that, the GPS is an enhancement layered onto a genuine skill. But if GPS is your first and only mode of navigation, you've acquired a prosthetic — and you're now dependent on infrastructure that can be knocked out, fail, or simply be unavailable. The same logic applies to AI writing tools, spell checkers, and LLMs in educational contexts. The question is never simply 'does this tool produce the desired output?' — a student who uses AI to write an essay and a student who writes it themselves may produce similar-looking documents, but they have acquired fundamentally different skills. One has built cognitive muscle; the other has acquired a dependency. The deeper and harder-to-acquire skill is also, almost always, the more resilient and transferable one. This means the design question for education is not 'should we allow AI?' but rather 'at what stage of skill development does AI introduction shift from enhancement to prosthetic replacement?' — and that question requires wisdom about human development that the technology itself cannot supply.

## Apprentice

Not all tools work the same way on the human mind. Some tools make you stronger — like weights in a gym, they build something that stays with you even after you put the tool down. Others do the work for you in a way that means you never build the underlying ability at all. The first kind is an enhancement; the second is a prosthetic.

GPS is a perfect example of the difference. If you already know how to navigate using a map, a compass, and the position of the sun, then GPS is a convenience layered on top of real skill. Take it away and you're still capable. But if GPS is the only way you've ever found your way around, you haven't learned navigation — you've just borrowed it. The same logic applies to AI writing tools in schools.

Two students can hand in essays that look identical — one written by the student, one assembled with AI help — but they've ended up in very different places. One has practiced thinking, structuring arguments, and finding words for ideas. The other has a document but not the skill. The real question for educators isn't 'should AI be allowed?' but 'at what point in a student's development does using AI prevent the skill from forming in the first place?' That's a question about human growth, and no algorithm can answer it.

## Adept

A productive distinction exists between technology as cognitive enhancement and technology as functional prosthetic. An enhancement amplifies a capacity that has already been internalized — when the tool is removed, the underlying competence persists and may even be stronger for having been exercised under load. A prosthetic, by contrast, substitutes for a function that was never developed or has atrophied. The difference is not in the tool itself but in the developmental sequence in which it is introduced.

The GPS case makes this concrete. A navigator who has built genuine wayfinding skill through dead reckoning, map reading, and environmental cue interpretation, and who then adopts GPS, retains full capability when the technology fails. Someone who has only ever navigated by GPS has acquired infrastructure dependency, not skill. Crucially, the prosthetic user may be indistinguishable from the skilled user in normal operating conditions — the deficit only becomes visible under stress or constraint.

This framework reframes the debate about AI in education. The output equivalence problem — the fact that AI-assisted and independently produced work can appear identical — obscures a more important divergence in what has actually been acquired. The pedagogically significant question is not whether AI should be permitted but at what stage of skill formation its introduction forecloses the development of the deeper competency it is meant to support. This requires a developmental theory of learning that is prior to and independent of any particular technology — a form of wisdom about human cognitive growth that cannot be derived from the tools themselves.

## Magus

The enhancement-prosthetic distinction maps onto a deeper epistemological fault line in the philosophy of technology: the difference between tools that are genuinely post-cognitive — applied after competence is internalized — and those that are pre-cognitive, introduced before the relevant neural and conceptual scaffolding has consolidated. Vygotsky's zone of proximal development gestures at this, but the concern here is less about scaffolding toward mastery than about the conditions under which scaffolding becomes permanent load-bearing structure, precluding the very internalization it was meant to facilitate.

The GPS example is paradigmatic precisely because it isolates the variable of developmental sequence from the variable of tool capability. The instrument is identical in both cases; what differs is the prior state of the user's cognitive architecture. For the trained navigator, GPS is an exaptation — a repurposing of existing competence. For the GPS-native, it is a constitutive dependency, and the apparent functional equivalence between the two users dissolves under any condition that removes the technological substrate. This has direct implications for how we interpret output-based assessments: surface equivalence in product masks radical divergence in the generative capacity that produced it.

Applied to large language models in educational contexts, the argument suggests that the standard policy debate — permissibility, detection, academic integrity — is operating at the wrong level of abstraction. The prior question is developmental and phenomenological: at what point in the formation of a given competency does LLM assistance shift from elaborating an emerging schema to substituting for the schema-formation process itself? This is not a question the technology can answer, nor one amenable to empirical resolution without a prior normative account of what it means to have genuinely acquired a skill — which is to say, it is irreducibly a question in the philosophy of education.
