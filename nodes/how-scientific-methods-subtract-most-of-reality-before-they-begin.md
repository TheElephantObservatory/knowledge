# How Scientific Methods Subtract Most of Reality Before They Begin

The pizza contains the cosmos, minus what we removed.

---

> Everything is made *with* more than it is made *of*. Pizza requires the Agricultural Revolution; atoms require the Scientific Revolution. Stripping away these relational, process-based dimensions — what Bonnitta Roy calls 'substracting' — may render up to 97% of reality structurally invisible.

Process relational philosophy proposes that reality does not bottom out in matter or information, but in experience and relation. To illustrate this, consider pizza: dough is made of wheat, but wheat is made *with* the Agricultural Revolution — and the Agricultural Revolution is made with human evolution, which is made with the entire Cosmos. You cannot point to the Agricultural Revolution inside the pizza, yet the pizza is unintelligible without it. The same logic applies to the atom: the atom is made *with* the Scientific Revolution, with the industries that support it, with the cultural innovations that enable those industries. This does not make the atom less real than the dough — it reveals that our conventional understanding of the atom subtracts roughly 90% of what it actually is. It strips away all the verb-like, process-relational attributes that enable the atom to exist at all. This act of subtraction — which Bonnitta Roy calls 'substracting,' meaning both subtracting and abstracting — is endemic to certain scientific methodologies. David Graeber's archaeological work makes the same point: we can only reconstruct history from things built to endure, never from basket weaving, tenderness, or pedagogy. The glue, the relations, the withness — these are systematically invisible to methods that privilege durable objects. Roy even speculates that the 97% of matter and energy physicists call 'dark' — which they cannot locate — may be precisely what this methodology structurally excludes. The deeper point is not skepticism about science, but a recognition that being wholly true within a partial context is still being in a partial context. What it means to be 100% true inside an extremely partial frame is itself part of the inquiry.

## Apprentice

Think about a pizza. It's made of dough, sauce, cheese. But dough is made of wheat — and wheat only exists because of thousands of years of agriculture, which only exists because of human evolution, which only exists because of the entire history of the cosmos. You can't point to the Agricultural Revolution inside the pizza, but the pizza makes no sense without it. The same is true of an atom: it only becomes knowable through centuries of scientific culture, institutions, and industry.

This insight, developed by philosopher Bonnitta Roy, suggests that when we describe something only by what it's made *of*, we throw away most of what it actually is — all the relationships, processes, and histories that make it possible. She calls this move 'substracting': subtracting and abstracting at the same time. Anthropologist David Graeber made a similar point about history: we reconstruct the past from durable objects like stone tools, but we can never recover the basket weaving, the tenderness, or the teaching that shaped daily life.

The provocation goes further. Roy speculates that the 97% of matter and energy physicists label 'dark' — because they cannot detect it — might correspond to exactly what these object-focused methods are built to miss. The point isn't that science is wrong. It's that being completely correct within a narrow frame is still being inside a narrow frame.

## Magus

Bonnitta Roy's process relational philosophy reframes the foundational question of ontology: reality does not bottom out in substance, structure, or information, but in experience and relation. The critical analytic distinction is between composition (*made of*) and co-arising (*made with*). Dough is made of wheat; wheat is made with the Agricultural Revolution; the Agricultural Revolution is made with the full arc of hominid evolution and cosmogenesis. These 'with' dimensions are not locatable inside the entity yet are constitutive of its intelligibility. Applied to the atom, this reveals that standard physical description — isolating intrinsic properties — performs a massive subtraction of the relational, processual, and historical conditions without which no atom is encountered or known. Roy estimates this subtraction at roughly 90% of what the atom actually is.

She names this operation 'substracting' — simultaneously subtracting and abstracting — identifying it as endemic to methodologies that privilege enduring, noun-like entities over verb-like relational process. David Graeber's critique of archaeological epistemology provides independent corroboration: material culture biases reconstruction toward the durable, systematically excluding the ephemeral — tenderness, pedagogy, basket weaving — that constituted the relational fabric of lived worlds. The withness, the glue, is methodologically invisible.

Roy's most provocative extension targets dark matter and dark energy: the 97% of the universe's mass-energy that physics cannot locate may index precisely what its object-privileging methodology structurally excludes. This is not528 skepticism but a second-order epistemological claim: being wholly true within a partial context does not escape partiality. The inquiry becomes reflexive — what does it mean for a framework to achieve internal completeness while remaining constitutively partial? Process relational philosophy positions this question not as a limitation to overcome but as the generative site of deeper ontological inquiry.
