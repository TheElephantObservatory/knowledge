# Why Impartial Ethics Requires a God You Cannot Be

The view from nowhere is still a view.

---

> Effective altruism smuggles in a God's-eye view it claims to have rejected — a perfectly impartial, disembodied perspective. Autopoethics counters that our boundedness isn't a bias to correct but the precondition for any moral agency at all.

Effective altruism and classical utilitarianism, despite presenting themselves as rigorously secular and rational, contain a hidden theological structure: the 'view from nowhere.' This is the assumption that a moral agent can and should adopt a perfectly impartial, disembodied perspective — seeing all suffering as equally weighted regardless of proximity, relationship, or context. But this is precisely the God's-eye view that rationality research has increasingly undermined. We are not disembodied logical processors. We are first-person, embodied, finite beings embedded in particular relationships and particular contexts. The view from nowhere is not just cognitively impossible — it is normatively problematic, because it asks us to act as though our boundedness doesn't exist, which leads to systematic errors. Autopoethics begins by taking seriously what rationality has already established: that subjectivity is real, that first-person experience is not an inconvenient bias to be corrected but a structural feature of what it means to be a living agent. The ethical implications of this are profound. It is not irrational to care more about some people than others. It is not irrational to prioritize your own maintenance, your relationships, your local context. These are not moral failures — they are the necessary conditions for any coherent moral action at all. The hard problem of ethics is not how to allocate your excess resources most effectively. That is actually the easy problem. The hard problem is maintaining the living system that makes such questions even askable.

## Apprentice

Effective altruism asks you to weigh all suffering equally — a stranger across the world should matter exactly as much as your own child. This sounds admirably rational, but it quietly assumes you can step outside your own life and see everything from a perfectly neutral, godlike vantage point. Rationality research has shown this is impossible. We are not floating calculators. We are living beings rooted in particular bodies, relationships, and places.

The insight here is that caring more about people close to you isn't a moral failure — it's a necessary feature of being a real person capable of doing anything good at all. If you burn yourself out trying to act like a disembodied impartial optimizer, you destroy the very system that makes ethical action possible.

This reframing, called autopoethics, argues that the truly hard problem of ethics isn't figuring out where to send your donations most efficiently. That's actually the easier question. The harder and more fundamental problem is sustaining yourself — your health, your relationships, your capacity to care — so that moral questions can even arise for you in the first place.

## Adept

Effective altruism and classical utilitarianism present themselves as rigorously secular, yet they harbor a concealed theological structure: the demand that moral agents adopt a perfectly impartial, disembodied perspective — what Thomas Nagel called "the view from nowhere." This perspective treats all suffering as fungible and equally weighted regardless of relational proximity, embodied context, or personal history. But this is precisely the God's-eye view that bounded rationality research, embodied cognition, and enactivism have progressively dismantled. The supposedly rational ideal turns out to be a secularized omniscience.

Autopoethics takes this critique as its starting point. If subjectivity is not an epistemic defect but a structural feature of living agency — as first-person phenomenology and autopoietic theory both suggest — then partiality is not bias. Caring more for those within your relational web is not irrationality leaking into your moral calculus; it is the expression of a finite, self-maintaining system operating coherently within its actual conditions. To demand otherwise is to demand that the agent act as though its boundedness doesn't exist, which produces systematic normative distortions.

The reframing is sharp: what effective altruism treats as the central ethical problem — optimal resource allocation across impartially weighted outcomes — is actually the easy problem of ethics. The hard problem is maintaining the living system whose coherence makes moral reasoning possible at all. Self-care, relational investment, and contextual prioritization are not concessions to weakness. They are preconditions for any sustainable ethical agency. Ethics that ignores its own enabling conditions is not rigorous — it is structurally incoherent.

## Magus

The critique advanced here identifies a cryptotheological kernel within effective altruism and classical utilitarianism: the normative demand for a "view from nowhere" — Nagel's phrase repurposed to expose what is functionally an omniscient, disembodied moral perspective secularized beyond recognition. The impartiality axiom, which treats all qualia of suffering as commensurable and context-independent, presupposes precisely the aperspectival stance that post-Cartesian rationality research — from Simon's bounded rationality through Varela's enactivism to contemporary 4E cognition — has rendered untenable as a description of actual cognitive agents. The claim is not merely that such impartiality is difficult; it is that the demand itself constitutes a category error, asking first-person, autopoietic systems to operate under third-person, allocentric norms.

Autopoethics regrounds normativity in the structural features of living agency. Drawing on the autopoietic tradition (Maturana, Varela) and its implicit ethical consequences, it holds that subjectivity is not epistemic noise but the constitutive condition of agentive coherence. Partiality — toward self-maintenance, proximal relationships, local context — is therefore not a deviation from rationality but its embodied expression. The asymmetry between self and other is not a bias to be corrected by utilitarian calculus; it is the topological structure of any bounded evaluative system.

The pivotal inversion: effective altruism's central question — how to allocate marginal resources across impartially weighted outcomes — is recast as the easy problem of ethics, tractable precisely because it operates within an already-functioning agentive system. The hard problem is the maintenance of that system itself: the metabolic, relational, and phenomenological integrity without which no moral question is askable. An ethics that systematically undermines its own enabling conditions is not merely impractical — it is performatively self-refuting.
