# Three Compounding Vectors Driving AI Capability Beyond Predictable Horizons

The map expires before the ink dries.

---

> AI capability is advancing along three simultaneous vectors — model intelligence, hardware cost, and agent orchestration — and compounding them together means strategic planning must treat human involvement as an adjustable parameter, not a fixed assumption, because the most dangerous mistake is underestimating where things will be in six months.

There are three simultaneous vectors of improvement in AI capability, and the strategic mistake most people make is tracking only one of them. The first is model capability — the raw intelligence of the underlying systems, which is improving at a surprisingly fast pace. The second is hardware cost — GPU and specialized chip economics are improving faster than Moore's Law because GPUs are architecturally simple, just one thing stamped out at massive scale, unlike the intricate complexity of CPUs. The third, and least appreciated, is agent frameworks — the orchestration layer that determines how models are composed, sequenced, and directed to solve complex multi-step problems. This third vector is growing at a speed we can barely measure, partly because it's software, and partly because the thing these models are best at is writing code. So the capacity to build agent frameworks has itself been accelerated by a factor of ten to thirty by the very models being orchestrated. When you compound these three vectors together — cheaper hardware, smarter models, and explosively improving orchestration — the strategic implication is that you cannot plan beyond a six-week horizon with any confidence. But that doesn't mean you abandon planning. It means your planning must have flexibility architecturally baked in. The human-in-the-loop should be parameterized — designed as a slider that can be adjusted as capability increases — rather than fixed at any particular level of automation. The most common strategic error isn't assuming too much right now; it's assuming too little six months out.

## Apprentice

Most people track AI progress by watching how smart the models get. But that's only one of three forces improving at the same time. The second is that the chips powering AI are getting cheaper faster than most technology historically has, because they're simpler to manufacture at scale than traditional processors. The third — and the one almost nobody appreciates — is the software that coordinates these models to solve complex, multi-step problems. This orchestration layer is improving at breathtaking speed, partly because AI itself is extraordinarily good at writing the code that makes orchestration better. The tools are accelerating their own improvement.

When you multiply these three trends together — smarter models, cheaper hardware, and rapidly improving coordination software — the combined pace of change becomes almost impossible to predict beyond a few weeks. This doesn't mean planning is useless. It means plans need to be designed with built-in flexibility. Rather than deciding today exactly how much human oversight a process needs, the smart move is to treat human involvement like a dial that can be turned up or down as capabilities shift.

The key strategic warning here is asymmetric: the biggest risk isn't assuming AI can do too much right now. It's assuming it will still be limited six months from now. Planning that locks in today's limitations as permanent fixtures will be obsolete almost immediately.

## Adept

This insight identifies three compounding vectors of AI capability improvement that most strategic actors fail to track simultaneously. The first — model capability — is widely followed but still routinely underestimated in its pace. The second — hardware economics — is declining faster than Moore's Law projections because GPUs, unlike CPUs, are architecturally simple devices manufactured through massive parallelized stamping, yielding steeper cost curves. The third and most underappreciated vector is agent frameworks: the orchestration layer governing how models are composed, sequenced, and directed through multi-step reasoning chains. This layer is improving at nearly unmeasurable speed because it is pure software, and because the models being orchestrated are themselves exceptional at writing the code that constitutes these frameworks. The recursive loop — AI accelerating the development of AI orchestration — represents a compounding factor of roughly 10x to 30x on development velocity.

The strategic implication of compounding these three vectors is that confident planning horizons have collapsed to approximately six weeks. But the response should not be to abandon planning — it should be to architect flexibility into every system design. Specifically, the human-in-the-loop should be parameterized rather than fixed: designed as an adjustable slider governing the degree of human oversight, capable of being dialed down as model and orchestration capabilities increase.

The most prevalent strategic error is directionally wrong in a specific way. Organizations are not primarily at risk of over-automating today; they are at risk of under-estimating capability six months out and building systems with rigid human dependencies that will become bottlenecks precisely when the technology is ready to move past them.

## Magus

The analytical framework here decomposes AI capability advancement into three orthogonal but multiplicatively compounding vectors, arguing that strategic failure overwhelmingly stems from univariate tracking. Model capability — the intensive margin of raw system intelligence — receives disproportionate attention. Hardware cost deflation, the extensive margin, is declining faster than Moore's Law due to the architectural simplicity of GPU dies relative to CPUs: they are fundamentally homogeneous stamp-and-replicate artifacts rather than heterogeneous logic designs, yielding superior scaling economics. The third vector, agent framework sophistication — the orchestration topology governing model composition, task decomposition, and sequential reasoning — is evolving at a rate that resists conventional measurement. This is partly attributable to its purely software-instantiated nature, but more critically to a recursive acceleration dynamic: large language models are disproportionately capable at code generation, meaning the substrate being orchestrated is simultaneously the primary tool accelerating orchestration development. Conservative estimates place this reflexive amplification at 10x–30x on framework development velocity.

The compound product of these three vectors collapses reliable strategic planning horizons to approximately six weeks. The prescribed response is not planning abandonment but architectural flexibility as a first-order design constraint. Human-in-the-loop governance should be parameterized — implemented as a continuously adjustable control surface rather than a discrete architectural commitment to a fixed automation level. This reframes human oversight from a structural constant to a tunable variable responsive to capability state.

The deepest claim is about the asymmetry of strategic error. The distribution of costly mistakes is not centered on premature over-automation but on systematic underestimation of near-term capability trajectories. Organizations that encode current limitations as permanent architectural assumptions are constructing precisely the rigidities that will become binding constraints when the compounding vectors deliver their six-month product — a capability surface that renders today's conservative designs obsolete.
