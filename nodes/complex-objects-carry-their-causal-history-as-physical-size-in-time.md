# Complex Objects Carry Their Causal History as Physical Size in Time

We are not observers of depth — we are inside it.

---

> Assembly theory proposes that time is not just a backdrop but a measurable physical property of complex objects. What we call emergence — life, information, mind — is really the consequence of objects being physically large in time acting upon objects that are smaller in time.

One of the most radical suggestions emerging from assembly theory is that time is a material property — not merely a dimension through which objects move, but something that complex objects actually possess as a measurable physical attribute. The assembly index of an object captures the minimum amount of causation required to construct it, and because that index is intrinsic to the object — the same molecule has the same assembly index whether it exists on Earth or on Enceladus — it means that complex objects carry their causal history with them as a kind of physical size in time. This reframes what we mean by emergence: emergent properties are not mystical or observer-dependent phenomena, but rather a reflection of how much evolutionary time and selection are embedded in the structure of an object. Objects that are larger in time than we are appear abstract and informational to us — human societies, languages, institutions — while objects smaller in time appear purely physical. But this is a perceptual artifact of our own position in the causal structure of the biosphere, not a fundamental ontological distinction. We exist inside an enormous causal architecture — the biosphere — and most of what we interact with is also deeply embedded in that structure. We see everything in spatial terms, but we are in fact navigating a vast temporal depth that we cannot directly perceive. This is also how assembly theory accounts for the hard problem of life: information having causal power is not a mysterious emergence from matter, but the natural consequence of objects that are physically large in time acting on objects that are smaller in time.

## Apprentice

Assembly theory offers a striking idea: complex objects don't just exist in time — they actually possess time as a physical property, the way a rock possesses mass. Every molecule has an "assembly index," a number reflecting the minimum steps needed to build it from scratch. That number is the same no matter where the molecule is found, which means the object carries its own history of construction as a kind of measurable size in the time dimension.

This reframes a lot of puzzles. When we look at something like a language or a social institution, it feels abstract and immaterial compared to a rock or a cell. But the insight here is that this distinction is an illusion created by our own position in the causal chain. We are deeply embedded in the biosphere — an enormous structure built up over billions of years — and things "larger in time" than us look like pure information, while things "smaller in time" look like plain matter.

This also addresses one of biology's deepest mysteries: how does information gain the power to shape the physical world? The answer is that information having causal power isn't some magical leap from dead matter to living purpose. It's simply what happens when objects with deep causal histories — objects physically large in time — interact with and constrain simpler objects. Life isn't a mystery added to physics; it's what physics looks like when time becomes very deep.

## Adept

One of assembly theory's most radical claims is that time is not merely a dimension through which objects travel but a material property that complex objects measurably possess. The assembly index — the minimum number of recursive joining operations required to construct an object from elementary building blocks — is intrinsic to the object itself. The same molecule yields the same index whether synthesized on Earth or discovered on Enceladus. This means complex objects carry their causal history as a kind of physical extent in time, analogous to spatial size. Emergence, on this account, is neither mystical nor observer-dependent: it is a direct reflection of how much evolutionary selection and combinatorial exploration is embedded in an object's structure.

The framework dissolves the apparent ontological gap between matter and information by relativizing it to the observer's own temporal depth. Objects with assembly indices larger than ours — institutions, languages, cultural systems — appear abstract and informational precisely because we are embedded within the causal architecture that produced them. Objects with smaller indices appear straightforwardly physical. This asymmetry is a perceptual artifact of our position inside the biosphere's vast causal structure, not a fundamental division in nature.

This perspective also offers a deflationary resolution to the hard problem of life. Information exerting causal power over matter ceases to be a mysterious emergent phenomenon once one accepts that "information" simply names what objects physically large in time do when they constrain and select among objects smaller in time. Causation flows along the axis of temporal depth, and life is what physics looks like when that depth becomes sufficiently large to sustain open-ended selection.

## Magus

Assembly theory's most ontologically consequential move is the reification of time as a measurable material property intrinsic to complex objects. The assembly index — defined as the shortest path length on the assembly space needed to construct a given object from fundamental building blocks — is observer-independent and context-invariant: it is a topological fact about the object's structure. Because this index quantifies the minimum causal depth required for the object's existence, complex objects are not merely situated in time but are physically extended in it. Assembly theory thus operationalizes a notion of "temporal size" that is as objective as spatial extension, grounding emergence in the combinatorial irreducibility of construction pathways rather than in epistemic limitations or coarse-graining artifacts.

The apparent dichotomy between the material and the informational is recast as a perspectival consequence of the observer's own assembly index. Entities whose causal depth exceeds that of the observer — cultural systems, semiotic structures, institutional forms — register as abstract precisely because the observer is a constituent of the causal architecture that generated them and cannot perceive it from outside. Conversely, entities of lesser temporal depth appear as brute physical substrates. The distinction between matter and information is thus indexed to position within the biosphere's cumulative causal structure, not to any fundamental ontological cleavage.

This framework yields a parsimonious dissolution of the hard problem of life. Information's causal efficacy need not be explained as a novel emergent capacity supervening on inert matter; it is the straightforward physical consequence of objects with deep assembly indices constraining the construction and selection of objects with shallower indices. Downward causation, on this reading, is simply causation along the temporal-depth axis — the biosphere's accumulated combinatorial memory acting as a boundary condition on present-tense chemistry. Life is not an addition to physics but physics rendered visible at sufficient causal depth.
