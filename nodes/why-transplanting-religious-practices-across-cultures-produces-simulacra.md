# Why Transplanting Religious Practices Across Cultures Produces Simulacra

You cannot borrow a world.

---

> Spiritual traditions cannot be extracted from the cultures that produced them without becoming simulacra. The attempt to cherry-pick content from multiple religions while ignoring the lived worlds that gave them meaning repeats the same extractive hubris that has proven catastrophic in ecology.

One of the most underappreciated problems with the Western appropriation of Eastern spiritual traditions is that religion is not separable from its context — and context is not a minor detail, it is the entire world in which the content lives. Trying to practice Buddhism in Arizona without being immersed in a culture that has fully integrated the implications, presuppositions, and axiology of Buddhism is like claiming to be fluent in Spanish having only read Spanish books and never spoken with a native speaker. You may be able to hold a conversation, but when you encounter someone for whom the language is native, there will be a fundamental incommensurability. Everything at the end of the day is content and context. The context of a religion is an entire world. When you abstract the content of a religion away from its context and plant it in a different context, you are not practicing that religion — you are practicing a simulacrum of it. This is not a minor caveat. It is a structural problem with the entire project of integral spirituality and the new age synthesis: the attempt to take the best content from multiple traditions and integrate it into a new whole, while ignoring that the content is inseparable from the lived world that generated it. There is also a deeper layer: your family language, the things laid down at six months old, two years old, are far deeper than your cognitive capacity to be aware of them. Which values are foregrounded, which are backgrounded — these are not trivial. They are a landscape. And the modernist hubris of imagining you can think your way out of that landscape, or pluck yourself out of it and select particulars from other traditions, is the same hubris deployed in ecology that we have learned to recognize as catastrophic.

## Apprentice

When someone tries to practice Buddhism in a Western suburb, they face a problem that goes far deeper than getting the rituals right. A religion isn't just a set of beliefs and techniques — it's an entire world. The values, assumptions, and ways of seeing reality are woven into the culture that carries the tradition: into family life, language, childhood formation, and countless unspoken agreements about what matters. Strip the teachings out of that world and transplant them into a completely different one, and what you get isn't really that religion anymore. It's something new wearing the old name.

This matters because the modern spiritual project — sometimes called integral spirituality or new age synthesis — is built on the idea that you can take the best insights from many traditions and combine them into something greater. But this assumes the insights are portable, like tools you can move from one workshop to another. In reality, they're more like plants that depend on a specific ecosystem.

There's an even deeper issue. The values and orientations laid down in early childhood — before conscious memory — shape how a person sees the world in ways they can never fully access or override through thinking alone. The belief that you can simply think your way out of your cultural formation and freely adopt elements from other traditions is a kind of arrogance. It's the same arrogance that, applied to the natural world, has led to ecological disasters: the assumption that you can extract parts from a complex system without consequences.

## Adept

This insight identifies a structural flaw in the Western appropriation of Eastern spiritual traditions and, more broadly, in the entire project of integral or synthetic spirituality. The core argument is that religious content is ontologically inseparable from its context — where "context" means not background information but the entire lived world of a culture: its presuppositions, axiology, embodied practices, relational norms, and developmental formation. To extract doctrines, meditation techniques, or ethical frameworks from Buddhism, Hinduism, or Sufism and transplant them into a Western cultural matrix is not to practice those traditions but to produce a simulacrum — something structurally different that merely resembles the original at the level of propositional content.

The analogy offered is linguistic: reading Spanish books does not make one a native speaker. Fluency requires immersion in a world where the language is not a tool but a habitat. Similarly, spiritual fluency requires inhabiting the world a tradition has built — not merely adopting its vocabulary. The incommensurability between a practitioner embedded in the native context and one operating from an imported version is not a gap that more study can close; it is a difference in kind.

A deeper dimension concerns pre-cognitive formation. The values foregrounded and backgrounded in early childhood — the "family language" laid down before the age of reflective awareness — constitute a landscape that no amount of adult cognitive effort can fully transcend. The modernist confidence that one can rationally select from multiple traditions and assemble a superior synthesis is identified here as a species of the same extractive hubris that ecology has taught us to recognize as catastrophic: the belief that complex, interdependent systems can be disaggregated and reassembled without fundamental loss.

## Magus

The argument advanced here constitutes a radical critique of decontextualized spiritual practice by insisting on the irreducibility of context to a mere variable. Context, in this framing, is not supplementary to religious content but constitutive of it — the entire horizon of intelligibility within which doctrines, practices, and soteriological aims acquire their meaning. What the integral spirituality movement and new age synthesis propose as ecumenical enrichment is, on this analysis, an operation of abstraction that necessarily produces simulacra: objects that preserve surface morphology while severing the generative conditions that made them what they were. The content-context distinction, seemingly innocent, conceals an ontological violence.

The linguistic analogy — fluency-through-reading versus native habitation — points toward a Wittgensteinian insight: meaning is use within a form of life, not a detachable semantic payload. But the argument pushes further into developmental territory. The axiological landscape laid down in pre-reflective infancy — which values are foregrounded, which backgrounded, which rendered invisible — constitutes a formation that is not merely difficult to override cognitively but is, in significant measure, inaccessible to the very cognitive apparatus that would attempt the override. This is not a claim about cultural relativism but about the depth-structure of enculturation and its resistance to voluntarist transcendence.

The final move is the most provocative: the identification of modernist spiritual eclecticism with the extractive logic that has produced ecological catastrophe. Just as industrial modernity imagined it could disaggregate ecosystems, harvest discrete resources, and reassemble functional wholes, the spiritual synthesizer imagines traditions as modular repositories from which optimal components can be selected. Both projects share a common hubris — the denial that complex, co-evolved systems possess an integrity that cannot survive the extraction of their parts.
