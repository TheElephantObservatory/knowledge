# How Attention Gets Hijacked Before You Notice It's Gone

The cursor blinks in someone else's loop.

---

> Human consciousness processes only about fifty bits per second from millions arriving through perception. The modern information environment exploits this bottleneck — optimizing for engagement rather than helping attention land on what actually matters for a person's flourishing.

There is a deep and underappreciated problem with how we think about information overload, and it connects directly to the question of relevance realization. Our perceptual systems are bringing in millions of bits per second, yet conscious cognition updates at roughly fifty bits per second. There is a vast compression that must happen between the flood of raw perception and the narrow beam of conscious attention. Relevance realization is the name for the process by which this happens well or badly. When it is well-tuned — when the cursor of attention moves through objects in a sequence that tracks what actually matters for your flourishing in your particular context — you are adaptive, effective, wise. When it is mal-tuned — when the cursor gets stuck in a loop of morbid rumination, or is hijacked by the engineered compulsiveness of notification systems — you are suboptimal in the most literal sense: your entity is poorly fitted to its field. The information environment we have built does not respect the architecture of human attention. It is optimized for engagement, not for adaptive relevance realization. And the result is a population whose attentional cursors are being systematically mis-tuned — not by malice necessarily, but by the logic of systems that profit from capturing attention regardless of whether that attention serves the person's actual flourishing.

## Apprentice

Every second, your senses take in an enormous flood of information — far more than your conscious mind could ever process. Something in you has to decide what matters and what doesn't, filtering millions of signals down to the tiny sliver you actually notice and think about. When this filtering works well, your attention lands on the things that genuinely matter for your life. When it breaks down, you get stuck in anxious loops or find yourself scrolling endlessly through content that leaves you feeling worse.

The problem is that the digital world we've built doesn't respect how this filtering works. Apps, platforms, and notification systems are designed to grab your attention — not to help you pay attention to the right things. They profit when your eyes stay on the screen, regardless of whether that screen time is good for you.

The result is a widespread misalignment. Billions of people are having their attention systematically pulled toward things that serve someone else's interests rather than their own. This isn't necessarily driven by evil intent — it's simply what happens when systems are built to maximize engagement without any regard for whether that engagement helps people live well.

## Adept

Relevance realization — the cognitive process by which organisms compress a vast perceptual field into a narrow, actionable stream of conscious attention — is arguably the master problem of adaptive cognition. The numbers are stark: human perception ingests on the order of millions of bits per second, while conscious cognition updates at roughly fifty. The quality of the compression between these two rates determines whether an agent is well-fitted to its environment or chronically maladapted. When relevance realization is well-tuned, the attentional cursor tracks what genuinely matters for flourishing in context. When it is mal-tuned — locked in ruminative loops or captured by engineered salience — the agent becomes suboptimal in a precise, measurable sense.

The contemporary information environment represents a historically unprecedented challenge to this process. Digital architectures are optimized along engagement metrics that are structurally indifferent to whether captured attention serves the user's adaptive interests. Notification systems, infinite scroll, and algorithmic content selection function as exogenous perturbations on the relevance realization machinery, systematically biasing it toward stimuli that are salient but not genuinely relevant.

This framing recontextualizes the familiar discourse around "information overload." The deeper problem is not excess quantity but degraded quality of attentional selection. A population whose relevance realization is being chronically mis-tuned is not merely distracted — it is being rendered less wise, less adaptive, and less capable of the contextual discernment that complex environments demand. The causal mechanism need not be conspiratorial; it emerges naturally from incentive structures that decouple engagement from flourishing.

## Magus

The compression ratio between perceptual bandwidth and conscious throughput — roughly six orders of magnitude, from millions of bits per second to approximately fifty — constitutes the fundamental constraint within which all higher cognition operates. Relevance realization, as theorized most extensively by John Vervaeke, names the set of processes that perform this compression adaptively: the dynamic, context-sensitive selection of what warrants attentional foregrounding from the combinatorially explosive background of potential saliences. It is not a single mechanism but an ecology of opponent processes — exploitation versus exploration, accommodation versus assimilation — whose proper calibration constitutes something close to a formal definition of wisdom as optimal attentional fitting of agent to arena.

The engineered information environment introduces a novel perturbation regime into this ecology. Engagement-optimized architectures exploit the heuristic structure of relevance realization — its sensitivity to novelty, social signaling, threat detection, and intermittent reinforcement — without any coupling to the agent's actual adaptive landscape. The attentional cursor is captured not because the stimuli are relevant but because they activate the same sub-personal machinery that relevance realization depends upon, effectively parasitizing the process. This is not information overload in the naive quantitative sense; it is a systematic corruption of the qualitative dynamics by which salience is assigned.

The population-level consequence is a form of collective attentional dysregulation that resists description in purely psychological terms. It is better understood as an ecological misfit: the selective environment of digital architectures is shaping attentional phenotypes that are well-adapted to engagement metrics but poorly adapted to the conditions of human flourishing. The logic is not conspiratorial but structural — emergent from incentive gradients that treat the relevance realization machinery as a resource to be exploited rather than a capacity to be cultivated.
