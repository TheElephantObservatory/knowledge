# Why Every Therapy Works and Why Every Therapy Fails

You were never at the wrong depth, only the wrong map.

---

> Human experience has a vertical structure — a stack of depths — and different therapeutic methods each work at specific depths. This explains why so many approaches both succeed and fail: they match some depths but not others. Growth is learning to inhabit the full range.

Human experience has a vertical structure — a stack of depths — and different therapeutic, coaching, and spiritual methods each specialize at different depths. This explains a paradox: why do so many different methods all work, and yet all also fail to work? The answer is that they work when the client is at the depth the method specializes in, and fail when the client is at a different depth. The shallowest depth is the depth of Parts — where the hallmark experience is separation, where we navigate life through a constellation of inner sub-personalities, each with good intentions but often pulling in opposite directions, generating stress and stuckness. Deeper than that is the depth of Process — a fluid, river-like layer of existential feeling and relational attunement where it is actually impossible to get stuck, because the flow carries you. Deeper still is Presence — an open awareness state that is quality-rich: love, courage, compassion, peace arise here not as achievements but as recognitions of who we are. And at the deepest depth is Non-Duality — the dissolution of the separation that defined the shallowest layer, a felt sense of belonging, of being exactly where one belongs in the world. Development, understood this way, is not the accumulation of improvements. It is the expanding capacity to consciously inhabit the full range of these depths — not just the deepest, but the entire span.

## Apprentice

There's a puzzling fact about therapy, coaching, and spiritual practice: dozens of very different methods all seem to work — and yet all of them also seem to fail. How can that be? The answer may lie in recognizing that human experience isn't flat. It has layers, like floors in a building, and different methods are designed for different floors.

At the shallowest level, we experience ourselves as a collection of inner parts — voices, impulses, sub-personalities — that often pull us in opposite directions. This is where we feel stuck and stressed. Go deeper and there's a flowing, river-like layer of feeling and connection where stuckness is actually impossible, because everything keeps moving. Deeper still is a spacious awareness where qualities like love, courage, and peace aren't things we have to earn — they're already there, waiting to be noticed. And at the very deepest layer, the sense of being separate dissolves entirely into a felt belonging.

The key insight is that growth isn't about getting better at one level or reaching the deepest one and staying there. It's about expanding your ability to move through the whole range — to know where you are, and to have access to every depth when life calls for it.

## Adept

This framework proposes that human experience is vertically structured into distinct depths, each with its own phenomenology, and that the apparent paradox of therapeutic pluralism — why so many contradictory methods all produce results, yet none works universally — resolves once we recognize that each method specializes at a particular depth. A method succeeds when the client's presenting experience matches the depth it addresses, and fails when the client is operating at a different layer.

Four depths are identified. The shallowest is Parts — the domain of inner multiplicity, where sub-personalities with competing agendas generate the characteristic experience of separation, inner conflict, and stuckness. Below that lies Process — a fluid, pre-reflective layer of existential feeling and relational attunement, phenomenologically river-like, where stuckness is structurally impossible because experience is always already in motion. Deeper still is Presence — open awareness rich with intrinsic qualities (love, compassion, courage, peace) that arise not as accomplishments but as recognitions. At the deepest layer is Non-Duality — the dissolution of the subject-object separation that defined the Parts level, experienced as radical belonging.

Critically, development within this model is not a ladder to be climbed. The goal is not permanent residence at the deepest depth. It is the expanding capacity to consciously inhabit the full vertical range — to recognize which depth one is at, to move fluidly between them, and to bring the resources of each layer to bear on lived experience. The entire stack, not just its depths, constitutes the territory of mature human functioning.

## Magus

The vertical-stack model of experiential depth offers a structural resolution to a persistent meta-theoretical problem: the simultaneous efficacy and insufficiency of virtually every therapeutic, coaching, and contemplative methodology. The resolution is elegantly parsimonious — each method implicitly specializes at a particular stratum of experiential depth, and its success or failure is a function of match or mismatch with the client's operative depth at the moment of intervention. This reframes methodological pluralism not as epistemic chaos but as an unrecognized division of labor across a phenomenological gradient.

The gradient itself is articulated as four depths with distinct structural signatures. Parts, the shallowest, is characterized by multiplicity and separation — the familiar terrain of IFS, ego-state therapy, and voice dialogue, where inner sub-personalities with divergent teleologies generate the phenomenology of stuckness. Process, the next depth, draws on Gendlin's felt sense and the relational-phenomenological tradition: a pre-reflective, kinesthetic flow of experiencing where stasis is structurally precluded. Presence maps onto the witnessing awareness of contemplative traditions — a quality-saturated openness where virtues like compassion and equanimity are disclosed as ontological features rather than developmental achievements. Non-Duality, the deepest stratum, marks the collapse of the subject-object architecture that constitutes the Parts level, yielding a felt sense of radical belonging and non-separation.

What distinguishes this framework from conventional stage models is its refusal of developmental teleology. Maturity is not identified with stable access to the deepest depth but with the capacity to consciously traverse the full vertical range. The entire stack is the developmental achievement. This positions the model closer to Wilber's notion of states versus stages, but with a sharper clinical pragmatics: the practitioner's task is depth-diagnosis before method-selection, not allegiance to a single modality.
