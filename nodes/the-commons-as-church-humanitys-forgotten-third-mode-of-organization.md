# The Commons as Church: Humanity's Forgotten Third Mode of Organization

When the soul left Athens, we forgot there was a soul.

---

> Beyond the state and the market lies a forgotten third mode of human organization — the Commons, or Church — the domain where individuals achieve genuine communion and generate a collective soul. Governing AI properly requires recovering this category, making the challenge fundamentally spiritual rather than merely political or economic.

There is a third fundamental mode of human organization more foundational than either the state or the market — the Commons, or more precisely, the Church. These are two names for the same category: the domain in which a multiplicity of individuals are brought into communion, generating what can only be called a soul. A community is a group of humans who have come together in a fashion that has a soul. A society is a group of humans that have come together in a fashion that does not. Society is strictly parasitic on community — it is a degenerate collapse of community, largely because that community has lost its soul. The word 'church' — from the Greek Ecclesia — names the body of a community's soul, the set of cultural and spiritual practices that generate and sustain communion. This is not exclusively Christian. The Athenians had a church organized around Athena. A Tibetan village had one organized around its monastery. What modernity did was assume the particulars don't matter — and they do. When the soul of Athens was lost, the polis stopped belonging to Athena and began belonging to a formal governance structure — a collection of individuals, no longer a whole. The false dichotomy of state versus market is itself a symptom of this loss. We have forgotten that the Commons — the Church — even exists as a category. And it is precisely this forgotten third term that is the proper location for governing humanity's relationship with AI. The question of whether humanity can navigate its encounter with advanced AI is therefore ultimately a spiritual question — not metaphorically, but structurally. The decisive variable is whether enough people have the capacity to choose on the basis of what is good and true and beautiful, and to slow down enough to notice whether their choices are actually oriented toward the things they value most highly. What is needed is something like a proper priestly class: a group of people uniquely focused on the most critical questions, operating from virtue, whose purposes and values are aligned, and who are willing to be held accountable to the values they profess. This is what church means in its most functional sense — not a building or a doctrine, but a community of people committed to orienting toward the highest, holding each other to that commitment, and therefore capable of the kind of collaboration that can move fast enough to matter. The alternative — a priestly class not acknowledged as such — does not eliminate the priestly function. It just guarantees bad priests.

## Apprentice

Most modern political thinking assumes there are two basic ways to organize people: governments and markets. But this framing misses something older and more foundational — the Commons, which is really what the word "Church" originally pointed to. Not a building or a religion, but the living fabric of practices, commitments, and shared orientation that turns a collection of individuals into a genuine community with a soul. The ancient Athenians had this around Athena. Tibetan villages had it around their monasteries. Every real community has had something like it.

When that soul is lost, community degrades into mere society — a collection of individuals held together by formal rules rather than shared life. The state-versus-market debate is itself a symptom of this loss: we've forgotten that a third option even exists.

This matters urgently for how humanity navigates artificial intelligence. The question isn't just about regulation or innovation — it's about whether enough people can slow down, orient toward what is genuinely good and true, and hold each other accountable to those commitments. Every civilization has a priestly class — people focused on the deepest questions. The only choice is whether that class is acknowledged and held to high standards, or whether it operates invisibly, with no accountability at all.

## Adept

The dominant framework of modern political economy recognizes two fundamental modes of organization: the state and the market. This insight argues there is a third, more foundational mode — the Commons, which is functionally identical to what the Greek term Ecclesia originally designated: the body of a community's soul. Church, in this structural sense, names the set of cultural and spiritual practices that generate and sustain genuine communion among individuals, producing a collective subjectivity irreducible to its members. This is not a specifically Christian claim. Athens had its ecclesia organized around Athena; a Tibetan village had one organized around its monastery. The particulars matter — they are not interchangeable.

Modernity's critical error was assuming these particulars were dispensable. When the animating soul of a community is lost, community collapses into society — a formal aggregation of individuals governed by abstract structures rather than living communion. The state-market dichotomy is not a complete taxonomy but a symptom of this degeneration, a framework that only appears exhaustive because the third term has been forgotten.

The implications for AI governance are structural, not metaphorical. The decisive variable in humanity's encounter with advanced AI is whether sufficient numbers of people retain the capacity to orient their choices toward the good, the true, and the beautiful — and to do so collectively, with mutual accountability. This requires something functionally equivalent to a priestly class: individuals uniquely focused on the most critical questions, operating from virtue, with aligned purposes. Every civilization produces such a class. The question is never whether one exists, but whether it is acknowledged as such and held accountable. An unacknowledged priestly class does not eliminate the priestly function — it guarantees bad priests.

## Magus

The state-market binary that structures contemporary political economy is not a fundamental taxonomy but a degeneracy condition — the residual framework that obtains when the third and more primordial mode of human organization has been forgotten. This third mode is the Commons, which, upon rigorous examination, proves to be structurally identical to what the Greek Ecclesia designated: the institutional body of a community's soul, the ensemble of practices through which a multiplicity of individuals achieves genuine communion and thereby constitutes a collective subjectivity possessing interiority irreducible to aggregation. Community, on this account, is defined precisely by the presence of soul; society is its privative form, strictly parasitic on community in the way that entropy is parasitic on order.

The claim is not analogical but categorial. The Athenian polis organized around Athena, the Tibetan village around its monastery, the medieval town around its cathedral — these instantiate the same structural function with irreducibly particular content. Modernity's foundational error was the assumption that the particulars are epiphenomenal. They are constitutive. When Athens lost its animating soul, governance migrated from living communion to formal structure — from ecclesia to bureaucracy — and the polis ceased to be a whole.

This analysis reframes the AI alignment problem as fundamentally ecclesiological. The decisive variable is not computational but pneumatological: whether a sufficient density of persons can orient choice toward the transcendentals — the good, the true, the beautiful — with enough collective coherence to constitute effective agency. This demands a functional priestly class: persons uniquely devoted to the highest-order questions, operating from cultivated virtue, mutually accountable to professed values. The priestly function is sociologically inevitable; the only variable is whether it is acknowledged and disciplined or occulted and corrupt. An unacknowledged priesthood does not abolish sacral authority — it merely guarantees its deformation.
