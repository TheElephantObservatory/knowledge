# Somatic Energy Work as a Bridge Between Psychological and Contemplative Knowing

The seam runs deeper than the ladder

---

> Working with felt bodily energy — somatic sensation, aliveness, energetic texture — serves as the most practical bridge between intellectual knowing and the deeper, wordless knowing that underpins wisdom, and religion has a specific obligation to incorporate this bridge rather than leaving it at the margins.

Subtle energy work — working with felt somatic stimulation, energetic sensing, the quality of aliveness in the body — functions as a bridge between propositional and non-propositional knowing, and that bridge is one of the most practically important moves available in any serious ecology of practices. The sequence, though not strictly linear, tends to go like this: you begin with propositional content, the verbal, psychological, narrative layer of experience. You then move closer to feeling what is actually happening in the body — not interpreting it but attending to it. From there you can begin to differentiate between content and process: not what the experience is about but how it is structured, how it moves, where it is blocked, what its texture and quality are. That move — from content to process — is a decisive developmental step because it makes the subject into object; you become construct-aware rather than construct-captured. If you then move participatorily into the experience of process rather than just observing it, it can open into more stabilized feelings of presence, or sometimes into a felt nothingness, a boundary, an unknownness — what Henry Bortoft calls the wholeness of something as an active absence presencing through the body. And from there, with sufficient stabilization, non-dual states become accessible. This is not a ladder you climb once; it is a seam you can work along repeatedly, and working along it is more effective than either pure talk therapy or pure formless contemplation in isolation. The Diamond approach case Bruce Alderman describes — a woman with a dead zone in her chest, the images of her mother's words, the golden honey-like quality that eventually wells up and releases a contracted identity into spacious receptiveness — illustrates how working the energetic seam can move fluidly between psychological release and mystical opening without requiring any metaphysical commitment about what energy ultimately is. Religion, understood in its fullest sense, has a specific obligation with respect to subtle energy and somatic practice that goes beyond merely tolerating it or leaving room for it at the margins. A religion that ignores bioelectricity, somatic stimulation, and energetic sensing is like a medical system that only does surgery and chemistry — functional in a narrow sense but far short of a full-spectrum response. The argument for why religion specifically should incorporate this material has several strands. First, religion is obligated to care for the health of the organism; practices that cultivate attunement to cellular stimulation and somatic aliveness are a fundamental component of general health, and a religion that is anti-life rather than life-friendly — Nietzsche's critique — fails at that basic level. Second, focused attention on stimulation patterns is excellent mind training, and the proliferation of generalized attentional capacity through a population is a properly religious concern. Third, intensified experience of somatic stimulation can provoke altered states, and probing altered states of consciousness is one of the fundamental functions of spirituality — there is a direct route from energy work to the kinds of state activation that religion has always been in the business of facilitating. Fourth, and perhaps most importantly, subtle body work provides a profoundly present realization of non-propositional knowing — it is an embodied tutorial on the kinds of knowing that resist verbal articulation, and enhancing non-propositional knowing is central to the enhancement of meaning and wisdom that religion exists to serve. The connection between energy work and religion is not necessary — people practice one without the other all the time — but it is deeply recommended, because a religion that can draw somatically sensitive people into socially virtuous and wisdom-cultivating practices, rather than leaving them outside the tradition or treating their experience as occult contamination, is a more complete religion.

## Apprentice

There is a kind of knowing that lives in the body — not ideas about the body, but the actual felt quality of being alive: warmth, tingling, contraction, openness, flow. When you learn to pay close attention to these sensations without immediately interpreting them through stories or psychology, something important shifts. You stop being trapped inside your narratives and start noticing how experience actually moves and feels. That shift — from being caught up in content to sensing the living process underneath — is one of the most powerful developmental moves a person can make.

This kind of body-level attention can open into experiences of deep presence, spaciousness, and even states that mystics have described for centuries. A striking example involves a woman who discovered a "dead zone" in her chest during therapeutic work. By staying with the felt quality of that deadness rather than just talking about it, a warm, honey-like sensation eventually arose and dissolved a long-held emotional contraction into openness. No metaphysical belief was required — just careful, sustained attention to what was actually happening in her body.

The argument here is that religion, in its fullest sense, should actively incorporate this kind of somatic practice rather than ignoring it. A tradition concerned with human flourishing, attention, altered states, and wisdom beyond words has every reason to take bodily energy work seriously — not as something exotic or marginal, but as a core practice that connects health, mental training, spiritual experience, and the deepest forms of meaning-making.

## Adept

Subtle energy work — attending to somatic stimulation, felt aliveness, and energetic texture in the body — functions as a critical bridge between propositional and non-propositional knowing. The developmental sequence moves from verbal-narrative content to direct somatic attending, then from content to process awareness (noticing how experience is structured rather than what it is about), and from there into participatory engagement with process itself. This content-to-process move is decisive because it renders previously subject-level constructs into objects of awareness — construct-awareness rather than construct-capture. With sufficient stabilization, this trajectory opens into presence, felt absence (what Bortoft calls "active absence presencing"), and eventually non-dual states. This is not a one-time ladder but a seam that can be worked repeatedly, and it proves more effective than either talk therapy or formless contemplation practiced in isolation.

The Diamond Approach case Bruce Alderman describes — a woman encountering a somatic dead zone, staying with its felt quality until a golden honey-like sensation wells up and dissolves a contracted identity into spacious receptiveness — illustrates how the energetic seam enables fluid movement between psychological release and mystical opening without requiring metaphysical commitments about the ontological status of energy.

The argument that religion has a specific obligation here rests on four strands: religion must care for organismic health, and somatic attunement is fundamental to that; focused attention on stimulation patterns is excellent mind training with population-level benefits; intensified somatic experience provokes altered states that spirituality has always been in the business of facilitating; and most importantly, subtle body work provides an embodied tutorial in non-propositional knowing — the very kind of knowing that meaning-making and wisdom depend upon. A religion that excludes this material is functional but narrow, like medicine restricted to surgery and pharmacology.

## Magus

The claim advanced here is that subtle energy work — understood as disciplined attention to felt somatic stimulation, energetic sensing, and the qualitative texture of bodily aliveness — occupies a uniquely strategic position within any serious ecology of practices, functioning as the operative bridge between propositional and non-propositional epistemic modalities. The developmental phenomenology follows a characteristic, if non-linear, trajectory: from narrative-psychological content to direct somatic attending; from content-identification to process-awareness (a move that accomplishes the subject-object shift central to constructive-developmental theory — construct-awareness replacing construct-capture); from observational process-tracking to participatory engagement; and from there into stabilized presence or what Bortoft theorizes as "active absence" — wholeness presencing through the body as felt nothingness or boundary-encounter. With sufficient stabilization, non-dual state access becomes available. This seam is iteratively workable and proves more generative than either discursive therapy or formless contemplation practiced in isolation.

Alderman's Diamond Approach case — the somatic dead zone, the imagistic surfacing of maternal introjects, the emergent golden honey-like quality dissolving contracted identity into spacious receptiveness — demonstrates how working this energetic seam enables fluid oscillation between psychological integration and contemplative opening without requiring ontological adjudication regarding the metaphysical status of "energy" itself. The phenomenological precision is what matters, not the metaphysics.

The normative argument for religion's specific obligation here is fourfold and cumulative: organismic health stewardship (contra Nietzsche's critique of life-denying religion); attentional capacity cultivation as a population-level religious concern; altered-state facilitation as a core spiritual function directly accessible through somatic intensification; and, most fundamentally, the enhancement of non-propositional knowing as the epistemic ground of meaning and wisdom. A religion that excludes somatic-energetic practice operates like a medical system limited to surgery and pharmacology — functional within its domain but categorically incomplete. The connection is not logically necessary but deeply recommended: traditions capable of integrating somatically sensitive practitioners into wisdom-cultivating communal structures gain something irreplaceable.
