# Public Narrative Construction for Sensemaking

Where the vague gesture goes to die

---

> Public writing isn't just clearer than private notes — it's a different cognitive act entirely. Cory Doctorow used continuous public narration to find signal in the chaos of a relentless news cycle, turning audience accountability into an engine for sensemaking.

The discipline of public note-taking as a cognitive tool for sensemaking is fundamentally different from private journaling — and the difference is epistemically significant. Writers have always kept commonplace books, but private notes allow you to cheat: you take shortcuts, use cryptic shorthand, and convince yourself you'll remember what you meant. When you write for an audience, even a small one, you're forced to externalize your reasoning fully. You can't hide behind vague gestures at meaning. But Doctorow's insight goes further than just 'writing clarifies thinking.' He describes using public writing as a real-time narrative-making tool during a period of information overload — the Trump era news cycle — where the sheer velocity of events made it impossible to track whether today's anxiety was a new development or just a reframing of yesterday's. The solution wasn't more data collection. It was narrative construction. By forcing fragments into stories — particularly stories about the role of technology — he could distinguish signal from noise, track the evolution of a theme across time, and find connections that wouldn't surface in a private notebook. This is a model for how writers, analysts, and sensemakers might cope with high-complexity, high-velocity information environments: not by cataloguing more, but by narrating continuously and publicly, using the audience as an accountability structure that enforces intellectual rigor.

## Apprentice

Most people think of writing as a way to record what they already know. But there's a stronger version of this idea: writing is how you figure out what you think in the first place. And crucially, writing for other people forces you to do this more honestly than writing for yourself. Private notes let you cut corners — you use shorthand, leave gaps, and trust your future self to fill them in. That future self rarely does.

Cory Doctorow pushed this further during the Trump years, when the news was moving so fast it was nearly impossible to tell whether something genuinely new was happening or whether you were just seeing yesterday's problem in a new costume. His solution wasn't to read more or take better notes. It was to write publicly, continuously, and to force the fragments of daily news into actual stories — particularly stories about technology and power.

The act of narrating out loud, for an audience, created a kind of discipline that private journaling couldn't. It forced connections, revealed patterns across time, and made it possible to distinguish real developments from noise. The audience isn't just a reader — it's an accountability structure that won't let you be vague.

## Adept

There is an epistemic asymmetry between public and private writing that goes underappreciated in discussions of note-taking and personal knowledge management. Private notes permit a kind of cognitive laziness: the writer can rely on implicit context, idiosyncratic shorthand, and the illusion of future recall. The result is often a graveyard of half-formed thoughts. Public writing forecloses these shortcuts. To externalize reasoning for an audience is to submit it to a standard of coherence that private notation never demands.

Doctorow's contribution to this idea is not merely that writing clarifies thinking — that much is well-established — but that public narrative construction functions as a real-time sensemaking instrument under conditions of information overload. During a period defined by high-velocity, high-volume news, the challenge wasn't data scarcity but interpretive saturation. The question wasn't 'what happened?' but 'does this matter, and have I seen it before?' Doctorow's practice of continuous public writing forced discrete events into thematic arcs, particularly around technology and political economy, making it possible to track the evolution of a phenomenon across time rather than experiencing each news cycle as episodic and unconnected.

This reframes the function of an audience. In this model, readers are not merely consumers of insight but structural participants in its production. The knowledge that someone else will read your reasoning imposes a discipline — a demand for legibility and logical closure — that transforms the writing act from personal record-keeping into genuine intellectual work. For analysts operating in complex, fast-moving domains, the implication is significant: narrate continuously and publicly, rather than cataloguing privately and synthesizing later.

## Magus

The distinction between private notation and public writing is not merely stylistic but epistemically constitutive. Private notes operate within a closed hermeneutic loop: the author encodes meaning in a register legible only to themselves, relying on tacit context and deferred elaboration that rarely materializes. This produces what might be called the commonplace book illusion — the sense of having captured an idea when one has merely gestured at it. Public writing ruptures this loop by introducing an intersubjective demand: the reasoning must be reconstructible by a mind without access to the author's private context. This constraint is generative, not merely corrective.

Doctorow's practice extends this epistemology into the domain of real-time sensemaking under conditions of informational hyperstimulation. The problem he identifies is not cognitive overload in the classical sense — an excess of data relative to processing capacity — but something closer to narrative dissolution: the inability to determine whether successive events constitute a developing situation or merely a recombination of prior elements. The solution he enacts is continuous public narration, specifically the imposition of thematic structure — technology, power, institutional decay — onto an otherwise entropic event stream. This is not post-hoc synthesis but concurrent narrative construction, where the act of writing for an audience enforces the kind of diachronic coherence that transforms discrete observations into an evolving argument.

The theoretical implication is that audience functions here not as reception but as epistemic infrastructure. The anticipated reader operates as a regulative ideal, enforcing standards of logical closure and terminological consistency that the solitary writer has no structural incentive to maintain. This suggests a revision of standard models of knowledge work: in high-complexity, high-velocity environments, the unit of sensemaking may not be the private archive but the public, iterative, narratively-structured text — where accountability to an interlocutor is the mechanism by which signal is distinguished from noise.
