# How Institutions Hide Variation to Survive and Adapt

The forest that looks like one tree

---

> Wisdom institutions face the same challenge as evolving organisms: they need enormous internal diversity to adapt, but too much visible difference destroys coherence. John Vervaeke argues that 'degeneracy' — overlapping redundancy beneath a stable surface — is the structural solution, and that art may be its natural mediator.

One of the deepest structural problems in designing adaptive wisdom institutions is what biologists call 'degeneracy' — a term John Vervaeke introduces from evolutionary theory to describe a counterintuitive solution to the robustness paradox. In any evolving system, you need variation for adaptation to occur, but variation is dangerous because most variants die. Nature's solution is not to reduce variation but to make it overlapping and redundant at the genotypic level, so that phenotypic expression remains stable. There is enormous genetic diversity underneath, but because the variants overlap and cancel each other out, the organism doesn't look radically different — and therefore doesn't get killed off. The variation is held in reserve, latent, ready to be expressed rapidly when the environment shifts. This is the mechanism behind the 'evolution of evolvability' — not just evolving traits, but evolving the capacity to evolve faster. Vervaeke proposes this as the precise structural challenge facing any new wisdom institution: how do you maintain a rich bank of genotypic variation — diverse practices, worldviews, developmental approaches, interpretive frameworks — while maintaining enough phenotypic stability that the institution can persist, build trust, and address the perennial? The institution has to look coherent enough to survive while harboring the internal diversity needed to adapt rapidly. This is not a contradiction to be resolved but a tension to be managed — and Vervaeke suggests that art may be the most natural mediator of exactly this tension, since aesthetic symbols can hold multiple interpretations simultaneously, providing the overlapping redundancy that keeps variation alive without fragmenting the whole.

## Apprentice

Any institution trying to cultivate wisdom faces a brutal dilemma. It needs to contain many different practices, perspectives, and ways of making sense of the world — because no one approach will be right for every situation. But if it looks chaotic and fragmented, people won't trust it, and it won't survive long enough to matter. Too much visible diversity tears it apart; too little hidden diversity leaves it brittle.

John Vervaeke borrows a concept from biology called 'degeneracy' to describe how nature solves this exact problem. In living organisms, there is enormous genetic diversity happening beneath the surface, but because the variants overlap and buffer each other, the creature looks stable on the outside. It doesn't get killed off for being too different. Yet when the environment suddenly changes, all that hidden variation is ready to be rapidly expressed. The organism can adapt fast precisely because it stored diversity quietly.

Vervaeke suggests that wisdom institutions need this same architecture — coherent on the surface, deeply diverse underneath. And he proposes that art may be the best tool for holding this tension, because a powerful symbol or image can carry many meanings at once without collapsing into a single interpretation. Art lets an institution be unified and plural at the same time.

## Adept

John Vervaeke identifies a structural paradox at the heart of designing adaptive wisdom institutions by drawing on the biological concept of degeneracy. In evolutionary theory, degeneracy refers to the condition where structurally different elements (genotypes) can produce functionally equivalent outputs (phenotypes). This is distinct from simple redundancy — it is overlapping variation that preserves surface stability while maintaining deep diversity. Nature doesn't reduce variation to achieve robustness; it makes variation overlapping so that the organism appears stable enough to survive selection while harboring an enormous latent repertoire ready for rapid expression when conditions shift. This is the mechanism underlying the evolution of evolvability itself — not just adapting, but increasing the capacity to adapt.

Vervaeke transposes this biological insight directly onto the problem of institution design. A wisdom institution must integrate diverse practices, developmental frameworks, worldviews, and interpretive traditions — the genotypic bank — while presenting enough phenotypic coherence to persist, build trust, and address perennial human concerns. This is not a contradiction to be resolved through compromise or homogenization but a generative tension to be architecturally managed.

The proposed mediator of this tension is art. Aesthetic symbols possess a natural degeneracy: they hold multiple valid interpretations simultaneously without fragmenting into incoherence. A rich symbol or ritual can mean different things to different participants while still functioning as a shared reference point. This makes art uniquely suited to the structural role of maintaining overlapping redundancy — keeping the institution's internal variation alive and accessible without destabilizing its outward coherence.

## Magus

Vervaeke's deployment of degeneracy — a concept drawn from Gerald Edelman's work in neural Darwinism and broader evolutionary theory — as the central design principle for wisdom institutions represents a precise structural claim, not a metaphor. Degeneracy describes the condition in which structurally non-isomorphic components converge on functionally equivalent outputs under standard conditions while diverging under perturbation, thereby constituting a latent reservoir of adaptive potential. It is categorically distinct from redundancy (identical components, identical function) and from mere diversity (different components, different functions). Degenerate systems achieve what Kirschner and Gerhart term "facilitated variation": phenotypic stability that does not come at the cost of genotypic impoverishment, enabling the evolution of evolvability itself.

The transposition to institutional design is rigorous. A wisdom institution must maintain a rich genotypic bank — heterogeneous contemplative practices, competing interpretive frameworks, divergent developmental models, incommensurable epistemologies — while sustaining sufficient phenotypic coherence to survive the selection pressures of cultural legitimacy, trust-building, and intergenerational transmission. Homogenization destroys adaptive capacity; unconstrained pluralism destroys institutional identity. The problem is not one of balance but of architecture: how to construct overlapping functional equivalences across structurally diverse elements.

Vervaeke's proposal that art serves as the natural mediator of this degenerate architecture is grounded in the semiotic properties of aesthetic symbols. Richly polysemous symbols — ritual, myth, image, enacted metaphor — instantiate precisely the overlapping-yet-non-identical mapping that degeneracy requires. They function as shared attractors in meaning-space that different interpretive communities can inhabit simultaneously without requiring propositional convergence. The aesthetic thus operates not as ornament but as the connective tissue enabling institutional degeneracy: the structural substrate through which coherence and diversity co-constitute rather than oppose each other.
