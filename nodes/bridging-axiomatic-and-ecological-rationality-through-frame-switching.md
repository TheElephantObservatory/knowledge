# Bridging Axiomatic and Ecological Rationality Through Frame-Switching

No god's eye, no free fall.

---

> Formal rationality and ecological rationality aren't opposed — genuine intelligence means actively constructing the right simplified frame for each situation, then knowing when to shift frames. This capacity links decision theory, cognitive science, and metamodernism as convergent responses to the same deep problem.

There is a synthesis available between two traditions in rationality research long treated as opposed. The axiomatic tradition — decision theory, expected utility maximization — abstracts away from context to produce idealized small worlds where optimal behavior can be calculated. The ecological tradition emphasizes adaptability to the actual environment over conformity to formal axioms. Both capture something real, and the mistake is to treat them as mutually exclusive.

The synthesis is this: what an intelligent agent must do is actively create the small-world idealization — recognize which aspects of an open-ended environment can be legitimately abstracted, then apply formal reasoning within that bounded frame. This requires ongoing relevance realization: a dynamic awareness of what lies beyond the current frame, sensitivity to when the frame needs to shift, and the meta-cognitive capacity to switch between frames fluidly.

David Chapman's concept of 'meta-rationality' captures this: the ability to hold multiple formal systems in mind and apply them where genuinely relevant, rather than treating any single formalism as a universal solvent. This is also precisely what metamodernism describes at the cultural level — a meta-contextual awareness of when different frameworks are appropriate, toggling between them without collapsing into either dogmatic commitment or relativistic abandonment. The convergence between cognitive science, rationality research, and cultural philosophy here is not coincidental: they are all circling the same problem — how does a finite, embedded agent navigate an open-ended world without pretending to a God's-eye view it cannot have, or surrendering to the conclusion that all perspectives are equally arbitrary?

## Apprentice

Two camps have long debated what it means to think rationally. One says: use logic and math to calculate the best decision. The other says: forget the math, real intelligence is about adapting to your actual situation. Both sides capture something true, but the real insight is that they need each other.

The key move is recognizing that smart thinking isn't about picking one approach — it's about knowing when to simplify a messy situation into something you can reason about formally, and when to step back and question whether your simplification still fits reality. This requires a kind of ongoing awareness: Am I looking at this the right way? What am I leaving out? Should I switch to a different lens?

David Chapman calls this capacity "meta-rationality" — the ability to hold multiple reasoning frameworks in mind and deploy the right one at the right time. Interestingly, this same pattern shows up in cultural philosophy under the name metamodernism: the skill of moving between different worldviews without either clinging rigidly to one or giving up and saying none of them matter. These fields are converging because they're all wrestling with the same question — how does a limited being navigate an endlessly complex world without pretending to know everything or concluding that nothing can be known?

## Adept

Rationality research has been split between two traditions: the axiomatic approach (expected utility theory, Bayesian decision theory) that defines optimal behavior within idealized small worlds, and the ecological approach (Gigerenzer, bounded rationality) that emphasizes adaptation to real environmental structure over conformity to formal axioms. The synthesis emerging from this tension is that intelligent agency consists precisely in the active construction of small-world idealizations — recognizing which features of an open-ended environment can be legitimately abstracted, applying formal reasoning within that bounded frame, and maintaining the meta-cognitive sensitivity to know when the frame must be revised or abandoned.

This is the core of what David Chapman terms "meta-rationality": not reasoning within a single formal system, but the higher-order capacity to select, deploy, and transition between formal systems based on contextual relevance. It requires what John Vervaeke calls "relevance realization" — a dynamic, pre-theoretical attunement to what matters in a given situation, operating beneath and prior to any explicit framework.

The convergence with metamodernism is structurally precise, not merely analogical. Metamodernism describes a cultural stance that oscillates between commitment and irony, holding frameworks seriously without absolutizing them. Cognitive science, rationality theory, and cultural philosophy are independently arriving at the same structural insight: a finite, embedded agent navigating genuine complexity must develop meta-contextual awareness — the capacity to frame and reframe without either collapsing into naive realism about any single frame or retreating into the relativistic claim that all frames are equally arbitrary.

## Magus

The apparent antinomy between axiomatic rationality — Savage's small-world decision theory, von Neumann-Morgenstern utility — and ecological rationality in the tradition of Gigerenzer and Simon dissolves once one recognizes that the construction of the small world is itself the primary cognitive achievement. The axiomatic tradition presupposes a well-defined decision space; the ecological tradition demonstrates that real environments resist such closure. The synthesis holds that rational agency is fundamentally the ongoing, fallible activity of carving tractable formal domains from an open-ended environment, applying optimization within those domains, and maintaining the meta-cognitive vigilance to detect frame failure. This is not bounded rationality in Simon's satisficing sense — it is the active, skilled production of the bounds themselves.

Chapman's meta-rationality names exactly this capacity: fluency across multiple formal systems coupled with the judgment to determine their domains of genuine applicability. The connection to Vervaeke's relevance realization framework is deep rather than superficial — relevance realization provides the sub-personal, dynamical-systems account of how an embedded agent continuously restructures its salience landscape, which is the precondition for any frame-construction activity. Without it, meta-rationality has no mechanism; without meta-rationality, relevance realization has no normative articulation.

The isomorphism with metamodernism — understood as Hanzi Freinacht's developmental cultural logic or Vermeulen and van den Akker's oscillatory structure — is not coincidental but diagnostic. All three discourses converge on the problem of perspectival multiplicity under finitude: how an agent without a God's-eye view maintains epistemic traction across incommensurable frameworks without collapsing into either foundationalist dogmatism or performative contradiction. The convergence suggests a deep structural invariant in how embedded cognition must relate to its own representational activity.
