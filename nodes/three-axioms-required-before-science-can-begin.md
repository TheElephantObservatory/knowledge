# Three Axioms Required Before Science Can Begin

The smallest house that holds everything

---

> Jim Rutt proposes that all of science rests on exactly three unprovable but necessary commitments: reality exists roughly as we perceive it, the universe is not perfectly uniform, and it operates according to deep regularities. Everything else — emergence, consciousness, meaning — is downstream.

Jim Rutt articulates a 'minimum viable metaphysics' built on exactly three axiomatic commitments — not derived from empiricism, but required before empiricism can even begin. The first is the Reality Principle: the world exists and is approximately as we perceive it, at least at the 'mezzo scale' of human experience — from a grain of sand to the size of the sun. This is not a provable claim but a pragmatic commitment; without it, all inquiry collapses into solipsism. The second is the Asymmetry Principle: the universe cannot be perfectly homogeneous, because perfect homogeneity produces nothing — no stars, no gradients, no emergence. This principle has two forks depending on whether the universe is deterministic (requiring initial non-homogeneity as a hard condition) or stochastic (in which case asymmetry may arise for free from quantum-level randomness). The third is the Lawfulness Principle: the universe operates according to deep regularities, even if our current knowledge of those regularities is incomplete. Crucially, lawfulness and determinism are not the same — a stochastic universe can still be lawful if its randomness has consistent statistical structure. The Copenhagen interpretation assumes base-level randomness, while de Broglie-Bohm pilot wave and Many Worlds interpretations preserve determinism; the question remains genuinely open. Rutt uses this distinction to defend the Lawfulness Principle against the objection that quantum randomness undermines it: randomness, if real, would simply be one more feature of the lawful layer, not a refutation of it. Together, these three commitments are the minimum required to do anything useful — to measure, to reason, to build science. Everything else, including emergence, consciousness, and the purpose of the universe, is downstream. This is not a palace of metaphysics but a foundation — the smallest possible set of load-bearing assumptions.

## Apprentice

Before you can do any science at all, you have to accept a few things you can't actually prove. Jim Rutt argues there are exactly three such commitments, and together they form the smallest possible foundation for making sense of the world. First: the world is real and roughly matches what we experience at everyday scales. You can't prove this — maybe it's all a dream — but without this assumption, there's no point asking any further questions.

Second: the universe isn't perfectly uniform. If everything were spread out in exactly the same way everywhere, nothing interesting could ever happen — no stars, no planets, no life. Something has to be uneven for anything to exist. Third: the universe follows reliable patterns. Even if some things happen randomly at the smallest scales, that randomness itself behaves in consistent, predictable ways. Lawfulness doesn't mean everything is predetermined — it means the rules don't change on a whim.

Rutt's point is that these three ideas are the minimum you need before you can measure anything, reason about anything, or build any kind of knowledge. Everything else — how complex things emerge, what consciousness is, whether the universe has a purpose — comes after. This isn't a grand philosophical system. It's the smallest set of load-bearing assumptions that keeps the whole enterprise of understanding from collapsing.

## Adept

Jim Rutt constructs what he calls a 'minimum viable metaphysics' — three axiomatic commitments that are not empirically derivable but are logically prerequisite to empiricism itself. The first, the Reality Principle, asserts that an external world exists and is approximately as perceived at the mezzo scale of human experience, from a grain of sand to the size of the sun. This is a pragmatic commitment, not a proof; its rejection leads to solipsism and the collapse of all inquiry. The second, the Asymmetry Principle, holds that perfect homogeneity is sterile — it produces no gradients, no structure, no emergence. In a deterministic universe, initial asymmetry must be a brute fact; in a stochastic universe, quantum randomness may generate asymmetry spontaneously, making this condition less demanding.

The third, the Lawfulness Principle, asserts that the universe operates according to deep regularities. Rutt is careful to decouple lawfulness from determinism: a universe with genuine base-level randomness (as in the Copenhagen interpretation) can still be lawful if that randomness exhibits consistent statistical structure. The de Broglie-Bohm and Many Worlds interpretations preserve determinism, but the question remains open — and crucially, the Lawfulness Principle survives regardless of which interpretation holds. Randomness, if real, is simply one more feature of the lawful substrate, not a refutation of it.

The force of this framework lies in its minimalism. These three commitments are the necessary and sufficient metaphysical preconditions for measurement, reasoning, and science. Everything downstream — emergence, consciousness, teleology — rests on this foundation. Rutt is not building a metaphysical palace but identifying the smallest possible set of load-bearing assumptions.

## Magus

Jim Rutt's 'minimum viable metaphysics' is an exercise in axiomatic parsimony: the identification of exactly three pre-empirical commitments without which the empirical enterprise cannot be coherently initiated. The Reality Principle posits an external world approximately isomorphic to perception at the mezzo scale — a commitment that is neither verifiable nor dispensable, since its negation forecloses all epistemic activity. The Asymmetry Principle asserts the non-homogeneity of the universe as a necessary condition for structure and emergence. This principle bifurcates along the determinism-indeterminism axis: under determinism, initial symmetry-breaking must be stipulated as a brute cosmological fact; under stochastic interpretations, quantum indeterminacy may furnish asymmetry endogenously, rendering the condition less ontologically costly.

The Lawfulness Principle — that deep regularities govern the universe — is where the framework does its most careful philosophical work. Rutt explicitly decouples lawfulness from determinism, a conflation that has plagued popular metaphysics since Laplace. Under the Copenhagen interpretation, base-level randomness is irreducible, yet the statistical structure of that randomness is itself lawful — consistent, measurable, and predictively powerful. The pilot wave and Many Worlds interpretations restore determinism at the cost of other ontological commitments, but the Lawfulness Principle is invariant across all three. Quantum randomness, if genuine, is subsumed within the lawful layer rather than standing as its negation. This move immunizes the framework against the most common objection from physics.

The result is a foundation, not an edifice. Emergence, consciousness, teleology, and all higher-order questions are explicitly downstream. The philosophical ambition is deliberately constrained: to specify the minimum load-bearing structure beneath which inquiry cannot go and above which everything else must be built. It is metaphysics as engineering — identifying the fewest possible points of failure in the architecture of knowledge.
