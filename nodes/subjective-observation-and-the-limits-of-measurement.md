# Subjective Observation and the Limits of Measurement

The irreducible weight of being there

---

> The word 'empirical' has been quietly hijacked by science to mean only measurable, third-person data — but observation has always included first-person experience, and reclaiming that broader meaning changes everything about how we understand knowledge.

One of the most underappreciated distinctions in how we talk about knowledge is the difference between empirical in the narrow scientific sense and empirical in the broader philosophical sense. In academic psychology and most scientific disciplines, the word empirical has been colonized by the objective vector — it has come to mean measurable, replicable, third-person data. But the word empiricism roots back to observation, and observation is not exclusively third-person. David Hume was an empiricist who grounded knowledge in first-person sensations and ideas — opening your eyes and seeing a coin, a screen, the world as it presents itself to you as a subject. That is also empirical. The subjective vector is genuinely empirical in this older, broader sense. What science does is take the intersubjectively agreed-upon measurements of the world and translate them into mathematics, building generalizable models that yield the same predicted outcomes regardless of who is doing the observing. That is a remarkable achievement, but it is a specific and narrow form of empiricism — one that deliberately abstracts away from the particular, the situated, and the idiographic. The subjective vector, by contrast, is always particular: every instance of it has its own unique characteristics and cannot be reduced to a general model without losing what is most essential about it. Recognizing that both vectors are genuinely empirical — just empirical in fundamentally different ways — is a prerequisite for understanding why they cannot simply be collapsed into one another, and why any adequate framework for human knowledge must hold both without pretending one is reducible to the other.

## Apprentice

Most people today hear the word 'empirical' and think of lab experiments, measurements, and data that anyone can verify. But the word originally just meant 'based on observation' — and observation doesn't have to be the kind that shows up on a graph. When you open your eyes and see the world as it appears to you, that is also a form of observation. The philosopher David Hume built an entire theory of knowledge on exactly this kind of first-person experience, and he was considered one of the great empiricists.

What modern science does is take a specific slice of observation — the kind where multiple people can agree on a measurement — and build general laws from it. That is genuinely powerful. But it deliberately sets aside everything that is personal, particular, and tied to a single perspective. Your experience of grief, or beauty, or a moment of clarity cannot be averaged across a population without losing the very thing that made it meaningful.

Recognizing that both kinds of observation count as genuinely empirical — just in different ways — matters because it stops us from treating personal experience as somehow less real than a measurement, while also stopping us from pretending that statistics can fully capture what it is like to be a particular person living a particular life.

## Adept

Within scientific disciplines, the term 'empirical' has undergone a quiet but consequential semantic narrowing. It has come to denote intersubjectively verifiable, quantifiable, third-person data — the kind that yields replicable results independent of the observer's identity or situation. This usage is coherent and productive within its domain, but it obscures the term's deeper philosophical roots. Classical empiricism, from Locke through Hume, grounded knowledge in observation broadly construed — including the immediate, first-person deliverances of sensation and reflection. On that account, perceiving the redness of an apple or the felt quality of an emotion is no less empirical than recording its wavelength.

The distinction matters because the two modes of inquiry operate on fundamentally different logics. Scientific empiricism achieves generalizability precisely by abstracting away from the particular and the situated — its models are nomothetic, designed to hold across instances. Subjective or phenomenological inquiry is irreducibly idiographic: each instance carries unique characteristics that resist compression into a general law without significant loss of meaning. These are not competing claims about the same territory; they are different orientations toward different aspects of experience.

The practical implication is that neither vector can be reduced to the other without distortion. Treating first-person observation as merely pre-scientific data waiting to be formalized misrepresents its epistemic character. Conversely, treating nomothetic models as exhaustive accounts of human experience overreaches their scope. Any adequate epistemological framework must hold both forms of empiricism in productive tension, recognizing their complementarity without collapsing their genuine differences.

## Magus

The semantic colonization of 'empirical' by the third-person, operationalist paradigm represents one of the more consequential conceptual elisions in modern intellectual life. Within the dominant scientific register, empirical has come to function as a near-synonym for intersubjectively measurable and nomologically generalizable — a usage that tacitly encodes a specific metaphysical commitment to observer-independence as the criterion of epistemic legitimacy. Yet the empiricist tradition from which this usage nominally descends was never so restrictive. Hume's empiricism was grounded in the phenomenology of impression and idea — the immediate, first-personal givenness of experience — and the project of naturalizing knowledge through observation was, for him, inseparable from attending to the texture of conscious life as it presents itself to a subject.

What the scientific revolution accomplished, epistemically speaking, was the construction of a methodological filter: by restricting evidential weight to measurements that are invariant across observers, it achieved the extraordinary capacity to build predictive, mathematizable models of nature. This is a specific and disciplined form of empiricism — one that deliberately brackets the particular, the situated, and the qualitative in order to extract the generalizable. The cost of that bracketing is not incidental. The idiographic dimension of experience — the very features that make a grief, a perception, or a moment of understanding this grief, this perception, this understanding — is precisely what the nomothetic apparatus must discard to function.

The epistemological stakes of recovering the broader sense of 'empirical' are therefore significant. It reframes first-person phenomenological inquiry not as a soft precursor to real science but as a genuinely distinct mode of evidential engagement with a genuinely distinct domain of phenomena. The two vectors are incommensurable not because one is rigorous and the other is not, but because they are empirical in structurally different ways — and any framework that fails to honor that structural difference will systematically misrepresent the architecture of human knowledge.
