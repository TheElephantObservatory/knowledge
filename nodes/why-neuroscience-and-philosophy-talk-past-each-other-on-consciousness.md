# Why Neuroscience and Philosophy Talk Past Each Other on Consciousness

The map had too few rooms.

---

> Science and philosophy use 'consciousness' to mean very different things, causing deep confusion. UTOK's distinction between 'mindedness' (sensory-motor awareness in bodied animals) and three separate meanings of 'consciousness' lets us finally ask the right questions in the right register.

One of the most important conceptual moves in UTOK is distinguishing between 'mindedness' and 'consciousness' — two terms routinely conflated in both scientific and philosophical literature, with serious consequences for clarity. Mindedness is a structural, located property: it refers to the sensory-motor loop that affords functional awareness and responsiveness in three-dimensional behavioral space, anchored specifically to complex active bodies with nervous systems, emerging from the Cambrian explosion onward. Consciousness, by contrast, has three distinct referents: first, functional awareness and responsiveness in any entity ('creature consciousness'); second, the subjective experience of being a self experiencing conscious contents — where the hard problem lives (Mind 2); and third, self-recursive awareness, or self-consciousness proper (Mind 3). The critical insight is that all the neuroscience and psychology being done today is essentially the science of Mind 2 — subjective consciousness as it arises in minded animals with brains and bodies — while broader philosophical debates about consciousness as a property of matter are a separate domain. Conflating these registers creates enormous confusion: neuroscientists and psychologists are largely talking about Mind 2, while philosophers and physicists are talking about the abstract property of consciousness. Having a vocabulary that separates these allows us to ask the right questions in the right register, and to recognize that Anil Seth's 'real problem of consciousness' is really the real problem of Mind 2: how does subjective experience arise from and relate to the neurocognitive and behavioral architecture of minded animals.

## Apprentice

In everyday conversation and even in expert debates, the words 'mind' and 'consciousness' are used almost interchangeably. But according to Gregg Henriques's Unified Theory of Knowledge (UTOK), this conflation is one of the biggest sources of confusion in both science and philosophy. The framework draws a sharp line between 'mindedness' — the capacity of animals with nervous systems to sense their environment and move through it with functional awareness — and 'consciousness,' which actually refers to at least three different things.

Mindedness is concrete and biological: it emerged when complex animal bodies with nervous systems appeared during the Cambrian explosion, roughly 540 million years ago. Consciousness, on the other hand, can mean basic awareness and responsiveness, or it can mean the felt experience of being a self — the inner movie of your life — or it can mean the ability to reflect on your own thoughts and awareness.

Why does this matter? Because neuroscientists studying the brain are mostly investigating that second meaning — how subjective experience arises in animals with bodies and brains. Meanwhile, philosophers debating whether consciousness is a fundamental feature of the universe are talking about something else entirely. Without clear vocabulary to separate these conversations, people end up talking past each other. This distinction gives us the tools to stop the confusion and direct each question to where it actually belongs.

## Adept

UTOK, Gregg Henriques's metatheoretical system, makes a pivotal conceptual move by formally separating 'mindedness' from 'consciousness' — terms that are chronically conflated across neuroscience, psychology, and philosophy of mind. Mindedness is defined as a structural, embodied property: the sensory-motor loop that enables functional awareness and behavioral responsiveness in three-dimensional space, anchored to complex active bodies with nervous systems and traceable to the Cambrian explosion. It is biologically located and empirically tractable.

Consciousness, by contrast, is given three distinct referents mapped onto UTOK's architecture of mind. The first is creature consciousness — functional awareness and responsiveness in any entity. The second, designated Mind 2, is subjective phenomenal experience: the felt quality of being a self with conscious contents, which is precisely where the hard problem of consciousness resides. The third, Mind 3, is self-recursive awareness — the capacity to reflect on one's own mental states, emerging with human language and culture.

This tripartite distinction has significant clarifying power. It reveals that virtually all contemporary neuroscience and psychology operates within the domain of Mind 2 — investigating how subjective experience arises from neurocognitive and behavioral architecture in minded animals. Broader philosophical debates about consciousness as a fundamental property of matter occupy a different register entirely. Conflating these registers produces systematic confusion. Notably, Anil Seth's 'real problem of consciousness' — understanding the neural and computational mechanisms that give rise to specific phenomenal states — is reframed as the real problem of Mind 2, properly situated within the science of minded organisms rather than the metaphysics of consciousness writ large.

## Magus

One of the most consequential taxonomic interventions in Gregg Henriques's Unified Theory of Knowledge is the formal decoupling of 'mindedness' from 'consciousness' — a disambiguation that cuts through decades of cross-disciplinary equivocation. Mindedness is theorized as an ontologically located, structurally specifiable property: the sensory-motor behavioral loop that constitutes functional awareness and responsiveness in three-dimensional ecological space, instantiated exclusively in complex active bodies possessing nervous systems and phylogenetically emergent from the Cambrian explosion. It is, in UTOK's Tree of Knowledge framework, a joint point — a phase transition in behavioral complexity that inaugurates a new dimension of existence.

Consciousness receives a triadic decomposition. Its first referent is creature consciousness — bare functional awareness and responsiveness, potentially attributable across a wide ontological range. Its second referent, Mind 2, designates subjective phenomenal experience: the qualitative interiority of being a self experiencing conscious contents, the precise locus of Chalmers's hard problem. Its third referent, Mind 3, is self-recursive meta-awareness — propositionally structured self-consciousness emerging at the human cultural joint point. The critical metatheoretical payoff is recognizing that the empirical research programs of neuroscience and psychology are overwhelmingly sciences of Mind 2, investigating the neurocognitive and embodied behavioral architecture from which subjective experience arises in minded animals.

This reframing exposes a persistent category error: neuroscientists and philosophers frequently talk past each other because they deploy 'consciousness' across incommensurable registers. Seth's 'real problem of consciousness' — explaining the mechanistic and computational bases of specific phenomenal states — is properly the real problem of Mind 2, situated within the biology of minded organisms, not within panpsychist or neutral monist metaphysics. The vocabulary UTOK provides is not merely terminological but architectonic: it disciplines inquiry by ensuring that explanatory frameworks are matched to the correct ontological stratum.
