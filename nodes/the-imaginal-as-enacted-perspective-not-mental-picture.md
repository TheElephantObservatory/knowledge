# The Imaginal as Enacted Perspective, Not Mental Picture

You cannot get there without living there first

---

> There is a forgotten distinction between the imaginary (mental picturing) and the imaginal (enacting a perspective so fully that your entire perceptual field reorganizes). Recovering it reveals that imagination is not peripheral to cognition but woven through its deepest operations, from gesture to predictive processing to transformative aspiration.

There is a crucial distinction between the imaginary and the imaginal that has been largely lost in modern discourse, and recovering it changes how we understand cognition, perception, and spiritual practice. The imaginary is the mental picturing most people associate with imagination — visualizing a sailboat, seeing it in your mind's eye. But five to ten percent of the population cannot form mental pictures at all, and they suffer no cognitive deficit whatsoever. This already tells us that mental picturing is not where the real action is. The imaginal is something else entirely: it is the enactment of a perspective, the inhabiting of an identity or a stance, such that one's entire perceptual field is reorganized. When you imagine you are standing in a river — feet sinking into mud, waist in flowing water, torso in open air — you are not picturing anything. You are trying to feel what the salience landscape of that situation would be like, how you would inhabit your body, what you would attend to. Children do this naturally when they pretend to be Superman: they are not picturing Superman, they are enacting what it would be like to be Superman. This is serious play, and it is cognitively foundational. Corbin argued that the imaginal interpenetrates and makes mutually accessible the intelligible and the sensible domains, but it also bridges the subjective and the objective, the individual and the collective, the conscious and the unconscious, and the aspirational and the actual — the person I am now and the person I am trying to become. That last dimension is especially important: all transformative aspiration, all virtue cultivation, all religious conversion, operates through the imaginal. You cannot get from here to there without imaginally inhabiting the there. And this is not peripheral to cognition — it is central. Gesture is imaginal. Predictive processing is imaginal: when you run, your cerebellum is predicting the terrain faster than your sensory loop can process it — you are not sensing the ground, you are imagining it, and true imagination is not hallucination because true predictions are true. Even deep learning is imaginal: Hinton's original wake-sleep algorithm includes a fantasy phase in which the system disconnects from the world and generates variations on its learned invariants, which is precisely what dreaming appears to do. The imaginal is not a supplement to cognition. It is woven through the whole of it.

## Apprentice

Most people think imagination means picturing things in your mind — a beach, a face, a red balloon. But some people cannot form mental pictures at all, and they think and function perfectly well. This suggests that the real power of imagination lies somewhere else entirely.

That somewhere else is what can be called the imaginal. The imaginal is not about seeing pictures — it is about stepping into a perspective. When a child pretends to be Superman, they are not visualizing a costume. They are inhabiting what it would feel like to be Superman — how they would move, what they would notice, what would matter. This kind of serious play reorganizes how someone experiences the world from the inside out. It bridges who you are now and who you are becoming, which is why every form of personal transformation — learning a virtue, converting to a faith, growing into a new role — depends on it.

Remarkably, this capacity is not some special spiritual add-on. It runs through the basics of how minds work. When you run across uneven ground, your brain is predicting the terrain faster than your senses can report it — you are, in a real sense, imagining the ground beneath your feet. Even artificial intelligence systems use a version of this when they disconnect from data and generate their own variations to learn better. The imaginal is not at the edges of thought. It is at the center.

## Adept

A distinction drawn from Henry Corbin's work — between the imaginary and the imaginal — has been largely collapsed in modern usage, and its recovery has far-reaching consequences for cognitive science, phenomenology, and transformative practice. The imaginary refers to mental picturing, the capacity to visualize objects or scenes in the mind's eye. The imaginal refers to something structurally different: the enactment of a perspective, the inhabiting of an identity or stance such that one's entire salience landscape is reorganized. A child pretending to be Superman is not generating visual imagery — they are enacting what it would be like to be Superman, restructuring attention, affordance, and felt embodiment accordingly. This is serious play, and it is cognitively foundational.

Corbin argued that the imaginal mediates between the intelligible and the sensible. But the bridging function extends further: it connects subjective and objective, individual and collective, conscious and unconscious, and — crucially — the aspirational and the actual. All transformative practice, from virtue cultivation to religious conversion, operates through imaginally inhabiting a not-yet-realized way of being. You cannot get from who you are to who you are becoming without this enactive bridge.

What makes this insight especially potent is that the imaginal is not confined to spiritual or contemplative contexts. Gesture is imaginal. Predictive processing is imaginal: the cerebellum anticipates terrain faster than sensory feedback can arrive, meaning locomotion itself involves a form of imagination — one that is not hallucination precisely because true predictions are true. Even Hinton's wake-sleep algorithm includes a generative fantasy phase structurally analogous to dreaming. The imaginal is not supplementary to cognition. It is distributed through its architecture.

## Magus

The conflation of the imaginary with the imaginal represents one of the more consequential conceptual losses in modern intellectual life. The imaginary — mental picturing, the generation of quasi-sensory representations — is what most discourse assumes imagination to be. Yet the existence of aphantasia, affecting perhaps five to ten percent of the population with no measurable cognitive deficit, already destabilizes this identification. The imaginal, as Corbin articulated it, names a fundamentally different operation: the enactive inhabitation of a perspective such that the entire salience landscape undergoes reorganization. It is not representational but participatory — not the image of a thing but the assumption of a stance within which things show up differently.

Corbin located the imaginal as the mundus imaginalis mediating intelligible and sensible domains. But the bridging topology is richer than this single axis suggests. The imaginal simultaneously mediates subjective and objective, individual and collective, conscious and unconscious, and — with particular consequence for any account of transformation — the aspirational and the actual. All virtue cultivation, all metanoia, all developmental transition requires the imaginal inhabitation of a not-yet-realized mode of being. The gap between current and aspirational selfhood is crossed imaginally or not at all.

What elevates this from phenomenological observation to a claim about cognitive architecture is its pervasiveness. Gesture is imaginal enactment. Predictive processing instantiates the imaginal: cerebellar forward models anticipate terrain faster than reafferent signals can close the sensory loop, constituting a form of imagination whose veridicality is guaranteed precisely by its predictive accuracy — true imagination is not hallucination because true predictions are true. Hinton's wake-sleep algorithm includes a fantasy phase — generative sampling disconnected from input — structurally isomorphic with dreaming. The imaginal is not an optional cognitive module. It is woven through perception, action, learning, and becoming as such.
