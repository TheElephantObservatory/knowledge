# Attention Misaligned: Agency and the Curated World

Most of us have a deeply miscalibrated relationship between our attention and our actual agency. We evolved to take in the world through our eyes, and that world couldn't be curated — what we saw was genuinely representative of what was around us. But now, when our information environment is algorithmically curated, our evolutionary predisposition still treats what comes through our eyes as 'the world.' So when Facebook dopes your news feed with negative content, you become depressed. When it shows you positive content, you become positive. Your political views shift based on the preponderance of what you're shown — not because you reasoned your way there, but because you evolved to trust your perceptual field as reality.

The consequence is a profound distortion of what feels important. Who becomes president can feel more urgent than how your kids are doing in school — even though you have almost no agency over the former and enormous agency over the latter, and the latter is far more consequential to your actual life. Studying climate change, AI risk, or democratic breakdown can become a kind of fascination porn — compelling in the way that slowing down to look at a car accident is compelling, for no good reason connected to anything you can actually do.

The corrective isn't to disengage from the world. It's to step back from the input feed and ask: what are the things I actually have agency over that I care about most? And then ensure the preponderance of your attention matches those things. Where you genuinely can't act, find people who can and proxy your support to them — donate, amplify, enable. That takes far less energy than stressing about something you can't resolve. And there's a deeper autopoietic logic here: if you orient your sensemaking toward domains where you're actually learning and increasing your agency, that state compounds. You grow your capacity over time. But if you're in a state of overwhelm and hopelessness, you'll scan from thing to thing without going deep enough to understand any of it — and you'll propagate more overwhelm. The question of how to center in your actual sense of agency, and grow it by acting on it, is not just a practical consideration. It's the foundational condition for being able to do anything meaningful at all.

## Apprentice

There is a mismatch between what grabs your attention and what you can actually do anything about. For most of human history, what you saw around you was real and relevant to your life. You evolved to trust your eyes. But today, algorithms decide what fills your screen, and your brain still treats that feed as if it were reality. The result is that global crises can feel more urgent than your own children's wellbeing, even though you have almost no power over the former and enormous power over the latter. This is not a moral failing. It is an evolutionary mismatch being exploited at scale. The fix is not to stop caring about the world. It is to honestly ask yourself: what do I actually have the power to change? Then point most of your attention there. For things beyond your reach, find people who are positioned to act and support them directly. This is more effective than anxious watching. And there is a compounding benefit: the more you act in domains where you have real agency, the more capable and clear-headed you become. Helplessness, by contrast, tends to spread.

## Adept

A core dysfunction of contemporary information environments is the decoupling of attentional salience from actual agency. Human perceptual systems evolved in conditions where the visual field was a reliable, uncurated sample of the agent's relevant environment. Salience was a reasonable proxy for actionability. Algorithmic curation breaks this correspondence entirely, yet the underlying heuristic persists. What surfaces in the feed feels important because it feels present, and what feels present triggers the same motivational architecture that once oriented action toward genuinely proximate concerns. The political, the catastrophic, and the viral colonize attention not because they are most consequential to the individual's life, but because they are engineered to activate evolved threat-detection and social-monitoring systems. The practical distortion is significant: people invest sustained cognitive and emotional energy in domains where their agency is near zero, while underleveraging domains where their agency is substantial and their impact compounding. The corrective proposed here is an agency-calibrated attention practice. Rather than disengaging from complex systems, one maps the landscape of genuine influence and orients sensemaking accordingly. Where direct agency is absent, the rational move is to proxy action through those who do have leverage, at minimal ongoing attentional cost. The deeper principle is autopoietic: systems that orient toward domains of active learning and real feedback grow their capacity over time. Systems trapped in passive, high-arousal consumption of unresolvable complexity tend toward overwhelm and cognitive fragmentation. Agency-alignment is therefore not merely a productivity heuristic but a precondition for coherent, compounding engagement with the world.

## Magus

The argument here engages a fundamental problem in the phenomenology of mediated attention: the collapse of the ecological validity of perception under conditions of algorithmic curation. In ancestral environments, the visual field constituted a roughly veridical sample of the agent's niche. Affordance perception and motivational salience were therefore reasonably well-coupled to actual agency gradients. The Gibsonian organism moved through a world where what was seen was, in the relevant sense, what was there. Contemporary information architectures sever this coupling while leaving the underlying perceptual epistemology intact. The feed is experienced as world, not as representation of world, because the cognitive systems that assign ontological weight to visual input did not evolve to distinguish between the two. This produces a systematic miscalibration: attentional resources, and the affective and motivational states they generate, are allocated according to engineered salience rather than genuine proximity of agency. The phenomenological consequence is a distorted topology of urgency in which distal, structurally unresolvable problems occlude proximate, high-leverage domains. This is not merely a resource-allocation inefficiency. It constitutes a form of heteronomous sensemaking, in which the subject's model of what matters is substantially constructed by systems optimizing for engagement rather than for the subject's own flourishing or efficacy. The corrective invokes an autopoietic logic: the organism that orients its sensemaking toward domains of genuine feedback and increasing competence undergoes structural coupling that expands its agency over time. The organism trapped in high-arousal, low-agency consumption of catastrophe undergoes the opposite. Agency-calibrated attention is thus not a therapeutic recommendation but an epistemological and ontological condition for the kind of self-organizing, world-responsive subjectivity that meaningful action presupposes.
