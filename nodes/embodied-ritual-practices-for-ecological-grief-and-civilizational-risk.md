# Embodied Ritual Practices for Ecological Grief and Civilizational Risk

Breathing in the darkness of a dying world

---

> Bruce Alderman proposes that embodied ritual practices — speaking as non-human beings, breathing in planetary suffering — offer religion a concrete way to dissolve the boundary between self and ecological community, using grief as the medium of participation rather than mere sentiment.

Bruce Alderman brings into the conversation a suite of embodied ritual practices — drawn from Joanna Macy's Work That Reconnects, Tibetan Tonglen and Chöd, shamanic Council of All Beings, and imaginal deep-time walks — as concrete examples of what a religion adequate to ecological grief and civilizational risk might actually do with bodies in space. The Council of All Beings is particularly striking: participants spend time alone in nature finding a non-human being to represent, then reconvene and speak from within that being's perspective — its needs, its gifts, its suffering — with humans initially absent from the circle entirely. This is not metaphor or poetry; it is a structured perspective-taking practice that attempts to dissolve the boundary between self and ecological community. The Tonglen adaptation takes the Tibetan practice of breathing in suffering and breathing out well-being and extends it to planetary scale — ingesting the darkness of ecological collapse and offering oneself as nourishment to the world. What these practices share is a movement from individual identity into participation with the broader living sphere — and a willingness to let grief, not just gratitude, be the medium of that participation. Vervaeke responds that this represents a conversion point between his spirituality and his cognitive science: the question of what dimensions any ecology of practices must include in order to cultivate wisdom adequate to the present crisis — one that calls for renewed engagement with ecological intelligence, collective intelligence, dialogical intelligence, and subconscious and algorithmic intelligence, all of which point toward the non-linear structure of reality that propositional frameworks cannot contain.

## Apprentice

Bruce Alderman highlights a set of bodily, participatory practices that could form the core of a spirituality genuinely responsive to ecological crisis. In one practice, the Council of All Beings, people spend time alone in nature, find a non-human creature or element to represent, then gather in a circle and speak from that being's perspective — its pain, its gifts, its needs — while humans are initially excluded from the conversation entirely. This isn't playacting; it's a disciplined attempt to feel and think from beyond the human boundary.

Another practice adapts the Tibetan meditation of Tonglen — breathing in suffering, breathing out compassion — to planetary scale. Practitioners deliberately take in the weight of ecological collapse and offer themselves as nourishment to the world. What links these practices is a willingness to let grief, not just gratitude or wonder, serve as the doorway into deeper connection with the living world.

John Vervaeke responds by recognizing this as a critical intersection between spirituality and cognitive science. Any serious "ecology of practices" aimed at cultivating wisdom for our current moment, he suggests, must engage ecological intelligence, collective intelligence, and the non-linear dimensions of reality that purely intellectual frameworks cannot capture.

## Magus

Bruce Alderman advances a suite of embodied ritual technologies — the Council of All Beings, Tonglen and Chöd adapted to ecological scale, Joanna Macy's Work That Reconnects, imaginal deep-time walks — as exemplars of what a religion adequate to civilizational risk must operationalize at the level of bodies in space. The Council of All Beings is paradigmatic: through solitary immersion in nature, participants identify with a non-human being, then reconvene to speak from within that being's participatory perspective — its suffering, its offerings, its claims — while the human subject is structurally absented from the initial circle. This is not representational metaphor but enacted ontological displacement: a practice that attempts to dissolve the self/world boundary through disciplined perspectival inhabitation. The Tonglen extension is equally radical — the Tibetan exchange of self and other is scaled to planetary scope, with practitioners ingesting the phenomenological weight of ecological collapse and offering their transformed presence as nourishment.

The common structure across these practices is a movement from individual identity into participatory enactment with the broader living sphere, mediated specifically by grief rather than positive affect alone. Grief here functions not as pathology but as an epistemological and relational medium — the affective correlate of genuine contact with the real conditions of ecological interdependence.

Vervaeke's response locates this at the precise conversion point between his cognitive science of relevance realization and his spiritual commitments. The critical question becomes one of adequacy conditions: what dimensions must any ecology of practices include to cultivate wisdom commensurate with the metacrisis? He identifies ecological, collective, dialogical, subconscious, and algorithmic intelligences as irreducible axes — each pointing toward the non-linear, transjective structure of reality that propositional frameworks systematically fail to capture, and that only participatory practices can disclose.
