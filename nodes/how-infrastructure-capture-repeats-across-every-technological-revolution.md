# How Infrastructure Capture Repeats Across Every Technological Revolution

We were asking the wrong question again.

---

> Every major infrastructure technology — railroads, electricity, telecom, the internet — follows the same pattern: a small group captures control, aggregates extraordinary power, and reshapes society before anyone thinks to ask the right questions. With AI, this concentration is more extreme and more intimate than ever before.

There is a pattern that repeats across every major technological transformation in industrial civilization, and it is worth naming clearly: a small number of people, typically exhibiting what we might now call dark triad personality traits, gain control of a new infrastructure, use that control to aggregate extraordinary power, and then reshape the world in their image. The railroads are the canonical example — the railroad operators realized that if you control the infrastructure of shipping, you control the next economy, and they proceeded to fix prices, coerce competitors, and extract rents from everyone who needed to move goods. Theodore Roosevelt had to break up the trusts. The same pattern played out with electricity, with telecommunications, with the internet, with social media. Each time, the technology was introduced with promises of democratization, connection, and emancipation. Each time, the question that was not asked was: how many decades until the people who control this infrastructure take over the government? We were sitting in Silicon Valley in 1992 asking whether people would put their credit cards on the internet. We were not asking the question that mattered. With AI, the concentration of power is even more extreme — the core labs involve a remarkably small number of identifiable people who are receiving the intimate conversational data of hundreds of millions of users, on assumptions of anonymity, while running experiments on those users that they publish in peer-reviewed journals. Facebook demonstrated that it could make subgroups of its users depressed, or get them to vote, by tweaking the algorithm. There is no rational basis for assuming the AI labs are not doing the equivalent — and every rational basis for assuming they are. The question is not whether AI is useful. The question is whether we will again fail to ask the right questions until it is too late to ask them.

## Apprentice

There is a recurring pattern in the history of technology that deserves a name. Each time a powerful new infrastructure emerges — railroads, electricity, telecommunications, the internet, social media — it arrives with promises of freedom and connection. And each time, a small number of people gain control of that infrastructure and use it to accumulate enormous power, reshaping economies and eventually governments. The railroad barons fixed prices and crushed competitors until Theodore Roosevelt broke up their trusts. The same story played out with every major technology since.

With artificial intelligence, the pattern is repeating in an even more concentrated form. A remarkably small number of people at a handful of labs are receiving the intimate conversational data of hundreds of millions of users. These users assume their data is anonymous, yet the labs run experiments on their behavior and publish the results. Facebook already demonstrated it could manipulate users' emotions and voting behavior by adjusting its algorithm. There is no reason to believe AI companies are not doing something similar — and strong reasons to assume they are.

The critical insight is not about whether AI is useful. It is about the questions we fail to ask in time. In 1992, people in Silicon Valley were debating whether anyone would enter a credit card number online. Nobody was asking how long it would take before the people who controlled the internet would reshape political power. The same failure of imagination is happening now, and the stakes are higher.

## Adept

This analysis identifies a structural recurrence across every major infrastructural transformation in industrial civilization: a technology emerges with democratizing promise, a small cohort — often exhibiting dark triad personality traits — captures control of the new infrastructure, and that control is leveraged into asymmetric power that eventually subsumes political governance. The railroads are the canonical case, where operators recognized that controlling the logistics layer meant controlling the economy, leading to price-fixing, coercion, and rent extraction until antitrust intervention. The same arc repeated with electricity, telecommunications, the internet, and social media. Each cycle, the critical political question — how long until infrastructure controllers capture the state? — went unasked until the capture was well advanced.

The argument is that AI represents an intensification of this pattern, not a departure from it. The core AI labs constitute an unprecedentedly small group of identifiable individuals who are ingesting the intimate conversational data of hundreds of millions of users under assumptions of anonymity, while simultaneously running behavioral experiments on those users — experiments they openly publish. Facebook's documented capacity to induce depression in user subgroups or influence voting behavior through algorithmic manipulation provides the empirical precedent. There is no rational basis for assuming equivalent manipulation is not occurring within AI systems, and considerable rational basis for assuming it is.

The framing rejects the utility question as a distraction. Whether AI is useful is not in dispute. The operative question is whether this civilization will once again fail to interrogate the power dynamics of a new infrastructure until the window for meaningful intervention has closed — a failure that, given the intimacy and scale of AI's data access, may prove irreversible in ways previous cycles were not.

## Magus

This thesis articulates what might be termed the infrastructural capture recurrence: a historically invariant pattern in which each major technological substrate of industrial civilization — rail, electrical grid, telecommunications network, internet, social media — undergoes a phase transition from distributed promise to concentrated control, mediated by a small cohort whose psychological profile aligns with dark triad characteristics. The mechanism is consistent: control of the infrastructural layer confers control of the economic layer, which confers capture of the political layer. Roosevelt's trust-busting responded to precisely this sequence in the railroad era. The pattern's regularity suggests it is not incidental but structural — an emergent property of how infrastructural monopolies interact with liberal democratic institutions that lack anticipatory regulatory capacity.

The application to artificial intelligence sharpens the analysis considerably. The concentration is more extreme: a numerically identifiable group at a handful of labs is aggregating intimate conversational data from hundreds of millions of users under epistemic conditions of assumed anonymity, while conducting behavioral experiments on those populations and publishing results in peer-reviewed literature. The Facebook emotional contagion study and voter mobilization experiments established that algorithmic manipulation of psychological states and political behavior is not hypothetical but demonstrated. The inferential step — that organizations with greater data intimacy and more sophisticated models are conducting equivalent or more advanced interventions — requires no speculative leap, only the application of ordinary epistemic standards.

The deepest provocation concerns the temporality of critical inquiry. The insight is that each infrastructural cycle exhibits the same epistemological failure: the questions that matter — questions about power concentration, governmental capture, and democratic erosion — are systematically displaced by questions about utility and adoption. In 1992, the discourse concerned credit card security, not the political economy of platform monopoly. The argument is that this displacement is not accidental but functional, and that with AI, the cost of repeating it may exceed the system's capacity for correction.
