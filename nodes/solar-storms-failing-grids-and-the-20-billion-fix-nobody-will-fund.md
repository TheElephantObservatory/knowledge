# Solar Storms, Failing Grids, and the $20 Billion Fix Nobody Will Fund

The lights go out everywhere, all at once.

---

> A Carrington-scale solar storm could destroy irreplaceable grid transformers worldwide, triggering simultaneous nuclear meltdowns — yet segmenting the grid with simple disconnect switches for roughly $20 billion could reduce this existential risk by 95%.

Jim Rutt and Bret Weinstein converge on what they consider one of the most underappreciated civilizational risks: the vulnerability of electrical grid infrastructure to a Carrington-scale geomagnetic event. The core argument is that a sufficiently powerful solar flare striking Earth head-on would induce electromagnetic fields in long continuous stretches of wire sufficient to destroy major transformers — components that take over a year to manufacture and source, and which are now predominantly made in China. The cascading consequence is that nuclear reactors, which require continuous electrical power to circulate cooling water, would be forced onto diesel backup, and if that supply chain fails, you get Fukushima-scale events at potentially hundreds of sites simultaneously. Rutt adds the additional layer that the Earth's magnetic field — which provides the primary natural shielding against such events — is currently weakening and its poles are migrating with increasing velocity, suggesting the planet's natural protection is diminishing precisely as solar activity is in an active phase. The probability of a Carrington-level event is estimated at roughly 1-in-500 per year, meaning a 2% annual risk that compounds over time. Rutt argues the mitigation is almost insultingly cheap: for roughly $20 billion, you could segment the grid into 50-mile sections using simple mechanical disconnect switches, reducing the risk by an estimated 95% by preventing the long continuous wire runs needed to build up dangerous field strength. Neither party has seriously engaged with this. What makes the conversation pointed is the specific data point that the United States had a stockpile of spare transformers — already inadequate — and sent a significant portion of it to Ukraine, leaving the domestic grid even more exposed.

## Apprentice

Jim Rutt and Bret Weinstein highlight what they consider one of civilization's most dangerously overlooked threats: a massive solar storm frying the electrical grid. The sun periodically hurls enormous bursts of energy toward Earth, and if one as powerful as the 1859 Carrington Event hit us today, it would generate electrical surges in the long stretches of wire that make up our power grid, destroying the giant transformers that keep everything running. These transformers take over a year to build and are mostly manufactured in China. Without them, the grid goes dark — potentially for years.

The nightmare scenario compounds from there. Nuclear power plants need constant electricity to pump cooling water. When the grid fails, they switch to diesel generators, but if diesel supply chains collapse in a grid-down world, you could face Fukushima-style meltdowns at hundreds of nuclear sites simultaneously. Rutt adds that Earth's magnetic field — our natural shield against solar storms — is currently weakening and its poles are shifting faster than usual, meaning our protection is dropping just as solar activity ramps up.

The striking part of this argument is how cheap the fix would be. For roughly $20 billion, the grid could be broken into 50-mile segments using simple mechanical switches, cutting the risk by an estimated 95%. The probability of such an event is about 1-in-500 per year — small in any given year, but compounding relentlessly over decades. Rutt also notes that the already insufficient U.S. stockpile of spare transformers was partly shipped to Ukraine, leaving the country even more exposed.

## Adept

Jim Rutt and Bret Weinstein identify Carrington-scale geomagnetic disturbances as a profoundly underpriced civilizational risk. The mechanism is well-understood: a coronal mass ejection striking Earth's magnetosphere induces geomagnetically induced currents (GICs) in long continuous conductors — principally high-voltage transmission lines — sufficient to saturate and destroy extra-high-voltage (EHV) transformers. These components have manufacturing lead times exceeding twelve months, with production capacity concentrated in China. The cascading failure mode is what elevates this from infrastructure disruption to existential territory: nuclear facilities require uninterrupted power for reactor cooling systems, and when grid power fails, they depend on diesel backup generators with finite fuel supplies. A simultaneous grid collapse across continental scales could produce Fukushima-type loss-of-coolant events at potentially hundreds of reactor sites.

Rutt introduces a compounding geophysical variable: the Earth's magnetic dipole moment is measurably weakening while the magnetic poles undergo accelerating excursion, reducing the magnetospheric shielding that attenuates incoming solar particle flux. This diminishing natural protection coincides with an active phase of the solar cycle, creating a convergence of increased vulnerability and increased threat probability. The annualized probability of a Carrington-class event is estimated at approximately 0.2%, which compounds to substantial cumulative risk over decadal timescales.

The policy failure Rutt emphasizes is the disproportion between risk magnitude and mitigation cost. Segmenting the transmission grid into approximately 50-mile sections using mechanical disconnect switches — at an estimated cost of $20 billion — would reduce GIC buildup by roughly 95%, effectively neutralizing the primary damage mechanism. This figure is trivial relative to the potential consequence. The additional data point that the United States depleted a significant fraction of its already inadequate transformer reserve by shipping units to Ukraine underscores the absence of serious strategic planning around this vulnerability.

## Magus

The Rutt-Weinstein convergence on geomagnetic infrastructure risk crystallizes a failure mode that sits at the intersection of heliophysics, grid engineering, nuclear safety doctrine, and geopolitical supply chain analysis. The physical mechanism — geomagnetically induced currents arising from rapid geomagnetic field variations during coronal mass ejection impact — is not speculative; it is the same phenomenon that destroyed Quebec's grid in 1989 at a fraction of Carrington-scale intensity. The critical variable is conductor length: GIC magnitude scales with the continuous uninterrupted length of grounded conductors, meaning the modern interconnected transmission network functions as an enormous antenna for magnetospheric perturbation. EHV transformer destruction follows from half-cycle saturation, a well-characterized failure mode that renders units irreparable. With global manufacturing capacity for such transformers measured in the low hundreds per year and production overwhelmingly concentrated in East Asian facilities, replacement timelines under mass-failure conditions extend into years or decades.

Rutt's geophysical addendum deserves careful attention. The dipole moment of Earth's magnetic field has declined approximately 9% over the past 170 years, with the South Atlantic Anomaly expanding and magnetic pole migration accelerating to roughly 55 km/year. Whether this presages a full polarity reversal or merely an excursion, the operational consequence is identical: reduced magnetospheric standoff distance and diminished attenuation of solar energetic particle events during precisely the epoch when Solar Cycle 25 is exhibiting higher-than-predicted activity.

The normative claim is stark in its cost-benefit asymmetry. Grid segmentation via mechanical sectionalization at ~50-mile intervals — an engineering intervention requiring no novel technology — carries an estimated price of $20 billion against a threat with ~0.2% annualized probability and civilization-terminal consequence. The revealed preference of policymakers, who allocated scarce transformer reserves to Ukrainian grid reconstruction while leaving domestic GIC vulnerability unaddressed, suggests this risk occupies a cognitive blind spot where low-frequency, high-consequence events are systematically discounted against immediate geopolitical imperatives.
