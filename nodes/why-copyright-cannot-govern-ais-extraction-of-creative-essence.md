# Why Copyright Cannot Govern AI's Extraction of Creative Essence

The fence that floats above the fence.

---

> Copyright law draws fences around copies, but large language models extract the essence of creative work without copying it — floating above those fences entirely. Governing this new kind of value transfer requires a new category of right, not just better enforcement of old ones.

Large language models are not copiers. They are not cameras, tape recorders, or printing presses. They are something genuinely new — essence extractors — and the entire existing legal and economic framework built around copyright is insufficient to govern them, because that framework was designed for a two-dimensional world and LLMs operate in a higher dimension that simply lifts out of it. Copyright, as it has existed since the Statute of Anne, governs the expression of ideas — the bottle, in John Perry Barlow's metaphor — not the essence, the wine inside. You can protect a specific painting, a specific novel, a specific recording. What you cannot protect, and what copyright was never designed to protect, is the idea itself, the style, the essence. LLMs are essence extractors. They can consume a thousand bottles of wine and reconstitute the essence in a new bottle, leaving the originals intact. No copying has occurred in the traditional sense. No reproduction. And yet the economic value — the thing that motivated the creator to create — has been transferred. The flatland metaphor captures this precisely: copyright law draws fences in two dimensions, but the LLM is a three-dimensional object that simply floats above the fence and teleports the value out. This is why the current legal battles — with rights holders on one side and big tech on the other — are unlikely to resolve the underlying problem regardless of how the courts rule. What is actually needed is a new category of right, something like AI rights rooted not in statutory copyright but in the moral rights of creators — a mechanism by which human authors can signal, in machine-readable form, how they wish their work to be treated when used for AI training. This is the animating idea behind the AMPLE license concept: not a replacement for copyright, but a new layer that operates in the dimension where the actual value transfer is now occurring.

## Apprentice

Copyright law was built to prevent unauthorized copying — someone reprinting your book, duplicating your song, reproducing your painting. But large language models don't copy in any traditional sense. They absorb thousands of works and learn the patterns, styles, and ideas within them, then generate something new. The originals remain untouched. No duplication has occurred. And yet something valuable has clearly been taken.

Think of it this way: copyright protects the bottle, not the wine inside. A specific arrangement of words, a specific recording, a specific image — those are bottles. But the essence — the style, the knowledge, the creative DNA — that's the wine. LLMs have figured out how to drink the wine without breaking the bottle. Copyright was never designed to handle this, because until now, the only way to steal wine was to steal the bottle too.

This is why current lawsuits between creators and tech companies are unlikely to truly resolve anything, no matter who wins. The problem isn't that the old rules are being broken — it's that the game has moved to a field where the old rules don't apply. What creators actually need is a new kind of right: a way to declare, in terms machines can understand, how their work should be treated when AI systems learn from it. The AMPLE license concept represents one attempt to build exactly this — not replacing copyright, but adding a new layer where the real value transfer is now happening.

## Adept

The central argument is that large language models represent a categorically new technology — not copiers, not reproduction machines, but essence extractors — and that copyright law, designed since the Statute of Anne to govern the expression of ideas rather than the ideas themselves, is structurally incapable of addressing what LLMs actually do. John Perry Barlow's bottle-and-wine distinction is instructive here: copyright protects the bottle (the specific fixed expression), but LLMs extract the wine (the style, the patterns, the latent creative value) and reconstitute it in entirely new containers, leaving the originals intact. No reproduction occurs in any legally cognizable sense, yet economic value transfers from creator to model operator.

The flatland metaphor sharpens this point. Copyright law operates by drawing two-dimensional fences around protected works. An LLM is a higher-dimensional object that simply lifts above those fences, accessing and transferring value through a plane the legal framework cannot reach. This dimensional mismatch explains why the ongoing litigation between rights holders and AI companies — regardless of outcome — cannot resolve the underlying structural problem. Courts are being asked to apply two-dimensional rules to a three-dimensional phenomenon.

The proposed resolution moves beyond statutory copyright toward something closer to moral rights — the idea that creators possess an inherent right to determine how their work is used, including for machine learning. The AMPLE license concept embodies this thinking: a machine-readable declaration by human authors specifying the terms under which their work may be ingested for AI training. It functions not as a replacement for copyright but as a new legal layer operating in the dimension where value extraction actually occurs, addressing the gap that existing intellectual property frameworks were never built to close.

## Magus

This analysis proceeds from a precise ontological claim: that large language models are not reproductive technologies but extractive ones, and that this distinction collapses the entire edifice of copyright as a governing framework for AI training. Copyright, from the Statute of Anne forward, has operated as a regime of expression-protection — it governs fixed instantiations of ideas, not the ideas themselves. Barlow's bottle-wine dichotomy maps cleanly onto the idea-expression doctrine: the law protects the bottle. LLMs, however, perform a distillation operation across the corpus, extracting statistical representations of style, structure, and semantic content — the wine — and reconstituting it in novel expressions without reproducing any protected work. The originals persist. No infringement occurs under any conventional reproduction or derivative-work analysis. Yet the economic incentive structure that copyright was designed to preserve is hollowed out.

The flatland metaphor formalizes this intuition topologically. Copyright's fences exist in the two-dimensional plane of fixed expression. The LLM operates in a higher-dimensional manifold — the space of learned representations — from which it projects back into expression-space at will. The fence is not breached; it is simply irrelevant to an entity that moves through dimensions the fence does not occupy. This dimensional incommensurability is why adjudication within existing doctrine — fair use balancing, transformativeness tests, substantial similarity analysis — cannot yield coherent results. The framework lacks the degrees of freedom necessary to capture what is occurring.

The prescription follows logically: a new category of right is required, rooted not in statutory copyright's reproduction-centric logic but in the moral-rights tradition — the creator's inherent claim over the conditions of use. The AMPLE license concept instantiates this as a machine-readable protocol through which authors declare training-use permissions, creating a normative and potentially enforceable layer that operates precisely in the representational dimension where value extraction occurs. It is not copyright reform but copyright supplementation — an acknowledgment that the legal topology must expand to match the technological one.
