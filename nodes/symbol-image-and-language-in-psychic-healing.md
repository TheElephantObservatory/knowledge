# Symbol, Image, and Language in Psychic Healing

The mythic pulse beneath the word

---

> The psyche speaks three distinct languages — image, symbol, and discourse — and genuine psychological healing requires recovering all three, not just the rational one. Myth isn't primitive; it's irreplaceable.

There is a tripartite structure to the languages through which the psyche knows and communicates with itself: symbol, image, and language. These are not interchangeable modes — each operates at a different layer of the psyche and carries a different kind of meaning. Language is omniscient, discursive, and propositional. Symbols are universal and abstract — they carry meaning that transcends any particular context. But image is the native tongue of the deeper unconscious, and in particular of the dynamics of personality and character. Images carry setting, emotion, and context in a way that neither symbols nor language can fully replicate. This is why myth — and the images embedded within myth — is not merely decorative or pre-scientific. The myth of Psyche and Eros, for instance, encodes something that a psychological treatise cannot: the impossibility of isolating the psyche from the dynamisms of the world system, from love, from nature, from the forces that both destroy and resuscitate the self. The motivating point of a genuine metapsychology, then, is not only to prove what is scientifically the case about the mind. It is to recover and cultivate the languages — imaginal, symbolic, and discursive — that allow for the resuscitation, resurrection, and anamnesis of the psyche. Healing the psyche requires first recovering the full range of languages through which the psyche can be in conversation with itself.

## Apprentice

Most of us assume that understanding the mind means putting things into words — explaining, analyzing, reasoning through. But this view misses something important. The deeper layers of the psyche don't primarily speak in sentences. They speak in images: scenes with mood, setting, and feeling baked in. Symbols work differently still — they carry meanings that float free of any particular story or context. These three modes aren't just stylistic variations on the same thing. They reach different depths and do different work.

This is why myth matters in a way that goes beyond history or literature. A story like Psyche and Eros isn't a clumsy attempt to explain psychology before psychology existed. It encodes something a clinical textbook cannot: what it actually feels like to be a self entangled with love, loss, destruction, and renewal. The image carries the whole situation at once.

The implication is significant. If the psyche has multiple native languages, then healing it isn't just a matter of correct diagnosis or rational insight. It requires recovering the ability to think and feel in images and symbols — to let the deeper mind speak in its own register. Resuscitating the psyche means restoring the full range of ways it can know itself.

## Adept

A recurring problem in depth psychology is the conflation of three genuinely distinct semiotic registers: discursive language, symbol, and image. Discursive language is propositional and omniscient — it can say anything about anything, but at the cost of abstraction from lived experience. Symbols operate at a higher level of generality still, carrying meanings that transcend particular contexts and cultures. Images, by contrast, are irreducibly contextual: they embed affect, setting, and relational dynamics in a single gestalt that neither symbol nor proposition can decompose without remainder.

This distinction has direct consequences for how myth is understood. The myth of Psyche and Eros, on this reading, is not pre-scientific psychology awaiting translation into clinical language. It is doing something that clinical language structurally cannot do: holding together, in imaginal form, the entanglement of selfhood with eros, with nature, with the forces of dissolution and regeneration. To translate the myth fully into propositional content is to lose precisely what makes it psychologically operative.

The argument that follows is that a serious metapsychology cannot restrict itself to the discursive register. The goal of psychological inquiry — and of psychological healing — is not only to produce accurate theoretical accounts of the mind but to recover the imaginal and symbolic languages through which the psyche conducts its own self-knowledge. Anamnesis, in this framework, is not merely recollection of forgotten facts. It is the reactivation of the psyche's capacity to be in conversation with itself across all three registers.

## Magus

The tripartite semiotics proposed here — image, symbol, discursive language — maps onto a stratified ontology of psychic depth that resists reduction to any single register. Discursive language, as the most surface-level mode, is propositional, diachronic, and capable of metalinguistic self-reference, but it purchases these capacities at the cost of evacuating the affective and contextual density that constitutes lived psychic reality. Symbols, operating at a higher degree of abstraction, achieve universality and transcontextual portability — the Jungian archetype being the canonical instance — but sacrifice the particularity of situation. Image occupies a structurally distinct position: it is the primary language of the deeper unconscious precisely because it preserves the indissoluble unity of affect, setting, relational field, and temporal unfolding that the other registers must analytically sunder.

The mythological corpus, on this account, is not a repository of protoscientific hypothesis but an archive of imaginal knowledge irreducible to its propositional paraphrase. The Apuleian myth of Psyche and Eros enacts, rather than merely describes, the constitutive impossibility of isolating the psychic subject from the dynamisms of eros, chthonic dissolution, and pneumatic resuscitation. The image-sequence of the myth performs what no metapsychological treatise can: the simultaneous holding of destruction and regeneration as a single psychic movement. Hillman's imaginal psychology and Corbin's mundus imaginalis both gesture toward this irreducibility, though the present formulation presses further into its therapeutic implications.

The normative claim that emerges is that genuine metapsychology must be triglossic — operating fluently across imaginal, symbolic, and discursive registers without collapsing one into another. Anamnesis, understood not as Platonic recollection of eidetic content but as the reactivation of the psyche's full semiotic range, becomes the orienting telos of psychological inquiry. Pathology, correspondingly, may be understood in part as the foreclosure of one or more of these registers — the reduction of psychic self-knowledge to the merely propositional.
