# How Social Science Hides Its Values While Claiming Objectivity

The facts were never neutral.

---

> Psychology and social science claim to be value-free, but their core concepts — competence, good parenting, moral development — smuggle in normative judgments disguised as objective descriptions. The remedy is not relativism but radical reflexivity: making hidden values visible and contestable.

One of the most consequential and least examined problems in psychological and social science is the treatment of values. The standard scientific norm — inherited from positivism — insists on a strict fact-value distinction: science studies what is; ethics concerns what ought to be. You cannot derive an ought from an is. This seems like intellectual hygiene. In practice, it is a form of self-deception that causes serious damage.

The psychological concepts we actually use are not value-neutral descriptions of natural kinds. They are normative categories through and through. 'Social competence' already encodes a judgment about what counts as competent. 'Authoritative parenting' already encodes a judgment about what counts as good parenting. 'Moral development' encodes a judgment about the direction in which development ought to go. You cannot identify an authoritative parent independently of the values that define what authority means in a given cultural context. When Western researchers travel to China and classify parenting styles using categories developed in a different moral universe, they are not making objective observations — they are imposing one value system while believing they are recording facts.

The deeper problem is this: by pretending to eliminate values from inquiry, researchers do not actually eliminate them — they hide them. The values get smuggled in through the choice of constructs, the design of instruments, the framing of questions, and then the results are presented as objective findings, as if the normative dimension were not there. This produces a kind of epistemological bad faith: the researcher believes they are describing the world as it is, while actually encoding a particular vision of how it ought to be, with no mechanism for examining or contesting that vision.

The alternative is not to abandon rigor but to practice what might be called radical reflexivity — a sustained, disciplined examination of how one's own values, cultural location, and pre-understandings structure what one sees and what one thinks. This does not collapse into relativism. It imposes stricter constraints, not looser ones, because every claim must now be corroborated across multiple mediated perspectives, and any inconsistency between them demands resolution rather than concealment behind a claim to objectivity.

## Apprentice

There is a widely accepted rule in science: stick to facts, leave values to philosophers. Science tells us what is; ethics tells us what ought to be. This sounds reasonable, but in psychology and social science, it quietly causes serious problems.

The concepts researchers use are not neutral descriptions of nature. When a psychologist measures "social competence," they have already decided what competent looks like. When a study classifies parents as "authoritative" or "permissive," it has already baked in assumptions about what good parenting means — assumptions shaped by a particular culture. When Western researchers apply these categories in China or Nigeria, they are not recording objective facts. They are projecting one culture's values onto another while believing they are being scientific.

The real danger is that by claiming to be value-free, researchers do not actually remove their values — they just hide them. The values slip in through the questions asked, the tools used, and the way results are framed, and nobody examines them because everyone assumes they are not there. The solution is not to give up on rigor. It is to become more rigorous by openly examining the values built into every concept, checking findings across different cultural perspectives, and treating disagreements as signals that something important has been hidden rather than resolved.

## Adept

The fact-value distinction inherited from logical positivism functions as a foundational norm in psychological and social science: researchers study what is, leaving prescriptive questions to ethicists. In practice, this distinction operates less as intellectual hygiene and more as a mechanism of concealment. The constructs that organize empirical research — social competence, authoritative parenting, moral development, psychological well-being — are not value-neutral natural kinds. They are normative categories that encode judgments about desirable endpoints, proper conduct, and developmental direction. One cannot operationalize "authoritative parenting" without presupposing a culturally specific account of legitimate authority. The construct does not describe a fact; it enacts a value.

This produces what can be called epistemological bad faith. By claiming value-neutrality, researchers do not eliminate normative commitments — they render them invisible. Values enter through construct selection, instrument design, sample framing, and interpretive conventions, then exit as "objective findings" insulated from normative scrutiny. When Western-derived parenting typologies are applied cross-culturally, the result is not empirical discovery but normative imposition operating under empirical cover.

The corrective is not relativism but radical reflexivity: a disciplined practice of surfacing the values, cultural locations, and pre-understandings that structure inquiry at every level. This approach does not weaken scientific claims — it strengthens them by requiring corroboration across multiple perspectival standpoints. Inconsistencies between perspectives become productive signals rather than noise to be suppressed. The standard of evidence becomes more demanding, not less, because the researcher can no longer appeal to a fictive objectivity to foreclose debate about the normative architecture of their work.

## Magus

The positivist inheritance in psychological science rests on a performative contradiction: the fact-value distinction, presented as a methodological safeguard, functions as the primary vehicle through which unexamined values achieve epistemic authority. The constructs that constitute the discipline's explanatory apparatus — social competence, authoritative parenting, moral development, executive function as adaptive capacity — are not extensional descriptions of mind-independent phenomena. They are intensional categories whose criteria of application are irreducibly normative. To identify an instance of "authoritative parenting" requires prior commitment to a substantive account of legitimate authority, developmental teleology, and the proper relation between autonomy and compliance. The construct does not map onto a natural kind; it constitutes a normative kind misrecognized as natural.

This misrecognition produces a distinctive epistemic pathology: values that enter inquiry through construct formation, operationalization, and instrument calibration are laundered through methodological procedure and returned as empirical findings stripped of their normative provenance. The cross-cultural application of Western-derived taxonomies exemplifies the structure precisely — a parenting typology developed within one moral-developmental framework is deployed as a universal observational grid, converting cultural difference into deviation from an unmarked norm. The researcher occupies the position of what Gadamer would recognize as naive objectivism: the belief that one's own horizon is not a horizon but a transparent window onto the real.

Radical reflexivity, as the alternative, does not entail the collapse into perspectival relativism that positivists fear. It imposes more stringent epistemic demands: every empirical claim must survive triangulation across genuinely distinct hermeneutic standpoints, and discrepancies between perspectives constitute data requiring theoretical resolution rather than methodological suppression. The result is not weaker objectivity but what might be termed mediated objectivity — knowledge that earns its claim to validity precisely by making its constitutive value commitments available for contestation.
