# Why Modern Problem-Solving May Not Be Enough for the Metacrisis

The fire brigade that helped build the fire.

---

> Modern optimism argues humanity always develops tools to fix the messes it creates. But the metacrisis challenges this narrative: the institutions and governance systems needed to deploy those tools may themselves be broken, making technical capability alone insufficient.

The environmental and civilizational challenges of the present moment — what the metamodern community calls the metacrisis — pose a specific philosophical test for the modern optimistic narrative. Hicks's response is structurally modernist: yes, we make messes, but we also develop the tools to clean them up; two steps forward, one step back; the green revolution feeds billions even as it creates new problems; urban sanitation was a catastrophe until it wasn't. This is a coherent and historically defensible position. But Dempsey's pushback is that this framing can function as a kind of ideological comfort — a narrative of progress that makes it psychologically easier to underestimate the severity of what is actually unfolding. The philosophical question is not whether the modern toolkit *could* solve these problems in principle, but whether the institutional, political, and motivational structures that actually exist are adequate to the scale and speed of the challenges. A genuine metamodern stance would hold both: the modern tools and the modern confidence in reason are necessary, but they are not sufficient if the social and political systems that deploy those tools are themselves part of the problem. The metacrisis is not just a technical problem awaiting a technical solution; it is also a crisis of the sensemaking and governance structures that would need to coordinate any solution.

## Apprentice

There's a familiar story about human progress: we create problems, but we also figure out how to solve them. Cities were once death traps of disease until we invented modern sanitation. Famines threatened millions until agricultural breakthroughs fed billions. Two steps forward, one step back — but always moving forward. This is the optimistic case, and it has real historical evidence behind it.

But there's a serious counterargument. Knowing how to solve a problem and actually solving it are very different things. Today's biggest challenges — climate change, ecological collapse, political fragmentation — aren't just waiting for the right technology. They require massive coordination between governments, institutions, and populations that currently struggle to agree on basic facts, let alone act together at speed.

The deeper insight is that both perspectives contain truth. The tools of modern science and reason are genuinely necessary — no one solves these problems by abandoning them. But confidence in those tools can become a kind of comfort blanket, making it easier to downplay how broken the systems are that would need to wield them. The real crisis isn't just environmental or technical. It's a crisis in our collective ability to make sense of what's happening and organize a response.

## Adept

Stephen Hicks advances a structurally modernist response to civilizational risk: history demonstrates a recurring pattern where humanity generates problems and subsequently develops the tools to address them. The green revolution, urban sanitation, and countless other examples illustrate a ratcheting progress — messy, nonlinear, but ultimately directional. This is not naive optimism; it is an empirically grounded reading of modernity's track record. The modern project, on this account, is self-correcting.

Dempsey's challenge targets not the logic of this argument but its psychological and institutional implications. The narrative of inevitable self-correction can function as ideological comfort, reducing the felt urgency of present crises. The philosophical pivot is crucial: the question is not whether modern tools could solve the metacrisis in principle, but whether existing institutional, political, and motivational structures are adequate to deploy those tools at the necessary scale and speed. This is a question about governance capacity, collective sensemaking, and coordination — not about technical possibility.

A genuinely metamodern position holds both claims in productive tension. Modern reason and its tools remain necessary conditions for addressing existential-scale challenges. But they are not sufficient if the social and political systems responsible for deploying them are themselves compromised — captured by short-term incentives, fragmented by epistemic polarization, or paralyzed by coordination failures. The metacrisis, properly understood, is not merely a set of technical problems but a crisis of the very sensemaking and governance architectures that any coherent response would require.

## Magus

Hicks's defense of modernity against metacrisis discourse operates through a familiar but rigorous dialectical move: modernity is acknowledged as generative of systemic risk precisely because it is also generative of systemic capacity. The green revolution, the sanitation revolution, the demographic transition — each instantiates a pattern where catastrophic externalities produced by modernization are subsequently metabolized by further modernization. Progress is not frictionless but recursive; the toolkit expands in response to the damage it produces. This is structurally coherent and historiographically defensible, drawing on the same logic that underwrites Pinker, Rosling, and the broader neo-Enlightenment project.

Dempsey's intervention reframes the debate from one of capability to one of adequacy under constraint. The critical question is not whether rational-technical capacity exists in principle but whether the actually-existing institutional ecology — governance structures, incentive architectures, epistemic commons — possesses the coordination bandwidth to deploy that capacity at the tempo and scale demanded by converging polycrises. The optimistic narrative, Dempsey suggests, risks functioning as what Sloterdijk might call an "immunological fantasy" — a self-reinforcing belief structure that dampens the very alarm signals needed to trigger adequate response.

The metamodern synthesis that emerges from this exchange refuses both the modernist confidence in autopoietic self-correction and the postmodern or collapsitarian rejection of Enlightenment tools. It insists instead on a double bind: the instruments of modern reason are necessary but structurally insufficient when the meta-systemic conditions for their deployment — collective sensemaking, legitimate governance, long-horizon coordination — are themselves degraded. The metacrisis is thus revealed as irreducibly reflexive: it is a crisis not only of what we face but of the very apparatus through which facing-toward-problems is organized.
