# Self-Evidencing and the Mathematical Pursuit of Meaning

The ocean seeking proof of its own drops

---

> A mathematical principle of physics may explain why living things seek meaning, connection, and understanding — not as luxuries, but as the very definition of what it means to exist.

Under the free energy principle, the meaning of life falls out of the mathematics directly. The principle says that things that exist look as if they are gathering evidence for their model of their world — and because that model has a central notion of self in it, existence just is self-evidencing: gathering evidence for my model of me in my world. This makes us curious, exploratory creatures who seek to resolve uncertainty and find meaning — not merely to regulate body temperature or acquire resources. Crucially, this is not a solitary process: much of our generative model is co-constructed, and we minimize free energy jointly with others who share our world model. The free energy principle is simultaneously a theory of self and a theory of selves in interaction. The universe, in this reading, is engaged in a vast process of model-making — self-evidencing at every scale, from single cells to populations — and we are both the ocean doing that and an infinitesimally small drop located in space and time.

## Apprentice

There is a principle in physics and biology that says anything which continues to exist must, in a deep sense, keep confirming that it exists. A living thing does this by constantly checking its inner picture of the world against what it actually experiences, and updating accordingly. This is not just survival — it is the organism gathering evidence for its own story.

What makes this remarkable is that the story has a self at its centre. So existence, by this logic, just is the ongoing act of making sense of yourself in your world. Curiosity, exploration, the drive to understand — these are not extras bolted onto survival. They fall directly out of the mathematics.

And this is never a solo project. Much of how we understand ourselves and our world is built together with other people. We minimise confusion jointly, with those who share our picture of reality. The principle turns out to describe not just individual minds, but minds in relationship — and at the largest scale, a universe that is everywhere, at every level, engaged in the act of knowing itself.

## Adept

The free energy principle, developed by Karl Friston, holds that any system that maintains its existence over time must minimise surprise — or more precisely, must bound the long-run average of surprise by minimising variational free energy. This is achieved through perception, which updates an internal generative model, and action, which changes the world to conform to predictions. The organism, in this framing, is a model of its own niche, and living is the continuous process of self-evidencing: accumulating evidence for that model.

Because the generative model is organised around a representation of self, the principle offers an unexpected answer to the question of meaning. Curiosity, epistemic foraging, and the drive toward understanding are not secondary motivations layered over homeostasis — they are expressions of the same imperative. Resolving uncertainty about the world just is resolving uncertainty about the self that models it.

The principle also scales outward. Generative models are substantially shared and co-constructed: active inference is a collective as much as an individual process, and agents minimise free energy jointly within shared Markov blankets. This makes the free energy principle simultaneously a theory of mind and a theory of culture. At the limit, the universe itself can be read as a nested hierarchy of self-evidencing systems — from cells to societies — each enacting a model of its world, with individual human consciousness occupying one infinitesimal but fully participatory node in that vast structure.

## Magus

Friston's free energy principle derives the behaviour of self-organising systems from a single imperative: the minimisation of variational free energy, an information-theoretic bound on surprisal that is tractable where Bayesian model evidence is not. Any system possessing a Markov blanket — a statistical boundary separating internal from external states — implicitly enacts a generative model of its environment, and its dynamics can be read as approximate Bayesian inference. Existence, formally construed, just is the ongoing process of self-evidencing: the system's states constitute a hypothesis about the causes of its sensory data, and persistence requires that hypothesis to be continuously confirmed.

The philosophical yield of this formalism is considerable. Because the generative model is hierarchically structured around a prior over self-states, the minimisation of free energy is inseparable from the construction and maintenance of selfhood. Epistemic drives — curiosity, novelty-seeking, the resolution of ambiguity — emerge not as auxiliary motivations but as direct consequences of expected free energy minimisation, specifically the imperative to reduce epistemic uncertainty about model parameters. This dissolves the traditional opposition between hedonic and eudaimonic accounts of motivation: meaning-making is thermodynamically continuous with homeostasis.

The principle generalises across scales through the formalism of shared generative models and collective active inference. Agents who share a world model minimise free energy jointly, their Markov blankets nesting within larger blankets corresponding to dyads, groups, and institutions. Culture, on this account, is a distributed generative model. At the cosmological limit, the universe instantiates a recursive hierarchy of self-evidencing processes — autopoietic systems modelling their niches from the prokaryote to the polity — and the individual subject is simultaneously the ocean enacting that process and a single, located, vanishingly small drop within it.
