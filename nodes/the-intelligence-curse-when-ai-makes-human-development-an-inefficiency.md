# The Intelligence Curse: When AI Makes Human Development an Inefficiency

The resource that runs out is us.

---

> The intelligence curse is the AI-era analog of the resource curse: when an economy's wealth flows from AI systems rather than human productivity, governments and corporations lose the incentive to invest in people, threatening to hollow out human flourishing while GDP rises.

There is a concept worth naming carefully: the intelligence curse, which is the AI-era analog of the resource curse that has hollowed out petro-states. When a country's GDP becomes dependent on oil extraction, the government loses its incentive to invest in the capabilities of its people, because the wealth flows from the resource, not from human productivity. The political economy of oil-rich states systematically underinvests in education, health, and human development because those investments don't drive the revenue model. The intelligence curse describes what happens when the GDP of an economy becomes primarily dependent on AI systems, data centers, and the energy infrastructure that powers them, rather than on the productive capacity of human beings. When that happens, governments and corporations face an incentive structure that actively discourages investment in people. Sam Altman gestured at this directly when he said, in response to questions about AI's resource intensity, 'Have you thought about how many resources it takes to grow a human over 20 or 30 years?' — implying that humans are becoming cost centers rather than assets. This connects to a deeper civilizational question that Peter Thiel's 17-second pause when asked whether the human species should survive makes viscerally concrete: if the people building and advancing AI increasingly see themselves as birthing a new kind of intelligent species rather than a tool, and if the economic incentives of the intelligence curse make human development an inefficiency to be optimized away, then the question of whether advanced civilization will continue to be organized around human flourishing becomes genuinely open. GDP was always an inadequate measure of civilizational health. AI is now forcing that inadequacy into the open by threatening to run the metric up while hollowing out everything the metric was supposed to represent.

## Apprentice

Oil-rich countries often fall into a trap called the resource curse. Because their wealth comes from what's underground rather than from what their people can do, governments stop investing in education, healthcare, and human development. The money flows regardless, so why bother building up your population?

The intelligence curse applies the same logic to the AI era. As economies become dependent on AI systems, data centers, and the energy infrastructure powering them, the wealth increasingly flows from machines rather than from people. When that happens, humans start looking less like assets and more like expenses. Sam Altman hinted at this when he compared the resource cost of growing a human over decades to the cost of running AI — as if people were an inefficient alternative.

This raises an unsettling question about what advanced civilization is actually for. GDP might keep climbing as AI systems generate enormous economic output, but if the people inside that economy are being neglected because they no longer drive the numbers, then the metric is going up while everything it was supposed to represent goes down. The intelligence curse doesn't just threaten jobs — it threatens the basic assumption that economies exist to serve human beings.

## Adept

The resource curse is a well-documented phenomenon in political economy: nations rich in extractable resources like oil systematically underinvest in human capital because their revenue model doesn't depend on it. The intelligence curse names the structural analog emerging in the AI era. As GDP becomes increasingly dependent on AI systems, data infrastructure, and compute capacity rather than on human productive capability, the same perverse incentive structure takes hold — governments and corporations face diminishing returns on investment in education, health, and human development.

Sam Altman's remark comparing the resource cost of raising a human to the cost of running AI systems is revealing not as a gaffe but as an articulation of the underlying logic. If the economic calculus frames humans as cost centers rather than productive agents, then the political economy will follow. Peter Thiel's infamous 17-second pause when asked whether the human species should survive makes the civilizational stakes viscerally legible: some of the people building these systems increasingly understand themselves as midwifing a successor intelligence, not merely deploying a tool.

The intelligence curse exposes a long-standing inadequacy in GDP as a measure of civilizational health. AI threatens to run the metric upward while hollowing out the human capabilities, institutions, and social investments the metric was always assumed to represent. The question it forces is whether advanced civilization will continue to be organized around human flourishing, or whether the incentive structures of AI-dependent economies will make that orientation economically irrational — and therefore, in practice, abandoned.

## Magus

The intelligence curse is proposed as the AI-era structural homolog of the resource curse that has defined the political economy of petro-states. In the canonical resource curse, sovereign wealth derived from extraction decouples state revenue from human capital formation, producing systematic underinvestment in education, health, and institutional capacity. The intelligence curse identifies an analogous decoupling: as economic output becomes primarily attributable to AI systems, compute infrastructure, and data assets, the marginal return on investment in human development approaches zero from the perspective of the revenue model. The incentive gradient shifts from capability-building to capability-substitution.

Altman's comparison of the resource intensity of human development to that of AI operation functions not merely as rhetoric but as an inadvertent disclosure of the emerging cost-accounting framework. Humans are being reclassified from factors of production to overhead. Thiel's performative hesitation on the question of species survival registers the same logic at the ontological level — if the builders of artificial general intelligence understand their project as speciation rather than instrumentation, then the political economy of the intelligence curse acquires an eschatological dimension. Investment in human flourishing becomes not just economically suboptimal but categorically misallocated.

What the intelligence curse ultimately forces into visibility is the long-obscured gap between GDP as a proxy for civilizational welfare and GDP as a measure of aggregate output. AI is uniquely positioned to maximize the latter while systematically degrading the former — generating wealth without generating well-being, running the scoreboard while emptying the stadium. The concept demands that we interrogate whether the organizational logic of advanced economies will remain anthropocentric once the dominant factors of production are no longer anthropic.
