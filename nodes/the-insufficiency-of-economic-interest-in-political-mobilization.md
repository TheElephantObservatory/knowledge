# The Insufficiency of Economic Interest in Political Mobilization

The void where a common myth should be

---

> Economic arguments alone cannot build a mass political movement. Any serious emancipatory politics must also offer what religion and patriotism already provide: meaning, identity, and a reason to belong.

One of the deepest failures of the contemporary left — illustrated vividly by the Bernie Sanders campaigns — is the assumption that economic consciousness is the master key to political mobilization. The theory was elegant: the working class, once made aware of its true material conditions, would transcend its cultural, religious, racial, and generational differences and unite as the 99% against the billionaire class. It didn't happen. And the reason it didn't happen reveals something important about human motivation that a purely Marxist framework cannot fully account for. People are not only economic beings. They are also religious beings, spiritual beings, artistic beings, beings who form identity through myth, through belonging, through transcendence. The GOP understands this. Evangelical Christianity and patriotism are not just ideological tools — they are genuine sources of meaning, identity, and motivational energy. The left, by contrast, has largely abandoned the terrain of meaning-making and has offered in its place ironic nihilism, self-pitying relativism, or narcissistic New Age spirituality — none of which are capable of generating the kind of revolutionary consciousness that a genuine political transformation requires. The lesson is that any serious emancipatory politics must be grounded not only in a structural critique of capitalism but in an affirmative, living value system — something that can compete with religion and patriotism on their own terms, not by dismissing them but by offering something deeper and more integrative.

## Apprentice

One of the most important lessons from recent progressive politics is that telling people the economic facts is not enough to win them over. The theory behind campaigns like Bernie Sanders's was straightforward: if working people understood how badly the system was rigged against them, they would set aside their differences — religious, racial, cultural — and unite around shared economic interests. It didn't work. And understanding why tells us something deep about human nature.

People are not just economic creatures. They are also spiritual and cultural beings who need a sense of meaning, belonging, and identity. Religion and patriotism — whatever their limitations — genuinely provide those things. They give people a story about who they are, what they stand for, and why it matters. The modern left has largely stopped offering anything like that. Instead, it has offered irony, relativism, or a vague personal spirituality that doesn't translate into collective purpose or political energy.

The conclusion is that any politics serious about changing the world needs more than a critique of inequality. It needs to offer people something to believe in — a shared vision of the good, a sense of purpose, a reason to sacrifice. It has to compete with religion and nationalism not by mocking them, but by offering something equally powerful and more honest. Economic justice matters, but it cannot carry a movement alone.

## Adept

The repeated failure of class-based political coalitions in the contemporary United States — most visibly in the Sanders campaigns — exposes a foundational weakness in the orthodox Marxist theory of political mobilization. The assumption that economic consciousness, once raised, would override cultural, religious, and racial identities proved empirically false. People did not behave as rational economic actors awaiting the correct analysis. They behaved as meaning-seeking beings for whom identity, belonging, and transcendence are motivational forces at least as powerful as material interest.

The right, particularly through evangelical Christianity and nationalist patriotism, has long understood this. These are not merely ideological smokescreens obscuring class interest — they are genuine architectures of meaning that provide community, moral orientation, and a sense of sacred purpose. The left, by contrast, has largely ceded the terrain of meaning-making, substituting ironic detachment, relativism, or depoliticized spirituality — none of which can generate the affective solidarity required for sustained political transformation.

The argument here is not that structural critique of capitalism should be abandoned, but that it is insufficient on its own. A genuinely emancipatory politics must also operate as a living value system — one capable of competing with religion and nationalism not by dismissing them as false consciousness, but by offering something more integrative and more profound. This requires recovering a tradition of left politics that takes seriously the human need for myth, beauty, ritual, and collective transcendence.

## Magus

The Sanders campaigns function as a natural experiment disconfirming the base-superstructure model of political consciousness in its vulgar form. The hypothesis — that proletarian solidarity would emerge once ideological mystification was pierced by materialist analysis — foundered on the irreducible heterogeneity of working-class identity formation. Race, religion, gender, and regional belonging did not dissolve into class consciousness; they constituted the very medium through which political subjectivity was experienced and expressed. This is not merely a tactical failure but a theoretical one, pointing to the limits of a framework that treats culture as epiphenomenal to economic structure.

What the right grasped intuitively, and what figures from Gramsci to Laclau and Mouffe theorized with varying degrees of explicitness, is that hegemony is not secured through correct analysis but through the articulation of affective investments, symbolic identities, and moral imaginaries into a coherent political bloc. Evangelical Christianity and ethnonationalism succeed not as false consciousness but as genuine libidinal economies — they organize desire, grief, pride, and the hunger for transcendence into durable political formations. The contemporary left's retreat into ironic detachment, therapeutic individualism, or commodified New Age spirituality represents a catastrophic abandonment of this terrain.

The implication is that emancipatory politics requires what might be called a counter-hegemonic mythopoetics — not the cynical deployment of symbolic resources for instrumental ends, but the authentic construction of a living value system capable of addressing the full range of human need: the need for beauty, for sacred community, for a narrative of collective becoming. This demands engagement with the traditions of religious and civic life not as obstacles to be overcome but as reservoirs of motivational energy to be critically transformed.
