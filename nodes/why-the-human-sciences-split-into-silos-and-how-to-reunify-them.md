# Why the Human Sciences Split Into Silos and How to Reunify Them

The map ate the territory.

---

> The human sciences split into separate disciplines not by necessity but because 19th-century thinkers treated social phenomena as fixed substances. Replacing that assumption with a process-based ontology dissolves artificial boundaries and opens the door to a genuinely integrative science of human life.

The disciplinary fragmentation of the human sciences is not an accident of intellectual history but a structural consequence of a substance ontology applied to social phenomena. When the founding disciplines of the 19th century carved up the human world into sociology, anthropology, history, linguistics, and so on, they implicitly treated their objects as natural kinds with fixed essences fully describable within a single autonomous discipline. The postmodern critiques that followed — showing these categories were historically contingent, value-laden, and built on exclusions — were largely correct, but the response was purely negative. What was needed, and remains largely undone, is a positive reconstruction built on a process social ontology: if we stop thinking of society, culture, art, and religion as substances and start thinking of them as ongoing dynamic ecology-like interactions that humans and social animals co-produce, the disciplinary boundaries dissolve not into nihilism but into a more accurate picture of how the social world actually works. This structural failure is reinforced by a self-reinforcing institutional loop: as disciplines mature, the sense that big questions are settled pushes scholars toward ever-narrower topics rewarded by job markets and journal gatekeepers, while the jargon each discipline develops makes it progressively harder for adjacent fields working on related problems to recognize their shared concerns. The opportunity hidden inside this crisis is that large numbers of highly trained people produced by this system but not absorbed by it might be redirected toward a genuinely integrative human science — one that sheds artificial 19th-century disciplinary distinctions and trains scholars to move across them not as dilettantes but as second-order theorists capable of cross-disciplinary translation.

## Apprentice

In the 1800s, scholars divided the study of human life into separate fields — sociology, anthropology, history, linguistics, and others — each claiming its own territory. This carving-up rested on an unspoken assumption: that "society," "culture," and "language" were distinct, stable things that could each be fully understood on their own terms. But human life doesn't actually come in such neat packages. These phenomena overlap, feed into each other, and constantly change.

Postmodern thinkers later pointed out that these divisions were arbitrary and historically biased, which was largely right. But the critique stopped at tearing things down without offering a better framework. The deeper move is to stop treating social phenomena as fixed objects and start seeing them as ongoing, ecology-like processes — dynamic interactions that humans and other social animals continuously produce together.

Meanwhile, a self-reinforcing cycle keeps the old system in place: established disciplines reward increasingly narrow specialization, and each field develops its own technical language that makes it harder to talk to neighbors working on closely related problems. Yet this same system produces many highly trained people who can't find traditional academic positions. That surplus represents an untapped opportunity — scholars who could be redirected toward building a genuinely cross-disciplinary human science, one that treats the old boundaries as obstacles to understanding rather than maps of reality.

## Adept

The fragmentation of the human sciences into autonomous disciplines — sociology, anthropology, history, linguistics, and so forth — is typically narrated as a contingent product of institutional history. This argument goes further: the fragmentation is a structural consequence of applying substance ontology to social phenomena. The 19th-century founders implicitly treated their objects of study as natural kinds possessing fixed essences, each fully describable within a single disciplinary frame. This ontological commitment, rarely made explicit, generated the very boundaries that later seemed natural and necessary.

Postmodern and post-structuralist critiques correctly identified these categories as historically contingent, value-laden, and constituted through exclusions. Yet the response remained largely deconstructive — a negative moment without a corresponding positive reconstruction. The proposal here is that a process social ontology supplies what deconstruction could not: if society, culture, religion, and art are reconceived not as substances but as ongoing, ecology-like interactions co-produced by human and non-human social agents, then disciplinary boundaries dissolve not into relativism but into a more empirically adequate picture of social reality.

This structural failure is compounded by an institutional feedback loop. As disciplines mature, foundational questions are treated as settled, pushing scholars toward ever-narrower specializations rewarded by hiring committees and journal gatekeepers. The specialized jargon each field develops further impedes cross-disciplinary recognition of shared problems. Yet the same system overproduces highly trained scholars who cannot be absorbed by it. This surplus workforce represents a latent resource: people who could be retrained not as disciplinary generalists but as second-order theorists capable of systematic cross-disciplinary translation — the core personnel of a genuinely integrative human science.

## Magus

The standard genealogy of disciplinary fragmentation in the human sciences treats it as a contingent outcome of 19th-century institutional politics and intellectual fashion. A more penetrating diagnosis locates its root in the unreflective application of substance ontology to social phenomena. When Durkheim, Tylor, Ranke, and Saussure constituted their respective domains, they tacitly presupposed that society, culture, history, and language were natural kinds — bounded entities with stable essences amenable to exhaustive description within autonomous theoretical frameworks. The disciplinary map thus encoded an ontological commitment that was never itself subjected to disciplinary scrutiny.

The post-structuralist and postmodern interventions — from Foucault's epistemic ruptures to Clifford's writing-culture critique — correctly demonstrated the historical contingency, normative saturation, and constitutive exclusions of these categories. Yet their achievement remained asymmetric: a thoroughgoing negative critique unaccompanied by any positive ontological reconstruction. The decisive move still largely unexecuted is the substitution of a process social ontology for the inherited substance framework. Reconceived as ongoing, ecology-like interactions co-produced across human and non-human social agents, phenomena like "culture," "society," and "religion" lose their apparent self-subsistence, and disciplinary boundaries appear as artifacts of reification rather than joints of nature.

This ontological misrecognition is stabilized by a self-reinforcing institutional loop: disciplinary maturation forecloses foundational inquiry, channeling scholars toward hyper-specialized niches rewarded by credentialing markets and peer-review gatekeeping, while field-specific terminologies progressively occlude shared problem-structures across adjacent domains. Paradoxically, the same system's overproduction of credentialed scholars who cannot be institutionally absorbed constitutes a latent resource — potential second-order theorists capable of systematic cross-disciplinary translation, the very personnel required for the integrative human science that the process-ontological turn makes both possible and necessary.
