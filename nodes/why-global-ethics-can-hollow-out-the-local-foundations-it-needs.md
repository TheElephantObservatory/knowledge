# Why Global Ethics Can Hollow Out the Local Foundations It Needs

The tree that forgot its roots.

---

> Global ethics frameworks that prioritize the most impactful problems worldwide systematically starve the local institutions and relationships that make global action possible in the first place — like a tree abandoning its roots to flower faster.

There is a portfolio problem embedded in global ethics that almost no one in the effective altruism community has fully confronted. When you zoom out to the global level and ask which problems are most important, most neglected, and most tractable, you generate a list — and then, given limited resources, you draw a cutoff. Everything below the cutoff gets nothing. But this global portfolio systematically excludes local problems that are genuinely important at the local scale, and that serve as the foundational infrastructure for everything the global portfolio is trying to achieve. If everyone who goes to university in Austria wants to work on global climate change, who is doing the local transformation? Who is maintaining the schools, the civic institutions, the local knowledge systems, the relational fabric that makes a society capable of contributing to global solutions at all? The global perspective, paradoxically, can hollow out the foundation it depends on. Local structures are not just small versions of global structures — they are the substrate from which global capacity emerges. Neglecting them in favor of global optimization is like a tree that stops investing in its root system because it wants to flower faster. The flowering depends on the roots. Autopoethics argues for a heat-map model of ethical obligation that starts from the center — self, relationships, local community, nation — and extends outward, rather than jumping immediately to the global and working backward. This is not parochialism. It is systems literacy applied to ethics.

## Apprentice

When people try to figure out where to do the most good in the world, they often make a ranked list of global problems — climate change, pandemics, poverty — and focus all their energy on whatever tops that list. Everything below the cutoff gets ignored. But this approach has a hidden flaw: it systematically drains attention and talent away from local problems that may seem small by global standards but are actually essential.

Consider what happens if every ambitious graduate in a country decides to work on global issues. Who maintains the local schools, civic organizations, and community relationships? These aren't minor concerns — they're the foundation that produces the kind of society capable of tackling global challenges at all. A community that neglects its own institutions eventually loses the capacity to contribute to anything.

The Autopoethics framework proposes a different model: ethical obligation works like a heat map, strongest at the center — self, close relationships, local community — and extending outward. This isn't about ignoring the wider world. It's about recognizing that global capacity grows from local roots, and cutting those roots in the name of global optimization is self-defeating.

## Adept

Effective altruism's core methodology — ranking problems by importance, neglectedness, and tractability, then allocating resources from the top down — contains an underexamined portfolio problem. The global optimization framework draws a cutoff line, and everything below it receives zero investment. But this systematically excludes local-scale problems that don't register as globally significant yet serve as the enabling infrastructure for global capacity itself. Local civic institutions, educational systems, relational networks, and knowledge traditions are not merely smaller versions of global structures; they are the generative substrate from which societies derive their ability to act on global challenges.

The paradox is structural: if every capable person in a given society orients toward globally-optimized cause areas, the local transformation work goes unattended. The schools degrade, civic trust erodes, institutional knowledge atrophies. The society that was supposed to be a node of global problem-solving hollows itself out. This is not a hypothetical edge case — it is a predictable consequence of applying single-portfolio logic to a multi-scale system. The analogy is precise: a tree that diverts resources from its root system to accelerate flowering will eventually collapse.

Autopoethics addresses this by proposing a heat-map model of ethical obligation, where moral intensity radiates outward from the center — self-maintenance, intimate relationships, local community, broader polity — rather than beginning at the global scale and working inward. This recentering is not parochial retreat but systems literacy applied to ethics. It recognizes that nested systems require investment at every scale, and that the global depends constitutively on the local, not merely instrumentally.

## Magus

The effective altruist framework's commitment to global cause prioritization embeds an unacknowledged portfolio allocation failure. By ranking problems along axes of scale, neglectedness, and tractability and then imposing a resource cutoff, the methodology generates a systematic exclusion of local-scale concerns — precisely those concerns that constitute the autopoietic infrastructure upon which global-scale capacity depends. This is not a marginal inefficiency but a structural contradiction: the optimization function cannibalizes its own boundary conditions. Local civic institutions, educational systems, relational networks, and embedded knowledge traditions are not reducible to smaller-magnitude instances of global problems. They are ontologically distinct — they are the generative substrate, the conditions of possibility for the very agency that global frameworks presuppose.

The failure is legible through systems-theoretic lenses. In any nested adaptive system, viability at higher scales is constitutively dependent on integrity at lower scales. A society whose most capable members uniformly orient toward globally-prioritized cause areas undergoes a predictable autopoietic degradation: institutional memory thins, civic trust decays, local feedback loops break. The node that was to serve as a contributor to global solutions becomes structurally incapable of sustaining its own reproduction. The arboreal metaphor — a tree defunding its root system to accelerate flowering — captures the recursive dependency with precision.

Autopoethics responds by proposing ethical obligation as a scalar heat map: moral intensity is highest at the center of the agent's relational field — self-constitution, intimate bonds, local community, polity — and attenuates outward. This is not communitarian parochialism but a rigorous application of multi-scale systems logic to normative theory. It insists that the global does not merely benefit from the local instrumentally but depends on it constitutively, and that any ethics blind to this dependency is not optimizing — it is self-undermining.
