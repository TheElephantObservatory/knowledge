# Bonnitta Roy’s Process-Based Critique of Complexity

No outside, only motion with respect to motion.

---

> Bonnitta Roy argues that complexity science secretly treats living processes as frozen objects — and that fusing it with process philosophy dissolves this blind spot, placing the observer inside the system rather than above it.

Bonnitta Roy's intellectual project sits at the intersection of two fields — process philosophy and complexity science — that have, as she discovered when searching Google Scholar, almost never been integrated. Her core critique of complexity science is the problem of reification: the subtle, often unconscious tendency to treat dynamic processes as if they were stable objects with clear boundaries, observable from a privileged position outside the system. Process philosophy, in the tradition of Whitehead, Hartshorne, and Griffin, challenges this directly. The move is analogous to what Einstein did with gravity: rather than treating it as a force — a third term you could go look for — Einstein reframed it as the relative motion of different inertial frames. No third term, no ether, just motion with respect to motion. Process philosophy makes the same move across the board: everything is in motion with respect to everything else, nothing is de-animated, and there is no privileged vantage point outside the system. Roy's synthesis of this process ontology with complexity science is what she calls 'insight practice' — a metacognitive discipline that turns the lens of inquiry back on itself, asking: what foundational assumptions define this circle of thinking, what does drawing this boundary include and exclude, and what constraints are built into the very structure of the inquiry? This is not mere academic reflexivity. Roy describes it as a double movement between insight and action — where theoretical insight opens new questions, and effective action in the world simplifies the search space. By sitting at the boundary between these two fields and hybridizing them, she aims not to produce new content within an existing framework, but to expand the framework itself — dissolving hidden reifications, unbinding the complexity space, and opening a mode of inquiry where the observer is always already inside the system, participating in its construction, never standing outside it.

## Apprentice

Most scientific thinking assumes you can stand outside a system and observe it neutrally — like watching fish in a tank. Bonnitta Roy argues this assumption quietly distorts complexity science, which studies living, dynamic systems but often ends up treating them as if they were fixed objects with clear edges. She calls this 'reification' — the habit of freezing what is actually flowing.

To correct this, Roy draws on process philosophy, a tradition holding that everything is always in motion relative to everything else. There is no still point outside the system from which to observe it. The observer is always already inside, participating in what they are studying. Roy's analogy is Einstein's reframing of gravity — not a mysterious force you go looking for, but simply the relative motion of different frames. No extra ingredient needed, just a shift in how you see.

The practical outcome is what Roy calls 'insight practice' — a discipline of turning inquiry back on itself. What assumptions define this way of thinking? What does drawing this boundary include and leave out? The goal is not to produce new answers within an existing framework, but to expand the framework itself, loosening hidden assumptions and opening up new possibilities for thought and action.

## Adept

Bonnitta Roy's project identifies a structural tension within complexity science: despite its commitment to dynamic, emergent phenomena, the field routinely reifies its subject matter — treating processes as bounded objects amenable to third-person observation from a position exterior to the system. This is not mere methodological sloppiness but an ontological default, inherited from the Cartesian architecture underlying most scientific inquiry. Roy's intervention is to hybridize complexity science with process philosophy in the tradition of Whitehead, Hartshorne, and Griffin — a lineage that treats becoming, not being, as the primary ontological category.

The conceptual move parallels Einstein's dissolution of gravitational force as a third-term entity: rather than positing something that acts between masses, general relativity redescribes gravity as the curvature of spacetime — motion with respect to motion, with no privileged inertial frame. Process philosophy generalizes this gesture: nothing is de-animated, no vantage point stands outside the relational field, and every act of observation is itself an event within the system being observed.

Roy's synthesis produces what she terms 'insight practice' — a metacognitive discipline that interrogates the boundary conditions of any given inquiry. What does the circle of thinking include and exclude? What constraints are structural rather than empirical? The practice operates as a double movement: theoretical insight opens the complexity space by dissolving hidden reifications, while effective action in the world simplifies and focuses the search space. The aim is not to generate new content within an existing paradigm but to perform genuine framework expansion — unbinding the ontological assumptions that constrain what complexity science can see.

## Magus

Roy's intellectual project constitutes a cross-domain hybridization that, as she notes, has found almost no precedent in the literature: the systematic integration of process ontology with the formal and methodological apparatus of complexity science. The diagnostic claim is that complexity science, despite its nominally anti-reductionist commitments, remains structurally captive to what Whitehead called the 'fallacy of misplaced concreteness' — the sedimented tendency to abstract dynamic relational processes into discrete, bounded, observer-independent objects. This reificatory move is not incidental but constitutive: it underwrites the possibility of the detached, third-person epistemic stance that complexity science, like most post-Cartesian inquiry, presupposes.

The corrective Roy proposes is not additive but ontological. Drawing on the Whiteheadian tradition — extended through Hartshorne and Griffin — she advances a thoroughgoing process metaphysics in which the actual occasion of experience, not the enduring substance, is the irreducible unit of reality. The Einsteinian analogy is precise: just as general relativity eliminates the gravitational field as a third-term mediator by recasting gravitation as geodesic motion within a curved manifold — frame-relative, with no absolute rest — process philosophy eliminates the reified object by recasting all entities as event-clusters within a relational field. No substrate, no exterior vantage, no de-animated residue.

The epistemological corollary is Roy's 'insight practice': a second-order, metacognitive discipline that recursively interrogates the boundary-drawing operations constitutive of any inquiry — what the framing includes, excludes, and forecloses. Crucially, this is not academic reflexivity in the weak sense but a bidirectional dialectic between theoretical insight and situated action, where each pole reshapes the other's possibility space. The ambition is paradigm expansion rather than normal science: to unbind the complexity space by dissolving the hidden ontological commitments that currently constrain what the field can coherently ask.
