# A Four-Layer Map of the Human Person for Clinical Practice

The narrator who cannot stop explaining the wound

---

> Gregg Henriques's unified framework gives clinicians a layered map of the human person — body, animal nervous system, felt experiential world, and self-narrating mind — so therapy becomes tracking where these layers conflict rather than reducing distress to a single theoretical lens.

One of the most practically consequential implications of the unified framework Henriques develops concerns what a clinician is actually doing when they sit with a person in psychological distress. Most therapeutic traditions operate from within a single theoretical paradigm — psychodynamic, cognitive-behavioral, somatic, existential — each of which illuminates one layer of the person while leaving others in shadow. The unified theory offers instead a generalizable map of the stack of information processing that constitutes a human being, which a clinician can use as a diagnostic orientation before selecting interventions. At the base is the living organism body — its metabolic history, its organ systems, its trajectory of energy and information processing. Nested within that is the animal nervous system, which Henriques describes as 'the skin turned inward' — a structure that maps the organism-environment relationship and builds up procedural behavioral path patterns and potentials over a lifetime. Above that is the global neuronal workspace: the integrated field of sensory, interoceptive, and proprioceptive information that generates the perceptual-motivational-emotional inner life of the animal, the felt sense of pleasure and pain, the mammalian attachment system, the primate relational value system. And above that is the egoic narrator — the self-conscious justificatory mind that tells the story of what is happening and feeds back onto the felt system, sometimes helpfully and sometimes in what Henriques calls neurotic loops, where the justificatory mind's reactions to felt experience create self-reinforcing cycles of maladaptation. What this gives a clinician is not a single theory to apply but a set of domains to listen across simultaneously: the body's history, the animal's core drives, the mammal's relational felt sense, and the narrator's story about all of it. The therapeutic task becomes tracking the interrelations between these layers — where they are in conflict, where the narrator is distorting or suppressing the felt system, where growth potential is being blocked — rather than reducing the person to any single level of analysis.

## Apprentice

Most therapists are trained in one main approach — maybe they focus on thoughts, or on the body, or on childhood relationships, or on finding meaning. Each approach captures something real, but each also has blind spots. Gregg Henriques argues that a unified theory of psychology can give clinicians a fuller picture by mapping the different layers that make up a human being.

At the bottom is the physical body with its biological history. Nested inside that is the nervous system, which over a lifetime builds up deep habits and behavioral patterns. Then there is the felt inner world — emotions, sensations, attachment needs, the raw experience of pleasure and pain. And on top of all that sits the storytelling mind, the part of us that narrates and explains what is happening to us.

The practical payoff for therapy is significant. Instead of picking one lens and hoping it fits, a clinician can listen across all four layers at once. The real diagnostic question becomes: where are these layers in conflict? Is the storytelling mind suppressing genuine feelings? Are deep bodily patterns overriding conscious intentions? Is a person's narrative about themselves creating a loop that makes their suffering worse? Therapy, in this view, is the art of tracking these inter-layer tensions and helping a person bring them into better alignment.

## Adept

Henriques's unified approach to psychology reframes the clinical encounter by offering a multi-layered ontology of the person that cuts across traditional therapeutic paradigms. Rather than defaulting to a psychodynamic, cognitive-behavioral, somatic, or existential lens — each of which privileges one stratum of human functioning — the clinician gains a generalizable diagnostic map. This map identifies four nested domains of information processing: the biological organism and its metabolic-energetic history; the animal nervous system, understood as an internalized model of the organism-environment relationship that accumulates procedural behavioral patterns; the global neuronal workspace that generates the perceptual-motivational-emotional field of felt experience, including mammalian attachment and primate relational valuation; and the egoic narrator, the self-conscious justificatory system that constructs and maintains a story about all of the above.

The clinical power of this architecture lies in its relational dynamics. Henriques identifies what he calls neurotic loops — self-reinforcing cycles in which the justificatory mind's reactions to felt experience amplify distress rather than resolve it. The narrator may distort, suppress, or over-explain the signals arising from the felt system, creating internal conflicts that manifest as psychological symptoms.

Therapy, on this account, is not the application of a single theoretical intervention but the disciplined practice of listening across all four layers simultaneously. The clinician tracks where the layers are misaligned — where the body's history contradicts the narrator's story, where core animal drives are being overridden by justificatory constructions, where relational felt sense is being denied expression. Growth becomes possible when these inter-layer conflicts are identified and the person is helped to move toward greater coherence across the full stack.

## Magus

Henriques's unified metatheoretical framework yields its most consequential applied dividend in the clinical setting, where it reconfigures the therapist's fundamental orientation from paradigm-bound intervention to multi-strata diagnostic listening. The architecture posits four nested levels of information processing constituting the person: the bioenergetic organism with its metabolic trajectory; the animal nervous system — characterized evocatively as 'the skin turned inward' — which maps organism-environment contingencies into procedural behavioral path patterns across developmental time; the global neuronal workspace that integrates sensory, interoceptive, and proprioceptive streams into the perceptual-motivational-emotional field of phenomenal experience, encompassing mammalian attachment dynamics and primate relational value computation; and the egoic justificatory narrator, the linguistically mediated self-conscious system that reflexively models and evaluates the outputs of the lower strata.

The critical clinical insight concerns the recursive feedback topology between the justificatory system and the felt experiential field. Henriques's concept of neurotic loops identifies a specific failure mode: the narrator's second-order reactions to primary felt states — shame about anxiety, rationalization of relational injury, intellectualized dismissal of somatic signals — generate self-reinforcing maladaptive cycles that entrench rather than resolve distress. These loops are not reducible to cognitive distortions, repressed drives, or somatic dysregulation alone; they are inter-strata phenomena requiring inter-strata recognition.

The therapeutic task thus becomes a form of layered auscultation: tracking the body's energetic and developmental history, the animal's consolidated behavioral repertoire, the mammalian-primate felt relational world, and the narrator's justificatory constructions simultaneously, while attending specifically to the points of conflict, suppression, and distortion between them. This reframes clinical competence not as mastery of a single modality but as the capacity to hold the full stack in view and intervene at the level — or across the levels — where coherence has broken down and growth potential is obstructed.
