# Why 'Many Islams' Solves One Problem and Creates Another

The map that dissolved the territory

---

> The categories scholars use to study Islam — 'religion,' 'culture,' 'orthodoxy' — smuggle in hidden assumptions that distort what they claim to describe. A metamodern approach treats these as historically constituted 'process social kinds,' enabling rigorous analysis that neither flattens everything into relativism nor retreats into false neutrality.

One of the central methodological crises in the study of Islam — and arguably in the humanities more broadly — is the failure of inherited analytical categories like 'religion,' 'culture,' and 'orthodoxy' to adequately describe the phenomena they purport to explain. These categories carry hidden normative commitments: when 'religion' is used as the master frame for Islam, it tends to privilege textualist and legalist expressions as the 'real' Islam, while marginalizing mysticism, philosophy, and popular practice as derivative or aberrant. The postmodern response to this problem has often been pluralization — there is no one Islam, only many Islams — but while this move has genuine value in countering reductive narratives, it carries a significant cost: it forecloses the ability to make substantive analytical distinctions. If everything a Muslim does is equally Islamic, the framework cannot engage honestly with phenomena like extremism or fundamentalism. Ahmed's response, and the metamodern paradigm more broadly, offers a different path: rehabilitating Islam and 'the Islamic' as their own analytically rigorous categories grounded in the tradition's own self-understanding, while recognizing that all such categories — religion, culture, orthodoxy, Islam itself — are process social kinds: historically constituted, anchored by specific social and discursive processes, and crosscutting in complex ways. Within Ahmed's schema, ISIS and Sufi poets are both doing Islamic meaning-making — but the former's hermeneutical engagement is described as 'very cloistered, very constrained, very deficient' compared to the broader gamut of the tradition. This is not a confessional judgment but an analytical one, grounded in the internal logic of the tradition's own hermeneutical richness. The metamodern move is to be explicit about value commitments while remaining methodologically rigorous — neither hiding behind false neutrality nor collapsing into relativism, but navigating the space between with intellectual honesty. The result is a methodology that can take Islamic phenomena seriously on their own terms, trace the genealogies of their anchoring processes, and engage with the full complexity of the tradition without either reducing everything to cultural diversity or retreating into confessional normativity.

## Apprentice

When scholars study Islam, they typically reach for familiar categories like 'religion,' 'culture,' or 'orthodoxy.' But these labels carry hidden baggage. Calling Islam a 'religion,' for instance, tends to make legalistic and text-focused expressions look like the 'real' Islam, while pushing mysticism, philosophy, and everyday practice to the margins. The categories shape what we see before we even start looking.

One popular response has been to say there's no single Islam — only many 'Islams.' This helps counter oversimplification, but it creates a new problem: if every expression is equally valid, it becomes impossible to say anything meaningful about differences — including the difference between a Sufi poet and a violent extremist. Everything just becomes 'cultural diversity.'

Shahab Ahmed's work offers a way out of this trap. Rather than abandoning the idea of Islam as a coherent subject of study, Ahmed grounds analysis in the tradition's own internal logic and self-understanding. Within this framework, both ISIS and Sufi poetry involve Islamic meaning-making, but the former's engagement with the tradition can be described as narrow and impoverished compared to its full richness. This isn't a religious judgment — it's an analytical one. The broader metamodern move here is to be honest about value commitments while staying methodologically rigorous, navigating between fake neutrality and anything-goes relativism.

## Magus

The inherited categorical apparatus of Islamic studies — 'religion,' 'culture,' 'orthodoxy' — constitutes not merely an imprecise toolkit but an active epistemic distortion. Each term carries sedimented normative commitments: 'religion' as master frame privileges textualist-legalist expressions as paradigmatically Islamic, rendering Sufism, falsafa, and popular practice as epiphenomenal or deviant. The crisis is not simply one of coverage but of constitution — the categories produce the very hierarchies they purport to discover.

Postmodern pluralization ('many Islams') performs necessary deconstructive work against reductive essentialism but purchases its gains at the cost of analytical traction. Radical pluralism cannot distinguish between hermeneutical engagements of vastly different scope and depth without smuggling in the very normative commitments it claims to have dissolved. Shahab Ahmed's intervention cuts through this impasse by grounding 'the Islamic' as an analytical category within the tradition's own self-understanding — its hermeneutical engagement with the Pre-Text, Text, and Con-Text of Revelation. Within this schema, both ISIS and Ibn ʿArabī participate in Islamic meaning-making, but the former's engagement is characterizable as hermeneutically cloistered and deficient relative to the tradition's own demonstrated gamut. This is an analytical judgment internal to the category's logic, not a confessional imposition.

The deeper metamodern commitment here reconceives all such categories — religion, culture, orthodoxy, Islam — as process social kinds: historically constituted through specific anchoring processes, genealogically traceable, and irreducibly crosscutting. This ontological reframing enables a methodology that is simultaneously value-explicit and epistemically rigorous, navigating the space between positivist false neutrality and postmodern relativism. The payoff is a framework that can take Islamic phenomena seriously as Islamic, engage their internal complexity without reductive flattening, and maintain the capacity for substantive critical distinction.
