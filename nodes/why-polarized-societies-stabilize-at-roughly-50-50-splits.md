# Why Polarized Societies Stabilize at Roughly 50-50 Splits

The blanket must remain porous.

---

> When self-modeling agents populate a shared world, assuming others think like you gives you a free theory of mind — but this drives groups toward echo chambers. Simulations of active inference reveal that the only dynamically stable escape from total convergence is a roughly 50-50 split between in-group and out-group.

When the observed is herself an observer, something qualitatively new emerges. If I am in the business of self-evidencing and my world is populated by things like me, the most efficient strategy is to assume you are like me — sharing my generative model, my common ground, my narrative — giving me a ready-made theory of mind for free. At the population level, everyone doing this simultaneously converges on shared world models: shared ideologies, conspiracy theories, theologies, languages. You can draw a Markoff blanket around an epistemic community. But if that blanket becomes impervious, you have a closed system — an echo chamber — and everything converges to equilibrium. Nothing changes. The blanket must remain porous. When you simulate active inference in populations, one striking result keeps emerging: the only stable non-equilibrium solution is a roughly 50-50 split between in-group and out-group. If one group gets too small, it gets absorbed. The 50-50 split is the only configuration where you get both the reassurance of in-group belonging and the epistemic allure of the out-group. Brexit. Trump versus Biden. Scottish independence. It keeps appearing because it is the only dynamically stable solution.

## Apprentice

When you meet someone new, the easiest shortcut is to assume they think roughly the way you do. This works surprisingly well — it gives you an instant ability to predict their behavior without having to learn everything about them from scratch. But when everyone in a community does this at once, something powerful happens: people rapidly converge on shared beliefs, stories, and ways of seeing the world. The group starts to think alike.

The problem is that a perfectly sealed community — one where no outside ideas get in — becomes an echo chamber. Nothing new happens. Beliefs harden. The system reaches a kind of intellectual dead end. So the boundary around a community needs to stay at least somewhat open to outside influence.

Here is where it gets striking. When researchers simulate populations of agents who are all trying to make sense of the world in this way, one pattern keeps showing up: the only arrangement that remains stable over time without collapsing into uniformity is a roughly even split between two groups. Not 80-20, not 70-30 — approximately 50-50. If one side shrinks too much, it gets absorbed. This may help explain why so many political and cultural divides settle near an even split — Brexit, closely contested elections, independence referendums. The pattern is not a coincidence of politics. It appears to be the only configuration where groups maintain both internal cohesion and enough tension with an outside perspective to keep evolving.

## Adept

The insight begins with a recursive twist in the Free Energy Principle: when the thing being modeled is itself a modeler, qualitatively new dynamics emerge. If each agent minimizes surprise through self-evidencing, and the environment is populated by similar agents, the most efficient strategy is to assume others share your generative model. This grants a theory of mind essentially for free — no costly inference required. At the population level, this mutual assumption drives rapid convergence toward shared world models: common languages, ideologies, theologies, even conspiracy theories. One can draw a Markov blanket around the resulting epistemic community.

But here lies the tension. If that blanket becomes impervious — if the community becomes informationally closed — the system reaches thermodynamic-style equilibrium. Beliefs ossify. Surprise is minimized to zero, and adaptive capacity vanishes. The blanket must remain porous to sustain non-equilibrium dynamics, meaning the community must remain open to information that challenges its shared model.

Simulations of multi-agent active inference reveal a remarkable attractor in this landscape: the only dynamically stable non-equilibrium configuration is an approximately 50-50 split between in-group and out-group. Any significant asymmetry causes the smaller group to be absorbed, collapsing the system back to consensus equilibrium. The even split uniquely sustains both the free-energy-reducing comfort of in-group model-sharing and the epistemic tension generated by an out-group whose models differ. This formal result maps strikingly onto empirical political phenomena — Brexit, Trump-Biden polarization, Scottish independence — suggesting these near-even divisions are not accidents of campaign strategy but expressions of a deep dynamical constraint on belief-forming populations.

## Magus

The recursion at the heart of this argument is deceptively simple yet generatively profound: when the observed system is itself an observer — when the entities populating an agent's umwelt are themselves self-evidencing — the inferential landscape undergoes a phase transition. Under the Free Energy Principle, each agent's imperative to minimize variational free energy is most efficiently discharged by projecting its own generative model onto conspecifics, thereby acquiring a theory of mind at negligible computational cost. This is not mere heuristic laziness; it is the variational optimum when the prior probability that others share your model structure is high. The consequence at the ensemble level is the spontaneous formation of epistemic communities whose shared generative models — ideologies, grammars, cosmologies — can be formally characterized by a Markov blanket drawn around the collective.

The critical thermodynamic constraint follows immediately. A Markov blanket that becomes statistically impermeable renders the enclosed system ergodic: all internal states converge to equilibrium, entropy production ceases, and the community loses its capacity for adaptive model revision. Sustained non-equilibrium steady states — the hallmark of living, thinking systems — require blanket porosity, a regulated exchange of prediction errors across the community boundary.

Multi-agent active inference simulations expose the deep structure of this requirement. The only dynamically stable non-equilibrium attractor is an approximately equiprobable partition: a 50-50 in-group/out-group split. Asymmetric partitions are unstable — minority factions fall below a critical mass for self-evidencing and are absorbed. The symmetric bifurcation alone sustains the dual imperative: free-energy reduction through in-group model-sharing and complexity reduction through out-group-generated prediction error. That Brexit, hyper-partisan elections, and secession referenda consistently converge near parity is, on this account, not a sociological curiosity but the signature of a variational fixed point governing epistemic population dynamics.
