# Information as the Mechanism of Physical Existence

To be is to have meaning.

---

> Information only becomes meaningful when it helps something survive. A new framework argues that meaning isn't a human invention — it's a physical necessity, and existence itself is proof that meaning works.

Shannon's information theory is fundamentally incomplete because it deals only with the accurate transmission of information — its quantity and fidelity — while remaining entirely silent on whether that information actually matters to anything. Complexity theorists Artemy Kolchinsky and David Wolpert have filled this gap by distinguishing between syntactic information — Shannon's statistical correlations between systems — and semantic information: the syntactic information a physical system has about its environment which is causally necessary for the system to maintain its own existence. Information becomes meaningful to an entity to the degree that it helps that entity resist entropic dissolution. All physical systems face entropy; to persist, a system must extract free energy from its environment, and to do that effectively, it needs accurate and relevant information about that environment. Kolchinsky and Wolpert even identify a connection between semantic efficiency — attending to the right information — and thermodynamic efficiency. This framework is fully generalizable: it applies not just to living organisms but to any physical system whatsoever. Crucially, this yields a profound philosophical insight: if meaning is defined as information causally necessary for an entity's continued existence, then the very fact that anything exists at all entails that meaning exists for it. To be is to have meaning. The persistence of any entity through time is itself evidence that meaningful information has been successfully processed. This generates a natural evolutionary logic: entities more effective at attending to meaningful information will be more viable and evolutionarily successful, creating a feedback cycle in which complexity demands more energy and information-processing capacity, which drives further complexification. What emerges is a picture of cosmic evolution as a single great learning process, in which the accumulation of meaningful knowledge is not incidental but is the very driving logic of how things persist, diversify, and grow in complexity across all levels of emergence.

## Apprentice

Claude Shannon's theory of information, developed in the mid-twentieth century, is extraordinarily powerful — it explains how to transmit messages reliably, compress data, and measure the capacity of communication channels. But it has a blind spot: it says nothing about whether any of that information actually matters. A string of random noise and a life-saving warning carry the same 'information' in Shannon's framework if they have the same statistical structure. Complexity theorists Artemy Kolchinsky and David Wolpert have proposed a way to close this gap. Their key idea is that information becomes meaningful to a physical system when it is causally necessary for that system to keep existing. Everything in the universe faces entropy — the tendency of things to fall apart. To persist, a system must extract useful energy from its surroundings, and to do that well, it needs accurate, relevant information about those surroundings. Meaning, in this view, is not a luxury added on top of existence — it is a condition of existence.

This leads to a striking philosophical conclusion: if anything exists at all, then meaning exists for it. To persist through time is already to have successfully processed meaningful information. And because systems that attend to the right information survive better, evolution — at every level, from molecules to minds — becomes a vast, ongoing process of learning what matters.

## Adept

Shannon's information theory is a theory of syntactic information: it quantifies statistical correlations between a source and a receiver while remaining entirely agnostic about semantic content. This is a deliberate and productive abstraction, but it leaves the question of meaning formally unaddressed. Kolchinsky and Wolpert's framework of semantic information fills this lacuna by defining meaning in thermodynamic terms. Semantic information is the subset of syntactic information a physical system possesses about its environment that is causally necessary for the system to maintain its own far-from-equilibrium state — that is, to resist entropic dissolution. Crucially, they demonstrate a formal connection between semantic efficiency and thermodynamic efficiency: a system that attends to the right information about its environment will extract free energy more effectively, and therefore persist longer.

The framework is deliberately substrate-neutral. It applies not only to biological organisms but to any physical system capable of maintaining a bounded, self-sustaining organization against entropic pressure. This generates a profound ontological corollary: if meaning is defined as information causally necessary for continued existence, then the bare fact of a system's persistence is sufficient evidence that meaningful information has been successfully processed. To be is to have meaning. From this foundation, an evolutionary logic follows naturally — systems more adept at identifying and acting on semantically relevant information are more thermodynamically viable, driving a feedback cycle in which complexity, energy demand, and information-processing capacity co-evolve. Cosmic evolution, on this account, is not merely a physical process but an epistemic one: the progressive accumulation of meaningful knowledge at every level of emergent organization.

## Magus

The Kolchinsky-Wolpert framework intervenes at the precise point where Shannon's formalism reaches its constitutive limit. Shannon information is defined over probability distributions and measures the reduction of uncertainty at a receiver; it is, by construction, indifferent to the referential or causal significance of the correlated variables. Kolchinsky and Wolpert's semantic information is defined relationally and dynamically: it is the syntactic mutual information between a system's internal states and its environment that is causally efficacious in maintaining the system's viability — operationalized as the degree to which that information reduces the system's probability of transitioning to a 'dead' or dissolved macrostate. This is not a mentalistic or intentional notion of meaning; it is fully physicalist, grounded in the non-equilibrium thermodynamics of open systems. The authors further establish that semantic efficiency — the degree to which a system's information-processing is concentrated on causally relevant environmental variables — is formally connected to thermodynamic efficiency, linking epistemics to the Landauer-Bennett framework of the physics of computation.

The ontological implications are considerable. If semantic information is defined as information causally necessary for a system's continued existence, then persistence is itself the criterion of meaningfulness: any system that maintains its organization through time has, by definition, successfully processed meaningful information. The Cartesian cogito finds a thermodynamic analogue — esse est habere sensum. This yields a rigorous, non-anthropocentric account of meaning that dissolves the traditional boundary between the semantic and the physical. The evolutionary corollary is equally significant: selection pressure operates precisely on semantic efficiency, generating a recursive dynamic in which increasing organizational complexity demands greater free-energy throughput and finer-grained environmental modeling, driving the co-evolution of complexity and cognition. The universe's trajectory toward greater differentiation and emergent organization is, on this account, not merely a consequence of physical law but of the logic of meaning itself.
