# Why Secular Culture Has Not Yet Grieved the Death of God

We are still fighting. We have not yet cried.

---

> Neither militant atheism nor fundamentalism is actually grieving the death of God — both are fight responses that foreclose genuine mourning. Until culture stops fighting long enough to collectively cry, no new attachment to positive value can form.

We are not grieving the death of God because we have not yet stopped fighting. The hermeneutics of suspicion — the pervasive cultural reflex that appearances are deceptive, that the moment of truth is always the revelation of how we have been deceived — is not the mature philosophical response to the collapse of theistic certainty. It is the sympathetic nervous system on overdrive. Both militant atheism and fundamentalist nostalgia share a single hidden presupposition: I will never be disappointed again. Fundamentalism says the loss never happened. Militant atheism says the loss proves the whole enterprise was worthless — and so offers an exit from genuine mourning by declaring the positive valence was always already a manipulation, a projection, a power structure, requiring only critique rather than grief. But this exit forecloses the very process by which positive value could be disentangled from its particular historical form and made available for a new attachment. Neither position is grieving. Both are the activated state — the retrieval behavior of an amygdala that has not yet received the signal that what is lost is truly irretrievable. Crying, physiologically, is a parasympathetic rebound: you push the sympathetic system to its limit, and then you release, and the parasympathetic floods in. The cultural analog requires something analogous — a moment of genuine acceptance that something is gone, not a strategic repositioning, not a new ideology, but a stopping of the fight long enough for the Gelassenheit, the letting-be, to begin. The question we have not yet answered is what that looks like at the level of cultural cognitive grammar — what it would mean to collectively stop fighting long enough to cry.

## Apprentice

When a society loses its foundational beliefs, you might expect a period of mourning — a time to feel the weight of what's gone and eventually find new sources of meaning. But that's not what happened after the decline of religious certainty. Instead, two opposing camps dug in. One insists nothing was really lost. The other insists nothing of value was ever there to begin with. Both positions, despite looking like opposites, share a hidden motive: avoiding the pain of genuine loss.

This insight draws on a simple observation about how crying actually works in the body. You don't cry while you're still in fight-or-flight mode. Tears come only after the struggle stops — when your nervous system finally accepts that the threat or the loss is real and irreversible. The release follows the surrender. The same pattern applies culturally: as long as we're locked in combat — debunking or defending — we never reach the point where we can actually feel what's gone and begin to let something new take its place.

The real question, then, isn't which side is right. It's whether an entire culture can stop fighting long enough to grieve — to accept that something genuinely valuable existed, that it is genuinely gone in its old form, and that only by sitting with that loss can its deeper worth be carried forward into something new.

## Adept

The hermeneutics of suspicion — the deeply ingrained cultural habit of treating every appearance as a mask for hidden power, projection, or manipulation — is typically presented as the intellectually mature response to the collapse of theistic metaphysics. This analysis reframes it as something far less composed: a sympathetic nervous system activation, a fight response masquerading as philosophical sophistication. Militant atheism and religious fundamentalism, despite their apparent opposition, share a single structural function — they both prevent genuine mourning. Fundamentalism denies the loss occurred. Militant atheism denies the lost object ever had real value. Both are retrieval behaviors: the cognitive equivalent of an organism that has not yet received the signal that what is gone is truly irretrievable.

The physiological model of crying is deployed here as more than metaphor. Tears are a parasympathetic rebound — they arrive only when the sympathetic activation reaches its limit and the organism finally stops fighting. The cultural analog would require something similar: a collective moment of Gelassenheit, of letting-be, in which the fight to either restore or demolish the old framework simply ceases. Only in that cessation can the positive valence once carried by theistic structures be disentangled from its historical form and made available for new attachment.

What remains genuinely unresolved is what this would look like at the level of cultural cognitive grammar — the deep structures that organize collective meaning-making. The challenge is not ideological but processual: not which new framework to adopt, but how to create the conditions under which an entire culture could stop its activated defense long enough to undergo the parasympathetic rebound that genuine grief requires.

## Magus

The hermeneutics of suspicion, read through this lens, is not the epistemological achievement it presents itself as but rather the cultural homolog of sympathetic hyperactivation — a system locked in retrieval behavior, scanning ceaselessly for the lost object by way of its negative image. Ricoeur's triad of Marx, Nietzsche, and Freud inaugurated not a post-theistic maturity but a chronic fight-state in which the revelation of deception substitutes for the metabolization of loss. The critical insight is that both militant atheism and fundamentalist restoration share a single affective presupposition — the foreclosure of disappointment — and thereby occupy identical positions within the grief process: the activated, pre-parasympathetic phase in which release has not yet occurred because the organism has not yet accepted the irretrievability of the object.

The physiological specificity matters. Lacrimation is parasympathetic rebound: the vagal surge that follows sympathetic exhaustion. It cannot be willed or strategized; it arrives precisely when the fight stops. Heidegger's Gelassenheit is invoked here not as quietism but as the structural prerequisite for what attachment theory would call reorganization — the phase in which libidinal investment, no longer bound to the lost object or to the defense against its loss, becomes available for new cathexis. The hermeneutics of suspicion, by declaring the positive valence always already illusory, forecloses exactly this reorganization by eliminating the object's value retroactively.

The unresolved problem is grammatical in the deepest sense: what would it mean for the cognitive grammar of an entire culture — its default inferential patterns, its presuppositional architecture — to undergo parasympathetic rebound? This is not a question about new beliefs but about the processual conditions under which collective meaning-making could pass through genuine deactivation rather than perpetual sympathetic oscillation between nostalgic retrieval and suspicious demolition.
