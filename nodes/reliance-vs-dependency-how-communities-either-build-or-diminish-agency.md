# Reliance vs. Dependency: How Communities Either Build or Diminish Agency

The sangha that teaches you to leave

---

> Dependency diminishes agency; reliance enhances it. The test of any genuine community of practice is whether it builds people's capacity to flourish beyond its walls — not whether it makes itself indispensable.

There is a meaningful distinction between dependency and reliance, and collapsing the two does real harm to communities of practice. A cult increases dependency — it makes people more reliant on the group and less capable outside of it, diminishing their agency and selfhood. A school, a church, a temple, a dojo — at their best — does the opposite: it enhances agency, selfhood, and personhood in the world beyond the place of practice. This is the razor's edge that any serious community of practice must walk: ensuring people do not become dependent in the pejorative sense, while also making clear that they can genuinely rely on one another. These are not the same thing. There is a form of reliance that is not dependency but mutual belonging — the recognition that we are inherently porous, that we participate in each other, and that this participation, when it enhances rather than diminishes agency, is not a problem to be solved but a feature of what it means to be human. The Buddha, the Dharma, the Sangha: you always need a sangha. The question is whether the sangha is cultivating your capacity to live well in the world or substituting for it. What a genuine community of practice aspires to is something like what a church or temple or mosque could do at its best — not to make itself indispensable, but to help people form the kinds of bonds and practices that can sustain them across a lifetime, including in contexts far beyond the original container. Planned obsolescence, rightly understood, is not abandonment — it is the goal of building in people the muscles and skills such that they no longer need the scaffolding.

## Apprentice

There is an important difference between depending on a group and being able to rely on one. A cult makes people weaker outside of it — more confused, more isolated, less capable of navigating the world on their own. A good school, church, or dojo does the opposite: it gives people skills, relationships, and inner resources they carry with them everywhere they go.

The tricky part is that genuine communities do ask people to show up, to trust, and to lean on each other. That kind of reliance is not the same as dependency. Reliance means recognizing that humans are built to participate in each other's lives — that we grow stronger through real bonds, not weaker. The question is always whether a community is cultivating your ability to live well in the wider world, or quietly replacing it.

The highest aspiration of any serious community of practice is a kind of planned obsolescence — not abandonment, but the deliberate goal of building in people the strength and skill to stand without the scaffolding. The community succeeds not when people cannot leave, but when they carry its gifts into every other part of their lives.

## Adept

A critical distinction separates dependency from reliance, and conflating the two causes genuine harm. Dependency, in the pejorative sense, diminishes agency — it renders a person less capable outside the group, more enmeshed in structures that substitute for selfhood rather than strengthening it. Reliance, by contrast, names something fundamentally different: a form of mutual belonging rooted in the recognition that human beings are inherently porous, that we participate in one another, and that this participation — when it enhances rather than erodes agency — is constitutive of personhood rather than a threat to it.

This distinction defines the razor's edge that any serious community of practice must walk. The Buddha, the Dharma, the Sangha: the sangha is always necessary. But the decisive question is whether the sangha cultivates one's capacity to live well in the world or substitutes for it. A cult collapses reliance into dependency, making the container indispensable. A genuine community of practice — a dojo, a temple, a school at its best — does the opposite, forming bonds and practices that sustain people across a lifetime, including in contexts far beyond the original container.

Planned obsolescence, rightly understood, emerges as the proper telos of such communities. It is not abandonment but the deliberate cultivation of capacities — muscles, skills, habits of attention — such that the scaffolding can eventually be released. The community succeeds precisely to the degree that its members can carry its gifts into the wider world without needing to remain inside it. The goal is not self-sufficiency as isolation, but agency enriched by belonging.

## Magus

The conflation of dependency and reliance represents a conceptual collapse with serious consequences for communities of practice. Dependency, understood pejoratively, names a relation in which participation in a collective structure progressively diminishes the participant's agential capacity and selfhood — the hallmark of cultic formation. Reliance, however, designates a structurally distinct relation: one grounded in the ontological porosity of persons, in the recognition that subjectivity is always already participatory. Drawing on the Buddhist tripartite refuge — Buddha, Dharma, Sangha — the insight locates the sangha as irreducible. The question is never whether community is necessary, but whether a given community's formative practices are oriented toward the enhancement or the attenuation of its members' capacity to inhabit the world beyond its boundaries.

This framing rejects both the atomistic individualism that treats all reliance as suspect dependency and the communitarian absorption that cannot distinguish belonging from enmeshment. The razor's edge is precisely the space between these failures: a community that makes genuine mutual reliance possible while refusing to render itself indispensable. What is at stake is a particular understanding of human formation — that persons are constituted through participation, and that the ethical criterion for evaluating any formative community is whether it produces persons capable of carrying its gifts into contexts the community itself cannot anticipate or control.

Planned obsolescence thus emerges not as a paradox but as the proper telos of formative practice. The scaffolding metaphor is precise: scaffolding exists to enable construction it does not itself constitute. The community that builds well builds toward its own non-necessity — not through abandonment, but through the successful internalization of capacities, dispositions, and relational skills that outlast and exceed the original container. This is the aspiration shared, at their best, by every serious tradition of formation: not permanence, but generativity.
