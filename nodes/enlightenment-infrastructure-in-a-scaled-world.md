# Enlightenment Infrastructure in a Scaled World

The house is too small for the family

---

> Modernism isn't broken at its core — it has delivered extraordinary gains in human welfare. The real problem is that an operating system built for a world of one billion people is now running on a planet it was never designed to handle.

There's an important pushback to be made against any narrative that frames modernism as fundamentally broken or something to be rejected. Modernism arguably began around 1694 with the invention of the Bank of England, the founding of the Royal Society, and the Glorious Revolution, reaching its fully integrated form with the Enlightenment in the mid-18th century. And it has done genuinely extraordinary things for humanity. In 1700, most people in the West lived in houses with dirt floors and smoky open-front fireplaces. The combination of displacing superstition with real knowledge-generating systems — empirical science on one side, technology on the other, and eventually their deep interaction — has utterly transformed what it means to be a human being. We now live so much longer, healthier, and more materially secure that we have the luxury of worrying about the very meaning crises being discussed. The more precise diagnosis isn't that modernism is broken in its essence, but that it was designed and scaled at a time when humanity was small — roughly a billion people — and our capacity to do harm to the planet was relatively limited. The problem isn't the Enlightenment values themselves; it's that the operating system was built for a world we no longer inhabit.

## Apprentice

It's tempting to look at today's crises — environmental collapse, meaning loss, social fragmentation — and conclude that the whole modern project was a mistake. But that conclusion moves too fast. The institutions and ideas that emerged in the late 1600s and flowered in the 18th century Enlightenment genuinely transformed human life. In 1700, most people in the West lived in homes with dirt floors, burned smoky fires indoors, and died young from diseases we now barely think about. Empirical science and practical technology, working together, changed all of that.

The more careful diagnosis is not that modernism failed, but that it was designed for a much smaller world. When these systems were built and scaled, there were roughly a billion people on Earth, and humanity's ability to damage its own habitat was limited. The Enlightenment values — reason, empirical inquiry, individual rights — aren't themselves the problem.

What's broken is the fit between those values and the vastly larger, more interconnected, more ecologically fragile world we now inhabit. The operating system needs an upgrade, not a replacement.

## Adept

A recurring move in contemporary critique is to locate the roots of modernity's pathologies in Enlightenment rationalism itself — to treat instrumental reason, disenchantment, or the nature-culture split as original sins that now demand wholesale rejection. This perspective pushes back sharply against that framing. Tracing the modern project to its institutional origins — the Bank of England, the Royal Society, and the Glorious Revolution, all clustering around 1694 — it argues that what followed was not a civilizational error but a genuine achievement. The empirical epistemology and technological capacity that emerged from this period produced transformations in life expectancy, material security, and freedom from superstition that constitute real moral gains.

The diagnostic precision matters here. The claim is not that modernism is without pathology, but that the pathology is architectural rather than foundational. The Enlightenment operating system was calibrated for a world of approximately one billion people with limited planetary leverage. Its core commitments — fallibilism, empirical inquiry, rights-based political order — were not designed with the assumption that human activity would become a geological force.

The implication is that the meaning crises, ecological pressures, and institutional failures of the present are better understood as scaling failures than as evidence of a corrupt foundation. The task is not to abandon Enlightenment values but to extend and re-engineer the systems that carry them into conditions of planetary-scale consequence.

## Magus

The hermeneutics of suspicion applied to modernity — running from Weber's disenchantment thesis through Horkheimer and Adorno's dialectic of Enlightenment to more recent degrowth and post-humanist critiques — tends to treat the Enlightenment's emancipatory program and its destructive consequences as internally related, even necessary expressions of the same logic. This perspective contests that genealogy. By periodizing the modern settlement with precision — the co-emergence of central banking, institutionalized empirical inquiry, and constitutional constraint on sovereign power in the 1690s, crystallizing into the high Enlightenment by the mid-18th century — it insists that the normative core of this settlement retains validity independent of its historical pathologies.

The analytical move is to disaggregate foundation from operating parameters. The epistemic commitments of the Enlightenment — fallibilism, intersubjective verification, the subordination of authority to evidence — are not implicated in the ecological and social crises of late modernity in any straightforward causal sense. What is implicated is the scalar mismatch between institutions designed for a low-leverage civilization of roughly one billion persons and the Anthropocene condition in which human metabolic activity functions as a planetary boundary variable. The 'meaning crisis,' on this reading, is partly an artifact of success: the material security produced by modern institutions creates the existential latitude in which such crises become thinkable.

The prescriptive corollary is neither restorationist nor revolutionary. It calls for what might be termed a second-order modernism — one that applies Enlightenment methods reflexively to the institutional forms that carry Enlightenment values, upgrading their operating assumptions to match a world of eight billion persons with civilizational-scale ecological leverage.
