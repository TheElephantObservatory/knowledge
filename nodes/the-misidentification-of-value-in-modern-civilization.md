# The Misidentification of Value in Modern Civilization

Trading living fields for financial ghosts

---

> The world's overlapping crises share a single root: modernity convinced us the universe contains no real value, so we've been destroying actual living value to produce financial abstractions we mistake for the real thing. Fixing this requires rebuilding a defensible philosophy of value from the ground up.

The metacrisis is fundamentally a crisis of value ontology. For most of human history, the dominant worldview held that the universe was saturated with intrinsic meaning and value — that humanity, consciousness, and ethical choice were woven into the fabric of reality itself. Modernity, particularly through what Zak Stein calls the 'cold stream' running from Hume onward, systematically dismantled this view, replacing it with a universe of mere matter and motion, where value is either a social construction, a biological artifact, or simply relative preference. The result is not philosophical neutrality but civilizational catastrophe: we are now destroying living fields of actual, embodied, intrinsic value — ecosystems, communities, meaning structures — in order to generate abstract, financialized, fungible tokens that we have mistaken for value itself. The metacrisis is therefore not primarily a technological or political failure; it is a profound misidentification of what value actually is. The corrective project — what Stein, Gafni, and Wilber are pursuing under the name David J. Temple — is a 'counterrevolution': not a regression to premodern dogma, but a reconstruction of value ontology that takes seriously both the scientific critique and the postmodern critique, and shows that value survives both. Drawing on what they call 'evolving perennialism,' they argue that the great lineage traditions, when properly interpreted, already contain a sophisticated discourse that treats value as ontically real — as primordial as space, time, matter, and motion — and that the clarified human will and desire can genuinely disclose, rather than merely project, that value. The civilizational stakes are acute: previous transvaluations — moments when the deep backdrop of how a civilization talks about value fundamentally shifts — have historically been accompanied by social collapse and dark ages. But we now have planetary-scale infrastructure, meaning we cannot afford the same collapse while we undergo this transition. Reconstructing a coherent, scientifically and philosophically defensible ontology of value is therefore not an academic exercise but a civilizational survival project.

## Apprentice

Something has gone quietly wrong at the foundation of modern civilization. For most of human history, people assumed the world itself was meaningful — that nature, community, and moral life mattered not just because humans decided they did, but because reality was genuinely saturated with worth. Over the past few centuries, a particular strand of Western philosophy gradually dismantled that assumption, leaving us with a picture of the universe as pure matter in motion, where value is just a feeling, a social habit, or a personal preference.

The consequences have been catastrophic in a way that's easy to miss. When value is treated as merely subjective, it becomes easy to destroy things that carry real, living worth — forests, communities, cultures, relationships — in order to accumulate things that only represent value abstractly, like financial instruments and metrics. We've been trading the real for the symbolic without noticing.

Thinkers including Zak Stein, Marc Gafni, and Ken Wilber argue that recovering from this mistake is not optional. History shows that when civilizations undergo deep shifts in how they understand value, the transition is often violent and chaotic. But now, with planetary-scale systems at stake, we cannot afford that kind of collapse. The project they are pursuing aims to reconstruct a coherent account of value — one that takes science and critical philosophy seriously, but shows that genuine, objective value survives both.

## Adept

The metacrisis — the interlocking cascade of ecological, political, epistemic, and social breakdowns — is increasingly diagnosed at the level of systems, incentives, or technology. A more penetrating diagnosis locates the root in value ontology. The dominant modern inheritance, traced by Zak Stein through what he calls the 'cold stream' running from Hume through logical positivism and into contemporary naturalism, systematically evacuated intrinsic value from the structure of reality. What remains is an ontology in which value is either a projection of biological drives, a contingent social construction, or irreducibly relative preference — none of which can ground genuine normative claims.

The practical consequence is a civilizational category error: living fields of embodied, intrinsic value — ecosystems, meaning structures, relational communities — are being liquidated to produce abstract, fungible, financialized tokens that are mistaken for value itself. This is not a market failure in the technical sense; it is a failure of ontological perception at scale.

The corrective project developed under the name David J. Temple by Stein, Marc Gafni, and Ken Wilber proposes what they call a 'counterrevolution' in value ontology. Drawing on 'evolving perennialism,' they argue that the great wisdom lineages already contain sophisticated frameworks treating value as ontically primitive — as real as space, time, and causation — and that a clarified will and desire can genuinely disclose rather than merely project that value. Crucially, this is not a regression to premodern metaphysics; it is a reconstruction that passes through, rather than around, the scientific and postmodern critiques. The urgency is historical: prior transvaluations have typically been accompanied by civilizational collapse, and planetary-scale infrastructure means that cost is no longer survivable.

## Magus

The diagnostic claim advanced by Stein, Gafni, and Wilber under the David J. Temple project is that the metacrisis is, at its generative core, a crisis of axiological ontology. The Humean severance — the categorical prohibition on deriving normative claims from descriptive ones — inaugurated a philosophical trajectory that progressively denaturalized value, rendering it epiphenomenal to a substrate ontology of matter and efficient causation. Logical positivism's emotivist inheritance, evolutionary debunking arguments, and social constructivist genealogies of normativity collectively consolidated a worldview in which value-talk is systematically reduced to something else: adaptive preference, intersubjective negotiation, or semiotic convention. The result is not philosophical neutrality but a structurally motivated incapacity to perceive or protect what the project terms 'living fields of intrinsic value.'

The civilizational pathology this generates is precise: when value is ontologically homeless, the symbolic representations of value — financialized instruments, quantified metrics, fungible tokens — fill the vacuum and are mistaken for value itself. The destruction of ecosystems, relational communities, and meaning-saturated cultural forms in order to generate abstract exchange-value is not an externality of capitalism but its axiological precondition. The category error is upstream of the political economy.

The reconstructive project invokes what they term 'evolving perennialism' — a hermeneutical stance that reads the great contemplative and philosophical lineages not as pre-critical dogma but as repositories of a sophisticated discourse in which value is treated as ontically primitive, coeval with space, time, and matter. The epistemological claim is that a disciplined, clarified intentionality can genuinely disclose rather than merely project axiological structure — a form of value-perception that is neither naive realism nor constructivism. The historical stakes are framed through the concept of transvaluation: civilizational shifts in the deep grammar of value-discourse have historically been catastrophic transition events. The novelty of the present moment is that planetary-scale sociotechnical infrastructure forecloses the option of managed collapse and reconstruction; the transvaluation must therefore be navigated with unprecedented philosophical precision and institutional intentionality.
