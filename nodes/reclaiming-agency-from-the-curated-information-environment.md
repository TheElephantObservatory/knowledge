# Reclaiming Agency from the Curated Information Environment

Escaping the gravity of fascination porn

---

> Algorithmic feeds exploit our evolved tendency to treat what we see as reality, warping our sense of what matters. The fix is simple but hard: redirect attention toward what you can actually change, and let that agency compound.

Most of us have a deeply miscalibrated relationship between our attention and our actual agency. We evolved to take in the world through our eyes, and that world couldn't be curated — what we saw was genuinely representative of what was around us. But now, when our information environment is algorithmically curated, our evolutionary predisposition still treats what comes through our eyes as 'the world.' So when Facebook dopes your news feed with negative content, you become depressed. When it shows you positive content, you become positive. Your political views shift based on the preponderance of what you're shown — not because you reasoned your way there, but because you evolved to trust your perceptual field as reality.

The consequence is a profound distortion of what feels important. Who becomes president can feel more urgent than how your kids are doing in school — even though you have almost no agency over the former and enormous agency over the latter, and the latter is far more consequential to your actual life. Studying climate change, AI risk, or democratic breakdown can become a kind of fascination porn — compelling in the way that slowing down to look at a car accident is compelling, for no good reason connected to anything you can actually do.

The corrective isn't to disengage from the world. It's to step back from the input feed and ask: what are the things I actually have agency over that I care about most? And then ensure the preponderance of your attention matches those things. Where you genuinely can't act, find people who can and proxy your support to them — donate, amplify, enable. That takes far less energy than stressing about something you can't resolve. And there's a deeper autopoietic logic here: if you orient your sensemaking toward domains where you're actually learning and increasing your agency, that state compounds. You grow your capacity over time. But if you're in a state of overwhelm and hopelessness, you'll scan from thing to thing without going deep enough to understand any of it — and you'll propagate more overwhelm. The question of how to center in your actual sense of agency, and grow it by acting on it, is not just a practical consideration. It's the foundational condition for being able to do anything meaningful at all.

## Apprentice

Humans evolved in a world where what you saw was a honest sample of your surroundings. If you noticed a lot of predators, there really were a lot of predators. That instinct still runs in us today — but now what we see is curated by algorithms designed to maximize engagement, not accuracy. So we absorb the mood and priorities of our feed as if they were the mood and priorities of reality itself.

The result is a strange inversion of importance. The next election can feel more urgent than your child's education, even though you have almost no influence over the former and enormous influence over the latter. Anxiety about climate change or political collapse can become a kind of compulsive watching — like slowing down at a car accident — disconnected from anything you can actually do.

The corrective is to ask, honestly, what you have real agency over and care about most, then make sure your attention reflects that. Where you genuinely can't act, find people who can and support them efficiently — donate, amplify, enable. More importantly, when you orient your attention toward domains where you're actually learning and doing, that capacity grows. Overwhelm, by contrast, keeps you skimming the surface of everything and mastering nothing. Aligning attention with agency isn't just good advice — it's the precondition for doing anything meaningful at all.

## Adept

There is a deep mismatch between the information environments humans evolved for and those we now inhabit. In ancestral contexts, perception was a reliable proxy for local reality — the distribution of what you saw roughly matched the distribution of what existed. That epistemological shortcut is now systematically exploited. Algorithmically curated feeds optimize for engagement, not representativeness, and our perceptual systems have no native defense against this: we continue to treat the contents of our visual field as ground truth about the world. The downstream effects are well-documented — emotional contagion, political polarization, and a chronic miscalibration of salience.

The more underexamined consequence is the distortion of perceived agency. Attention migrates toward high-stimulus, low-agency domains — geopolitical crises, civilizational risks, electoral outcomes — while high-agency, high-consequence domains like local relationships, professional craft, and community are systematically underweighted. This produces what might be called fascination without traction: the phenomenology of engagement without the feedback loops that make engagement meaningful.

The proposed corrective is an agency-first reorientation of sensemaking. Rather than filtering the feed, the move is to invert the question: identify the domains where you have genuine leverage, and let those anchor your attention. Where direct agency is absent, proxy action — targeted support of those who do have leverage — is far more metabolically efficient than sustained anxiety. Crucially, this is not merely a productivity heuristic. There is an autopoietic logic at work: sensemaking oriented toward actionable domains generates learning and increasing competence, which expands the scope of future agency. Overwhelm, by contrast, is self-reinforcing and self-limiting. The alignment of attention with agency is, in this framing, the foundational condition for any meaningful epistemic or practical development.

## Magus

The argument proceeds from a straightforward but underappreciated point in evolutionary epistemology: the human perceptual system was calibrated in environments where the information stream was ecologically valid — where the statistical distribution of percepts was a reliable, if noisy, sample of the agent's actual niche. This calibration underwrites a deep inferential habit: treat the preponderance of what appears in the visual field as evidence about the structure of the world. Algorithmic curation breaks this assumption silently. The feed is not a sample; it is a constructed artifact optimized against engagement metrics. Yet the inferential habit persists, generating systematic first-order errors in world-modeling and, derivatively, in affective and motivational states. The experimental literature on emotional contagion via curated feeds provides empirical traction on what is, at root, an epistemological vulnerability.

The second-order consequence, less frequently theorized, is a structural distortion in the agent's model of their own efficacy. Salience, under algorithmic mediation, migrates toward high-volatility, low-agency domains. The phenomenology is one of urgency and concern, but the action-relevant feedback loops are absent or attenuated. This is not mere distraction; it is a systematic miscalibration of the agent's perceived locus of control, with compounding effects on motivation, identity, and practical reasoning. The concept of fascination porn gestures at this — the affective signature of engagement decoupled from any telos of action or understanding.

The normative proposal is an inversion of the standard attention-management frame. Rather than curating inputs, the move is to reconstruct the agent's sensemaking around a prior map of actual agency — identifying domains of genuine leverage and weighting attention accordingly. Proxy action is introduced as a metabolically efficient resolution for domains of concern without direct leverage. The deeper claim is autopoietic: sensemaking anchored in actionable domains generates recursive competence development, expanding the agent's effective niche over time. Overwhelm, conversely, instantiates a dissipative attractor — high arousal, low integration, no accumulation. The alignment of attention with agency is thus not a therapeutic recommendation but an ontological precondition for the kind of iterative, world-engaging cognition that makes development possible at all.
