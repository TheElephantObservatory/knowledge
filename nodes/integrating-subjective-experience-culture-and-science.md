# Integrating Subjective Experience, Culture, and Science

The three eyes that see one world

---

> The same world is known three irreducibly different ways — through personal experience, shared culture, and objective science — and no coherent picture of how these relate has ever been achieved. UTOK proposes one.

There is a profound puzzle at the heart of human knowledge that has never been adequately solved: we encounter the same world through three irreducibly different modes of knowing — the subjective, the intersubjective, and the objective — and we have no coherent picture of how they relate. Donald Davidson captured this precisely when he wrote that we need an overall picture which not only accommodates all three modes of knowing but makes their relations to one another clear, because without such a picture we should be deeply puzzled that the same world is known to us in these three different ways. What UTOK argues is that these three vectors are not merely philosophical categories but have a real evolutionary and historical sequence. Subjective phenomenological knowing came first — mammals opening their eyes and experiencing the world as subjects. Then, as primates began using propositional language, intersubjective cultural knowing emerged: shared systems of justification that bind people together around what is and what ought to be, creating collective identity from the inside. And only much later, out of that intersubjective cultural matrix, did modern empirical natural science emerge — a method for generating knowledge that is maximally generalizable, repeatable, and independent of any particular subject's perspective. The tragedy of the Enlightenment is that in factoring out the subject to achieve objectivity, science came to act as though the subject didn't exist at all. The architecture of the scientific vector simply has no internal way to frame and hold the subject. UTOK's contribution is to provide three distinct conceptual pillars — the Tree of Knowledge for the objective scientific vector, the iQuad Coin for the subjective phenomenological vector, and the Garden for the intersubjective cultural vector — that together offer a coherent, non-fractured picture of how the same world can be known in three genuinely different ways.

## Apprentice

There is a deep puzzle that rarely gets named clearly: we know the world in three genuinely different ways. There is the private, felt experience of being a subject — what it is like to see red or feel pain. There is the shared world of culture — the stories, values, and agreements that bind communities together. And there is the world of science — facts that hold regardless of who is looking or where they stand. These three are not the same thing, and no one has ever given a satisfying account of how they fit together.

The philosopher Donald Davidson noticed this gap and said we urgently need a picture that shows how all three relate. UTOK takes that challenge seriously and argues that the three modes are not just philosophical categories — they appeared in a real historical sequence. Felt experience came first, with the earliest animals. Shared cultural meaning came next, when language made collective belief possible. Objective science came last, emerging from within culture as a special method for stripping away the personal.

The tragedy is that in achieving objectivity, science effectively erased the subject from its picture of the world. UTOK responds by offering three distinct frameworks — one for each mode of knowing — that together form a coherent, unified map of how the same world can be genuinely known in three different ways.

## Adept

Donald Davidson identified what he called a fundamental philosophical puzzle: human beings access the same world through three irreducibly distinct epistemic vectors — the subjective, the intersubjective, and the objective — yet no adequate account exists of how these vectors relate to one another or why a single world should be knowable in such radically different ways. This is not merely an academic concern. The absence of a coherent tripartite epistemology leaves modern thought fractured, unable to integrate first-person phenomenology, second-person cultural meaning-making, and third-person scientific description into a unified picture.

UTOK advances the claim that these three vectors are not only philosophically distinct but historically and evolutionarily sequenced. Phenomenological subjectivity — the felt interiority of experience — emerged with sentient animals long before language. Intersubjective cultural knowing arose when propositional language enabled shared systems of justification, producing collective identity and normative frameworks. Empirical natural science emerged later still, out of that cultural matrix, as a method engineered to maximize generalizability and minimize dependence on any particular subject's perspective. The Enlightenment's great achievement was also its epistemic wound: in factoring out the subject to secure objectivity, the scientific framework generated no internal resources for representing subjectivity at all.

UTOK's response is architecturally tripartite. The Tree of Knowledge system provides a map of objective, scientifically described reality organized by emergent complexity. The iQuad Coin offers a conceptual structure for the subjective phenomenological vector. The Garden frames the intersubjective cultural domain. Together, these three pillars constitute a proposed solution to Davidson's puzzle — a non-reductive, non-fractured account of how one world admits of three genuinely different modes of knowing.

## Magus

Davidson's triangulation thesis — that the subjective, intersubjective, and objective constitute irreducible but mutually implicating epistemic vectors — identifies a lacuna at the center of post-Kantian epistemology. His demand for an 'overall picture' that not only accommodates all three but renders their relational structure intelligible remains, decades later, unmet by analytic philosophy, phenomenology, and the philosophy of science alike. The failure is not incidental: each tradition tends to privilege one vector while treating the others as derivative or eliminable, producing the familiar reductionisms — eliminative materialism, social constructivism, transcendental idealism — each of which purchases coherence within its domain at the cost of explanatory reach across the full tripartite field.

UTOK reframes the problem by grounding the three vectors in an evolutionary and semiotic developmental sequence rather than treating them as co-eternal philosophical categories. Phenomenological subjectivity — Nagel's 'what it is like,' the felt interiority that Chalmers isolates as the hard problem — is phylogenetically prior, emerging with the sentient nervous systems of early vertebrates. Intersubjective knowing, constituted through propositional language and the normative scaffolding of shared justificatory practices, is the distinctively primate and human achievement that Brandom's inferentialism and Tomasello's shared intentionality research illuminate from complementary angles. Objective scientific knowing is the most recent and most culturally constructed of the three: a methodological achievement that systematically brackets the subject to produce maximally transferable, observer-independent knowledge claims. The Enlightenment's epistemic asymmetry — its success in elaborating the objective vector while rendering the subjective vector invisible to its own methods — is thus not a contingent oversight but a structural consequence of the scientific framework's constitutive design.

UTOK's proposed resolution deploys three conceptual systems in explicit correspondence with the three vectors: the Tree of Knowledge as a complexity-theoretic ontology for the objective domain, the iQuad Coin as a structural account of first-person phenomenological experience, and the Garden as a framework for the intersubjective cultural field. The theoretical ambition is Davidsonian in scope — to provide not merely three parallel descriptions but a relational architecture that makes intelligible why one world should be genuinely, irreducibly knowable in three different ways.
