# The Ethics of Acknowledging Civilizational Death

Sitting at the bedside of the world

---

> Stephen Jenkinson asks what it means to love something that is dying but won't admit it — and extends this from the deathbed to civilization itself. The question is not how to fix what is ending, but how to be honestly present to it.

There is a particular kind of ethical and relational challenge that arises when you love someone who is dying but who refuses to acknowledge that they are dying. Stephen Jenkinson, drawing from his years in palliative care, frames this as a question of etiquette: how do you conduct yourself as if what is happening is actually happening, when the other person won't agree that it is? This is not a trivial or merely personal question. Jenkinson extends it to the cultural scale: how does a civilization behave when it is in the early stages of dying but collectively refuses to acknowledge it? The question becomes — what is the etiquette of your relationship with a dying culture? How do you act with integrity, with honesty, with love, in the presence of something that is ending, when the dominant mood is denial? This reframes the entire crisis not as a problem to be solved or a system to be optimized, but as a dying that needs to be witnessed and accompanied. And just as with a dying person, the answer to the question 'are they dying or not?' determines everything about what you do next. If you pretend alongside them that they are not dying, you may be kind in a shallow sense, but you deprive them — and yourself — of the possibility of dying well. The same logic applies to a civilization whose acquisitive, extractive way of doing things has become utterly exhausted. The question is not how to fix it, but how to be present to what is actually happening.

## Apprentice

When someone you love is dying but refuses to admit it, you face a strange and painful choice: do you play along with their denial, or do you act as though what is actually happening is actually happening? Stephen Jenkinson, who spent years working with dying people, calls this a question of etiquette — not manners in a trivial sense, but the deeper question of how you conduct yourself with honesty and love in the presence of something that is ending.

Jenkinson extends this question far beyond the individual deathbed. He asks whether the same dynamic applies to an entire civilization — one whose way of extracting, consuming, and accumulating has run out of road, but which collectively refuses to acknowledge it. If that is the situation, then the relevant question is not 'how do we fix this?' but 'how do we accompany this?'

The stakes of getting the diagnosis right are enormous. If you pretend alongside a dying person that they are not dying, you may avoid conflict, but you rob both of you of the chance to meet the ending honestly. The same may be true of a civilization. Witnessing what is actually happening — without flinching, without false hope — becomes an act of integrity, and perhaps of love.

## Adept

Jenkinson's framing draws on his clinical and ceremonial work with the dying to articulate what he calls the etiquette of accompanying something that is ending. The core problem is epistemological and ethical at once: when a dying person refuses to acknowledge their dying, those who love them must decide whether to collude in the denial or to hold the reality of what is happening, even alone if necessary. This is not cruelty — it is the precondition for what Jenkinson calls 'dying well,' a phrase that carries genuine moral weight in his framework.

The move that gives this insight its cultural force is the scalar extension: Jenkinson applies the same logic to Western industrial civilization, whose acquisitive and extractive metabolism he regards as terminally exhausted. The question of whether a civilization is dying or merely in crisis is not academic — it determines the entire orientation of one's response. A crisis calls for intervention, optimization, repair. A dying calls for presence, witness, and accompaniment. Collapsing these two categories, or defaulting to the crisis frame out of habit or hope, is itself a form of denial.

What emerges is a reframing of the contemporary ecological and civilizational predicament that resists both techno-optimism and paralytic despair. The etiquette question — how do you act with integrity in the presence of a dying that the dying refuses to name? — opens a space for a different kind of engagement: one grounded in honest perception rather than problem-solving, and in relational fidelity rather than outcome management.

## Magus

Jenkinson's concept of civilizational etiquette operates at the intersection of thanatology, cultural critique, and what might be called an ethics of witness. Drawing on the phenomenology of the deathbed — specifically the relational asymmetry that obtains when one party holds the knowledge of dying and the other refuses it — he constructs an analogy that is not merely rhetorical but structurally load-bearing. The dying person's denial is not simply a psychological defense; it reorganizes the entire relational field, imposing on those who love them a choice between complicity and a kind of solitary truth-bearing that is itself a form of grief.

The scalar transposition to civilization is where the argument becomes philosophically demanding. Jenkinson is not making a metaphorical claim — that civilization is 'like' a dying person — but a diagnostic one: that the acquisitive, growth-dependent, extractive logic of late modernity has reached the limits of its metabolic viability, and that the dominant cultural response is structured denial. This positions the question of civilizational dying not within the register of systems theory or political economy, but within the register of accompaniment — a tradition with deep roots in hospice practice, contemplative ethics, and indigenous ceremonial life.

The ethical consequence is precise and uncomfortable. To adopt the crisis frame — to treat the present moment as a problem awaiting a sufficiently ingenious solution — is, on Jenkinson's account, a form of collusion with the denial. It forecloses the possibility of what he calls dying well at the civilizational scale: a conscious, witnessed, relationally honest encounter with ending. The etiquette question thus becomes a test of ontological seriousness — whether one is willing to perceive what is, rather than what one wishes were the case.
