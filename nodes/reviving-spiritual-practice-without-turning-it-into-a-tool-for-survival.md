# Reviving Spiritual Practice Without Turning It Into a Tool for Survival

How to save the world without trying

---

> Reviving religious practices to address civilizational crisis creates a paradox: pursuing them as tools destroys what makes them transformative, yet ignoring the crisis betrays their deepest purpose. The resolution resembles wu wei — practices done for their own sake whose aliveness naturally reorients civilization.

There is a paradox at the heart of any serious attempt to revive religious practice in the context of civilizational crisis. The practices that are most needed — contemplative attunement, embodied interoception, participatory dialogue, the full ecology of transformative ritual — cannot be pursued instrumentally without being destroyed. The moment you practice them in order to save the world, you introduce the very cognitive structure — means-ends rationality, propositional goal-orientation — that these practices exist to liberate us from. And yet, to practice them in serene indifference to the domicide unfolding around us — the comprehensive ontological collapse that exceeds even the Bronze Age collapse or the Hellenistic catastrophe in its scope — is to fail religion at a more fundamental level. So we face two kinds of failure simultaneously: the crippling failure of instrumentalism, which kills the practices by making them tools, and the failure of withdrawal, which renders the practices irrelevant to the most urgent crisis humanity has faced. The resolution, if there is one, resembles the Taoist logic of wu wei — trying not to try, saving the world without making saving the world the object of the practice. The practices must be pursued for their own sake, because that is the only way they come alive, and yet that very aliveness is what makes them capable of responding to the domicide. This is not a solved problem. It is a meta-task that any serious revisioning of religion for our times must hold at its center: how to cultivate intrinsically rewarding practices that have the side effect of reorienting civilization — without collapsing that side effect back into the instrumental frame that generated the crisis in the first place.

## Apprentice

There is a deep contradiction in trying to use spiritual practices to fix the world. Contemplation, ritual, deep listening, embodied awareness — these things only work when you do them for their own sake. The moment you turn them into instruments for solving a problem, even a genuinely urgent one, you introduce exactly the mindset they are meant to free you from: the calculating, goal-driven thinking that arguably created the crisis in the first place.

But the alternative is no better. Practicing in peaceful detachment while civilization undergoes what may be its most comprehensive collapse — a destruction not just of structures but of our basic sense of home and meaning — feels like a betrayal of what religion is supposed to be about. So there are two ways to fail: making the practices into tools, which kills them, or retreating into private serenity, which makes them irrelevant.

The way through this paradox, if there is one, looks something like the Taoist idea of "trying not to try." You do the practices wholeheartedly, for their own sake, because that is the only way they actually come alive. And it turns out that this aliveness is precisely what equips people to respond to crisis — not as a goal, but as a natural overflow. This is not a neat solution. It is an ongoing tension that any honest attempt to reimagine religion for our time must sit with rather than resolve.

## Adept

Any serious attempt to revive transformative religious practice in response to civilizational crisis encounters a structural paradox. The practices most needed — contemplative attunement, embodied interoception, participatory knowing, the full ecology of ritual transformation — are intrinsically non-instrumental. They operate precisely by dissolving the means-ends rationality and propositional goal-orientation that dominate modern cognition. Yet the moment these practices are pursued in order to address the crisis, that instrumental framing reintroduces the very cognitive structure they exist to overcome. The practice is hollowed out from within.

The opposite failure is equally devastating. To pursue these practices in withdrawal from what might be called domicide — a comprehensive ontological collapse exceeding even the Bronze Age or Hellenistic catastrophes in scope — is to betray religion at a level deeper than doctrine. It reduces transformative practice to spiritual escapism while the conditions for human dwelling are systematically destroyed. Two failures thus operate simultaneously: instrumentalism, which kills the practice by conscripting it, and quietism, which renders it irrelevant to the defining emergency of our epoch.

The resolution, to the extent one exists, follows the logic of wu wei — the Taoist principle of effortless action, or trying not to try. Practices must be pursued for their own sake, because intrinsic engagement is the only condition under which they genuinely transform. Yet that very transformation — the reorientation of attention, desire, and relational capacity — is what makes practitioners capable of responding to domicide. The side effect must never collapse back into the instrumental frame. This paradox is not a problem to be solved but a meta-task: the central challenge for any serious revisioning of religion adequate to our historical moment.

## Magus

A structural aporia inhabits the intersection of religious revitalization and civilizational crisis response. The practices most urgently required — contemplative attunement, embodied interoception, participatory sensemaking, the full ecology of transformative ritual — are constitutively non-instrumental. Their efficacy depends on the dissolution of precisely the means-ends rationality and propositional goal-orientation that instrumentalizing them would reinstate. This is not a contingent difficulty but a transcendental constraint: the cognitive frame that would deploy these practices as civilizational therapeutics is isomorphic with the cognitive frame they exist to deconstruct. Instrumentalism does not merely weaken the practices; it ontologically inverts them.

Yet the counter-position — pursuing contemplative and ritual practice in serene withdrawal — fails religion at a more fundamental level than any doctrinal error. The current crisis constitutes domicide: a comprehensive ontological collapse of the structures of dwelling, meaning, and participatory belonging that exceeds the Bronze Age collapse or the Hellenistic catastrophe in both scope and reflexivity. To practice in indifference to domicide is to sever the very responsiveness to reality that contemplative traditions claim to cultivate. Quietism and instrumentalism thus constitute a double bind, not a spectrum with a comfortable midpoint.

The paradox admits no propositional resolution but may yield to a performative one, structurally analogous to the Taoist logic of wu wei — Edward Slingerland's "trying not to try" captures the phenomenology precisely. Practices pursued for their intrinsic transformative reward generate exactly the capacities — attentional reorientation, affective flexibility, relational attunement, ontological openness — that constitute adequate response to domicide. The side effect must remain a side effect; the moment it is thematized as purpose, the instrumental frame reasserts itself. This irreducible tension is not a problem awaiting solution but the meta-task defining any adequate revisioning of religion for the present catastrophe: cultivating intrinsically rewarding practices whose civilizational consequences must never be allowed to become their justification.
