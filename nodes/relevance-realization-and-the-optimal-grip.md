# Relevance Realization and the Optimal Grip

The art of ignoring the infinite

---

> Cognition is not about processing more information — it's about ignoring most of it intelligently. John Vervaeke's theory of relevance realization explains how minds home in on what actually matters, and why that filtering capacity is the core of intelligence.

John Vervaeke's theory of relevance realization proposes that the fundamental criterion of cognition is not computation in the abstract, but the capacity to intelligently ignore most of the available information in an environment and home in on what actually matters. The world contains, in Shannon's sense, an effectively infinite amount of information — no organism could process all of it. The cognitive challenge is not to process more, but to filter better: to develop mechanisms that reliably distinguish meaningful signals from noise in ways that serve the organism's goals and survival. Vervaeke and colleagues identify a set of opponent processes built into cognitive dynamics — compression versus particularization, assimilation versus accommodation, integration versus differentiation — that together allow the brain to find what he calls the optimal grip on its context. A system that simultaneously integrates and differentiates its functions is complexifying, and complex systems develop emergent functions; cognitive learning is therefore not merely the accumulation of information but a genuine developmental process in which the brain becomes more complex, more capable, and more finely tuned to the structure of its world. Vervaeke captures this with the concept of transjectivity — meaning is neither purely subjective nor purely objective, but arises in the dynamic relational exchange between an agent and its arena, a formulation that applies not just to minded animals but to every level of complexity at which entity-field relationships generate meaning. This bridges directly with Friston's free energy principle: both frameworks converge on the question of which subset of the infinite sea of environmental information is relevant, which signals the nervous system should attend to, and which can be safely ignored.

## Apprentice

Every moment, your senses are flooded with far more information than any brain could handle. The question isn't how to process it all — that's impossible — but how to reliably pick out what actually matters. John Vervaeke's theory of relevance realization proposes that this filtering capacity is the central achievement of cognition, not a side feature of it.

The brain manages this through pairs of competing processes: compressing information into general patterns while also staying alert to specific details, absorbing new experiences into existing frameworks while also revising those frameworks when needed. These tensions don't cancel each other out — they work together to help the mind find what Vervaeke calls the optimal grip on a situation. Over time, this process doesn't just accumulate knowledge; it genuinely develops the brain, making it more capable and better tuned to the world.

Crucially, meaning in this view isn't something that exists purely inside your head or purely out in the world. It arises in the relationship between an agent and its environment — a concept Vervaeke calls transjectivity. This connects to other major theories in cognitive science, suggesting that what brains fundamentally do is navigate an ocean of information by learning, moment to moment, what to care about.

## Adept

Vervaeke's theory of relevance realization reframes the central problem of cognition: not computation per se, but the capacity to intelligently filter an effectively infinite informational environment. Drawing on Shannon's information theory, the argument is that no organism could process the total available signal; the adaptive challenge is therefore selective attention at a principled level — distinguishing meaningful structure from noise in ways that reliably serve the organism's goals.

The mechanism Vervaeke proposes involves opponent processing across several axes: compression versus particularization, assimilation versus accommodation, integration versus differentiation. These are not failures of resolution but productive tensions that together enable what he calls the optimal grip — a dynamically calibrated relationship between agent and context. Critically, a system that simultaneously integrates and differentiates is undergoing complexification, and complex systems generate emergent functions. Cognitive learning is therefore a genuine developmental process, not mere information accumulation; the brain becomes structurally more capable as it becomes better tuned to the world's affordance structure.

The concept of transjectivity captures the ontological upshot: meaning is neither subjective projection nor objective feature, but arises in the relational exchange between agent and arena. This formulation scales beyond minded animals to any level of complexity at which entity-field relationships generate meaningful distinctions. The convergence with Friston's free energy principle is direct — both frameworks center on the question of which subset of environmental information a system should model, predict, and attend to, framing relevance not as a psychological luxury but as the constitutive problem of adaptive systems.

## Magus

Relevance realization, as developed by Vervaeke and colleagues, proposes that the constitutive problem of cognition is not computation in the abstract but the principled reduction of an informationally intractable environment to an actionable subset. The Shannonian framing is precise: the world's informational density is effectively unbounded, and no finite system can instantiate a complete model of it. The adaptive imperative is therefore not greater processing bandwidth but more finely calibrated filtering — mechanisms that reliably carve signal from noise relative to the organism's teleonomic structure.

The theoretical architecture invokes opponent processing as the engine of this calibration: compression and particularization operate in productive tension, as do assimilation and accommodation in the Piagetian register, and integration and differentiation at the systems level. These paired dynamics are not competing failure modes but the generative dialectic through which a cognitive system achieves what Vervaeke terms the optimal grip — a context-sensitive, dynamically updated attunement to the affordance landscape. The key implication is complexification: a system that simultaneously integrates and differentiates undergoes structural elaboration, and structural elaboration generates emergent functional capacities. Learning, on this account, is not propositional accumulation but ontogenetic development of the cognitive system itself.

Transjectivity names the relational ontology that follows: meaning is neither a subjective imposition nor an intrinsic objective property, but an emergent feature of the agent-arena coupling at whatever level of complexity that coupling obtains. This positions relevance realization within a broader theoretical convergence with Friston's free energy principle, which formalizes the same core question — which generative model best predicts the sensory consequences of action — and treats the minimization of surprise as the unifying imperative of adaptive systems. Both frameworks dissolve the classical subject-object dichotomy by locating cognition in the dynamics of relational exchange rather than in either pole independently.
