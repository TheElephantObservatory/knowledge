# Grieving the Death of God and World as the Precondition for Recovering the Sacred

> Before any post-metaphysical spirituality can take root, we must genuinely grieve a double death — the transcendent God and the immanent sacred depth of the world itself. Only through completed grief can the transjective relational space open where the sacred, understood as mattering, love, and orientation toward ultimacy, might be recovered naturalistically.

Nietzsche's announcement of the death of God remains, for John Vervaeke, one of the most consequential philosophical events in Western history — not because of its propositional content but because of what was never done with it. Nietzsche's deepest failure was existential rather than intellectual: having placed the announcement in the mouth of a madman who asks whether we have even felt this event yet, Nietzsche immediately converts the insight into a program of self-aggrandizement and will to power. The grief is bypassed. And this bypass is not incidental. Drawing on the psychology of attachment and loss, Vervaeke argues that authentic movement into new relational configurations — new ways of being connected to what is real and what matters — requires the completion of grief for what has been lost. Without that completion, every constructive project built atop the loss is structurally compromised.

But the diagnosis deepens. Nietzsche saw only half the catastrophe. The transcendent God above has died, yes — but the immanent sacred, the numinous depth encountered in time, nature, and embodied life, is collapsing simultaneously. Time no longer gestates meaning; it accelerates past comprehension. The natural world registers not as wilderness charged with presence but as a biosphere dying under human impact. To counsel people to recover the sacred by falling in love with being is, on this account, to ask them to make love to a corpse. We face a double death, and we remain stuck in the shock phase of grief.

The consequences ramify across every domain where 'the real' once announced itself. The transcendent no longer feels real. The immanent presents as entropic and decadent. The cultural sphere reads as manipulative. The virtual is experienced as artificial. People flee toward partial surrogates — intimate relationships, substances, extreme experience, comedy — that offer momentary contact with the confirming-and-surprising signature of the real but cannot sustain it. What is dissolving is not merely meaning but shareability itself: the capacity of reality to serve as common ground where genuine differences can be adjudicated. Much of what presents as ideological rage, political acting out, and distorted meaning-seeking may be the surface expression of grief that has nowhere to go. The culture has constructed elaborate interruptive mechanisms — psychological, theological, social — that prevent people from entering the grief space.

David Michael Levin's phenomenological work, drawing on Heidegger's Gelassenheit, examines how each human sense has been captured by subject-object framing, severing participatory engagement with the world. Levin proposes that crying — not as emotional discharge but as a practice of the self — constitutes a threshold experience in which ordinary vision is interrupted and a more fundamental mode of seeing becomes possible. Joanna Macy's biography enacts this trajectory: years of intellectual engagement with ecological collapse broke through into sustained weeping, which opened into grief work with others and then into the broader transformative project she calls the Work That Reconnects.

The constructive gesture points toward two interrelated moves. First, the 'transjective' — the relational ontological space between subject and world that is neither purely subjective nor purely objective. If the transcendent above and the immanent below have both collapsed as reliable sources of realness, this in-between territory may be where a new sense of the real can be cultivated. Second, the sacred itself can be recovered phenomenologically and naturalistically through a layered structure: mattering (being connected to something with reality and value beyond one's egocentric frame), deepening contact with reality through that connection (the phenomenology of falling in love with what is real), and orientation toward what is ultimately real — the experiential referent behind terms like God, Dao, and Brahman. The paradigm case is agapic love: a parent loves a child not instrumentally but because they want the child to exist beyond their own existence. This capacity for agapic concern can be understood as an exaptation of the care structures fundamental to primate mammalian life. The sacred is not an exception to nature but its deepest expression.

The urgent question, then, is not merely theological but ecological and ontological: how do we grieve both God and the world, dismantle the mechanisms that prevent that grief, and cultivate the relational conditions under which the sacred — as mattering, love, and ultimacy — might genuinely re-emerge?

## Apprentice

Something enormous has been lost in modern Western culture, and we haven't properly dealt with the loss. When Nietzsche declared that 'God is dead,' he was pointing to the collapse of the shared framework that once gave life meaning, purpose, and a sense of the sacred. But Nietzsche himself never actually grieved this loss — he rushed past it into ideas about power and self-overcoming. And that rushing past is exactly the problem.

Think of it like losing someone you deeply love. You can't just skip the mourning and jump into a new relationship and expect it to work. The grief has to be completed first. Vervaeke argues that our entire culture is stuck in this unfinished grief, and it shows up everywhere — in political rage, in addiction, in the desperate search for something that feels real in a world that increasingly doesn't.

But the situation is actually worse than Nietzsche realized. It's not just the God 'above' — the transcendent source of meaning — that has died. The sacred quality of the world itself is also collapsing. Nature feels less like a place of wonder and more like a dying system. Time doesn't feel like it's building toward anything meaningful; it just accelerates. So we're dealing with a double loss.

People try to find substitutes — intense relationships, substances, extreme experiences — but these only offer brief flashes of contact with something real. They can't sustain it.

The hopeful part is that the sacred doesn't require supernatural beliefs to be recovered. At its core, the sacred is about mattering — being connected to something real and valuable beyond yourself. The deepest example is love, especially the kind of love where you want something to flourish for its own sake, not for what it gives you. When that love also draws you into deeper contact with reality, and ultimately orients you toward what is most fundamentally real, you're touching what the great traditions called God, Dao, or Brahman — understood not as supernatural beings but as names for this deepest orientation. Before we can get there, though, we have to grieve what we've lost.
