# Information Technology and the Collapse of Shared Truth

The printing press is burning again

---

> More information alone doesn't produce better thinking or healthier societies. When the foundations of how people know what to know collapse, civilizations don't stabilize — they clash, sometimes violently, for generations.

Open knowledge does not make open minds. You can have all this information available, but it doesn't actually make democracy better or make a real difference societally without some shift in our consciousness, in our culture, in our institutions at a deeper level. This connects to a historical analogy: the printing press in the 1450s was a revolutionary information technology, and what you actually got in the 16th and 17th centuries were massive religious wars in Europe. The printing press may have even contributed to those wars because it allowed Protestants to disseminate ideas. What's going on today is a civilizational clash of that magnitude. Imagine you're in 1517 — the whole way you made sense of the world for a thousand years had been that you listened to the Catholic Church and the king. Suddenly people are saying no, read the Bible yourself in English or German. That level of radical shift in the ground of sensemaking is partly what's happening today. We're witnessing the breakdown of trust in science and rational bureaucracy — the epistemic authority of modernity — just as the breakdown of trust in the Catholic Church destabilized Western Europe. These moments of civilizational clash over where knowledge comes from — does it come from science, from religion, from somewhere else — are incredibly destabilizing, and it will take a long while before the dust settles.

## Apprentice

Most people assume that giving everyone access to information automatically leads to better decisions, a more informed public, and a healthier democracy. But history suggests otherwise. When the printing press arrived in the 1450s, it didn't usher in an age of enlightened reason — it helped ignite a century of brutal religious wars across Europe. The technology spread ideas faster, but it also shattered the shared framework people used to decide which ideas were trustworthy in the first place.

Something similar appears to be happening now. For centuries, science and expert institutions served as the agreed-upon referees of truth in modern societies. That agreement is breaking down. People are losing trust not just in specific institutions, but in the whole idea that credentialed expertise should settle questions of fact. This is less a political problem than a deeper cultural one — a crisis in how societies collectively decide what counts as knowledge.

These transitions are historically slow and painful. The wars of the Reformation lasted well over a hundred years before Europe found a new equilibrium. The suggestion here is that we may be at a similarly early and turbulent stage — and that no amount of additional information will resolve it without a corresponding shift in culture, values, and the shared structures through which people make sense of the world.

## Adept

The assumption embedded in most open-knowledge and information-access projects is that epistemic abundance translates into epistemic improvement — that wider access to facts and arguments naturally produces better-reasoned publics and more functional democracies. This view underestimates the degree to which collective reasoning depends not on the volume of available information but on the stability of shared sensemaking frameworks: the meta-level agreements about which sources, methods, and authorities are legitimate arbiters of truth.

The historical parallel to the Gutenberg press is instructive. Print technology democratized access to text and fractured the Catholic Church's monopoly on scriptural interpretation, but the immediate consequence was not enlightenment — it was the Wars of Religion. The printing press destabilized the existing epistemic authority structure faster than new legitimating frameworks could be constructed. The result was prolonged civilizational conflict over the most fundamental question: where does authoritative knowledge come from?

The argument here is that contemporary digital information technology is producing an analogous rupture. The epistemic authority of modernity — grounded in scientific consensus, credentialed expertise, and rational-bureaucratic institutions — is losing its taken-for-granted legitimacy at a civilizational scale. This is not simply a crisis of misinformation or media literacy; it is a deeper crisis of epistemic regime transition. As in the sixteenth century, the destabilization precedes by a long interval the eventual consolidation of new sensemaking norms. Information abundance, in this framing, is as likely to accelerate the crisis as to resolve it.

## Magus

The liberal epistemic project — premised on the Millian wager that free circulation of ideas tends toward truth and democratic self-governance — contains a suppressed assumption: that the meta-epistemic substrate remains stable. That is, it presupposes a functioning consensus on the criteria of credibility, the hierarchy of epistemic authorities, and the social institutions empowered to adjudicate contested claims. When that substrate fractures, information abundance does not produce epistemic improvement; it produces what might be called a sensemaking interregnum, in which the proliferation of claims accelerates precisely because no shared adjudicative framework constrains them.

The Reformation analogy illuminates the structural dynamics of such transitions. The Gutenberg press did not merely redistribute access to existing knowledge — it delegitimized the Church's monopoly on hermeneutic authority, triggering a prolonged and violent contest over the very ground of epistemic legitimacy. Luther's insistence on sola scriptura was not simply a theological claim; it was an epistemological revolution, relocating the site of authoritative interpretation from institutional hierarchy to individual conscience. The subsequent century of confessional warfare was less a conflict over doctrinal content than a civilizational struggle to reconstitute epistemic order on new foundations.

The present moment exhibits homologous features. The epistemic regime of modernity — organized around scientific rationality, Weberian expert authority, and credentialed institutional knowledge — is undergoing a legitimation crisis of comparable magnitude. The collapse is not merely sociological but structural: the second-order norms that once governed first-order disagreements are themselves in contest. In this framing, proposals centered on information access or media literacy misdiagnose the pathology. The crisis is not informational but constitutive — a breakdown in the shared ontological and normative infrastructure through which modern societies have organized the production, validation, and distribution of authoritative knowledge. Resolution, if historical precedent holds, will require not better data but the slow, conflictual construction of new epistemic institutions capable of commanding legitimate authority.
