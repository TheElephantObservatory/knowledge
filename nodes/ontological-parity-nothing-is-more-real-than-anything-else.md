# Ontological Parity: Nothing Is More Real Than Anything Else

Santa Claus exists. So does everything else.

---

> Lawrence Cahoone, drawing on Justus Buchler, argues that metaphysics should ask not 'what is real?' but 'how is anything real?' — replacing the binary of existence with a relational question about the orders and contexts in which things function.

One of the deepest moves in Lawrence Cahoone's ordinal naturalism is his reformulation of what metaphysics is actually asking. Drawing on the Columbia naturalist Justus Buchler, Cahoone argues that the fundamental question of metaphysics is not 'what is real?' but rather 'how is anything real?' — in what manner, and in what order of relations, does something function? This shift is radical. It means that unicorns are as real as horses, but they obtain in the order of literary history rather than biology. Santa Claus is as real as any physical object — but the claim 'I discovered Santa Claus isn't real' is better understood as a shift in how he is real: from a physical agent to a cultural-literary figure. The principle Buchler calls ontological parity — that nothing is more real or existent than anything else — is not a claim that everything is equally important or equally useful. It is a claim that the question of existence is always a question of context, order, and relation. What changes when we learn something is 'not real' is not its existence per se, but the order of relations in which it functions. This reframing dissolves a huge class of pseudo-problems in philosophy and everyday reasoning, because it stops treating reality as a binary property that things either have or lack, and starts treating it as a relational, contextual, and always-situated phenomenon.

## Apprentice

Most people think of reality as a simple yes-or-no question: either something exists or it doesn't. Unicorns aren't real, horses are. But philosopher Lawrence Cahoone, building on the work of Justus Buchler, proposes a different way of thinking. Instead of asking whether something is real, we should ask how it is real — in what context, and through what kinds of relationships.

Consider Santa Claus. When a child learns Santa "isn't real," what actually changes? Santa doesn't vanish from the world. He still appears in stories, songs, advertisements, and cultural traditions. What shifts is the kind of reality Santa has: he moves from being understood as a physical person who delivers gifts to being understood as a cultural and literary figure. His existence doesn't disappear — it gets relocated.

This idea, called ontological parity, doesn't mean everything is equally important or useful. A fictional character and a living person obviously matter in different ways. But the claim is that "real" is never a simple on-off switch. It's always a question about where and how something functions. This reframing clears away a surprising number of confusions in both philosophy and everyday thinking, because it replaces a blunt binary with a richer, more accurate picture of how things actually show up in the world.

## Adept

Lawrence Cahoone's ordinal naturalism, rooted in the metaphysics of Columbia naturalist Justus Buchler, executes a decisive shift in the framing of ontological inquiry. The fundamental question of metaphysics is reformulated from 'what is real?' to 'how is anything real?' — that is, in what order of relations, and through what modes of functioning, does something obtain? This is not a minor terminological adjustment but a structural transformation of the metaphysical enterprise itself.

Buchler's principle of ontological parity holds that nothing is more or less real than anything else. Unicorns are as real as horses; they simply obtain in different orders — literary history rather than biology. When someone declares that Santa Claus "isn't real," the more precise description is that Santa has been reassigned from the order of physical agency to the order of cultural-literary existence. Existence is not gained or lost; the relational context in which something functions is what shifts. Parity is not a claim about equal importance, utility, or causal efficacy — it is a claim about the illegitimacy of grading things by degrees of reality.

The philosophical payoff is substantial. A large class of traditional pseudo-problems — debates over the reality of abstract objects, fictional entities, social constructs, possibilities — dissolves once reality is no longer treated as a binary property. Instead, reality becomes relational, contextual, and always situated within specific orders of natural complexity. This positions ordinal naturalism as a genuinely post-foundationalist metaphysics that avoids both reductive physicalism and inflationary Platonism, offering a framework where the question is never whether something exists but always how and where it functions.

## Magus

Cahoone's retrieval and extension of Buchler's ordinal metaphysics performs what may be the most consequential reorientation available within naturalist ontology: the displacement of the existential question ('is X real?') by the ordinal question ('in what order of relations does X obtain, and how does it function therein?'). Buchler's principle of ontological parity — that no complex is more or less real, more or less natural, than any other — is not an epistemically naïve leveling but a rigorous refusal of the hierarchical ontologies that have structured Western metaphysics from Plato's divided line through contemporary physicalist reductionism. Parity is a constraint on metaphysical judgment, not on discriminations of scope, relevance, or efficacy.

The ordinal framework reconceives what it means for something to "not be real." The child's discovery that Santa Claus is not a physical agent is redescribed as a reassignment across orders: from the order of active physical causation to the orders of cultural production, literary convention, and ritual practice. Nothing is subtracted from the ontological inventory; what changes is the topology of relations in which the complex is located. This move generalizes: fictional entities, mathematical structures, social institutions, and possibilities all obtain — the philosophical task is to articulate their ordinal locations, not to adjudicate their admission into being.

The dissolution of pseudo-problems here is not merely therapeutic but architectonic. By refusing the binary of existence and non-existence, ordinal naturalism renders otiose the interminable debates between nominalism and realism, eliminativism and inflationism, that have consumed analytic metaphysics. It replaces them with a descriptive cartography of orders — a metaphysics adequate to the irreducible plurality of natural complexes without recourse to foundational substances, privileged levels, or transcendent grounds.
