# Why Art Transforms Where Arguments Cannot

You have to live inside the turning.

---

> Arguments and evidence alone cannot produce genuine transformation. What truly changes people is encountering lives they find attractive — and art, uniquely, lets us inhabit other perspectives, producing a nonpropositional persuasion that no philosophical argument can replicate.

There is a profound argument about why arguments and evidence, while necessary, are not sufficient to produce the kind of transformation we are talking about. What seems to really bring about metanoia — genuine turning — in people is confronting other people who are living lives that they find attractive. This is where art, broadly construed, becomes indispensable. Art does something that philosophy and science cannot do alone: it allows you to inhabit a character, to look through their eyes, to indwell a particular stance and orientation toward reality. Consider Moby Dick — you have a massive network of allusion and ambiguity, and characters who carry a nonpropositional persuasion. You are attracted to Ahab, then you pull back. You sympathize with Ishmael, then you pull back from him too. And where you are when you are pulling back from both — that is not the same thing as an argument. It is closer to what Gadamer describes when you become more aware of your standpoint, your fundamental indexicality of who and what you are and where you are. Literature that honestly confronts the advent of the meaning crisis — Heart of Darkness, Death in Venice, The Plague — serves as a call for literature about the advent of the sacred, literature that can help awaken us from the meaning crisis. This opens people not only to philosophy and science but to a creative endeavor that is itself a form of participation in the project of meaning-making that no propositional system can fully replace.

## Apprentice

There's a limit to what logical arguments and scientific evidence can do when it comes to deeply changing how someone sees the world. You can present someone with all the right reasons, and they might agree intellectually without anything shifting inside them. What actually seems to produce real transformation — a genuine turning of the whole person — is encountering other people whose way of living strikes you as deeply attractive.

This is where art becomes essential. A great novel doesn't just tell you what to think; it lets you step inside another person's experience and look through their eyes. Consider how reading Moby Dick pulls you toward Captain Ahab's obsessive intensity, then pushes you back, then draws you toward Ishmael's openness, then unsettles you again. The place you end up — caught between those attractions and repulsions — isn't something any argument could have delivered. It's a shift in where you stand.

Literature that honestly faces the loss of meaning in modern life — works like Heart of Darkness or The Plague — doesn't just diagnose the problem. It opens a door, calling for new creative work that can help people rediscover a sense of the sacred. This kind of art is itself a form of meaning-making, a participatory act that no system of propositions can replace.

## Adept

The argument here cuts to a fundamental limitation of propositional persuasion: arguments and evidence, however rigorous, cannot by themselves produce metanoia — the genuine reorientation of a person's entire way of being. What catalyzes such transformation is confrontation with embodied exemplars, people living lives one finds genuinely attractive. This is not mere rhetoric; it points to a mode of knowing that is participatory rather than spectatorial, closer to what Polanyi called indwelling than to what analytic philosophy calls justification.

Art, broadly construed, becomes indispensable precisely because it enables this participatory knowing. Literature in particular allows a reader to inhabit a character's stance toward reality — not merely to understand it propositionally but to feel its pull and its limits. Melville's Moby Dick exemplifies this through its massive network of allusion and ambiguity: the reader is drawn toward Ahab's monomaniacal intensity, then repelled; drawn toward Ishmael's reflective openness, then unsettled. The cognitive space one occupies when pulling back from both characters is not equivalent to any argument. It is closer to what Gadamer describes as heightened awareness of one's own standpoint — one's fundamental indexicality, the irreducible situatedness of perspective.

This insight reframes the meaning crisis as partly a creative challenge. Works that honestly confront the collapse of meaning — Conrad's Heart of Darkness, Mann's Death in Venice, Camus's The Plague — function as calls for a corresponding literature of the sacred, art capable of awakening rather than merely diagnosing. Such creative endeavor is itself a form of participation in meaning-making that no propositional system can fully replace, opening people not only to philosophy and science but to transformative engagement with reality.

## Magus

The claim advanced here is that propositional adequacy — the marshaling of valid arguments and compelling evidence — is necessary but structurally insufficient for metanoia, understood not as mere opinion change but as the radical reorientation of the participatory framework through which a person discloses reality. What precipitates such turning is encounter with enacted exemplarity: persons whose mode of existence exerts what might be called nonpropositional persuasion, an attraction that reorganizes salience landscapes prior to and beneath explicit reasoning. This locates the transformative locus not in the logical space of reasons but in the existential space of indwelling, in Polanyi's sense.

Art functions as the privileged medium for this encounter precisely because it enables perspectival inhabitation without propositional collapse. Melville's Moby Dick operates through a dense network of allusion and structural ambiguity that positions the reader in oscillating identification — drawn toward Ahab's totalizing will, then toward Ishmael's hermeneutic openness, then repelled from both. The reflective space generated by this double withdrawal is not reducible to any argument; it instantiates something closer to Gadamer's wirkungsgeschichtliches Bewußtsein, a heightened awareness of one's own fundamental indexicality — the irreducible situatedness that conditions all understanding. The reader undergoes, rather than merely cognizes, a shift in horizon.

This framework issues a generative challenge. If literature confronting the advent of the meaning crisis — Conrad, Mann, Camus — diagnoses the withdrawal of participatory meaning, then what is called for is a corresponding literature of the sacred's return: art capable not merely of representing but of enacting the re-enchantment of the real. Such creative work constitutes genuine participation in the project of meaning-making, a form of poiesis that no propositional system can subsume, and that opens the possibility space within which philosophy and science themselves become transformatively operative.
