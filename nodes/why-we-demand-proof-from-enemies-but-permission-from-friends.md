# Why We Demand Proof From Enemies But Permission From Friends

The question precedes the evidence.

---

> People apply different evidentiary standards depending on whether they want a claim to be true. For welcome beliefs, we ask 'Can I believe it?' and stop at the first supporting reason. For unwelcome ones, we ask 'Must I believe it?' — and the answer is almost always no.

There is a simple but profound asymmetry in how human beings evaluate evidence depending on whether they want a claim to be true or false. When we want to believe something, we ask: 'Can I believe it?' — meaning, can I find even one piece of evidence that gives me permission to hold this view? If we can, we stop. But when we do not want to believe something, we ask: 'Must I believe it?' — and the answer is almost always no, because in politics and complex empirical domains, there is almost never a single piece of evidence so airtight that it compels belief with no room for doubt. This asymmetry, identified by the psychologist Tom Gilovich, explains why intelligent, well-intentioned people can look at the same facts and reach radically different conclusions. It is not primarily a failure of intelligence. It is a failure of motivation. The question we ask of evidence is determined before we look at it, by which side of the conclusion we are already on. The practical implication is humbling: the antidote is not better arguments directed at the other side. It is the discipline of turning the question around and asking where your own side is doing exactly the same thing.

## Apprentice

When someone encounters a claim they want to be true, they tend to ask a very lenient question: 'Can I believe this?' If they can find even one reason to say yes, they accept it and move on. But when they encounter a claim they don't want to be true, they ask a much stricter question: 'Must I believe this?' Since almost nothing in the real world comes with absolutely undeniable proof, the answer is nearly always no — and so they reject it.

This asymmetry, identified by psychologist Tom Gilovich, explains something that otherwise seems baffling: how smart, honest people can look at the same set of facts and walk away with completely opposite conclusions. The disagreement isn't really about intelligence or access to information. It's about which question each person is asking — and that question is set by what they already want to be true before they even look at the evidence.

The uncomfortable implication is that arguing harder rarely changes minds, because the other side is simply applying a different standard of proof. The more productive move is to turn the lens inward and ask where you yourself might be granting easy passage to beliefs you find comforting while demanding impossible proof for beliefs you find threatening.

## Adept

Psychologist Tom Gilovich identified a deceptively simple asymmetry in motivated reasoning: when a conclusion is desired, people ask 'Can I believe it?' — a threshold so low that a single piece of supportive evidence suffices. When a conclusion is unwanted, the question shifts to 'Must I believe it?' — a threshold so high that it is almost never met in complex empirical or political domains, where evidence is inherently ambiguous and contestable. The asymmetry is not in the evidence itself but in the interrogative frame applied to it, and that frame is set by prior motivation, not by rational assessment.

This distinction cuts deeper than the familiar observation that people are biased. It specifies the mechanism: differential evidential standards operating beneath conscious awareness. It explains why polarization persists among educated, well-informed populations — greater informational access simply provides more raw material for both the permissive and the restrictive question. Sophistication in reasoning can actually amplify the asymmetry, because skilled reasoners are better at constructing justifications in whichever direction their motivation demands.

The practical consequence is a reorientation of epistemic effort. Directing better arguments at opponents is largely futile when those arguments will be processed under the 'Must I believe it?' standard. The more tractable intervention is reflexive: identifying where one's own side is asking the lenient question and deliberately imposing the stricter one. This is not a call for radical skepticism but for symmetrical scrutiny — the discipline of applying the same evidentiary standard regardless of whether the conclusion is welcome or threatening.

## Magus

Tom Gilovich's 'Can I vs. Must I' framework isolates the precise locus of asymmetry in motivated cognition: not the weighting of evidence per se, but the meta-cognitive question that determines what counts as sufficient evidence. The 'Can I believe it?' heuristic terminates search upon encountering the first confirming datum — a satisficing strategy in the service of directional goals. The 'Must I believe it?' heuristic demands something approaching logical compulsion, a standard that complex, probabilistic domains structurally cannot satisfy. The asymmetry is therefore not a contingent bias but a near-guaranteed generator of divergent conclusions whenever prior commitments differ, even holding informational exposure constant.

This mechanism interacts perniciously with what Kahan has termed 'identity-protective cognition' and what Mercier and Sperber describe as the argumentative function of reasoning. Higher cognitive ability does not attenuate the effect; it amplifies it, because the capacity to generate and evaluate arguments is deployed asymmetrically in service of the motivationally determined question. The result is that epistemic communities can become increasingly sophisticated and increasingly wrong simultaneously — a phenomenon visible in expert polarization across ideologically charged empirical questions.

The normative implication resists the standard prescriptions of the rationalist tradition. Neither better evidence nor better argumentation addresses the root cause if the interrogative frame remains unchallenged. Gilovich's insight suggests that the critical epistemic virtue is not open-mindedness in the abstract but the specific discipline of inverting one's own default question — subjecting welcomed beliefs to the 'Must I?' standard and unwelcome beliefs to the 'Can I?' standard. This is a form of adversarial self-collaboration, deliberately internalizing the opponent's evidentiary posture before it is externally imposed.
