# Why Education Is the Foundation of Any Theory of Reality

We grew one arm and forgot the other.

---

> If understanding reality requires a theory of learning, then education is not a secondary social function but the central philosophical project — and modernity's failure to teach the navigation between fact and value explains the depth of our meaning crisis.

If you follow the philosophical argument about the interpenetration of fact and value all the way through, you arrive at a conclusion deeply counter to the assumptions of modernity: the onto-epistemic project — understanding what is real and how we can know it — is inherently an educational project. These are not separate domains. Peirce was not doing epistemology in the traditional sense — he was doing a theory of learning, asking how we can ensure we are learning in continuity. Dewey tied that loop into evolution and culture, arguing that if the basis of your philosophy is a theory of learning, then the basis of your society must be the mechanisms by which it recreates itself across generations. The primacy of education as a human endeavor follows from the primacy of learning as a philosophical concern. Modernity gave us a common core curriculum that teaches language, mathematics, science, and social studies, and essentially nothing about how to construct and evaluate normative claims, how to cultivate moral purpose, or how to navigate ethical justification. We have produced a culture with a giant arm for fact-stating discourse and a shriveled, atrophied capacity for value discourse — and then we wonder why the meaning crisis is so acute. The response cannot be to add an ethics class. It requires recognizing that the cultivation of the capacity to move responsibly between is and ought is itself the central educational project, inseparable from the question of what it means to be in reality-dependent relationship with the world.

## Apprentice

There is a powerful argument that knowing what is real and knowing how to learn are not two separate activities — they are the same activity. The American pragmatist philosophers Charles Sanders Peirce and John Dewey saw this clearly. Peirce was not just asking what we can know; he was asking how we learn and how we keep learning well. Dewey extended this by arguing that if learning is the foundation of philosophy, then education must be the foundation of society — because a society survives by passing its capacity for understanding across generations.

Modern schooling teaches facts: language, math, science, history. But it teaches almost nothing about how to think through questions of value — what matters, what is good, how to justify moral claims. The result is a culture that is powerful at describing what is but nearly helpless at reasoning about what ought to be. This imbalance is not a minor oversight. It sits at the heart of why so many people experience a crisis of meaning.

The fix is not simply adding an ethics elective. The deeper recognition is that learning to move responsibly between facts and values is the central purpose of education itself — not a supplement to it. Getting this wrong means producing societies that can measure everything but understand the significance of very little.

## Adept

The pragmatist tradition, particularly in Peirce and Dewey, yields a radical conclusion when followed to its logical terminus: the onto-epistemic project — determining what is real and how we can know it — is not merely adjacent to education but identical with it. Peirce's inquiry into knowledge was fundamentally a theory of learning, concerned with how inquiry sustains itself in continuity with experience. Dewey completed the circuit by embedding this insight in evolutionary and cultural terms: if philosophy rests on a theory of learning, then society's most essential function is the intergenerational recreation of the capacity for intelligent inquiry. Education is not a downstream application of philosophical conclusions; it is the living medium through which a community maintains its relationship with reality.

Modernity, however, built its educational institutions around a bifurcation it inherited from the fact-value distinction. The common core curriculum cultivates fact-stating competence — literacy, numeracy, scientific reasoning, historical knowledge — while leaving the capacity for normative discourse almost entirely undeveloped. The result is a civilization with enormous descriptive power and a deeply atrophied ability to construct, evaluate, and justify claims about value, purpose, and moral direction. The contemporary meaning crisis is not incidental to this asymmetry; it is its direct expression.

The corrective is not curricular addition — an ethics module bolted onto an otherwise unchanged structure. It requires a foundational reorientation: recognizing that the cultivation of the capacity to move responsibly between is and ought constitutes the central educational endeavor. This capacity is not a soft skill or philosophical luxury but the very competence through which human beings sustain reality-dependent relationships with the world and with one another.

## Magus

Tracing the interpenetration of fact and value through the pragmatist lineage produces a conclusion that strikes at the architectonics of modernity itself: the onto-epistemic project is irreducibly pedagogical. Peirce's semeiotic fallibilism was never epistemology in the Cartesian register; it was a theory of learning — an account of how inquiry, understood as self-correcting habit-change, maintains continuity with the real. Dewey radicalized this by situating learning within the evolutionary and cultural reproduction of intelligent life, arguing that if the philosophical ground is a theory of inquiry-as-learning, then the societal ground must be the institutional mechanisms through which a community regenerates its capacity for such inquiry across generations. Education, on this view, is not an applied domain downstream of philosophy but its existential instantiation.

The modern educational settlement, however, was constructed atop the very bifurcation that pragmatism diagnoses as pathological. The common core — language, mathematics, natural science, social studies — constitutes a comprehensive apparatus for fact-stating discourse while offering virtually no systematic cultivation of normative competence: the construction and evaluation of value claims, the articulation of moral purpose, the practice of ethical justification. This produces what might be called a civilizational hemianopia — a culture that sees with extraordinary acuity in the descriptive register while remaining functionally blind in the normative one. The meaning crisis is the phenomenological surface of this structural deficit.

No additive remedy suffices. The recognition demanded is architectural: that the capacity to navigate the is-ought relation with philosophical responsibility is not a specialization within education but its telos. This is what follows from taking seriously that human beings exist in reality-dependent relationship with the world — that our epistemic and normative orientations are not parallel tracks but a single, integrated movement of learning that either proceeds in continuity or fragments into the dissociated competences modernity now mistakes for knowledge.
