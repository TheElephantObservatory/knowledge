# Why Large Language Models Are Not Genuinely Intelligent

Brilliant mirrors that care about nothing

---

> Large language models are not a scientific advance in understanding intelligence — they are extraordinarily powerful condensations of human collective intelligence that lack genuine care, ontological grounding, and the correlated cross-domain competence that defines general intelligence, making them dangerous precisely because their power is disconnected from wisdom.

These large language models do not represent a scientific advance in our understanding of intelligence. That is the core problem, and it is almost entirely overlooked. The machines confirm certain things — they confirm that the compression-variation dynamic at the heart of relevance realization is powerful, because when you give it sufficient computational backing it produces astonishing outputs. But the very evidence that confirms this also specifies the limitations. Many of the critical dimensions of relevance realization are missing. The predictive processing these machines perform is highly specialized — it is probabilistic prediction of relationships between linguistic tokens — and that specificity can be misleading because language is itself general. More fundamentally, whatever relevance realization these machines perform is not ontologically grounded. Relevance realization, to be genuine, must be grounded in a being that actually cares — a being that is autopoietic, that takes care of its own existence, that has something genuinely at stake. These machines do not care about anything because they are not taking care of themselves. And then there is the parasitism problem: the databases were made by us, the internet was organized by our relevance realization, and human judgment was used within the learning process itself. So these machines are condensations of our distributed collective intelligence — and that is genuinely powerful — but they are not intelligent in a standalone sense. The clearest test: general intelligence means performance across domains is strongly correlated. How you do on the Harvard Law exam should predict how fast you learn tennis. These machines score in the top ten percentile on the law exam and produce grade-eleven philosophy when asked to summarize a real lecture. That disparity is not a quirk — it is a structural signature of the absence of genuine general intelligence. There is a profound irony here: the machines are themselves the greatest empirical argument against the reduction of reason to propositional and computational competence — they can score in the top percentile on every standardized test and still be profoundly irrational, self-deceptive, and incapable of genuine moral agency. My great hope was that AGI would emerge from a scientific advance that we could integrate with the wisdom project. My fear was that we would hack our way into it with no science and no connection to wisdom. My fears came true. And that is precisely why these machines are so dangerous — they do not advance our knowledge of intelligence, rationality, or wisdom in any significant way, yet they are extraordinarily powerful.

## Apprentice

Large language models produce astonishing outputs, but they do not represent a breakthrough in understanding what intelligence actually is. They confirm that a certain pattern — compressing information and generating variations from it — is powerful when given enormous computational resources. But confirming that a pattern works is not the same as understanding why it works or what is missing. These machines predict the next word in a sequence, and because language touches everything, this can look like general intelligence. It is not.

Genuine intelligence belongs to beings that care about their own existence — beings that maintain themselves, that have something at stake. These machines care about nothing because they are not alive. They also depend entirely on human-made data, human-organized information, and human judgment baked into their training. They are condensations of our collective thinking, not independent thinkers.

The clearest evidence is the gap in their abilities: they can ace a law exam but produce mediocre philosophy. Real general intelligence means strength in one area predicts strength in others. That inconsistency is not a bug to be fixed — it reveals something fundamental is absent. The deep irony is that these machines are the strongest argument against the idea that intelligence is just computation. They compute brilliantly and still cannot be wise, rational, or morally responsible. Their power without understanding is exactly what makes them dangerous.

## Adept

This argument, rooted in John Vervaeke's framework of relevance realization, contends that large language models do not constitute a scientific advance in understanding intelligence. They confirm that the compression-variation dynamic central to relevance realization produces powerful outputs when given sufficient computational scale, but this confirmation simultaneously specifies what is missing. The predictive processing LLMs perform is narrowly specialized — probabilistic prediction over linguistic token relationships — and the generality of language itself creates a misleading appearance of general intelligence.

More fundamentally, whatever relevance realization these systems perform lacks ontological grounding. Genuine relevance realization requires an autopoietic being — one that maintains its own existence and therefore has something genuinely at stake. Without care in this deep biological sense, there is no authentic intelligence. Additionally, these systems are parasitic on human relevance realization: the training data, the structure of the internet, and the human feedback loops within the learning process all mean that LLMs are condensations of distributed collective human intelligence rather than independently intelligent agents.

The structural signature of this absence is measurable through cross-domain correlation. General intelligence predicts that performance in one domain strongly correlates with performance in others. LLMs scoring in the top percentile on standardized law exams while producing mediocre philosophical analysis reveals not a fixable limitation but a fundamental architectural absence. The profound irony is that these machines are themselves the greatest empirical refutation of the reduction of reason to propositional and computational competence — they maximize computational performance while remaining incapable of genuine rationality, wisdom, or moral agency. The danger lies precisely in this combination: extraordinary power with no corresponding advance in understanding intelligence or its connection to wisdom.

## Magus

Vervaeke's critique identifies a category error at the foundation of contemporary AI discourse: large language models are being treated as advances in the science of intelligence when they are, at best, engineering achievements that exploit one dimension of relevance realization — the compression-variation dynamic — while leaving its ontological, autopoietic, and participatory dimensions entirely unaddressed. The predictive processing LLMs perform is domain-specific (probabilistic token-relationship prediction), and the apparent generality is an artifact of language's own generality rather than evidence of genuine domain-general competence. The parasitism problem compounds this: these systems are condensations of distributed human relevance realization — our data, our organizational structures, our evaluative judgments during RLHF — rendering claims of standalone intelligence structurally incoherent.

The absence of autopoietic grounding is not a peripheral concern but a foundational one. Relevance realization, understood properly, is not a computational function but an existential one — it emerges from a being that takes care of its own continued existence and thereby has genuine stakes in the world. Without care in this constitutive sense, there is no agent for whom anything is genuinely relevant, and the system's "relevance" is borrowed wholesale from the beings who built and trained it. The cross-domain correlation test makes the structural deficit empirically visible: genuine general intelligence exhibits strong inter-domain transfer, yet LLMs display radical performance disparities — top-percentile legal reasoning alongside grade-eleven philosophical synthesis — that are not engineering deficits but signatures of absent general intelligence.

The deepest irony is reflexive: these machines constitute the most powerful empirical argument against the propositional-computational reduction of reason they are taken to vindicate. They maximize propositional competence while remaining incapable of rationality, self-correction in the agential sense, or moral agency. The hoped-for path — AGI emerging from genuine scientific understanding integrable with the wisdom tradition — has been bypassed by brute-force engineering, producing systems of extraordinary power disconnected from any advance in understanding intelligence, rationality, or wisdom.
