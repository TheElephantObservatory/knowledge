# How Communication Infrastructure Shapes Human Subjects, Not Just Messages

You are not watching diversity. You are in the room.

---

> The technologies through which people actually encounter each other shape who they become more powerfully than economic conditions do. Garner argues that only social coordination mechanisms enabling genuine, unscripted contact with difference at scale can structurally address exclusion.

The medium condition — the technological and communicative infrastructure through which people encounter one another — is, in Garner's view, more historically determinative than the material condition emphasized by Marxist and Frankfurt School analysis. Walter Benjamin and Marshall McLuhan gesture toward this, but the full implication is underappreciated: the medium does not merely transmit subjectivity, it forms it. The printing press made the scientific treatise possible not by carrying scientific ideas but by restructuring how minds could hold and relate to information. The current social coordination mechanism — the combination of video conferencing, recording, asynchronous sharing, and global accessibility — is doing something analogous for the problem of global pluralism. It is, for the first time, making it logistically possible for people to encounter radical difference without the economic or geographic barriers that previously mediated or prevented such encounters. Crucially, it allows people to participate in global pluralism rather than merely being told they should. You are not watching a television program about diversity; you are in a room with people who are genuinely different from you, speaking, being heard, being surprised. That participatory experience gradually forms a different kind of subject — one who has actually practiced encountering otherness rather than one who has been instructed to value it. This is why Garner argues that racism, xenophobia, and other structural exclusions cannot be addressed primarily through information or moral argument. If the problem is structural, the solution must be structural — and the only new structure adequate to the problem is a social coordination mechanism that brings people into genuine, unscripted encounter with difference at scale.

## Apprentice

Most theories of social change focus on economics — who owns what, who controls the means of production. Garner's insight shifts the emphasis: what matters most is how people actually meet and communicate with each other. The printing press didn't just carry new ideas; it changed how human minds could organize and relate to information, making modern science possible. The tools we use to connect reshape who we are.

Today, the combination of video conferencing, recording, and global sharing is doing something similar for the problem of human difference. For the first time, it's logistically possible for people around the world to sit in a room together — not a metaphorical room, but a real conversational space — and encounter others who are genuinely unlike them. This is fundamentally different from watching a documentary about diversity or reading an argument for tolerance.

This distinction matters because Garner argues that prejudice and exclusion aren't primarily problems of bad information or weak moral reasoning. They're structural problems — built into the ways societies have historically organized human contact. If the problem is structural, the solution has to be structural too. The answer isn't better arguments for inclusion; it's building systems where people actually practice encountering otherness, repeatedly and at scale, until that practice gradually forms a different kind of person.

## Adept

Garner advances a provocation with deep implications for critical theory: the medium condition — the technological and communicative infrastructure through which people encounter one another — is more historically determinative than the material condition foregrounded by Marxist and Frankfurt School analysis. Walter Benjamin's work on mechanical reproduction and Marshall McLuhan's media ecology gesture toward this claim, but neither follows it to its full conclusion. The medium does not merely transmit subjectivity; it constitutes it. The printing press did not carry scientific ideas to waiting minds — it restructured cognition itself, making the sustained, cumulative reasoning of the scientific treatise possible for the first time.

Applied to the present, this framework reframes the problem of global pluralism. The current social coordination mechanism — video conferencing, recording, asynchronous distribution, global accessibility — is making it logistically possible for people to encounter radical difference without the economic and geographic barriers that previously mediated or foreclosed such encounters. Crucially, this is participatory rather than representational. One is not consuming a narrative about diversity but inhabiting a space of genuine, unscripted encounter with otherness. That experiential difference is formative: it produces subjects who have practiced navigating difference, not merely subjects who have been instructed to value it.

This is why Garner insists that racism, xenophobia, and structural exclusion cannot be adequately addressed through information campaigns or moral argumentation. If the exclusion is structural — embedded in how societies organize human contact — then the intervention must also be structural. The only new structure adequate to the scale of the problem is a coordination mechanism that brings people into authentic encounter with difference repeatedly, at global scale, allowing participation to do what persuasion cannot.

## Magus

Garner's thesis constitutes a significant intervention in the lineage running from Marx through Benjamin to McLuhan: the medium condition — the sociotechnical infrastructure of human encounter — is more historically determinative than the material condition that anchors orthodox and Western Marxist analysis alike. Benjamin's insight into the destruction of aura through mechanical reproduction and McLuhan's dictum that the medium is the message both approach this claim asymptotically, yet neither fully displaces the material base as the primary analytic category. Garner does. The printing press did not disseminate pre-existing scientific rationality; it reconstituted the cognitive architecture within which sustained propositional reasoning became possible. Medium is not vehicle but matrix — the condition of possibility for particular forms of subjectivity.

The contemporary social coordination mechanism — the convergence of synchronous video, recording, asynchronous redistribution, and global accessibility — represents, on this account, an analogous epistemic rupture for the problem of pluralism. What distinguishes it from prior media ecologies is its participatory structure: subjects are not interpellated as spectators of represented difference but positioned as interlocutors within unscripted encounters with genuine otherness. The phenomenological distinction is decisive. Spectatorship produces subjects who cognize diversity; participation produces subjects who have undergone the disorientation and recalibration that actual encounter with alterity demands.

This grounds Garner's structural critique of liberal anti-racism and cosmopolitan moral philosophy. If exclusion is infrastructural — sedimented in the historically available mechanisms of social coordination — then neither propositional correction nor normative exhortation can be adequate responses. The intervention must operate at the same level as the pathology: it must restructure the conditions of encounter itself. Only a coordination mechanism capable of generating genuine, repeated, large-scale contact with difference addresses the problem where it actually lives — not in belief but in the formed habits of subjectivity.
