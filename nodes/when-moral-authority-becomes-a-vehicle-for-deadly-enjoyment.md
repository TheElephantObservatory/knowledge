# When Moral Authority Becomes a Vehicle for Deadly Enjoyment

The part of you that loves the whip.

---

> Moral authority can be secretly colonized by the very destructive enjoyment it claims to forbid. Political and personal structures are often unified not by their stated values but by a perverse, unacknowledged pleasure in hatred, suffering, and self-punishment operating from both sides of any conflict simultaneously.

One of the most important and underappreciated political insights in psychoanalytic thought is that the superego can become an agent of the id — that moral authority can be colonized by deadly enjoyment. The standard early model of psychoanalysis presented the id as instinct and the superego as moral constraint, as if these were simply opposed. But what Lacanian and post-Lacanian thought reveals is that the superego can moralize the very enjoyment it appears to prohibit. Fanatical groups that commit genocidal violence and experience their superego as sanctioning that violence are the extreme case, but the structure is pervasive. A Christian family that hates the Muslim other but also loves to hate the Muslim other — that gets a perverse enjoyment from the existence of the enemy — is held together by that enjoyment. The political totality is unified not by its stated values but by the excess that cannot be spoken, the enjoyment that would be humiliating to acknowledge. This is what Isabel Millar calls patho-politics: the politics of suffering and enjoyment, where the things we enjoy and the things we suffer are more entangled than we are willing to think. It moves us beyond liberal hedonism — the idea that we simply enjoy our individual comforts — toward a recognition that our political structures are organized around a much more paradoxical economy of pleasure and pain. And crucially, this means that in any internal struggle — political or personal — we are usually identifying with only one half of the dynamic. We say 'don't beat yourself up' while identifying with the half being beaten, without recognizing that some part of us is taking pleasure in the beating. To understand the structure, you have to see the perverse enjoyment operating from both ends simultaneously.

## Apprentice

Standard thinking assumes that our moral conscience simply opposes our darker impulses — that the part of us enforcing rules is fighting the part that wants to break them. But one of the most important insights from psychoanalytic thought is that our inner moral authority can actually become a vehicle for the very cruelty and destructive pleasure it claims to prohibit. The voice that says "you should be ashamed" may itself be enjoying the shaming.

This plays out politically in striking ways. A community that defines itself against an enemy — hating the outsider in the name of righteousness — often draws a hidden pleasure from the existence of that enemy. The group is held together not by its stated values but by the thrill of opposition it cannot admit to. Isabel Millar calls this "patho-politics": the recognition that our political lives are organized around tangled economies of pleasure and suffering far more paradoxical than simple self-interest.

The same structure operates personally. When someone says "stop beating yourself up," they address the part being beaten — but ignore that another part is doing the beating and taking satisfaction in it. To understand any internal or political struggle, you have to see the hidden enjoyment operating on both sides at once. The conflict itself is the source of a pleasure no one wants to name.

## Adept

Classical psychoanalytic models positioned the id and superego as fundamentally opposed — instinct versus moral constraint. But Lacanian and post-Lacanian thought exposes a far more disturbing structural possibility: the superego can become an agent of the id, moralizing the very enjoyment it ostensibly prohibits. Moral authority does not merely fail to contain destructive drives; it can be colonized by them, lending the full weight of righteousness to what is essentially deadly jouissance. Fanatical groups that experience their superego as sanctioning genocidal violence represent the extreme case, but the structure is pervasive wherever communities organize around the unacknowledged pleasure of enmity.

Isabel Millar's concept of patho-politics names this entanglement precisely: political formations are unified not by their explicit ideological commitments but by the excess enjoyment that cannot be spoken — the jouissance that would be humiliating to acknowledge. A community that hates its designated other also loves to hate, and it is this perverse enjoyment, not the stated values, that provides the libidinal glue of the political totality. This moves analysis decisively beyond liberal hedonism's assumption that subjects simply pursue individual comfort, toward recognition of a paradoxical economy where pleasure and suffering are structurally inseparable.

The insight carries an important reflexive implication. In any conflict — political or intrapsychic — subjects typically identify with only one position in the dynamic. The injunction "don't beat yourself up" addresses the subject as victim of self-punishment while leaving invisible the part that takes pleasure in administering it. To grasp the full structure requires seeing perverse enjoyment operating simultaneously from both ends, which is precisely what makes it so resistant to conscious acknowledgment.

## Magus

The proposition that the superego can function as an agent of the id represents one of the most consequential and underappreciated structural insights in psychoanalytic political theory. Where the early topography positioned Über-Ich and Es in straightforward opposition — moral constraint against instinctual pressure — Lacanian reformulation reveals their potential complicity: the superego does not merely fail to contain jouissance but can become its privileged vehicle, lending moral sanction to precisely the deadly enjoyment it appears to prohibit. The obscene supplement is not external to the law but generated by its very operation, such that the imperative "Enjoy!" speaks from within the structure of prohibition itself.

Isabel Millar's concept of patho-politics theorizes this entanglement at the level of political formation. The libidinal coherence of a political totality resides not in its explicit ideological articulation but in the surplus enjoyment that remains structurally unspeakable — the jouissance of enmity that would be humiliating to avow. The community that defines itself through righteous opposition to its designated other is held together by the pleasure extracted from that opposition, a pleasure that operates as the Real kernel around which symbolic identification crystallizes. This displaces liberal hedonism's impoverished account of political motivation, substituting a paradoxical economy in which suffering and enjoyment are not opposed but mutually constitutive — the Freudian insight into masochism extended to the socius.

Crucially, this structure is self-occluding through partial identification. The subject positioned within an intrapsychic or political conflict invariably identifies with one pole of the dynamic — typically the position of the one beaten rather than the one beating — thereby rendering invisible the jouissance operating from the other end. Full structural apprehension demands recognizing perverse enjoyment as simultaneously distributed across both positions, which is precisely what the economy of disavowal is organized to prevent.
