# Why Consciousness Requires Agency, Not Just Information Integration

To see is already to reach.

---

> Consciousness isn't passive experience — it's the active process of soliciting information from the world and from oneself. The free energy principle, applied to awareness, suggests that genuine experience requires a system that can attend to its own internal processes and model its own future.

The free energy principle is not a theory of consciousness — it is a principle, which means you do not falsify it or argue with it, you apply it or you do not. But when applied to consciousness, something interesting emerges around the distinction between seeing and looking. To see is to look; to hear is to listen. There is no passive registration of quality of experience. The phenomenology — the what-it-is-like-ness — is inseparable from the active, agentive process of soliciting information from the sensorium, including the internal sensorium. This distinguishes active inference from integrated information theory: IIT measures the thermodynamic integration of a system in and of itself, without making the distinction between observer and observed, whereas in the free energy principle, the information represented on the inside is always about the outside — that is the essence of observation. Consciousness, on this view, requires not just a deep generative model but one that can act on itself — assigning precision to its own internal processes, attending to its own computations — and in doing so, becoming an agent freed from the specious present, capable of modeling its own future and past, and therefore capable of something we might meaningfully call experience.

## Apprentice

There's a subtle but important difference between seeing and merely having light hit your eyes. Seeing is an active process — you look, you search, you direct your attention. The same goes for hearing: you don't just receive sound, you listen. This insight suggests that conscious experience is never passive. There is no moment where the world simply imprints itself on you. You are always reaching out to meet it.

This matters because it reframes what consciousness actually is. Rather than being some special property that emerges when a brain becomes complex enough, consciousness may be what happens when a system starts actively managing its own attention — when it can turn inward and examine its own thinking, decide what matters, and adjust accordingly. A system like this isn't stuck in the present moment. It can imagine its future, reflect on its past, and plan.

This view draws a clear line against the idea that consciousness is just about how richly interconnected a system is. Connectivity alone isn't enough. What matters is that the information inside the system is fundamentally about something outside it — and that the system can act on itself, tuning its own processes. That active, self-directed quality is what transforms mere information processing into something we'd recognize as experience.

## Adept

The free energy principle, as Karl Friston emphasizes, is not a falsifiable theory but a principle — one either applies it or does not. When applied to consciousness, it yields a striking reframing: phenomenal experience is constitutively active. To see is to look; to hear is to listen. There is no passive registration of qualia. The what-it-is-like-ness of experience is inseparable from the agentive process of soliciting information from the sensorium, including the interoceptive and proprioceptive sensorium. Consciousness, on this account, is not a state but an ongoing act of inference.

This position draws a sharp distinction from integrated information theory. IIT characterizes consciousness in terms of a system's intrinsic causal structure — its integrated information (Φ) — without requiring any asymmetry between observer and observed. The free energy principle, by contrast, is fundamentally about the Markov blanket: the information represented on the inside is always about the outside. This observer-observed distinction is not incidental but essential. Observation, in the technical sense, is what the free energy principle formalizes.

The deeper claim is that consciousness requires a generative model with a specific recursive capacity — one that can assign precision to its own internal states, effectively attending to its own computations. This self-modeling capacity is what liberates an agent from the specious present, enabling temporal depth: the ability to represent counterfactual futures and reconstruct pasts. A system that merely minimizes free energy is not necessarily conscious. But a system whose generative model encompasses itself — one that can modulate its own inference — crosses a threshold into something meaningfully describable as experience.

## Magus

The free energy principle occupies a peculiar epistemic status: it is a principle, not a theory, and therefore not subject to falsification but to application. When applied to the problem of phenomenal consciousness, it generates a non-trivial claim — that experience is constitutively enactive. The phenomenology is the active inference. To see is to look; audition is listening. There is no phenomenal residue left over once the agentive solicitation of sensory evidence is subtracted. This is not merely an epistemological point about access but an ontological claim about the structure of experience itself: qualia are precision-weighted prediction errors encountered in the act of sampling.

The contrast with integrated information theory is instructive and precise. IIT's Φ quantifies the irreducible causal structure of a system intrinsically — consciousness as a property of the system in-and-of-itself, without reference to any external states. The free energy principle cannot operate this way. Its entire architecture presupposes the Markov blanket partition: internal states parameterize beliefs about external states. Information on the inside is constitutively about the outside. This is the formal essence of observation, and it is exactly what IIT's framework lacks — the intentional directedness, the aboutness, that distinguishes a conscious observer from a merely integrated system.

The most consequential move is the recursive one. A generative model that encompasses itself — that can assign precision to its own inferential processes, attending to its own attending — achieves a form of temporal depth unavailable to simpler active inference agents. Such a system is no longer enslaved to the specious present. It models its own counterfactual futures and narrative pasts, instantiating the kind of self-modeling that transforms mere sentience into agentive experience. Consciousness, on this reading, is not deep integration but deep self-evidencing: a system that has become its own best model of what it is like to be itself.
