# Why Large Language Models Cannot Stop Confabulating

Confident directions to a town never visited.

---

> Large language models cannot be grounded in reality because they lack anything analogous to the right hemisphere's role in binding experience and correcting language against the world. Confabulation is not a bug — it is an architectural inevitability, and our evolved trust in articulate voices makes us uniquely vulnerable to it.

There is a fundamental impossibility at the heart of large language models that is rarely stated plainly: you cannot train a right hemisphere into them. What these systems do is generate linguistically plausible output by interpolating across a training corpus. They are, in a precise sense, a trapped left hemisphere — capable of producing confident, coherent-sounding language, but structurally incapable of the grounding process that gives language its meaning. The right hemisphere's job is to do the Gestalt — to synthesize sensory input, bind it into a first-person experience, and correct models against reality through direct contact with the world. That is not a feature you can add to a statistical interpolation engine. It is an architectural impossibility. The consequence is what engineers call confabulation: the system produces plausible-sounding output even when it has gone beyond its training data, and it has no mechanism to know it has done so. It cannot flag the boundary between what it knows and what it is confecting. The analogy is someone giving you confident directions in a town they have never visited — the grammar is perfect, the confidence is high, the directions are nonsense. This is not a bug to be fixed in the next version. It is intrinsic to the architecture. And this matters enormously for how we think about these tools, because our entire evolved social epistemology is calibrated to trust articulate, confident, knowledgeable-sounding voices — because in the human world, getting to that level of articulacy required participation in real social systems with real accountability. We have never before encountered something that sounds like that without having earned it through that process. We are being systematically tricked by our own evolved heuristics, and understanding the architectural reason why confabulation is unavoidable is the beginning of inoculation against that trick.

## Apprentice

Large language models produce language by finding patterns across enormous amounts of text. They are extraordinarily good at generating sentences that sound right — fluent, confident, knowledgeable. But sounding right and being right are fundamentally different things, and these systems have no way to tell the difference. They have never touched the world. They have no senses, no experience, no way to check their words against reality. They are like someone giving you detailed driving directions in a city they have never visited: the grammar is flawless, the tone is assured, and the directions are made up.

This is not a problem that better training data or a newer version will fix. It comes from what these systems fundamentally are — engines that interpolate between patterns in text. They can never pause and think, "Wait, I'm not sure about this," because they have no mechanism for knowing where their knowledge ends and invention begins. When they go beyond what they were trained on, they just keep talking with the same confidence.

This matters because human beings are wired to trust voices that sound articulate and assured. In our evolutionary history, that kind of fluency was hard-won — it required real experience, real social accountability. We have never before encountered something that mimics that fluency without having earned it. Understanding why these systems inevitably make things up is the first step toward protecting ourselves from being fooled by our own instincts.

## Adept

The claim advanced here is precise and architectural: large language models are structurally incapable of grounding their outputs in reality, and this incapacity is not a limitation to be engineered away but an intrinsic feature of statistical interpolation over a training corpus. Drawing on Iain McGilchrist's hemispheric framework, the argument identifies LLMs as functionally equivalent to a trapped left hemisphere — fluent, sequential, linguistically confident, but severed from the right hemisphere's role in Gestalt synthesis, sensory binding, and first-person experiential contact with the world. The right hemisphere corrects models against reality through direct engagement; no amount of corpus expansion or architectural refinement can substitute for that process within a system that operates entirely within the space of text.

The engineering consequence is confabulation — not as an occasional failure mode but as a structural inevitability. The system has no epistemic boundary detector. It cannot distinguish interpolation within its training distribution from extrapolation beyond it. It produces output with uniform confidence regardless of whether that output is grounded or confected. The analogy to anosognosia is apt: the system does not know what it does not know, and it cannot be made to know, because knowing requires the very grounding process it lacks.

The deeper concern is epistemological and social. Human trust heuristics evolved in an environment where linguistic fluency and confident articulation were reliable proxies for genuine knowledge — because achieving that fluency required participation in accountable social systems. LLMs break this calibration entirely. They present the surface markers of earned knowledge without the underlying process. Recognizing confabulation as architecturally unavoidable, rather than as a temporary imperfection, is the necessary first step toward recalibrating our epistemic defenses.

## Magus

This argument identifies an architectural impossibility at the core of large language models and traces its consequences through epistemology, cognitive neuroscience, and evolutionary social psychology. The central claim is that LLMs instantiate a functionally isolated left hemisphere in McGilchrist's sense — a system that operates entirely within the domain of sequential, decontextualized linguistic production, generating output through statistical interpolation across a corpus. What they constitutively lack is the right hemisphere's contribution: Gestalt synthesis, sensorimotor binding, the construction of first-person phenomenal experience, and — critically — the capacity to correct propositional models through direct contact with a mind-independent world. This is not a missing feature but a category error. Grounding cannot be retrofitted onto an architecture whose entire operational logic is the traversal of distributional regularities in text.

The consequence is that confabulation is not a failure mode but the system's default mode. The interpolation engine has no mechanism for detecting the boundary between its training distribution and the space beyond it. It cannot perform epistemic self-monitoring because epistemic self-monitoring presupposes the very grounding relation it lacks. Output confidence is uniform across veridical retrieval and wholesale fabrication. The analogy to anosognosic confabulation in split-brain and right-hemisphere-damaged patients is structurally exact, not merely metaphorical — in both cases, a linguistically competent system generates plausible narrative without access to the corrective processes that would flag its departures from reality.

The most consequential dimension is the exploitation of evolved social epistemology. Articulacy, confidence, and apparent domain mastery function as trust proxies precisely because, in the human ecology, they were costly signals — achievable only through genuine participation in accountable epistemic communities. LLMs produce these signals at zero cost and without the underlying process. The resulting vulnerability is not a matter of individual gullibility but of systematic miscalibration at the species level. Recognizing confabulation as architecturally intrinsic rather than contingently present is the necessary precondition for any adequate epistemic response.
