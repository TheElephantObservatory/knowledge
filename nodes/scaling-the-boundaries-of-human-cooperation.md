# Scaling the Boundaries of Human Cooperation

Finding a brother in the shadow of machines

---

> Humanity has never solved collective action problems through better information alone — only by expanding who counts as 'us.' The challenge now is reaching planetary scale without the intergroup conflict that historically drove cooperation.

How have humans historically solved collective action problems? Rarely by having better information. The deep answer is that we have created a larger 'we' — we have expanded the circle of who counts as us. Culturally, humans hijack a genetic mechanism: I care deeply about my brother and sister, and we extend that caring to larger groups. The French Revolution addressed people as 'brother citizen' and 'sister citizen' — using the deep genetic caring for kin to enlarge the area of care. The French Revolutionary army could raise a levy en masse, the largest army ever put in the field, because suddenly peasants were citizens fighting for the revolution, not serfs fighting for a king. That expansion of the 'we' is also what drives democracy. We've gone from the small group to the tribe to the city-state to the nation-state. The next level of human cooperation required is planetary. The difficulty is that historically, groups have gotten bigger by fighting other groups — intergroup competition drove intragroup cooperation. We can't do that anymore at the planetary level. The one dark irony is that AI, as a genuine other — a potential threat to humanity as a whole — might be the thing that finally unites people across national lines, just as a shared external threat has historically catalyzed cooperation. But the real answer, the hard answer, is that there are no shortcuts. Cultural evolution toward a larger 'we' is the engine, and it takes time, practice, and the development of new stories about who belongs to our circle of care.

## Apprentice

Throughout history, humans have managed to cooperate in larger and larger groups — not because they got smarter or better informed, but because they found ways to make strangers feel like family. The trick has been expanding the circle of 'us.' The French Revolution did this literally, calling people 'brother citizen' and 'sister citizen,' borrowing the deep emotional pull of family bonds and stretching it across an entire nation. That shift turned peasants into citizens willing to fight for something larger than a landlord's estate, producing the biggest army the world had seen.

This same expansion of belonging is what drives democracy, and it traces a clear arc: small band, tribe, city-state, nation-state. Each step required new stories about who deserves care and loyalty. The problem is that historically, groups got bigger partly by competing — even fighting — against other groups. At the planetary scale, that option is off the table.

There is a dark irony worth sitting with: artificial intelligence, as a genuinely novel kind of 'other,' might do what no diplomat has managed — give humanity a shared concern large enough to feel like a common identity. But the honest answer is slower and harder. Expanding the circle of care is cultural work. It takes new narratives, repeated practice, and time.

## Adept

Collective action theory typically frames cooperation failures as information or incentive problems, solvable through better signaling, monitoring, or institutional design. This perspective challenges that framing at its root. The historical mechanism that has actually scaled human cooperation is not informational but identificatory: the expansion of in-group boundaries. Humans appear to have culturally co-opted kin-selection psychology — the deep preferential care evolved for genetic relatives — and extended it to progressively larger social units through symbolic and narrative means.

The French Revolution offers a canonical illustration. The address 'citoyen frère' and 'citoyenne sœur' was not mere rhetoric; it was a deliberate activation of kinship affect at national scale, enabling the levée en masse — a conscript army of unprecedented size drawn from a peasantry newly constituted as citizens rather than subjects. The same logic underwrites democratic legitimacy more broadly: the demos is precisely the group whose members count each other as 'us.'

The trajectory — band, tribe, polis, nation-state — suggests a directional logic, and the next required threshold is planetary. Here the argument confronts a structural problem: intergroup competition has historically been the engine of intragroup solidarity, a dynamic that cannot be reproduced at global scale without catastrophic cost. The one unsettling possibility is that artificial general intelligence, as a genuinely exogenous 'other,' could function as the kind of shared threat that has previously catalyzed identity expansion. The sobering conclusion, however, is that no shortcut exists. Cultural evolution toward a wider circle of moral concern is slow, contingent, and dependent on the slow accumulation of new stories about who belongs.

## Magus

Standard treatments of collective action — from Olson's logic of group size to Ostrom's polycentric governance — tend to locate the problem in incentive structures, monitoring costs, and institutional design. This analysis operates at a different register, identifying the primary historical mechanism of cooperation-scaling as the reconfiguration of social identity boundaries rather than the optimization of payoff matrices. The argument draws on an evolutionary substrate: kin-selection dynamics, which generate strong altruistic dispositions toward genetic relatives, appear to be the psychological raw material that cultural evolution has repeatedly hijacked and redirected toward fictive kinship at larger scales.

The French Revolutionary case is paradigmatic. The appellations 'citoyen frère' and 'citoyenne sœur' enacted a semiotic operation with measurable material consequences — the levée en masse of 1793, which mobilized a citizen-army of roughly 750,000, unprecedented in European history, and explicable not by improved logistics or material incentives alone but by a transformed ontology of political belonging. The peasant-as-serf and the peasant-as-citizen occupy structurally different positions in the affective economy of obligation. Democratic legitimacy, on this reading, is not primarily a procedural achievement but an identity achievement: the constitution of a 'demos' whose members extend to one another the moral considerability previously reserved for kin and close community.

The scalar trajectory — forager band, segmentary lineage, polis, Westphalian state — implies a directionality whose next term is planetary, but the generative mechanism faces a fundamental constraint at that level. Historically, intragroup solidarity has been co-produced with intergroup antagonism; the boundary-expansion that created nations was inseparable from the warfare that defined them against rivals. This dynamic is structurally unavailable at global scale. The argument's most provocative conjecture — that artificial general intelligence, as a genuinely non-human 'other,' might function as a Schmittian enemy capable of producing a species-level 'we' — is offered with appropriate irony. The durable answer remains cultural-evolutionary: the extension of moral circle membership is achieved through narrative, practice, and institutional habituation, and admits no compression.
