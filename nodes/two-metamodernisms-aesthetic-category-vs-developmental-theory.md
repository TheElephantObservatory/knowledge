# Two Metamodernisms: Aesthetic Category vs. Developmental Theory

You can't cash the check without signing it.

---

> Metamodernism functions as two very different things — a modest aesthetic label and an ambitious developmental theory — and the tension between them reveals that even the modest version was quietly smuggling in evolutionary assumptions it refused to own.

There's a genuinely important distinction between metamodernism as a cultural studies category — essentially an aesthetic periodization like Baroque or Neoclassicism — and metamodernism as a developmental theory of cultural evolution. The cultural studies version, originating with Vermeulen and van den Akker's 2009 paper, was doing something relatively modest: identifying a new sensibility in art and culture characterized by oscillation between irony and sincerity. Then the integral crowd — particularly Hanzi Freyja — essentially appropriated the term and gave it a much stronger developmental thrust, importing the full apparatus of stage theory, transcend-and-include logic, and hierarchical complexity. What's interesting is that the cultural studies people were already implicitly using developmental logic — the very idea that metamodernism comes after and through postmodernism presupposes some kind of directional movement — but they wanted to have it both ways: the mimetic charge of being 'what comes next' without the philosophical commitment to a developmental sequence. The honest move would have been to either fully commit to a theory of cultural change or to limit the term to aesthetic analysis.

## Apprentice

The word "metamodernism" gets used in two quite different ways, and the gap between them matters. One version comes from cultural studies scholars who noticed, around 2009, that artists and writers were doing something new — blending irony with genuine feeling, cycling between cynicism and hope. They gave this sensibility a name, much like we label other cultural periods.

But then a second group, particularly the thinker Hanzi Freyja and others from the integral theory world, took the same word and loaded it with a much bigger claim: that metamodernism represents a higher stage of cultural development, a step up from postmodernism in the way that postmodernism was a step up from modernism. This version comes with a whole framework of stages, growth, and increasing complexity.

The interesting wrinkle is that the first group wasn't being entirely honest about what they were doing. By calling something "meta" modernism and positioning it as what comes after postmodernism, they were already implying a direction — a sense that culture evolves and progresses. They wanted the excitement of naming "the next thing" without actually committing to a theory of cultural progress. The more intellectually consistent move would have been to either embrace the developmental logic or stick purely to describing an aesthetic style.

## Adept

A critical fault line runs through metamodernism discourse: the term operates simultaneously as a cultural studies periodization and as a developmental theory of cultural evolution, and these two projects carry vastly different intellectual commitments. The cultural studies version, originating in Vermeulen and van den Akker's 2010 paper in the Journal of Aesthetics and Culture, proposed something relatively contained — an emergent structure of feeling characterized by oscillation between modernist sincerity and postmodern irony. This was aesthetic taxonomy, akin to identifying Mannerism or Romanticism as a distinct sensibility.

The developmental version, advanced most forcefully by Hanzi Freyja and adjacent integral theorists, appropriated the term and imported the full apparatus of stage theory: transcend-and-include logic, hierarchical complexity, directional cultural evolution. Here metamodernism becomes not merely a sensibility but a position on a developmental sequence, with postmodernism as a necessary but surpassable predecessor.

The insight that makes this more than a terminological dispute is that the cultural studies version was already implicitly relying on developmental logic it refused to acknowledge. The prefix "meta-" and the explicit framing as what comes "after and through" postmodernism presupposes directionality — a sequence with normative implications. Vermeulen and van den Akker wanted the rhetorical force of naming the next cultural epoch without the philosophical burden of defending a theory of cultural progress. This amounts to having it both ways: claiming the mimetic energy of periodization while disavowing the developmental commitments that periodization entails. Intellectual honesty demanded either a full commitment to theorizing cultural change or a retreat to purely descriptive aesthetic analysis.

## Magus

The metamodernism discourse is riven by an unacknowledged equivocation between two incommensurable projects: aesthetic periodization within cultural studies and developmental stage theory within the integral-philosophical tradition. Vermeulen and van den Akker's foundational 2010 intervention performed a recognizable move in cultural taxonomy — identifying an emergent structure of feeling (in Raymond Williams's sense) defined by epistemic oscillation between modernist commitment and postmodern deconstruction. The claim was nominally descriptive and synchronic: a new sensibility had crystallized in contemporary art, architecture, and literature. Hanzi Freyja's subsequent appropriation of the term effected a categorical transformation, grafting onto it the full scaffolding of hierarchical complexity theory, Kegan-adjacent constructive-developmental psychology, and Wilberian transcend-and-include logic. Metamodernism thereby became not a cultural dominant but a position on a developmental sequence with normative directionality.

What renders this more than a dispute over intellectual property is the performative contradiction embedded in the cultural studies version itself. The very grammar of "metamodernism" — the prefix, the temporal positioning as post-postmodern, the explicit rhetoric of supersession — presupposes diachronic movement that is difficult to distinguish from developmental logic. Vermeulen and van den Akker's oscillation thesis already implies that the metamodern subject has metabolized postmodern skepticism and emerged with capacities unavailable at the prior cultural moment. This is stage-theoretic reasoning operating under aesthetic cover.

The cultural studies camp thus occupied an unstable position: claiming the mimetic surplus of epochal nomination — the considerable rhetorical power of declaring what comes next — while disavowing the teleological and developmental commitments such nomination entails. The honest resolution requires either embracing a defensible theory of directional cultural change or restricting the term to a purely synchronic aesthetic descriptor, forfeiting the sequential framing that gave it its intellectual charge.
