# Corporate Authority and the Loss of Ownership

The state as the manufacturer’s bailiff

---

> When you buy a device but the manufacturer controls what it can do, you haven't really bought it — you've rented it on their terms. Doctorow argues this isn't an inconvenience but a fundamental transfer of power, enforced by law.

The walled garden critique of platform technology is often framed as a debate about consumer choice or corporate monopoly — but Doctorow's deeper argument, developed through fiction and through his long-running public writing, is that it's actually a question about who controls the material conditions of your life. The bread toaster that only toasts authorized bread is a reductio ad absurdum, but it's not far from the actual logic of the iPhone ecosystem, where a device you own cannot run software its manufacturer hasn't approved and taxed. The DMCA and its equivalents make it a legal felony to circumvent these locks — meaning the state enforces the manufacturer's control over your property after you've purchased it. What makes this a profound structural problem rather than just an inconvenience is the asymmetry of power it encodes: the manufacturer retains perpetual authority over the device's behavior, the user retains only the right to use it within sanctioned parameters. Doctorow's fictional extrapolation — appliances that only accept authorized inputs, from bread to dishes — makes visible a trajectory that is already underway. The question isn't whether walled gardens are convenient or even whether they produce better user experiences in the short term. The question is whether the normalization of manufacturer-controlled devices represents a fundamental transfer of technological self-determination away from individuals and toward corporations, enforced by law. This is the utopian/dystopian axis that runs through all four novellas in Radicalized: technology as a tool of mastery versus technology as a tool of self-determination.

## Apprentice

When you buy a toaster, you can put any bread in it. But what if the toaster only worked with bread the manufacturer had approved — and it was actually illegal to modify it to accept other bread? That sounds absurd, but it's close to how smartphones and many modern devices actually work. You paid for the device, but the company that made it still controls what software can run on it, takes a cut of every transaction, and is protected by law if you try to work around those restrictions.

Cory Doctorow has spent years — through fiction and through public writing — arguing that this isn't just a consumer annoyance. It's a question of who controls the tools you depend on. The more of daily life runs through manufacturer-controlled devices, the more power shifts away from individuals and toward the corporations that make those devices. You have the right to use your property, but only within the boundaries they set.

Doctorow's fiction, including the novellas collected in Radicalized, uses exaggerated scenarios to make this trajectory visible. The point isn't that appliances will literally lock out unauthorized bread. The point is that the underlying logic — manufacturer retains control, user gets access — is already the norm, and normalizing it further has serious consequences for individual freedom and self-determination.

## Adept

The critique of platform enclosure is often reduced to antitrust concerns or debates about app store fees, but Doctorow's argument operates at a more fundamental level: the question of who holds sovereignty over a device after the point of sale. In the iPhone ecosystem, ownership is formally transferred to the consumer while effective control remains with the manufacturer. Apple determines which software may run, extracts rent from every transaction, and is legally shielded by provisions like the DMCA's anti-circumvention clause, which makes it a federal offense to bypass these restrictions. The consumer owns the hardware in a narrow property-law sense while being a tenant in the software environment.

This asymmetry is not incidental — it is the business model. And Doctorow's fictional extrapolations, most explicitly in Radicalized, use reductio ad absurdum scenarios (appliances that only accept authorized consumables) to defamiliarize an arrangement that has become invisible through normalization. The bread toaster that requires licensed bread is not a prediction; it is a clarifying mirror held up to existing practice.

What elevates this beyond standard tech criticism is the structural claim: that state enforcement of manufacturer control — through intellectual property law — represents a fundamental realignment of the relationship between individuals and their tools. The question is not whether walled gardens produce better user experiences, but whether the legal and technical architecture of platform technology encodes a permanent transfer of technological self-determination from users to corporations. This is the axis on which Doctorow's utopian and dystopian imaginaries turn: technology as an instrument of individual mastery versus technology as an instrument of corporate dominion.

## Magus

Doctorow's intervention into the political economy of platform technology is most precisely understood not as consumer advocacy but as a theory of post-purchase sovereignty — a critique of the legal and technical mechanisms by which manufacturers retain perpetual authority over devices after title has nominally passed to the consumer. The DMCA's anti-circumvention provisions, and their international equivalents under WIPO treaty obligations, function here not merely as intellectual property instruments but as a state-enforced extension of the manufacturer's contractual will into the domain of physical property. The result is a bifurcated ownership structure: the user holds the res, the manufacturer holds the usus and the abusus, to invoke the Roman law tripartite.

This structural condition is what Doctorow's fiction in Radicalized renders legible through satirical extrapolation. The locked appliance — the toaster that refuses unauthorized bread — operates as a Brechtian Verfremdungseffekt, defamiliarizing an arrangement so thoroughly normalized that its power-political dimensions have become phenomenologically invisible to most users. The satirical exaggeration does not distort the underlying logic; it clarifies it by removing the ameliorating friction of convenience and familiarity that ordinarily obscures the asymmetry.

The deeper theoretical claim is that platform enclosure represents a historically novel form of what might be called infrastructural capture: the progressive subordination of the material conditions of digital life to private governance regimes backed by public law. This is not reducible to monopoly in the classical antitrust sense, nor to simple rent extraction, though it encompasses both. It is, rather, a question about the locus of technological self-determination — whether the individual retains meaningful agency over the tools that increasingly mediate labor, communication, and domestic life, or whether that agency has been structurally transferred to corporate actors whose interests are systematically misaligned with those of users. Doctorow's utopian/dystopian axis is, finally, a question about the conditions of possibility for technological autonomy under late-platform capitalism.
