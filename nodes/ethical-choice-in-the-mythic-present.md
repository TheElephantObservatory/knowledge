# Ethical Choice in the Mythic Present

When heaven and earth collide in the imagination

---

> In times of radical upheaval, the boundary between myth and reality dissolves — both catastrophe and utopia suddenly feel possible, and ordinary life no longer tells you who you are. The choices you make in that charged interval carry unusual weight.

When we enter a liminal space — a space between worlds — something psychologically profound happens: heaven and earth collide in the imagination. Normally, when the world is just running, our fantasies stay safely contained in the realm of myth, in stories we watch on screens. But when the world shifts radically, those mythic possibilities flood into actual experience. The worst-case scenarios you thought you'd only ever see in a movie suddenly feel real. And simultaneously, utopian possibilities — things like stopping the entire capitalist world system — which seemed like pure fantasy, also suddenly feel real, because in a sense, they just happened. This creates what Zak Stein calls an 'imaginal potency': a dizzying sense of choice, because the usual grooves of ordinary life that tell you who you are and what you do have dissolved. Usually you don't have to ask whether you're a hero or a villain — you're just a person going about your day. But in the space between worlds, that question becomes live. The choices you make are more pronounced because you're at the beginning of a new fork in the road. This is what ancient traditions encoded in the concept of kairos — pregnant time, potent time — not happy time, not celebratory time, but time that is ethically charged, where what you do with each moment matters more than it ordinarily does. And this is not a novel human experience. The flood myth, for instance, is precisely about this: the moment when everything that divided people before becomes secondary because an overwhelming collective opening of the psyche toward fear, toward love, toward fundamental choice, arrives all at once.

## Apprentice

Most of the time, life runs on familiar tracks. You know roughly who you are, what you're supposed to do, and the dramatic scenarios you see in films stay safely on the screen. But when the world shifts dramatically — a pandemic, a revolution, a collapse — something strange happens to the imagination. The worst disasters you thought were fictional start to feel plausible. And at the same time, the wildest hopes — things that seemed completely impossible — suddenly feel within reach, because the ordinary machinery that made them impossible has just visibly broken down.

This psychological state has a name: Zak Stein calls it 'imaginal potency.' The usual grooves of daily life that quietly answer the question 'who am I?' have dissolved, and you're left facing that question raw. Are you someone who helps, or someone who hoards? A person who builds something new, or one who retreats? These questions are always there in ordinary life, but they're usually answered automatically. In a moment of upheaval, they become urgent and real.

Ancient traditions recognized this. The concept of kairos — from Greek thought — names exactly this kind of time: not happy time, but charged time, where what you do with each moment matters more than usual. The flood myths found across human cultures encode the same recognition: overwhelming collective crisis strips away the ordinary divisions between people and forces a fundamental reckoning with fear, love, and choice.

## Adept

Liminal space — the threshold condition between one stable social order and another — produces a distinctive alteration in the relationship between myth and lived experience. Under ordinary conditions, mythic narratives function as a kind of psychological container: apocalyptic fears and utopian longings circulate safely within the symbolic register of story and screen, quarantined from the texture of daily life. When the world undergoes radical disruption, that containment fails. The symbolic and the real collapse into one another, and scenarios previously experienced only as narrative — civilizational collapse, systemic transformation — acquire the felt quality of genuine possibility.

Zak Stein theorizes this condition as 'imaginal potency': the heightened sense of agency and openness that arises when the habitual structures of identity and routine — what we might call the sedimented scripts of ordinary selfhood — are suspended. The question of who one is and what one stands for, normally answered implicitly by the momentum of everyday life, becomes explicit and pressing. This is not merely disorientation; it is a genuine expansion of the ethical horizon, because the fork in the road is visible in a way it ordinarily is not.

This phenomenology maps precisely onto the ancient concept of kairos — time that is pregnant, potent, and ethically charged, as distinct from chronos, the neutral flow of sequential moments. The cross-cultural prevalence of flood mythology can be read through this lens: the flood is not primarily a story about water, but about the psychological and moral structure of collective liminality — the moment when overwhelming shared crisis dissolves prior social distinctions and opens a collective psyche toward its most fundamental possibilities.

## Magus

The liminal interval — Turner's betwixt-and-between, the threshold between dissolved and not-yet-constituted social structures — generates a specific phenomenological condition in which the ordinarily stable partition between the symbolic and the real undergoes collapse. In Lacanian terms, the imaginary and symbolic registers, which under normative conditions metabolize mythic content into narrative form safely removed from the real, lose their insulating function. The consequence is a flooding of lived experience with material that had been contained within the cultural unconscious: eschatological dread and utopian longing alike acquire ontological weight, presenting not as narrative possibility but as genuine imminence.

Zak Stein's concept of 'imaginal potency' names the subjective correlate of this structural condition. The habituated interpellations of ordinary social life — the Althusserian hailing that quietly constitutes the subject as a particular kind of agent with a particular kind of trajectory — are suspended. What remains is a condition of radical underdetermination with respect to self-constitution, experienced simultaneously as vertigo and as heightened ethical salience. The agent at the liminal threshold cannot rely on the sedimented scripts of quotidian selfhood to answer the question of who they are; that question presents with unusual phenomenological force, and the choices made within the interval carry a disproportionate weight in the constitution of whatever self and world will emerge on the other side.

This structure is precisely what the category of kairos encodes in classical Greek thought — and what the comparative mythology of the flood encodes across cultures. The flood narrative is not, at its core, an etiological account of geological event; it is a phenomenological and ethical cartography of collective liminality, mapping the moment when shared extremity dissolves the ordinarily operative social distinctions and precipitates a collective confrontation with the most fundamental axes of human possibility: annihilation or renewal, contraction or expansion, the closing or opening of the moral imagination.
