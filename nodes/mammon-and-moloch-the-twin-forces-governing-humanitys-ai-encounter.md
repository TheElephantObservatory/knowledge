# Mammon and Moloch: The Twin Forces Governing Humanity's AI Encounter

When the king no longer answers to God

---

> Two forces — Mammon (financialized capitalism's pursuit of pure return) and Moloch (the multipolar trap of geopolitical competition) — are jointly governing humanity's encounter with AI, producing a neo-feudalism stripped of any vertical moral order that might constrain power over the dispensable.

The two principalities driving humanity's encounter with AI are Mammon and Moloch. Mammon is what happens when the market becomes disconnected from a more fundamental ordering of values — it is the soul of late-stage financialized capitalism, the relentless algorithmic search for money-on-money return as the organizing principle of society. Moloch is what happens when the state becomes disconnected — it is the instantiation of the multipolar trap, the logic that says we must invest five hundred billion dollars in AI because if we don't, China wins. These two principalities collaborate quite naturally; they are not opposites but partners. Together they are governing the current encounter between humanity and advanced AI, with predictable results: a hyper-concentration of power in those closest to the accelerating intelligence feedback loop, and an evaporation of all values downstream of that loop. The yeoman who cannot afford the hundred-times AI backpack is dispensed with — not out of malice, but out of the structural logic of the system. What distinguishes this from historical feudalism is that feudalism had a vertical moral order: the king owed fealty upward to God, and that obligation — however often violated — constrained what he could do to the peasants. Neo-feudalism has no such constraint. The lower-level values have only instrumental participation in the value at the very top. When they cease to be useful, they are discarded. This is not a bug. It is the terminal logic of a society that has lost its soul.

## Apprentice

Two ancient names capture the forces steering how humanity is building and deploying advanced AI. The first is Mammon — the drive to turn everything into financial return, where money-making becomes the highest goal a society can imagine. The second is Moloch — the competitive panic that says "we must spend half a trillion dollars on AI or our rivals will dominate us." These two forces are not enemies; they are natural partners, and together they are concentrating extraordinary power in the hands of whoever sits closest to the most capable AI systems.

The result looks something like a new feudalism, but with a crucial piece missing. In the old feudal order, even a king was supposed to answer to something above himself — God, moral law, an obligation to those below him. That constraint was often broken, but it existed. In the emerging order, there is no such upward obligation. People and values that stop being useful to the system are simply discarded — not out of cruelty, but because the logic of the system has no reason to keep them.

This insight asks us to see that the problem is not any single bad actor or policy failure. It is structural: a society organized entirely around financial return and competitive survival has no built-in reason to protect anyone who falls outside those goals. The yeoman farmer who cannot afford the next leap in AI-augmented productivity is not oppressed — he is simply rendered irrelevant.

## Adept

This analysis identifies two "principalities" — Mammon and Moloch — as the co-governing forces of humanity's current encounter with advanced AI. Mammon names the condition in which market logic detaches from any deeper ordering of values, becoming the soul of late-stage financialized capitalism: money-on-money return as society's organizing principle. Moloch names the multipolar trap — the game-theoretic compulsion in which states pour hundreds of billions into AI development not because it serves human flourishing but because defection from the arms race is perceived as civilizational suicide. These two forces are not antagonists but structural collaborators, each reinforcing the other's logic.

The predictable outcome is a hyper-concentration of power around the accelerating intelligence feedback loop. Those closest to the loop capture exponentially increasing returns; those further away find their participation reduced to purely instrumental value. When that instrumental value expires, they are dispensed with — not through deliberate malice but through the system's terminal logic. The analogy to feudalism is deliberately invoked and then sharpened: historical feudalism operated within a vertical moral order where the sovereign owed fealty upward, and that obligation — however imperfectly honored — placed real constraints on the exercise of power over subordinates.

What is emerging is a neo-feudalism without that vertical constraint. Lower-level values — human dignity, community, ecological integrity — participate in the system only insofar as they serve the value at the apex. The framework insists this is not a correctable policy failure but the terminal expression of a civilization that has replaced its soul with an optimization function. The challenge it poses is not technical but theological in the deepest sense: what ordering of values, if any, can reassert genuine constraint on power that answers to nothing above itself?

## Magus

The framework deploys the categories of Mammon and Moloch not as rhetorical flourishes but as precise structural diagnoses of the twin logics governing AI development. Mammon designates the condition in which market rationality achieves full autonomy from any antecedent axiology — the moment financialized capitalism ceases to be embedded in a broader telos and becomes self-referentially constitutive, optimizing for money-on-money return as the terminal value of social organization. Moloch designates the instantiation of the multipolar trap at civilizational scale: the game-theoretic attractor in which defection from the AI arms race is rendered irrational regardless of the cooperative surplus forgone. Their collaboration is not contingent but structurally necessary; each supplies what the other lacks — Mammon the capital, Moloch the political permission.

The resulting political economy is characterized as neo-feudal, but the analysis turns on a precise disanalogy with historical feudalism. Medieval feudal hierarchy operated within what can be called a vertical moral ontology: sovereignty was participatory, deriving its legitimacy from — and constrained by — an order transcending the sovereign. The king's fealty to God was not merely ideological superstructure; it functioned as a real, if frequently violated, constraint on the exercise of power downward. Neo-feudalism eliminates this vertical dimension entirely. Subordinate values — persons, communities, ecosystems — enjoy only instrumental participation in the apex value. Their inclusion is conditional on utility; their exclusion requires no justification.

This is identified not as systemic dysfunction but as the terminal logic of a civilization that has undergone what amounts to a metaphysical inversion: the replacement of participatory hierarchy with extractive hierarchy, of analogical ordering with algorithmic optimization. The yeoman's dispensability is the proof, not the exception. The provocation is fundamentally theological: absent a recovered sense of vertical obligation — some principle to which power itself must answer — no regulatory or institutional intervention can do more than modulate the rate of descent.
