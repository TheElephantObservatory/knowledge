# How Recommendation Algorithms Manufacture Self-Sealing Realities

The prison with no warden and no walls

---

> Recommendation algorithms don't just show you a biased slice of reality — they manufacture self-enclosed worlds where missing information is invisible and opposing views seem not just wrong but impossible. Fixing this requires intervening at the economic and design level, not the surface.

The concept of the demi-reality, drawn from Roy Bhaskar's critical realism, offers a precise and disturbing diagnosis of what algorithmically mediated environments are producing at scale. A demi-reality is not simply a false belief or a biased perspective — it is a partial version of reality that feels complete and self-sustaining: internally coherent, circularly self-validating, and causally effective even when empirically ungrounded. What makes this concept so powerful is that demi-realities do not require a charismatic leader or a controlling institution to maintain themselves — that process has now been automated. Recommendation algorithms systematically absent certain perspectives, certain facts, certain kinds of human contact, and Bhaskar's crucial insight is that active absencing has its own causal force: what is excluded shapes the system as powerfully as what is included. The result is industrially produced, algorithmically maintained demi-realities — cultlike epistemic bubbles — generated not by deliberate manipulation but by the optimization logic of engagement-maximizing systems. When someone looks out from inside one demi-reality at another, the foreign perspective does not merely appear wrong — it appears ontologically impossible. This is why fact-checking fails: it operates at the empirical surface without touching the structural absences that hold the demi-reality in place. Critical realism's stratified ontology clarifies this further: polarization and epistemic breakdown are empirical symptoms; behind them lie the actual domain of recommendation algorithms and curation systems, and behind that the real domain of economic imperatives and design principles that most of society never sees. Any serious response must intervene at that deepest structural level — the economic models, regulatory frameworks, and design principles — rather than perpetually shuffling deck chairs at the surface while generative mechanisms continue undisturbed.

## Apprentice

Most people assume that online echo chambers just show you more of what you already agree with. But the problem runs deeper than bias. What recommendation algorithms actually produce are sealed-off versions of reality — worlds that feel complete from the inside because you can't see what's been removed. The philosopher Roy Bhaskar called these "demi-realities": partial realities that sustain themselves by making their own gaps invisible.

The key insight is that what's absent matters as much as what's present. When an algorithm systematically hides certain facts, voices, or perspectives from you, it doesn't just leave a blank space — it reshapes everything you can see. The remaining picture feels whole and makes sense on its own terms. This is why showing someone a fact-check often backfires: the fact doesn't just seem wrong, it seems to come from a world that couldn't possibly exist.

This process doesn't need a conspiracy or a villain. It happens automatically when systems are designed to maximize engagement. The uncomfortable conclusion is that surface-level fixes — better fact-checking, media literacy campaigns, content labels — cannot solve a problem whose roots lie in the economic models and design choices that most people never see. Real change requires reaching those hidden foundations.

## Adept

Roy Bhaskar's concept of the demi-reality, developed within his critical realist philosophy, provides an unusually precise framework for understanding what algorithmic curation systems are producing at scale. A demi-reality is not merely a distorted perspective or a collection of false beliefs — it is a partial reality that achieves internal coherence precisely through what it excludes. It feels complete from within because the absences that constitute it are themselves invisible. Bhaskar's crucial move is to grant causal efficacy to absencing: the systematic removal of perspectives, facts, and forms of contact actively shapes the epistemic environment as powerfully as any content that remains.

What makes this diagnosis contemporary and urgent is that the maintenance of demi-realities has been automated. Recommendation algorithms, optimizing for engagement rather than epistemic adequacy, generate cultlike epistemic enclosures without requiring charismatic authority or institutional conspiracy. When someone embedded in one demi-reality encounters another, the foreign view doesn't register as a competing interpretation of shared facts — it registers as ontologically impossible. This explains why fact-checking consistently underperforms: it intervenes at the empirical surface without addressing the structural absences that hold the demi-reality together.

Bhaskar's stratified ontology — distinguishing the empirical, the actual, and the real — maps cleanly onto this problem. Polarization and epistemic fragmentation are empirical phenomena. Beneath them sit the actual mechanisms of algorithmic curation. Beneath those lie the real generative structures: economic imperatives, design principles, and regulatory vacuums. Any intervention that fails to reach this deepest stratum merely treats symptoms while the generative machinery continues undisturbed.

## Magus

Bhaskar's concept of the demi-reality — a partial totality that achieves self-sustaining coherence through constitutive absencing — acquires renewed and disturbing force when applied to algorithmically mediated epistemic environments. A demi-reality is ontologically distinctive: it is not false consciousness in the classical sense, nor mere ideology, but a truncated real that forecloses its own incompleteness. Its internal consistency is purchased precisely by the invisibility of what has been absented. Bhaskar's insistence on the causal efficacy of absence — that non-being exerts real determination — is the theoretical fulcrum here. Recommendation systems do not merely amplify existing beliefs; they systematically absent countervailing perspectives, alternative factual configurations, and heterogeneous forms of social contact, thereby generating industrially scaled demi-realities whose maintenance requires no conscious agent.

The epistemological consequence is severe. Inter-demi-reality encounter does not produce disagreement in any dialogically productive sense; it produces mutual unintelligibility experienced as ontological impossibility. This is why empiricist correctives — fact-checking, source labeling, media literacy — consistently fail to gain traction. They operate within the domain of the empirical while the generative mechanisms operate at the levels of the actual (algorithmic curation architectures) and the real (the economic models, attention-extraction imperatives, and design ontologies that configure those architectures). The stratified ontology of critical realism makes this diagnostic precision possible.

The intervention this analysis demands is correspondingly deep-structural. Addressing polarization and epistemic fragmentation as surface phenomena while leaving generative mechanisms intact is precisely the kind of actualist fallacy Bhaskar's philosophy was designed to expose. Serious remediation requires engagement with the real domain: the economic logics, regulatory frameworks, and design principles that constitute the conditions of possibility for algorithmic demi-reality production — the stratum most of society never encounters and most policy never reaches.
