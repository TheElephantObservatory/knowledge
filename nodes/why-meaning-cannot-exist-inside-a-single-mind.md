# Why Meaning Cannot Exist Inside a Single Mind

You are my best chance of correcting my bias.

---

> Meaning is never private — it is communally made through dialogue, skilled action, and participatory belonging. When people treat knowledge as just holding correct propositions, they cut themselves off from the deeper engines of understanding and become more vulnerable to manipulation.

There is no such thing as private meaning — this is one of the deepest shared insights between Heidegger and Wittgenstein, who founded the continental and analytic traditions respectively. Meaning must be communally created, curated, shared, and shaped. What this implies for practice is profound: when people come into dialogue locked in what might be called propositional tyranny — the belief that all knowledge is the possession of a coherent set of propositions held by an individual — they are actually ghettoizing themselves from where most of the machinery of relevance realization takes place. Relevance realization, which is the core of understanding, meaning, and wisdom, operates primarily through non-propositional processes: your know-how in skilled action, your perspectival knowing of what it is like to inhabit a situation, and your participatory knowing through belonging and coupling. If people remain locked at the propositional level, they are cut off from these deeper engines of meaning-making. Worse, this disconnection makes them more manipulable — economic and political forces can trigger and exploit those non-propositional processes precisely because people are kept ignorant of them. Educating people in non-propositional knowing is therefore not merely a philosophical nicety; it is an act of empowering their agency, their selfhood, and their capacity for genuine connection. The goal of dialogical practice is to shift understanding away from mere logicality toward something more fundamental: reducing self-deception and increasing genuine connection, which go together. You are my best chance of correcting my bias, because it is very hard for me to see my own bias. This is why Socrates practiced in dialogue rather than writing treatises — logos, the shared communal processing of meaning, requires actual participation with others who hold different perspectives.

## Apprentice

One of the most important ideas in modern philosophy is that meaning cannot be created alone. Both Heidegger and Wittgenstein — founders of two very different philosophical traditions — arrived at the same conclusion: meaning is something we make together. It is not a private possession stored inside an individual mind.

The problem is that most people assume knowledge is simply about having the right beliefs or holding correct statements. But this misses the vast majority of how understanding actually works. Most of what makes you intelligent and adaptive operates below the level of words: your bodily skills, your feel for a situation, your sense of belonging to something larger than yourself. When people only pay attention to the verbal, propositional layer, they lose access to these deeper capacities.

This has real consequences. Economic and political forces routinely manipulate people by targeting exactly those non-verbal processes that people have been taught to ignore. Teaching people to recognize and develop these deeper forms of knowing is therefore an act of genuine empowerment. And the best way to do this is through honest dialogue — not debate aimed at winning, but conversation aimed at reducing self-deception. Other people are our best mirrors for seeing what we cannot see in ourselves. This is why Socrates never wrote books; he practiced philosophy face to face.

## Adept

Heidegger and Wittgenstein, despite founding the continental and analytic traditions respectively, converge on a radical claim: there is no such thing as private meaning. Meaning is irreducibly communal — created, curated, and sustained through shared practices and dialogue. This convergence is not incidental; it points to something fundamental about the structure of cognition and understanding that transcends the analytic-continental divide.

The practical implication targets what John Vervaeke calls "propositional tyranny" — the assumption that all knowledge reduces to a coherent set of propositions held by an individual. This assumption systematically obscures the non-propositional dimensions of knowing where relevance realization primarily operates: procedural know-how in skilled action, perspectival knowing of what it is like to inhabit a situation, and participatory knowing through coupling and belonging. Relevance realization — the process by which cognition zeroes in on what matters — is the core machinery of understanding, meaning, and wisdom. When people remain locked at the propositional level, they are ghettoized from these deeper cognitive processes.

This disconnection carries political stakes. Non-propositional processes do not stop operating simply because people are unaware of them; instead, economic and political forces exploit them precisely because they remain unexamined. Educating people in non-propositional knowing is therefore an act of empowering agency and selfhood. Dialogical practice aims not at mere logical validity but at reducing self-deception and increasing genuine connection — two goals that are deeply intertwined. Others are our best corrective for cognitive biases we cannot see in ourselves. This is the Socratic insight: logos as shared communal processing requires actual participation across different perspectives.

## Magus

The convergence of Heidegger and Wittgenstein on the impossibility of private meaning constitutes perhaps the most consequential agreement in twentieth-century philosophy — all the more striking for having emerged from thinkers who inaugurated rival traditions. The private language argument and the existential analytic of Dasein's thrown being-in-the-world arrive, by radically different routes, at the same destination: meaning is constitutively communal, irreducible to mental states of isolated subjects. Significance is always already shared; it presupposes the Mitsein, the form of life, the background of intelligibility that no individual can generate alone.

What Vervaeke identifies as "propositional tyranny" — the reduction of all knowing to the possession of justified true beliefs — represents not merely an epistemological error but a systematic occlusion of the cognitive ecology in which relevance realization operates. Procedural, perspectival, and participatory knowing constitute the transjective machinery through which salience landscapes are dynamically configured. Relevance realization, understood as the opponent processing that enables cognitive agents to zero in on what matters, is primarily non-propositional in character. Confinement to the propositional level does not neutralize these processes; it renders them opaque to the agent, creating precisely the conditions under which they become exploitable by external forces — what amounts to a manufactured alienation from one's own cognitive agency.

Dialogical practice, in this framework, is not reducible to argumentation or logical analysis. Its telos is the reciprocal reduction of self-deception through perspectival confrontation — the Socratic recognition that the other is my best chance of correcting biases structurally invisible to me. Logos here recovers its pre-Socratic resonance: not proposition but participatory disclosure, the communal processing through which meaning is not merely transmitted but constituted. Empowering non-propositional literacy is thus simultaneously an epistemic, ethical, and political act.
