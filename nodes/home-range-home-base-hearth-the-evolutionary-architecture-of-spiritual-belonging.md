# Home Range, Home Base, Hearth: The Evolutionary Architecture of Spiritual Belonging

To be lost is to need a world that holds you.

---

> The human longing for a spiritual home is not metaphorical — it reflects deep evolutionary structures of homing (belonging, orientation, ritual transformation) that operate at the level of worldview, and whose absence defines the meaning crisis.

The human quest for a spiritual home is not a metaphor or a cultural preference — it is a deep functional need that can be traced through the evolutionary and developmental architecture of homing itself. Drawing on John and Kellyanne Allen's work on the psychology of belonging and the evolution of home, Vervaeke identifies three nested structures. The home range is the territory shaped by Niche Construction — the animal is not merely pressured by the environment but actively shapes it, and culture is this process placed on high speed through distributed cognition. The home range grounds the sense of belonging, which maps directly onto the mattering dimension of the meaning-in-life literature. The home base is the shared defensible space — prospect and refuge — that gives the relevance realization machinery an origin point, enabling orientation: an origin-to-horizon relationship that allows interpolation and extrapolation of exploration rather than mere habit and repetition. The hearth then condenses within the home base, becoming simultaneously the center of ritual transformation, serious play, and shamanic healing — all the functions that Ross ano and others have identified as evolutionarily ancient. These three structures — home range, home base, hearth — stack into what Henriques frames phylogenetically and ontogenetically as organism, animal, mammal, primate, and person: each level co-opting the spatial and temporal structures of the level below and embedding them in relational value, belonging, and finally shared propositional narrative. What people seeking a spiritual home are looking for is not a physical place but the homing functions themselves — belonging, orientation, and ritual transformation — operating at the level of cultural and ontological environment. They want to be homed by a worldview. The meaning crisis is precisely the absence of a world-homing worldview that creates alignment across the wisdom traditions. Transcendent naturalism, the argument goes, is not merely consistent with providing these homing functions — it entails them, and does so in a way that is also chirotic: it orients people within the specific civilizational moment they actually inhabit, making the spiritual quest inseparable from the collective awakening required by that moment.

## Apprentice

When people say they are searching for a spiritual home, they usually mean something more than finding a community or a building. Drawing on research into the psychology of belonging and the evolution of home, John Vervaeke argues that "homing" is a layered biological function with deep roots. Animals shape a home range where they belong. Within that range they establish a home base that gives them a point of orientation — a place from which to explore outward. And within that base, a hearth emerges as the center of transformation, ritual, and healing.

These three layers — belonging, orientation, and transformation — are not just about physical space. They stack upward through evolution and human development, eventually operating at the level of culture and meaning. What people seeking a spiritual home actually need are these homing functions working at the level of their entire worldview: a way of seeing reality that tells them where they belong, how to orient, and how to grow.

The meaning crisis, in this framing, is what happens when no shared worldview provides those functions anymore. The proposal of transcendent naturalism is that a scientifically grounded yet spiritually serious perspective can restore all three layers of homing — and can do so in a way that speaks directly to the particular civilizational challenges of this moment in history.

## Magus

Vervaeke's analysis recruits the Allen and Allen framework on the evolution of home to disclose three nested homing structures as functional prerequisites for meaning. The home range, constituted through niche construction and accelerated by culturally distributed cognition, instantiates belonging — the mattering dimension of the meaning-in-life literature. The home base, a defensible prospect-refuge configuration, furnishes relevance realization with an origin point from which orientation becomes possible: not repetition but genuine interpolative and extrapolative exploration along an origin-to-horizon axis. The hearth condenses within the home base as the site of ritual transformation, serious play, and shamanic healing — the evolutionarily ancient functions Rossano and others have documented.

These structures are not merely analogical. They stack through Henriques' phylogenetic-ontogenetic sequence — organism, animal, mammal, primate, person — each level co-opting the spatiotemporal and relational architecture of the level below and embedding it in progressively higher-order value structures: from metabolic niche to territorial belonging to affiliative bonding to propositional narrative and shared worldview. The spiritual home is thus the full vertical integration of homing functions operating at the cultural-ontological level. What is sought is not a place but a worldview that homes — that provides belonging, orientation, and transformative practice simultaneously.

The meaning crisis, on this account, is the absence of any worldview capable of generating alignment across these homing functions and across the wisdom traditions that historically provided them. Transcendent naturalism is positioned as not merely consistent with but as entailing such homing — and as chirotic: it indexes the spiritual quest to the specific civilizational inflection point, rendering personal transformation and collective awakening structurally inseparable. The claim is strong: a worldview adequate to the moment must home persons within that moment or fail as worldview altogether.
