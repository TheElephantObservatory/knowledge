# Effective Altruism's Unexamined Assumption About Civilizational Stability

You cannot give from a ground that is gone.

---

> Effective altruism treats civilizational stability as a free background condition, but the complex society generating surplus resources is itself a living system requiring constant maintenance. Redirecting resources outward without accounting for this hidden dependency risks eroding the very capacity to do good.

Effective altruism rests on an assumption so foundational it is almost never examined: that the self-organizing system you are standing on will continue to function. The entire framework presupposes a stable civilizational substrate — affluent societies generating surplus resources that can be redirected toward distant problems. But a living, autopoetic system is not a stable given; it is an ongoing achievement, continuously maintained against entropy. The moment you treat it as a fixed background, you have made a category error.

The autopoetic lens reveals an asymmetry between resources deployed within the logic of a self-organizing system and resources exported outside it. Inside the system, expenditures maintain the organizational complexity that makes further action possible. Outside the system, interventions may be cheaper in immediate terms — a cataract surgery costs far less than training a guide dog — but this comparison abstracts away from the systemic context that makes either intervention possible. If organizational complexity is eroded in the process of exporting resources, the long-run capacity to do good at any scale is diminished.

This does not mean global causes are irrelevant. It means the framework for evaluating them must be grounded in enlightened self-interest — a first-person perspective that takes seriously the information asymmetry of proximity. I have far more information about my own needs, context, and capacities than about distant strangers. This is not a bias to be corrected; it is a structural feature of how meaningful information is organized in a complex world. Ethics that ignores this asymmetry does not transcend bias — it introduces a potentially more dangerous one: the assumption that the ground beneath our feet will hold no matter how much we extract from it.

## Apprentice

Effective altruism asks a powerful question: how can we do the most good with our resources? But it quietly assumes something enormous — that the wealthy, complex society generating those resources will just keep running on its own. In reality, a functioning civilization is more like a living organism than a machine. It has to be constantly maintained, repaired, and renewed. It doesn't just persist by default.

When we compare the cost of helping someone nearby versus someone far away, the distant intervention often looks like a better deal. A surgery overseas costs a fraction of a comparable service at home. But this comparison hides something important: the nearby system — with all its expensive complexity — is what makes your ability to help anyone possible in the first place. If you drain resources from it without noticing, you may eventually lose the capacity to act at all.

This doesn't mean we should ignore global problems. It means we should take seriously the fact that each person knows far more about their own situation and surroundings than about distant contexts. That local knowledge isn't a flaw to overcome — it's a genuine advantage. An honest ethics has to account for the reality that the ground we stand on needs tending, not just the fields far away.

## Adept

Effective altruism operates on an almost entirely unexamined presupposition: that the civilizational substrate generating redistributable surplus is a stable given rather than an ongoing autopoietic achievement. A self-organizing system — whether biological or social — maintains its complexity against entropy through continuous internal work. The moment this maintenance is treated as a costless background condition, a fundamental category error has been committed. Resources circulating within the system's own logic are not merely consumed; they sustain the organizational coherence that makes all further action, including altruistic action, possible.

This reveals a deep asymmetry that standard cost-effectiveness analysis obscures. Comparing the price of a cataract surgery abroad to a guide dog at home abstracts away the systemic context in which either intervention is conceivable. The domestic expenditure is expensive partly because it is embedded in a web of institutional complexity — training infrastructure, regulatory standards, professional knowledge — that itself constitutes civilizational capacity. Exporting resources outside this web may yield impressive marginal returns in the short term while quietly degrading the generative base.

The corrective is not parochialism but what might be called grounded first-person ethics. The information asymmetry between proximate and distant contexts is not a cognitive bias to be overcome through impartial reasoning; it is a structural feature of how actionable knowledge is distributed in complex systems. An agent genuinely has more reliable information about nearby needs and systemic dependencies than about distant ones. Ethics that dismisses this asymmetry in favor of abstract impartiality doesn't transcend perspective — it simply substitutes a thin, decontextualized model for the rich situational knowledge that effective action actually requires.

## Magus

The effective altruist framework implicitly models civilizational complexity as an exogenous constant — a fixed platform from which utilitarian calculations can be launched outward. The autopoietic critique identifies this as a profound category error. Drawing on the Maturana-Varela insight that living systems are operationally closed, continuously producing the components that constitute them, the argument extends this logic to the socioeconomic order itself. Affluent societies are not reservoirs of surplus passively awaiting redistribution; they are dissipative structures whose organizational complexity is maintained only through ongoing energetic and informational throughput. The surplus that effective altruism seeks to optimize is not separable from the systemic processes that generate it.

The implications for cost-effectiveness reasoning are severe. Standard comparisons — QALYs per dollar across contexts — perform an abstraction that strips away the systemic embedding of each intervention. A dollar spent maintaining institutional complexity within the generating system and a dollar spent on a marginal intervention in a distant context are not fungible in the way the framework assumes. The former sustains the conditions of possibility for the latter. This is not a sentimental preference for the proximate but a thermodynamic and organizational claim: extracting resources from a self-maintaining system without accounting for maintenance costs accelerates entropic degradation of the very capacity being leveraged.

The epistemological dimension is equally fundamental. The information asymmetry between an agent's knowledge of proximate versus distant systems is not a bias amenable to debiasing techniques — it reflects the actual topology of reliable causal knowledge in a complex world. Enlightened self-interest, properly understood, is not egoism but an acknowledgment that first-person situational knowledge constitutes an irreducible epistemic resource. Impartialist ethics that overrides this asymmetry substitutes a low-resolution model for high-resolution local knowledge, introducing systematic error precisely where it claims to have achieved objectivity.
