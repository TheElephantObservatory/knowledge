# Cognitive Stages Constrain Which Ideas Can Be Genuinely Understood

You can say the word before you can think it.

---

> The ideas people can genuinely hold — not just parrot — depend on the cognitive scaffolding they've built. Two people can use the same word and mean structurally different things, which is why measuring the complexity of reasoning matters more than matching its content.

One of the most striking findings to emerge from developmental psychology is that the stages people pass through in moral reasoning, self-conception, and value-making are not arbitrary cultural constructs — they are constrained by hierarchical complexity. You cannot hold certain ideas in your mind until you have built the cognitive scaffolding beneath them. The word 'society,' for instance, demands a systemic level of cognition just to mean anything. A child can repeat the word 'honor' at age five, but the concept doesn't functionally come online — in the sense that they can justify it to others at the requisite level — until much later. This is the core insight of the neo-Piagetian tradition: development is not a smooth continuum but a sequence of qualitatively distinct stages, each requiring the coordination of lower-order concepts into higher-order wholes. Michael Commons developed this model with a deliberately non-semantic approach — defining levels of cognitive complexity in terms of the logical structure of tasks rather than their content. Kurt Fischer's dynamic skill theory added that variability across domains is not a bug but a feature: a person can perform at level 11 in their area of expertise and level 8 in an unfamiliar domain. What the scale measures is not a person's global stage but the complexity of a specific performance in a specific context. Crucially, two people can use the same word — 'honor,' 'integrity,' 'society' — and be operating at radically different levels of cognitive complexity. A fire-breathing atheist evangelist may sound like they are operating at an individuative-reflective stage of faith development when in fact the structure of their reasoning is deeply conformist — the content changed, the structure did not. This is why content-matching approaches to developmental assessment are unreliable, and why structural measurement is so valuable.

## Apprentice

There are certain ideas you literally cannot think until you've built up the mental machinery to handle them. A five-year-old can say the word "honor," but they can't actually reason about what it means in the way an adult can. This isn't about intelligence or education — it's about a sequence of mental stages, where each new level requires you to coordinate and combine the thinking you could do at the level before.

This has a surprising consequence: two people can use the exact same words and be thinking in fundamentally different ways. Someone who loudly rejects the beliefs they grew up with might sound independent-minded, but the structure of their thinking — how they arrive at conclusions, how they handle disagreement — can be just as conformist as the group they left. The content changed, but the underlying architecture didn't.

This is why researchers like Michael Commons and Kurt Fischer focused on measuring the structure of how people reason rather than what they say they believe. Commons built a scale based on the logical complexity of tasks, not their subject matter. Fischer added that people don't sit at one fixed level — they might reason with great sophistication in one area and much less in another. The key insight is that development isn't a smooth slide but a staircase, and where someone stands on it shapes what ideas they can genuinely hold.

## Adept

The neo-Piagetian tradition's central claim is that cognitive development proceeds through qualitatively distinct stages of hierarchical complexity, each requiring the coordination of lower-order operations into higher-order structures. This is not a metaphor — it is a formal constraint. Certain concepts, such as "society" understood as a systemic whole, are literally unavailable to a mind that has not yet constructed the requisite level of abstraction. The word can be spoken; the concept cannot be functionally deployed.

Michael Commons' Model of Hierarchical Complexity (MHC) operationalizes this insight by defining stages in terms of the logical structure of task performance rather than semantic content. This non-semantic approach is what gives the model its cross-domain validity. Kurt Fischer's dynamic skill theory complements it by demonstrating that individuals do not occupy a single global stage — performance varies across domains and contexts, with a person potentially operating at a metasystematic level in their expertise and a concrete level in unfamiliar territory. What is measured is the complexity of a specific performance, not a trait of the person.

The practical consequence is profound for developmental assessment. Two individuals can articulate identical positions — rejecting religious authority, championing individual conscience — while operating at structurally different levels of complexity. James Fowler's stages of faith development illustrate this well: a militant atheist whose reasoning is structured by in-group conformity and binary opposition has not reached the individuative-reflective stage, regardless of how the content appears. Content-matching approaches to stage assessment systematically confuse surface agreement with structural equivalence, which is precisely why structural measurement — attending to how reasoning is organized rather than what it concludes — remains indispensable.

## Magus

The hierarchical complexity constraint on cognition — formalized in Commons' Model of Hierarchical Complexity and elaborated through Fischer's dynamic skill theory — represents one of developmental psychology's most consequential and underappreciated findings. The claim is not merely that development proceeds in stages, but that each stage constitutes a qualitatively new order of operations defined by the coordination of elements from the preceding order. This is a recursively structured sequence with formal properties: an order-n task requires the non-arbitrary coordination of order-(n-1) actions. The constraint is architectural, not cultural. "Society" as a systemic concept, "integrity" as a reflective self-evaluation — these are not vocabulary items but cognitive achievements gated by prerequisite structure.

Commons' deliberate non-semantic formulation is methodologically crucial. By defining complexity in terms of task structure rather than content domain, the MHC avoids the circularity that plagued earlier stage models reliant on content-coded responses. Fischer's contribution — that an individual's functional level varies dramatically across domains and is context-sensitive, bounded by an optimal level achievable only under supportive conditions — dissolves the naive expectation of stage-global consistency without abandoning the stage construct itself. The unit of analysis becomes the performance-in-context, not the person-as-stage.

The implications for developmental assessment are sharp. Content-matching heuristics — coding a response as "postconventional" because it endorses autonomy, or "individuative-reflective" because it rejects institutional religion — systematically conflate surface features with structural organization. A fire-breathing atheist evangelist whose reasoning is organized around dichotomous in-group loyalty and authority substitution has changed propositional content while preserving the formal structure of conventional-stage cognition. Only structural scoring — analyzing the hierarchical complexity of the coordinative operations actually performed — can disambiguate these cases. This is the methodological backbone that makes reliable cross-cultural and cross-domain developmental comparison possible.
