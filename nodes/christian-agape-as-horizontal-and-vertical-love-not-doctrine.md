# Christian Agape as Horizontal and Vertical Love, Not Doctrine

The scaffold mistaken for the cathedral.

---

> Christianity's core is not a set of beliefs but a dual movement of love — horizontal communion with other people and vertical relationship with an infinite that delights in your flourishing. Doctrines, rituals, and theology are scaffolding for that participatory center, not the center itself.

The center of the way of Christ is agape — love — and this love has both a horizontal and a vertical dimension that together constitute something structurally unique among the world's religious traditions. The horizontal dimension is communion: a religion that takes at its very heart your relationship with the people around you, and commits you at the level of the sacred to those relationships. The lived suffering of other people is not merely your concern — it is woven of the same fabric as the nature of the sacred itself. The vertical dimension is a relationship with the infinite whose character is also agape — an infinite that by its very nature seeks to love you completely, and does so in a way that holds and nurtures you while simultaneously expanding your self. The analogy is a non-narcissistic parent who is overjoyed to witness their child become more fully who they are. For God, that is the essential experience. Now notice what this means for the practices — prayer, fasting, singing, the Eucharist. None of those are the point. All of them are scaffolding to support the actual point, which is to love God with all of yourself and to love your neighbor. The propositions — the theology — exist largely to help people move toward that center. This is a profound inversion of how most modern people understand religion: as primarily a set of beliefs or doctrines to which one assents. The beliefs are downstream of the participatory reality. And this is why the church, when it falls into propositional tyranny — when it becomes an ideological version of what is fundamentally anti-ideological — has committed a deep tragedy against its own essence.

## Apprentice

The deepest claim of Christianity, on this reading, is not a list of things you must believe. It is a commitment to love — in two directions at once. Horizontally, it binds you at the level of the sacred to the people around you. Other people's suffering is not an afterthought; it is inseparable from the nature of the sacred itself. Vertically, it describes a relationship with an infinite being whose fundamental character is love — a love compared to a non-narcissistic parent who is overjoyed to watch their child become fully themselves.

Prayer, fasting, singing, communion — none of these are the point. They are supports for the actual point: to love God completely and to love your neighbor. Even the theology — the formal beliefs — exists mostly to help people move toward that living center.

This flips the common modern understanding of religion on its head. Most people think of religion as a package of doctrines you either accept or reject. But here, the beliefs come after the lived reality of love, not before it. When the church forgets this and turns itself into an ideology — demanding assent to propositions above all else — it betrays its own deepest nature.

## Adept

This perspective identifies agape as the structural center of Christianity and argues that it operates along two irreducible axes. The horizontal axis is communion: the sacred commitment to the lived reality of other persons, such that their suffering is not merely a moral concern but is ontologically woven into the fabric of the sacred itself. The vertical axis is a relationship with the infinite whose essential character is also agape — an infinite described by analogy to a non-narcissistic parent, one whose deepest joy is witnessing the child's full becoming. Together, these two dimensions constitute something structurally distinctive among the world's religious traditions.

The implications for practice are radical. Prayer, fasting, liturgy, the Eucharist — all are reframed as scaffolding rather than substance. They exist to support the participatory reality of loving God and neighbor. Theology and doctrine are similarly downstream: they are tools that orient people toward the living center, not the center itself.

This constitutes a profound inversion of the dominant modern framing of religion as primarily propositional — a set of beliefs to which one assents or doesn't. The insight here is that Christianity, rightly understood, is fundamentally anti-ideological. Its core is participatory, relational, and enacted. When the institutional church collapses this into propositional tyranny — demanding doctrinal conformity as the primary marker of belonging — it commits a tragedy against its own essence, converting a living relational reality into the very kind of ideological structure that its center inherently resists.

## Magus

The argument locates agape as the architectonic principle of Christianity and identifies a biaxial structure that, it is claimed, is without precise parallel in the comparative phenomenology of religion. The horizontal axis constitutes a sacralization of interpersonal communion: the suffering of the other is not an ethical addendum but is co-constitutive with the nature of the sacred as such — an ontological claim, not merely a deontic one. The vertical axis describes a relation with the infinite whose essential modality is also agape, figured through the analogy of non-narcissistic parenthood: an infinite whose kenotic delight consists in the progressive actualization of the finite self's own becoming. The two axes are mutually implicating; neither is intelligible without the other.

From this center, all liturgical and ascetic practice — prayer, fasting, eucharistic participation — is repositioned as scaffolding: instrumental to the participatory reality rather than constitutive of it. Propositional theology occupies an analogous status. Doctrines function as orienting heuristics for movement toward the relational center; they are not themselves the object of ultimate commitment. This represents a sharp challenge to the post-Enlightenment reduction of religion to doxastic content — the assumption that religious identity is fundamentally a matter of propositional assent.

The critical corollary is ecclesiological. When the institutional church elevates propositional conformity to the status of primary criterion — when it becomes, in effect, an ideological apparatus — it enacts a structural betrayal of its own ground. What is fundamentally anti-ideological, because participatory and relational at its root, is converted into precisely the kind of closed propositional system it exists to transcend. The tragedy is not incidental but essential: it is the tradition's own center that judges the distortion.
