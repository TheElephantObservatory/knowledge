# Energy, Information, and the Growth of Complexity

The second law is a teacher in disguise

---

> The universe isn't just growing more complex — it's growing more meaningful. Energy, information, and order are deeply linked, and their joint rise across cosmic history suggests that learning itself is written into the laws of nature.

If energy, information, and structural order are all related, then we should expect to see a clear correlation between these measures in a complexifying universe. As entities move further from equilibrium, they require more energy to sustain their informational richness and organizational depth. Eric Chaisson charts this trajectory across big history using free energy rate density as a single unified metric. Stars and galaxies have among the lowest energy rate densities of organized structures. Plant life is well higher. Animals evolved from fish and amphibians to reptiles, mammals, and birds with rising energy rate density values still. Brains have among the highest values of any living thing. And social-cultural evolution continues the trend upward. What this reveals is not merely a quantitative increase but a qualitative one: because increasing energy density and structural complexity drive ever-increasing semantic information processing, the universe is not just getting more energetic — it is getting more meaningful. As Chaisson puts it, information basically is a form of energy. Cosmic complexification is, at its deepest level, a learning process: life reduces Shannon entropy — ignorance — in order to extract the energy it needs to reduce Boltzmann entropy — disorder. The second law of thermodynamics, usually understood as the engine of decay and disorder, turns out to be the very impetus for learning. Novel computational mechanisms emerge out of earlier ones to process new kinds of information. As Seth Lloyd frames it, the history of the universe is a history of information processing revolutions, each arising naturally from the previous one, and we can prove mathematically that a universe that computes must, with high probability, give rise to a stream of ever more complex structures. Learning, then, is not a peculiarity of biological life — it is the natural consequence and driver of a universe that is fundamentally computational and complexifying. The arrow of cosmic evolution is, at its core, an arrow of deepening meaning.

## Apprentice

Most people think of the universe as winding down — stars burning out, things falling apart. But zoom out far enough and a different pattern emerges. Across the entire sweep of cosmic history, from galaxies to cells to brains to cultures, structures have been getting more organized, more energy-hungry, and more informationally rich. Physicist Eric Chaisson measured this using a single yardstick — how much energy flows through a given structure relative to its mass — and found a consistent upward trend from stars all the way to human brains and societies.

What makes this more than a curiosity is what it implies about information and meaning. As living things grow more complex, they don't just consume more energy — they process more information. They learn. They reduce ignorance in order to survive. The second law of thermodynamics, usually seen as the rule that says everything decays, turns out to be the very pressure that drives this learning. Disorder is the threat; complexity is the response.

Seth Lloyd extends this further: the universe is, at its core, a computing system, and a universe that computes will naturally and mathematically tend to produce ever more complex information-processing structures. Learning isn't something biology invented — it's something the universe has been doing all along. The arrow of time, seen this way, is not just an arrow of entropy but an arrow of deepening meaning.

## Adept

Eric Chaisson's free energy rate density (FERD) offers a rare unified metric for cosmic complexification — measuring energy throughput per unit mass across structures as diverse as galaxies, ecosystems, nervous systems, and technological civilizations. The resulting gradient is striking: stars sit near the bottom, brains and cultural systems near the top, with biological evolution tracing a consistent upward arc from fish to mammals to social organisms. This isn't merely a quantitative trend; it marks a qualitative transition in the universe's capacity for semantic information processing.

The thermodynamic framing is essential here. Far-from-equilibrium systems must continuously dissipate energy to maintain their organizational integrity — but in doing so, they develop increasingly sophisticated mechanisms for extracting that energy from their environments. This requires modeling the environment: reducing Shannon entropy, or ignorance, in order to reduce Boltzmann entropy, or physical disorder. Chaisson's claim that information is fundamentally a form of energy collapses the distinction between epistemic and physical complexity, suggesting that the universe's informational richness and its thermodynamic structure are two faces of the same process.

Seth Lloyd's computational universe framework supplies the formal backbone. In a universe that physically instantiates computation, the emergence of successive information-processing revolutions — each building on prior computational substrates — is not contingent but probable. Lloyd demonstrates mathematically that such a universe will, with high likelihood, generate a cascade of increasingly complex structures. The implication is profound: learning is not a biological accident but a cosmological imperative, and the arrow of cosmic evolution is, at its deepest level, an arrow of accumulating meaning.

## Magus

Chaisson's free energy rate density operationalizes a conjecture with deep roots in non-equilibrium thermodynamics and information theory: that energy flux, structural order, and semantic complexity are not merely correlated but co-constitutive. By normalizing energy throughput against mass, FERD renders commensurable the organizational depth of structures across twelve orders of magnitude — from stellar nucleosynthesis to cortical computation — and reveals a monotonic gradient that tracks the universe's complexification trajectory. Crucially, this metric captures not static energy content but dynamic dissipative throughput, foregrounding the Prigoginian insight that far-from-equilibrium systems sustain their negentropic organization precisely through irreversible energy flow.

The epistemological stakes sharpen when Chaisson's claim — that information is a form of energy — is taken seriously as a physical thesis rather than a metaphor. On this view, the reduction of Shannon entropy (epistemic uncertainty about environmental states) is not merely instrumentally related to the reduction of Boltzmann entropy (thermodynamic disorder) but is its mechanistic precondition. Adaptive systems must construct internal models of their free-energy landscapes; the sophistication of those models co-evolves with the complexity of the dissipative structures that instantiate them. Meaning, construed as structured informational constraint, becomes a thermodynamic variable.

Lloyd's quantum computational framework provides the formal grounding for this trajectory's necessity rather than contingency. Demonstrating that a universe governed by quantum mechanics is a universal computer, Lloyd shows that the probability measure over computational histories strongly favors the emergence of increasingly complex information-processing architectures, each arising naturally from prior substrates. The succession of computational revolutions — chemical, genetic, neural, cultural — is thus not a sequence of improbable accidents but the high-probability output of a universe whose dynamics are intrinsically generative. The second law, conventionally the engine of thermodynamic degradation, is recast as the selection pressure driving the universe's recursive self-elaboration into ever-deeper structures of knowing.
