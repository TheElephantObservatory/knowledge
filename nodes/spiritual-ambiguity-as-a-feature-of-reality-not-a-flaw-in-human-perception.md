# Spiritual Ambiguity as a Feature of Reality, Not a Flaw in Human Perception

The universe refuses to confess.

---

> Spiritual ambiguity may not be a failure of human understanding but a genuine feature of reality itself — and embracing it, rather than resolving it, could be the deeper ground for both knowledge and the sacred.

There is a second, deeper moment of non-theism that goes beneath the familiar debate between theists and atheists. The first moment is recognizing that common theism and modern atheism share a set of presuppositions — about what god would have to be, about what kind of evidence would settle the question — and challenging those shared presuppositions. But there is a deeper layer still. The philosopher John Hick made an argument that the clearest conclusion we can draw from millennia of debate about the existence of god is the spiritual ambiguity of the universe. If the universe were intelligible enough to resolve this question, it probably would have been resolved by now. Across multiple epistemic frames, ontological frames, cultural frames, and historical periods, the ambiguity persists. The standard response is to treat this as a limitation — our finite minds cannot grasp the infinite. But what if that is wrong? What if spiritual ambiguity is not an accident of human limitation but a proper ontological feature of reality itself? This is not a counsel of despair. If we think of knowledge as requiring perfect clarity, then yes, this is catastrophic. But if we reconceive knowledge as inherently self-transcending, frame-breaking, and ecstatic — as we have good reason to do from cognitive science — then a spiritually ambiguous universe is not one that is closed off from us. It may be precisely the kind of universe that grounds the core of insight and inference. Consider love: people consistently report that love is both the greatest source of meaning in their lives and the greatest source of suffering. That profound ambiguity is not a defect in love — it is at the very nexus of human being. The wisdom traditions that have been most honest have always known this. What we need is a sense of the sacred that properly acknowledges and inhabits spiritual ambiguity rather than trying to resolve it into a clean ontological verdict.

## Apprentice

The debate between believers and atheists usually assumes both sides know what kind of thing god would have to be and what kind of evidence would settle the question. But what if those shared assumptions are the real problem? Philosophers like John Hick have pointed out something striking: after thousands of years of argument, the universe remains stubbornly ambiguous on the question of the divine. No culture, no era, no framework of thought has been able to close the case.

The usual explanation is that our minds are too small to grasp the answer. But there is a more radical possibility: what if the ambiguity is not a bug but a feature? What if reality itself is spiritually ambiguous at its core — not because we are limited, but because that is how things actually are?

This is not a reason for despair. Consider love: people describe it as both the deepest source of meaning and the deepest source of suffering. That tension is not a flaw in love — it is what makes love so powerful and transformative. The same may be true of spiritual life. Instead of demanding a clean answer about whether god exists, the deeper move may be to develop a relationship with the sacred that lives honestly inside the ambiguity, treating it not as a problem to solve but as the very ground of genuine insight.

## Adept

A familiar first move in non-theistic philosophy is to notice that conventional theism and modern atheism share deep presuppositions — about the nature of the divine, the structure of evidence, the kind of ontological verdict that would settle the matter — and to challenge those presuppositions from underneath. But there is a second, deeper moment. Drawing on John Hick's argument, the most robust conclusion from millennia of cross-cultural, cross-epistemic debate about god's existence is the persistent spiritual ambiguity of the universe. No framework — ontological, cultural, historical — has resolved the question. The standard interpretation treats this as epistemological limitation: finite minds confronting the infinite. The deeper interpretation reframes ambiguity as an ontological feature of reality itself.

This reframing becomes generative rather than catastrophic once the operative model of knowledge shifts. If knowledge requires perfect representational clarity, then irreducible ambiguity is a dead end. But cognitive science gives strong reasons to reconceive knowledge as inherently self-transcending, frame-breaking, and ecstatic — a process that thrives precisely in conditions of productive indeterminacy. A spiritually ambiguous universe is not epistemically closed; it may be the very condition that grounds genuine insight and inference.

The analogy to love is instructive. Love is consistently reported as both the greatest source of meaning and the greatest source of suffering — and this ambiguity is not a defect but constitutive of its transformative power. The wisdom traditions most honest about human experience have always recognized this. What emerges is not agnosticism as intellectual paralysis, but a call for a sense of the sacred that inhabits spiritual ambiguity rather than seeking to collapse it into a determinate metaphysical claim.

## Magus

Beyond the first moment of non-theism — the critical recognition that classical theism and Enlightenment atheism operate within a shared presuppositional matrix concerning divine ontology and evidential criteria — lies a second, more radical moment. John Hick's argument from the persistent irresolvability of the theistic question across epistemic, ontological, cultural, and historical frames yields a striking meta-philosophical conclusion: the spiritual ambiguity of the universe is not merely a contingent feature of our epistemic situation but may constitute a proper ontological datum. The standard deflationary reading — that ambiguity indexes the asymptotic gap between finite cognition and infinite reality — preserves the assumption that determinacy is the default structure of the real. The deeper move is to refuse that assumption and to treat irreducible ambiguity as disclosive rather than privative.

This move becomes philosophically tractable once knowledge is reconceived along lines suggested by contemporary cognitive science: not as the accumulation of determinate representations but as an inherently self-transcending, frame-breaking, ecstatic process. Under this model, a spiritually ambiguous universe is not epistemically foreclosed but is precisely the kind of reality that sustains the generative indeterminacy at the core of insight and inferential practice. The ambiguity is not noise; it is signal.

The phenomenology of love provides a structural parallel. Love is reported with remarkable consistency as simultaneously the deepest source of meaning and the deepest source of suffering — an ambiguity that is not accidental but constitutive, situated at what might be called the nexus of human being. The most epistemically honest wisdom traditions have always inhabited this space. What is called for, then, is not agnosticism as suspension of judgment but a sacral orientation that takes spiritual ambiguity as its proper dwelling — refusing the collapse into determinate ontological verdict while remaining fully open to the transformative demands of the real.
