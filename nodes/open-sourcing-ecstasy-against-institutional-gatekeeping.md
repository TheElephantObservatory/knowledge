# Open-Sourcing Ecstasy Against Institutional Gatekeeping

Fire for every hearth, light for every mind

---

The history of civilization has a hidden underground current running through it — a multi-thousand-year game of cat and mouse between what Jamie Wheal calls the Prometheans and the priests. The Prometheans are the lightbringers, the rebels, the ones who kept reaching for non-ordinary states of consciousness as a way to seed culture, spark innovation, and renew meaning — from the Eleusinian Mysteries in ancient Greece, through the psychedelic experiments of the 1960s, through MK-Ultra, through Burning Man, through the contemporary neuroscience and psychedelic renaissance. And arrayed against them, in every era, have been the institutional authorities — whether in black cassocks or black suits — working to suppress, marginalize, or co-opt those tools. The thesis that emerges from this long view is that non-ordinary states are not a fringe curiosity or a form of hedonism — they are a foundational technology of human civilization, repeatedly rediscovered and repeatedly suppressed. The strategic implication for our current moment is that the only way to prevent these tools from being shut down again by institutional authority is to open-source them — to make techniques of ecstasy, as Mircea Eliade called them, available to all humans rather than gatekept by any elite. Because if access is decentralized, no single authority can extinguish the flame.

## Apprentice

Hidden beneath the surface of history runs a long, recurring struggle over who gets access to extraordinary states of mind. On one side are the rebels and visionaries — people who believed that certain experiences, whether through ritual, plant medicines, or other means, could unlock creativity, renew culture, and give life deeper meaning. On the other side, in every era, stand the authorities: churches, governments, institutions that have consistently moved to suppress or control these practices. This pattern stretches from ancient Greek mystery cults all the way to the psychedelic experiments of the 1960s and the current renaissance in consciousness research. The core argument is that these non-ordinary states are not fringe or frivolous — they are one of the oldest and most powerful tools human civilization has ever used to refresh itself. And the lesson of history is sobering: every time these tools have been concentrated in the hands of a few, they have been shut down. The only way to protect them now is to make them widely available — to spread knowledge of these practices so broadly that no single authority can suppress them again.

## Adept

Jamie Wheal's historical thesis identifies a recurring civilizational dynamic he frames as a conflict between Prometheans and priests — a multi-millennial struggle over access to non-ordinary states of consciousness. The Prometheans, in this reading, are the initiators and culture-renewers: the architects of the Eleusinian Mysteries, the alchemists, the Romantic poets, the psychedelic pioneers of the twentieth century, and today's researchers working at the intersection of neuroscience and contemplative practice. Against them, in every epoch, institutional authorities have deployed suppression, co-optation, or criminalization — from the Roman dismantling of mystery cults to the DEA scheduling of psychedelics following the 1960s counterculture. The deeper claim is that non-ordinary states function as a foundational technology of civilization — not a luxury or aberration, but a recurring mechanism by which cultures metabolize stagnation, generate meaning, and catalyze innovation. Drawing on Mircea Eliade's concept of 'techniques of ecstasy,' the argument holds that shamanic and ecstatic practices represent a universal human inheritance, not the property of any particular tradition or elite. The strategic conclusion for the present moment is pointed: because historical suppression has repeatedly succeeded when access was centralized or gatekept, the only durable protection is radical decentralization. Open-sourcing these tools — making the maps, methods, and practices broadly available — removes the single point of failure that institutional authority has always exploited.

## Magus

Wheal's historiographical intervention reframes the longue durée of Western esotericism not as a marginal counter-tradition but as a structurally necessary counterpoint to institutional consolidation — a dialectic between what he terms Promethean transmission and sacerdotal capture. The Eleusinian Mysteries, operative for nearly two millennia, represent the paradigm case: a controlled, initiatory technology for inducing ego dissolution and cosmological reorientation, later theorized by Wasson, Hofmann, and Ruck as entheogenically mediated. Their suppression under Theodosius I in 392 CE marks not merely a religious transition but an epistemological foreclosure — the institutional church monopolizing access to transcendent experience and, with it, the legitimating authority that such experience confers. This pattern recurs with structural regularity: the alchemical underground, the Romantic reaction to Enlightenment disenchantment, the mid-twentieth-century psychedelic research programs terminated not by scientific failure but by political anxiety, and the MK-Ultra episode as a revealing inversion — the state itself pursuing non-ordinary states as instruments of control rather than liberation. Eliade's 'techniques of ecstasy' provides the anthropological substrate: the argument that shamanic practice constitutes a universal archaic technology, predating and underlying all subsequent religious institutionalization. The epistemological stakes are significant — if non-ordinary states are generative of cultural meaning rather than merely expressive of it, then their suppression constitutes not moral regulation but civilizational self-impoverishment. The open-source imperative that follows is not merely tactical but structural: decentralization as the only configuration that defeats the recurring logic of capture, because distributed transmission cannot be extinguished by targeting any single node of authority or lineage.
