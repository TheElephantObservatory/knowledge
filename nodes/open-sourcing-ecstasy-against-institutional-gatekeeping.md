# Open-Sourcing Ecstasy Against Institutional Gatekeeping

Fire for every hearth, light for every mind

---

> Non-ordinary states of consciousness are not a fringe curiosity — they are a foundational technology of civilization, repeatedly rediscovered and repeatedly suppressed. The only defense against suppression is radical decentralization: make the tools available to everyone.

The history of civilization has a hidden underground current running through it — a multi-thousand-year game of cat and mouse between what Jamie Wheal calls the Prometheans and the priests. The Prometheans are the lightbringers, the rebels, the ones who kept reaching for non-ordinary states of consciousness as a way to seed culture, spark innovation, and renew meaning — from the Eleusinian Mysteries in ancient Greece, through the psychedelic experiments of the 1960s, through MK-Ultra, through Burning Man, through the contemporary neuroscience and psychedelic renaissance. And arrayed against them, in every era, have been the institutional authorities — whether in black cassocks or black suits — working to suppress, marginalize, or co-opt those tools. The thesis that emerges from this long view is that non-ordinary states are not a fringe curiosity or a form of hedonism — they are a foundational technology of human civilization, repeatedly rediscovered and repeatedly suppressed. The strategic implication for our current moment is that the only way to prevent these tools from being shut down again by institutional authority is to open-source them — to make techniques of ecstasy, as Mircea Eliade called them, available to all humans rather than gatekept by any elite. Because if access is decentralized, no single authority can extinguish the flame.

## Apprentice

Beneath the surface of recorded history, there is a recurring struggle between two forces. On one side are the rebels and visionaries — people who used altered states of consciousness, whether through ritual, plant medicines, or other means, to generate new ideas, renew culture, and reconnect people with a sense of meaning. On the other side are the institutional authorities of every era, whether religious or governmental, who have consistently moved to suppress or control those same tools.

This pattern stretches from the secret ceremonies of ancient Greece all the way through the psychedelic experiments of the 1960s, government programs like MK-Ultra, and today's renaissance of clinical research into psychedelics. The argument, developed by thinker Jamie Wheal, is that these non-ordinary states are not recreational or marginal — they are a core technology that humans have always used to solve the deepest problems of culture and meaning.

The practical conclusion is urgent: if these tools are controlled by any single institution or elite, history suggests they will eventually be shut down again. The only way to protect them is to make them widely and openly available — to decentralize access so thoroughly that no authority can extinguish them.

## Adept

Jamie Wheal's framework identifies a long-running civilizational dialectic between what he terms Prometheans — those who have historically leveraged non-ordinary states of consciousness to seed cultural renewal, epistemological breakthroughs, and meaning-making — and the institutional authorities who have repeatedly moved to suppress or co-opt those same capacities. The lineage runs from the Eleusinian Mysteries through Sufi ecstatic practice, through the Harvard Psilocybin Project and MK-Ultra, through Burning Man and the contemporary psychedelic renaissance in clinical neuroscience.

The central thesis is that non-ordinary states are not a peripheral phenomenon but a foundational cognitive technology — one that civilizations have repeatedly rediscovered as a mechanism for collective sense-making, creative breakthrough, and the renovation of shared meaning structures. Mircea Eliade's framing of shamanic practice as "techniques of ecstasy" provides a useful historical anchor: these are learnable, transmissible methods, not mystical accidents. The suppression of these techniques by institutional authority — whether ecclesiastical or governmental — is therefore not incidental but structurally predictable, since they represent a competing locus of epistemic authority and social cohesion.

The strategic implication Wheal draws is that open-sourcing these techniques — radically decentralizing access to the underlying practices rather than allowing them to be gatekept by any therapeutic, religious, or governmental institution — is the only historically robust defense against their recurring suppression. Decentralization functions here not merely as an ethical preference but as a resilience strategy: if no single node controls access, no single authority can extinguish the flame.

## Magus

Wheal's historiographical intervention reconstructs a subterranean counter-tradition running through Western and cross-cultural intellectual history: a recurring agonism between entheogenic and ecstatic practice as a vector of cultural generativity and the hegemonic institutions — sacerdotal, juridical, biomedical — that have cyclically moved to pathologize, criminalize, or instrumentalize those same epistemic technologies. The Eleusinian Mysteries, Dionysian rites, Sufi sama, the kykeon hypothesis advanced by Wasson, Hofmann, and Ruck, the Harvard Psilocybin Project, MKULTRA's coercive appropriation, and the current MAPS-mediated clinical renaissance all constitute nodes in a single, discontinuous genealogy of contested access to non-ordinary states.

The theoretical core of this argument draws on Eliade's morphology of shamanism — specifically his characterization of ecstatic technique as a learnable, transmissible technology of consciousness rather than spontaneous mystical event — and reframes it as a claim about civilizational infrastructure. Non-ordinary states, on this reading, are not epiphenomenal to culture but constitutive of it: mechanisms for the periodic dissolution and reconstitution of shared semiotic frameworks, the renovation of collective meaning structures under conditions of entropic drift. Their suppression is therefore not contingent but structurally overdetermined, since they represent a competing locus of noetic authority that threatens the epistemic monopolies on which institutional legitimacy depends.

The strategic corollary — open-sourcing the techniques of ecstasy — is framed not as libertarian preference but as a historically-informed resilience argument. Distributed, non-proprietary access to these practices renders them topologically resistant to the centralized interdiction that has terminated each prior cycle. The analogy to cryptographic decentralization is implicit but precise: if no single node controls the protocol, no single authority can revoke it.
