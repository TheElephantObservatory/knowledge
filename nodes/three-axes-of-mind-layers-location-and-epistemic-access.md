# Three Axes of Mind: Layers, Location, and Epistemic Access

What Mary knew before she saw red

---

> The Map of Human Mindedness distinguishes three independent axes — evolutionary layer, inside-versus-outside spatial orientation, and the epistemological vector by which something is knowable — and argues that conflating them is the root of most confusion in the science of mind.

The Map of Human Mindedness organizes the domains of mind along three independent axes of division, and understanding all three is necessary to use the map correctly. The first axis is vertical: the layer of evolutionary complexification through the Tree of Knowledge, moving from the bio-physiological layer through the mind-animal plane into the culture-person plane. This gives us Mind 1, Mind 2, and Mind 3 as genuinely distinct ontological layers, not just descriptive categories. The second axis is spatial: what is happening between the animal and its environment versus what is happening within the animal. This gives us the 'b' and 'a' subdivisions — overt minded behavior on the outside, implicit neurocognitive processing on the inside. The third axis is epistemological: the vector by which something is knowable. Mind 2 — subjective consciousness — is constituted by an interior epistemic vector. It arises from the inside out and is not directly available to the exterior behavioral vantage point. This is not merely a methodological limitation; it is an ontological feature of what subjective experience is. Science, as an institution, is built on systematic empiricism anchored in a third-person behavioral view. That means science is structurally positioned to study everything except the interior epistemic vector directly. This is why the knowledge argument — Mary learning everything about color vision but then actually seeing color — points to something real: perspectival knowledge is categorically different from propositional knowledge, as John Vervaeke's vocabulary helps clarify. The Map of Mind does not resolve this gap, but it names it precisely enough that researchers can stop accidentally conflating the three axes.

## Apprentice

The Map of Human Mindedness proposes that the mind isn't one thing but operates across three genuinely different layers. The first layer is the biological machinery of the body. The second is the animal mind — the felt, conscious experience of being alive. The third is the cultural-personal layer, where language, identity, and shared meaning live. These aren't just useful labels; they represent real differences in how the world is organized.

But layers alone aren't enough. Within each layer, there's a difference between what an organism does outwardly — its visible behavior — and what's happening inside it, in its neural processing. This inside-outside distinction is the second axis of the map.

The third axis is the most provocative. It asks: how is something knowable? Subjective experience — what it feels like to see red or taste chocolate — is known from the inside out. Science, by design, studies things from the outside in, using observation and measurement. This means science is structurally unable to directly access the felt quality of experience. The famous thought experiment about a color scientist who knows everything about color but has never seen it illustrates this gap. The map doesn't claim to solve the problem of consciousness, but by clearly naming these three separate axes, it helps researchers avoid accidentally mixing them up — which is where much of the confusion in mind science originates.

## Adept

The Map of Human Mindedness, developed within Gregg Henriques' unified metatheoretical framework, organizes mental phenomena along three independent axes that are routinely collapsed in both philosophy of mind and behavioral science. The first axis is vertical: the Tree of Knowledge's layers of complexification, yielding Mind 1 (bio-physiological), Mind 2 (mind-animal, subjective consciousness), and Mind 3 (culture-person, linguistically mediated selfhood). These are treated as ontologically distinct layers, not merely heuristic categories — each emerges through a genuine phase shift in informational organization.

The second axis is spatial, distinguishing overt minded behavior (the 'b' subdivision — what occurs between organism and environment) from implicit neurocognitive processing (the 'a' subdivision — what occurs within the organism). This inside-outside distinction operates within each layer and is orthogonal to the vertical axis.

The third axis is epistemological and carries the deepest philosophical weight. Mind 2 — subjective experience — is constituted by an interior epistemic vector. It arises from the inside out and is not directly available to the exterior behavioral vantage point that grounds scientific methodology. This is not a gap that better instruments will close; it is a structural feature of what first-person experience is. Frank Jackson's knowledge argument and John Vervaeke's distinction between perspectival and propositional knowledge both illuminate why: knowing-by-being is categorically different from knowing-by-description. The map does not dissolve the hard problem, but it names the three axes precisely enough to prevent researchers from inadvertently treating an epistemological boundary as a mere methodological inconvenience.

## Magus

Henriques' Map of Human Mindedness constitutes a metatheoretical coordinate system for mental phenomena, structured by three orthogonal axes whose independent variation is the source of most persistent confusions in consciousness studies and behavioral science. The vertical axis tracks genuine ontological emergence through the Tree of Knowledge system: Mind 1 (bio-physiological processes), Mind 2 (animal-mind subjective experience), and Mind 3 (culture-person justification systems). These are not epistemic conveniences but represent discrete complexification thresholds — each layer is governed by novel selection dynamics irreducible to the layer beneath.

The spatial axis cross-cuts each layer, distinguishing the 'b' domain (minded behavioral investment in organism-environment transactions) from the 'a' domain (implicit neurocognitive processing interior to the organism). This recapitulates, at a metatheoretical level, the classical behavior-mechanism distinction while embedding it within a layered ontology that prevents the reductive slide from behavioral description to neural identity claims.

The epistemological axis is where the framework achieves its sharpest critical purchase. Mind 2 is constituted by an interior epistemic vector — subjective experience is that which is known from the first-person vantage as a condition of its existence. Systematic empiricism, institutionally anchored in third-person behavioral observation, is therefore structurally orthogonal to this vector. Jackson's Mary scenario and Vervaeke's taxonomy of knowing — particularly the perspectival-propositional distinction — jointly demonstrate that this is not an instrumentation problem but a category boundary. The Map of Mind does not pretend to resolve the explanatory gap; rather, it furnishes a notation precise enough to render visible when theorists inadvertently project findings along one axis onto claims properly indexed to another, thereby converting tractable disciplinary questions into intractable pseudo-paradoxes.
