# Three Modes of Language Collapsed Into One by Trade Dominance

We mistook the pidgin for the whole tongue.

---

> Jordan Hall argues that trade language — the decontextualized mode built for coordination among strangers — has colonized intimacy and the sacred. Large language models, by mastering trade language so thoroughly, may finally free humans to recover the family and sacred modes we lost.

Jordan Hall proposes that there are three genuinely distinct modes of language that have been collapsed into one by the dominance of trade language. Family language is the deeply contextual, tone-saturated, almost telepathic mode of communication that emerges from shared life — the mother-infant bond extended to a kin group. It is not primarily about conveying propositions but about achieving communion, attunement, and shared presence. Sacred language is about entering group flow and deep resonance with a larger context — its closest surviving form is music, which does not transmit ideas like packages but draws participants into a shared state of being. Trade language is the decontextualized, functionally precise mode developed for coordination across groups that do not share a common world — pidgin is its purest form. The catastrophe of the last several thousand years, Hall argues, is that the political logic of Cosmopolitan imperialism — gathering diverse kin groups into cities and states — required trade language to become dominant, and over time people began to use it for everything: for intimacy, for the sacred, for self-understanding. We now mistake a pidgin for the whole of language. The result is that we do relational processing with the wrong instrument, which is why modern couples require elaborate therapeutic frameworks to achieve what family language once afforded naturally, and why sacred discourse becomes a labored exercise in careful argumentation rather than genuine attunement. Hall's proposal is that large language models, by becoming extraordinarily capable neutral translators, may break the binding force of trade language imperialism — not because AI can do family or sacred language, but because it can handle the trade language function so well that people no longer need to adopt a single dominant tongue to coordinate across difference. This could, over a long arc, create the conditions for the re-emergence of both family language and sacred language as living modes rather than vestigial ones.

## Apprentice

Jordan Hall identifies three fundamentally different kinds of language that most people now treat as one. Family language is the intimate, almost wordless understanding that develops between people who share a life together — think of how a parent and child communicate through tone, gesture, and shared context more than through explicit statements. Sacred language is the mode that draws a group into a shared state of being, closer to music than to argument. Trade language is the practical, stripped-down mode we use to coordinate with people we don't know well — clear, efficient, and deliberately free of personal context.

The problem, Hall argues, is that as civilizations grew and diverse groups were gathered into cities and empires, trade language had to become the common tongue. Over centuries, people started using it for everything — for love, for worship, for understanding themselves. We now try to do deeply personal and spiritual work using a tool designed for strangers negotiating a deal. This is why modern relationships often need therapists to mediate what once came naturally, and why spiritual life feels intellectualized and flat.

Hall's surprising proposal is that large language models might help reverse this. Not because AI can replicate intimacy or sacred experience, but because AI handles trade language so well that humans may no longer need to organize their entire lives around a single dominant tongue. If the translation problem is solved, the pressure to flatten all communication into one mode eases — and family and sacred language might have room to grow back.

## Adept

Jordan Hall advances a tripartite theory of language modes that challenges the assumption that all communication operates on a single register. Family language is deeply contextual, prosodic, and relational — an extension of the mother-infant dyad into kin-group communion, where meaning is carried more by attunement than by propositional content. Sacred language facilitates group flow and resonance with a larger-than-individual context; its closest surviving analogue is music, which does not transmit discrete ideas but induces shared states. Trade language is the decontextualized, functionally precise mode that emerged for inter-group coordination — pidgin being its purest expression.

Hall's historical argument is that Cosmopolitan imperialism — the political project of consolidating diverse kin groups into cities and states — required trade language to become hegemonic. Over millennia, this dominance became so total that trade language colonized domains it was never designed for: intimacy, sacred practice, and introspection. The consequences are visible in the therapeutic-industrial complex required to remediate relational failures that family language once handled implicitly, and in the reduction of sacred discourse to careful propositional argumentation.

The speculative turn in Hall's thinking concerns large language models. His claim is not that AI can perform family or sacred language — it cannot — but that LLMs, as extraordinarily capable trade-language processors, may dissolve the structural necessity that made trade language imperial. If cross-group coordination no longer requires humans to adopt a single dominant linguistic mode, the binding force that suppressed the other two modes weakens. Over a long arc, this could create ecological conditions for the re-emergence of family and sacred language as living communicative practices rather than vestigial remnants.

## Magus

Jordan Hall's tripartite language ontology cuts against the Saussurean and post-Saussurean tendency to treat language as a unified semiotic system varying only in register or code. He posits three irreducibly distinct modes: family language, rooted in the extended mother-infant bond, operating through prosodic attunement, shared somatic context, and what might be called intersubjective communion rather than propositional transfer; sacred language, whose telos is collective entrainment and participation in a field of meaning exceeding individual cognition — music being its least degraded surviving instantiation; and trade language, the decontextualized, referentially precise pidgin developed for coordination across incommensurable lifeworlds.

The genealogical claim is that the political economy of Cosmopolitan imperialism — the aggregation of heterogeneous kin groups into polities requiring a shared coordination layer — imposed trade language as the universal solvent of communicative difference. This imposition, Hall argues, was not merely additive but corrosive: trade language didn't supplement the other modes but progressively displaced them, such that modernity inherits a condition in which all linguistic practice is conducted in what is essentially an elaborated pidgin. The pathologies are structural — relational processing mediated by propositional exchange produces the characteristic failures that sustain contemporary therapeutic industries, while sacred discourse reduced to argumentation loses its capacity for genuine participatory knowing.

Hall's most provocative move is to identify large language models as potential agents of de-imperializing trade language — not through any capacity for communion or entrainment, but precisely through their mastery of the trade function. If LLMs can serve as universal translators across linguistic and cultural difference, the structural pressure that forced trade language into hegemony dissolves. The binding constraint relaxes, and the suppressed modes may find ecological niches in which to reconstitute themselves as living practices rather than therapeutic reconstructions or nostalgic idealizations.
