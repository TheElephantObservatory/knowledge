# Why Science Cannot Remove the Scientist from Its Own Ontology

The knower was never outside the picture.

---

> Modern science methodologically removes the knowing subject from its picture of reality, then mistakes that removal for a complete ontology. A genuinely coherent naturalism must account not only for physics but for the physicists — the knowers whose intelligence and situatedness make science possible in the first place.

One of the deepest errors of modern empirical natural science is that it factors out the subject of knowing through method and analytics — as though you can actually get the knower out of the picture. But you cannot. Any intelligible framework has to include a theory of the physicists, not just a theory of physics. UTOK addresses this by marrying the unique epistemological portal of the human subject to the third-person behavioral view that natural science provides — what can be called a transjective vantage point. Transjectivity is not a third static perspective alongside the subjective and objective; it is the dynamic interplay between them across time. Behavioral investment cannot be located inside the organism or inside the environment — it is the unfolding workflow of work-effort and consequence feeding back through the whole system. The error is mistaking the methodological factoring-out of the subject for a complete ontology. Reductive materialism generates a worldview that cannot situate itself — it cannot account for the scientists generating the physics, the biology, the chemistry. What extended naturalism requires is an ontology that shows not only what is derivable from the natural sciences, but what is presupposed by them: namely, scientists, an intelligible world, real measurement, and the knower in relation to the known. This is a neoplatonic move — it says yes, but you are presupposing intelligence and an intelligible world, and I want to ask: what kind of ontology makes those presuppositions coherent rather than illusory? The argument for emergence is symmetrically equal to an argument for emanation, and rationality is not just inferential derivation — it is also presupposition upward.

## Apprentice

Science works by being objective — by removing personal bias and the individual observer from its findings. This is a powerful method, but somewhere along the way, a subtle error crept in: people started treating this methodological move as though it described the whole of reality. If your picture of the world has no place for the person doing the picturing, something essential is missing. You need a theory of the physicists, not just a theory of physics.

The concept of transjectivity offers a way forward. Rather than choosing between a purely subjective view (what it's like from the inside) and a purely objective view (what can be measured from the outside), transjectivity names the living, dynamic interplay between the two. Think of how an animal invests effort in its environment and gets consequences back — that process doesn't live neatly inside the animal or outside it. It unfolds across both, over time.

This insight pushes toward a deeper question: what kind of world must already be in place for science to work at all? There must be knowers, an intelligible world, and real relationships between them. A complete picture of nature has to include these preconditions, not sweep them under the rug. The goal isn't to reject science but to expand our understanding of nature so it can honestly include the minds that do the understanding.

## Adept

Reductive materialism commits a foundational error by conflating methodological procedure with ontological completeness. The factoring-out of the knowing subject — essential for controlled empirical inquiry — gets mistaken for a demonstration that subjectivity is ontologically dispensable. But any intelligible framework must include a theory of the physicists, not merely a theory of physics. The Unified Theory of Knowledge (UTOK) addresses this gap by articulating a transjective vantage point: the dynamic, temporally unfolding interplay between first-person experiential access and third-person behavioral description. Transjectivity is not a static midpoint between subjective and objective but the living process through which behavioral investment — the organism's work-effort and its environmental consequences — flows across the system boundary.

This reframing carries serious ontological implications. Extended naturalism, as distinct from reductive naturalism, demands an ontology that accounts not only for what the natural sciences derive but for what they presuppose: scientists capable of measurement, an intelligible world amenable to rational inquiry, and the epistemic relation between knower and known. The move here is recognizably neoplatonic in structure — it asks what kind of reality must obtain for the presuppositions of scientific practice to be coherent rather than accidental.

Crucially, the argument runs in both directions. If emergence describes how complex properties arise from simpler substrates, then emanation describes the downward presuppositional logic — the conditions of intelligibility that higher-order realities impose on our understanding of lower-order ones. Rationality is not exhausted by inferential derivation from below; it also operates through presupposition upward. A naturalism that cannot situate its own practitioners within its ontology is, by its own standards, incomplete.

## Magus

The methodological bracketing of subjectivity in empirical natural science — indispensable for experimental control — has been illicitly promoted to an ontological thesis: that the knowing subject is eliminable from a complete description of nature. This is the core error of reductive materialism. It generates a worldview structurally incapable of situating itself, unable to account for the very scientists producing physics, chemistry, and biology. Gregg Henriques's UTOK framework responds by constructing a transjective epistemology — not a static third perspective alongside the subjective and objective, but the dynamic, diachronic interplay between the first-person epistemological portal and the third-person behavioral view. Behavioral investment, the central unit of analysis, is irreducibly relational: it cannot be localized within the organism or within the environment but unfolds as a recursive workflow of work-effort and consequence feeding back through the entire organism-environment system.

The deeper philosophical move is explicitly neoplatonic in character. Extended naturalism requires an ontology that discloses not merely what is derivable from natural-scientific inquiry but what is presupposed by it: intelligible structure, real measurement, rational agents, and the epistemic relation binding knower to known. The challenge to reductive materialism is symmetrical: the argument for emergence — complex properties arising from simpler substrates — is structurally mirrored by an argument for emanation, the downward logic of presupposition whereby higher-order conditions of intelligibility constrain and contextualize lower-order descriptions.

Rationality, on this account, is not exhausted by inferential derivation upward from foundational physical facts. It also operates through presupposition — asking what must already be the case for derivation itself to be coherent. The question is not whether intelligence and intelligibility exist within nature, but what ontological architecture renders their existence non-accidental. Any naturalism that cannot answer this question without self-referential incoherence has not yet earned the title of a complete worldview.
