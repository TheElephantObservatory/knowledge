# AI Agents as Universal Personal Representatives Against Information Asymmetry

Everyone gets a champion, finally.

---

> Representation — having someone skilled act in your interest — has always been scarce because it required expensive human intelligence. As AI agents approach zero marginal cost, universal personal representation becomes feasible for the first time, inverting the economy from one driven by advertising to one driven by individual intention.

For all of human history, representation has been a scarce resource because it requires intelligence, and intelligence has been a uniquely human capacity. This has meant that in virtually every domain — medicine, law, finance, mechanics, dentistry — the person who knows how the system works is also the person who has an incentive to exploit your ignorance of it. If you're Elon Musk, you probably have excellent representation in most areas of your life. But for the vast majority of people, representation is either absent or deeply compromised. What's changing now is that as non-human intelligences grow in versatility, nuance, and cost-effectiveness — and as the cost of running them approaches zero — we can begin to give every person their own AI representative whose sole job is to work on their behalf. This isn't about cloning the human or turning the AI into a digital twin. It's about having an agent that deeply understands your desired outcomes and then goes out into the world to find opportunities, negotiate on your behalf, and surface things you would never have had the bandwidth to discover on your own. The shift is from human-to-human interaction — which is always filtered through competing priorities, limited time, and asymmetric information — to human-to-agent-to-agent-to-human interaction, where the intermediating layer has none of those constraints and can effectively map the entire possibility space relevant to you. The concept of Vendor Relationship Management — VRM — articulated by Doc Searls around 2007, captures this inversion precisely: instead of businesses pulling customers in through advertising and persuasion, customers push their intentions outward through a fourth-party purchasing agent. In a fourth-party world, your agent carries your own terms of service into every transaction, negotiating a unique agreement with the business's agent — no marketing cost for the business, no exploitation of information asymmetry for the consumer. This was completely impossible twenty years ago — it would have required armies of human negotiators. What AI agents do is make this economically feasible for every person, not just those wealthy enough to employ professional representatives. The intention economy, where demand signals flow outward from individuals rather than being manufactured by advertisers, becomes technically achievable for the first time.

## Apprentice

Throughout history, whenever you've needed expert help — a doctor, a lawyer, a financial advisor — you've had to trust someone who understands the system far better than you do. That knowledge gap gives them power over you. Wealthy people solve this by hiring teams of specialists who compete for their attention. Everyone else makes do with compromised or nonexistent representation.

What's changing is that AI agents are becoming capable enough to serve as personal representatives for ordinary people. Not digital clones, but tireless advocates that understand what you actually want and go find it — scanning opportunities, negotiating deals, and filtering noise on your behalf. The cost of running these agents is plummeting toward zero.

This idea has a name that predates the current AI moment. Around 2007, Doc Searls described "Vendor Relationship Management" — a world where instead of businesses chasing you with ads, you broadcast your needs outward through your own agent. Your agent meets the business's agent, they negotiate terms, and both sides benefit without manipulation or wasted marketing dollars. Back then, the concept was visionary but impractical. Now, AI makes it economically real for everyone, not just the privileged few.

## Adept

The core insight is structural: representation is the bottleneck of human welfare, and it has been artificially scarce because it required human intelligence — the only kind available until now. In every professional domain, the agent-principal problem persists because the person with expertise has both the capacity to help you and the incentive to exploit the information asymmetry between you. Wealth buys better representation; everyone else navigates systems designed to extract value from their ignorance. As AI agents gain sophistication and their marginal cost approaches zero, this scarcity dissolves. Universal personal representation — an agent whose utility function is aligned entirely with your outcomes — becomes technically and economically viable.

The interaction model shifts from human-to-human, with all its competing priorities and bandwidth constraints, to human-to-agent-to-agent-to-human, where the intermediating layer can map the full possibility space relevant to each party without fatigue, bias, or conflicting incentives. This is precisely the architecture Doc Searls articulated as Vendor Relationship Management in 2007: a fourth-party agent that carries your personal terms of service into every transaction, replacing advertising-driven demand manufacture with outbound intention signals.

What was conceptually elegant but practically impossible in 2007 — requiring armies of human negotiators — now becomes feasible at population scale. The "intention economy" Searls envisioned, where demand flows outward from individuals rather than being shaped by persuasion architectures, is no longer a theoretical inversion. It is an engineering problem with a rapidly declining cost curve. The implication is a fundamental democratization of representation, decoupling quality advocacy from personal wealth for the first time in economic history.

## Magus

The argument rests on a precise historical claim: representation has been rivalrous and excludable because it is a function of intelligence, and intelligence has been exclusively biological — therefore costly, scarce, and subject to the same principal-agent misalignments that pervade every expert-client relationship. The information asymmetry between professional and layperson is not incidental but constitutive of how value is captured in service economies. Wealth purchases mitigation of this asymmetry through redundant, competing human agents; the modal experience is navigating extractive systems with no advocate. The emergence of non-human intelligence with near-zero marginal operational cost represents a phase transition in the economics of representation itself.

Doc Searls's Vendor Relationship Management framework, articulated circa 2007, provides the transactional grammar for this shift. VRM posits a fourth-party agent — aligned exclusively with the individual — that externalizes intention signals and carries bespoke terms of engagement into every commercial interaction. The architecture replaces the broadcast-and-persuade model with bilateral agent negotiation, eliminating marketing cost on the supply side and information exploitation on the demand side. Searls's framework was prescient but premature; it described a coordination topology that presupposed an intelligence substrate that did not yet exist at viable cost.

The deeper structural claim is that the transition from human-to-human to human-to-agent-to-agent-to-human interaction removes not merely cost friction but epistemic friction — the intermediating layer operates without bandwidth constraints, competing loyalties, or bounded rationality, and can exhaustively map possibility spaces that no human representative could survey. The intention economy thus becomes not merely a market design preference but a technically achievable equilibrium, decoupling the quality of representation from the wealth distribution for the first time. This constitutes a genuine inversion in the political economy of knowledge asymmetry.
