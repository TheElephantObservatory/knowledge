# Why Psychology Cannot Agree on What Mind Means

Three ghosts wearing the same name.

---

> Psychology has never resolved what 'mind' actually refers to — neurocognitive processing, subjective experience, or self-conscious reflection. Gregg Henriques argues these are three ontologically distinct phenomena, and that placing each at its proper joint point in nature's emergent hierarchy finally gives psychology a coherent subject matter.

One of the deepest structural failures in the history of psychology is that the field never achieved what every other natural science takes for granted: a clear ontological correspondence between the science and the thing in the world it is studying. Physics studies energy and matter. Biology studies life. Nobody disputes what biology is about. But when you arrive at psychology and ask 'what is mind?', the field dissolves into foundational disagreement. Gregg Henriques argues this is not a failure of effort or intelligence — it is a failure of conceptual architecture. The word 'mind' has been used to refer to at least three entirely different things: first, neurocognitive activity, the information-processing behavior of the nervous system and the animal's sensorimotor engagement with its environment; second, subjective conscious experience, the felt inner life that is epistemologically unavailable to third-person scientific observation; and third, self-conscious justification, the propositional, reflective, self-narrating mind that Descartes identified as uniquely human. These are not three aspects of the same thing — they have different ontological referents and different epistemological access conditions. Conflating these three has produced the fragmentation, the behaviorist-versus-mentalist wars, the consciousness-versus-cognition splits, and the inability to build cumulative knowledge across subfields. This failure is not incidental — it is structurally guaranteed by what Henriques calls the Enlightenment Gap: when Newtonian physics established third-person, exterior, measurable, observer-independent observation as the gold standard for real knowledge, it created an unresolvable tension with the interior, first-person, felt dimension of experience. The result is a civilizational split in which scientific knowledge and subjective knowing operate in separate, mutually suspicious silos, with no shared descriptive metaphysics to place them in right relation. Psychology sits at the precise epicenter of this gap — it is the discipline that should bridge matter and mind, third-person and first-person, the generalizable and the idiographic particular. The Tree of Knowledge system resolves this by placing each definition of mind at a distinct joint point in the emergent stack of nature: minded animals at the transition from living organisms, subjective experience as the inner dimension of that animal mindedness, and justificatory self-consciousness at the transition into the culture-person plane. Once those joint points are specified, psychology finally has what biology has always had — a principled answer to the question of what it is actually studying. Henriques argues this is not merely academic housekeeping: solving the problem of psychology — giving mind a clear ontological address in the emergent stack of nature — is the move that could close the Enlightenment Gap and enable a second Enlightenment, one in which matter and mind are placed in right relation, and scientific, subjective, and social knowledge are understood as distinct but coherently related epistemic modes.

## Apprentice

Every mature science knows what it studies. Biology studies life. Physics studies matter and energy. But psychology has never settled the most basic question: what is mind? Gregg Henriques argues this isn't because psychologists aren't smart enough — it's because the word 'mind' has been used to mean three completely different things. First, there's the brain doing its information-processing work — animals navigating their environments, sensing and responding. Second, there's the felt inner life of experience — what it's like to see red or feel pain. Third, there's the uniquely human capacity to reflect on our own thoughts, build narratives about ourselves, and justify our beliefs to others. These aren't just three angles on the same thing. They refer to different realities and require different ways of knowing.

The confusion traces back to a deeper civilizational problem. When modern science established objective, measurable observation as the only path to real knowledge, it left subjective experience — the felt dimension of being alive — without a legitimate home. Psychology got stuck in the middle of this split, expected to be a science of something that doesn't fully fit the rules of science.

Henriques proposes a solution: each meaning of 'mind' belongs at a specific transition point in nature's layered emergence — from matter to life to animal mind to human culture. Once you place each one correctly, psychology finally knows what it's studying, and the ancient divide between objective knowledge and inner experience begins to heal.

## Adept

Gregg Henriques identifies a foundational crisis in psychology that is structural rather than empirical: the discipline has never achieved ontological clarity about its subject matter. The term 'mind' conflates at least three distinct referents — neurocognitive processing (the functional information-processing of nervous systems and sensorimotor engagement), subjective conscious experience (the first-person phenomenal dimension inaccessible to third-person observation), and self-conscious justification (the propositional, reflective, self-narrating capacity Descartes identified as distinctively human). These referents differ not merely in emphasis but in ontological status and epistemological access conditions. Their systematic conflation has generated psychology's recurring fractures: behaviorism versus mentalism, cognition versus consciousness, nomothetic versus idiographic approaches.

This confusion is not accidental but structurally produced by what Henriques calls the Enlightenment Gap. The Newtonian revolution established third-person, exterior, quantitative observation as the criterion of legitimate knowledge, thereby rendering first-person subjective experience epistemically suspect. Psychology, positioned precisely at the intersection of matter and mind, inherited this unresolved tension as its defining problem rather than recognizing it as such.

The Tree of Knowledge system offers a resolution by mapping each meaning of mind onto a distinct joint point in nature's emergent complexity hierarchy: minded animal behavior at the Life-to-Mind transition, subjective experience as the interior dimension of that transition, and justificatory self-consciousness at the Mind-to-Culture transition. This move gives psychology what biology has always possessed — a principled ontological address for its subject matter. Henriques argues that this clarification is not merely disciplinary but civilizational: resolving psychology's foundational incoherence is the key to closing the Enlightenment Gap and enabling a coherent integration of scientific, phenomenological, and humanistic ways of knowing.

## Magus

Henriques's diagnosis cuts to the metatheoretical bone: psychology's fragmentation is not a contingent historical accident but an inevitable consequence of ontological equivocation at the foundational level. The lexeme 'mind' indexes three phenomena with distinct ontological referents and non-interchangeable epistemological access conditions: (1) neurocognitive functional processing — the behavioral investment system of nervous systems operating via sensorimotor loops and information processing; (2) subjective phenomenal experience — the first-person qualia-bearing dimension that is constitutively unavailable to third-person empirical methodology; and (3) self-conscious justificatory cognition — the propositional, linguistically mediated, recursively self-referential capacity that generates the Cartesian cogito and undergirds the culture-person plane. The failure to parse these as categorically distinct has made cumulative theoretical integration across psychology's subfields structurally impossible.

This equivocation is itself a symptom of a deeper epistemic fracture Henriques terms the Enlightenment Gap: the post-Newtonian privileging of exterior, quantitative, observer-independent description as the exclusive criterion of epistemic legitimacy created an irresolvable aporia for any science whose explanandum includes irreducibly interior phenomena. Psychology occupies the precise fault line — tasked with bridging res extensa and res cogitans without a descriptive metaphysics adequate to the task.

The Tree of Knowledge system resolves this by specifying joint points in the emergent complexity stack where each referent of 'mind' finds its ontological address: animal mindedness at the Life-Mind transition, phenomenal interiority as its subjective dimension, and justificatory self-consciousness at the Mind-Culture transition. This is not taxonomic tidying but a foundational respecification that, Henriques contends, constitutes the necessary precondition for closing the Enlightenment Gap — enabling a second Enlightenment in which scientific, subjective-phenomenological, and intersubjective-normative epistemic modes are recognized as distinct yet coherently situated within a unified natural ontology.
