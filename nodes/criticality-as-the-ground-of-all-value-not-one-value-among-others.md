# Criticality as the Ground of All Value, Not One Value Among Others

Before good and evil, the capacity to care

---

> The capacity to make sense of the world isn't just one value among many — it's the precondition for valuing anything at all. Criticality, the process that sustains this capacity, functions as a meta-norm: the ground that makes all other norms possible.

A subtle but important philosophical move is made in this conversation regarding the normative status of criticality and the collapse of the is-ought distinction. Brett Anderson claims that the process occurring at the border between order and chaos — self-organized criticality as it manifests in relevance realization — is not merely descriptively optimal but genuinely good in some non-trivial sense. Vervaeke supplements this by invoking the collapse of the fact-value distinction (Putnam), the collapse of the theory-data distinction (Quine), and the argument from natural ethical facts (Casebeer). The deeper argument is that intelligibility functions as a meta-norm: if a system is not oriented toward intelligibility — toward the ongoing capacity to make sense of its world — then all of its other norms are rendered meaningless, because they cannot be evaluated, revised, or applied. Criticality, as the process by which systems maintain the capacity for ongoing complexification and adaptive response, is therefore not merely one value among others but the condition of possibility for valuing anything at all. This is what Vervaeke identifies with Plato's notion of the Good — not a specific content but the meta-normative ground that makes all other normative claims possible.

## Apprentice

Most people think of "what's true" and "what's good" as completely separate questions. But a powerful philosophical argument suggests they may not be. The idea starts with a simple observation: living systems thrive when they operate at a sweet spot between rigid order and total chaos. At this edge, they can adapt, learn, and grow. This isn't just a description of how things happen to work — it may point to something genuinely good.

The key move is this: if a system loses the ability to make sense of its world, then none of its other values matter anymore. You can't pursue justice, beauty, or truth if you can't even figure out what's going on. The ongoing capacity to understand and respond to reality isn't just useful — it's the foundation that every other value depends on.

This echoes an ancient idea from Plato, who spoke of "the Good" not as any particular thing we should want, but as the deeper condition that makes wanting anything meaningful in the first place. The modern version grounds this in how complex systems actually work: staying at the creative edge between order and chaos is what keeps the whole project of meaning alive.

## Adept

Brett Anderson and John Vervaeke develop a striking claim: self-organized criticality — the dynamic process by which systems maintain themselves at the edge of order and chaos — is not merely descriptively optimal but genuinely normative. The argument hinges on the concept of intelligibility as a meta-norm. If a cognitive system loses its capacity for relevance realization, its ability to adaptively frame and reframe its engagement with the world, then all of its other normative commitments become inert. They cannot be evaluated, revised, or applied. Criticality is therefore not one value competing alongside others but the condition of possibility for valuing as such.

This move draws on several philosophical collapses: Putnam's dissolution of the fact-value dichotomy, Quine's undermining of the theory-data distinction, and Casebeer's naturalistic account of ethical facts. Together, these create conceptual space for treating a process description — how systems optimally self-organize — as simultaneously a normative claim about what is good. The is-ought gap doesn't need to be bridged because, at the level of intelligibility, it was never fully open.

Vervaeke explicitly identifies this meta-normative ground with Plato's Form of the Good — understood not as a specific moral content but as the structural precondition that renders all other normative discourse meaningful. The Good is what makes it possible to distinguish better from worse at all. Criticality, as the dynamic that sustains ongoing complexification and adaptive sense-making, occupies precisely this role: it is the process without which no norm can function as a norm.

## Magus

The philosophical move at stake is the identification of self-organized criticality — specifically as it manifests through relevance realization — with a genuinely meta-normative function. Anderson's claim that the process at the border of order and chaos is not merely descriptively but normatively significant receives its deepest grounding through Vervaeke's synthesis of Putnam's collapse of the fact-value distinction, Quine's holistic undermining of the theory-data boundary, and Casebeer's naturalized virtue ethics. The convergence of these positions dissolves the conceptual barriers that would otherwise prevent a process ontology from carrying normative weight.

The core argument proceeds transcendentally: intelligibility — the ongoing capacity of a system to make sense of and adaptively engage its world — functions as a necessary presupposition of all normative evaluation whatsoever. Any norm presupposes a normative agent capable of recognizing, applying, and revising that norm, which in turn presupposes the sustained operation of relevance realization at criticality. A system that has lost criticality has not merely lost one good among others; it has lost the capacity for goods to show up as goods. The is-ought distinction collapses here not through a naive naturalistic fallacy but because the very machinery required to draw normative distinctions is itself a natural process with specifiable optimal conditions.

This is what Vervaeke retrieves from Plato's Form of the Good: not a supreme object of valuation but the anormative — or rather, meta-normative — ground that constitutes the possibility space within which valuation operates. The Good is to norms what light is to visibility in the allegory of the Cave. Criticality, understood as the dynamical regime that sustains the ongoing capacity for complexification, integration, and adaptive responsiveness, is the processual instantiation of this Platonic insight within a naturalistic, 4E cognitive science framework.
