# Soul Expression Unfolds Backward: Living Before Knowing

The thread you follow before you can name it.

---

> Soul expression is not a plan you execute but a thread you follow — recognized fully only in retrospect, felt in real time as a subtle resonance in the heart, and continually unearthed from the conditioning of social and economic expectations.

Soul expression is not primarily a matter of self-knowledge — it is something that unfolds through living and can only be fully recognized in retrospect. Abigail Lynam observes that the path of discovery looks different for different people, but what seems consistent is that soul expression is something we are given to, something we discover as we peel away what obscures our true nature. We can look back in hindsight and trace a path that leads all the way to some early shaping experience — but we could not have known its meaning the day it happened. Geoff Fitch adds that there is a discernment in the heart that registers, in real time, whether we are following that thread or not — a felt sense of contact with something that wants to move through us, that is personal but not fully of the ordinary self's making. This is not the same as having a plan or a vocation in the conventional sense. It also cannot be separated from the social and economic structures that tell us what we are supposed to be doing at thirty or forty or fifty, what our parents wanted for us, what the industrial system needs from us. Soul expression requires unearthing oneself from all of that conditioning — not as a one-time act of liberation but as an ongoing process of following threads whose full meaning will only become visible later, from a different vantage point.

## Apprentice

There is a deep part of each person that wants to express itself through the way they live — but it doesn't come with a roadmap. You can't sit down and figure it out like a career plan. Instead, it unfolds through experience. You follow what pulls you, make choices that feel right for reasons you can't fully explain, and only later — sometimes years later — can you look back and see a coherent thread running through it all. An early experience that seemed random turns out to have been shaping everything.

Abigail Lynam suggests that this process looks different for everyone, but what's consistent is that it involves peeling away layers of conditioning — the expectations of parents, the pressures of economic life, the cultural scripts about what you should be doing at a given age. Geoff Fitch adds that even though the full picture only emerges in hindsight, there is a felt sense available in the moment — a quiet knowing in the heart that registers whether you are in contact with this deeper current or drifting away from it.

This isn't about one dramatic moment of self-discovery. It's an ongoing process of following threads whose meaning isn't yet clear, trusting a sense of direction that can't be fully justified in conventional terms, and gradually freeing yourself from stories about who you're supposed to be.

## Adept

Soul expression, as explored by Abigail Lynam and Geoff Fitch, resists the framework of self-knowledge as prerequisite for authentic living. It is not something one arrives at through introspection and then enacts. Rather, it is something one is given to — a current that moves through a life and becomes legible only retrospectively. Lynam observes that while the path of discovery varies across individuals, a consistent feature is the peeling away of what obscures one's true nature, revealing a thread that can be traced back to early shaping experiences whose significance was invisible at the time they occurred.

Fitch contributes a phenomenological dimension: a felt sense of discernment located in the heart that registers, in real time, whether one is following this deeper thread or not. This is not intuition in the casual sense but a somatic and affective contact with something that wants to move through the person — something personal yet not reducible to the ordinary self's intentions or preferences. It operates beneath the level of plans, vocations, and identity narratives.

Critically, this process cannot be abstracted from the social and economic conditioning that shapes what people believe they should be doing with their lives. Soul expression requires ongoing disentanglement from parental expectations, industrial-era notions of productivity, and age-graded cultural scripts. This is not a singular act of liberation but a recursive practice of following threads whose full meaning becomes visible only from a later vantage point — a kind of living hermeneutic in which the interpreter and the text are the same unfolding life.

## Magus

The insight articulated by Lynam and Fitch constitutes a fundamental reorientation of the relationship between self-knowledge and soul expression. Against the Socratic inheritance that positions self-knowledge as the precondition for the well-lived life, this perspective holds that soul expression is ontologically prior to its recognition — it is something one is given to rather than something one constructs. Its intelligibility is essentially retrospective: the meaning of formative experiences becomes available only from a later hermeneutic position, rendering the soul's path a kind of nachträglichkeit operating at the level of vocation rather than trauma.

Fitch's contribution specifies the epistemological mode through which contact with this current is maintained in real time. The discernment he locates in the heart is neither cognitive judgment nor mere affect but a felt registration of alignment or misalignment with something that wants to move through the person. This formulation carefully navigates between voluntarism and determinism: the soul's expression is personal but not of the ordinary self's making, suggesting an agential locus that exceeds ego-identification without dissolving into transpersonal abstraction. It resonates with Hillman's acorn theory while grounding it in embodied, moment-to-moment phenomenology.

The structural dimension is equally essential. Soul expression does not unfold in a vacuum but against — and through — the resistance of social and economic conditioning: internalized parental projections, industrial temporalities, normative life-stage scripts. The unearthing of oneself from these formations is not a punctual emancipatory event but a recursive, lifelong practice of following threads whose significance remains partially opaque. The full topology of the path is visible only from a vantage point one has not yet reached, making soul expression an exercise in prospective faith underwritten by retrospective coherence.
