# Pistis: How 'Faith' Was Mistranslated Into Delusion

The surfer knows what the map cannot hold.

---

> The Greek word pistis — translated as 'faith' — never meant believing propositions without evidence. It meant embodied, relational knowing, like a surfer's mastery of waves. Both new atheists and fundamentalists attacked or defended a concept that neither side actually understood.

There is a fundamental difference between belief as a mental operation and belief as an existential commitment — and this distinction maps directly onto two different ontologies. When we treat belief as a strictly mental operation, we are operating within a substance ontology: we imagine that a mind, as a discrete object, holds propositions about other discrete objects. This is the model that the new atheist movement implicitly assumed when it attacked faith — and it is also the model that fundamentalist religion implicitly assumed when it defended faith. Both sides were fighting over the same bad map.

The alternative is to understand belief — what the Greeks called pistis — as something closer to mastery or embodied wisdom. Plato actually had three categories: doxa, which is opinion or folk explanation; episteme, which is theoretical understanding of underlying principles; and pistis, which is the deeply intimate, experiential relationship with a domain that comes from sustained engagement. When a surfer tries to explain how they surf, they can give you maybe thirty percent of the story in words. The rest lives in their body, in their relationship with the water, in something that cannot be fully articulated. That unreachable remainder is not a deficiency — it is the most real part. Pistis is that kind of knowing.

When Paul uses the word pistis in his epistles, he is writing in the same cultural context as Plato, for the same audience. He cannot mean anything other than this embodied, relational, lived knowing. The English word 'faith' has been catastrophically mistranslated and misunderstood — particularly in the aftermath of the Enlightenment and the new atheist reaction to fundamentalism — as meaning maximally delusional belief: the willingness to assert propositions without evidence. But that is almost precisely the opposite of what the word means. Faith, properly understood, is not the substitution of words for reality. It is the cultivation of a capacity for relationship with reality — the ongoing, embodied, humble practice of being in right relationship with the whole of what is real.

## Apprentice

There are two very different things people mean when they say 'belief.' One is a mental act — holding a statement to be true in your head, like checking a box. The other is something more like a surfer's relationship with the ocean: a deep, bodily, lived knowing that can never be fully put into words. When a surfer tries to explain how they read a wave, they can maybe articulate thirty percent of it. The rest lives in their muscles, their timing, their feel for the water. That inarticulate remainder isn't a failure of understanding — it's the deepest kind of understanding there is.

Plato actually had a word for this kind of knowing: pistis. It sat alongside opinion and theoretical knowledge as a distinct category — the intimate, experiential grasp that comes from sustained practice. When the apostle Paul used the same word, writing in Greek for a Greek-speaking audience, he meant the same thing: not blind assertion, but a cultivated capacity for relationship with reality.

The English word 'faith' has been disastrously misread — especially in modern debates between atheists and fundamentalists — as meaning the willingness to believe things without evidence. But both sides of that argument were fighting over a misunderstanding. Faith, in its original sense, is not the opposite of evidence. It is the kind of knowing that goes deeper than words can reach.

## Adept

A critical distinction separates belief as mental operation from belief as existential commitment, and this distinction maps onto two different ontologies. Treating belief as a mental act — a mind holding propositions about objects — presupposes substance ontology: discrete subjects representing discrete things. This is the implicit framework shared by both new atheist critics and fundamentalist defenders of faith. Both camps fought over the same impoverished model, disagreeing about whether propositional commitment without evidence was virtuous or delusional, while never questioning whether that model captured what 'faith' actually meant.

Plato's epistemology offers a corrective. He distinguished doxa (opinion, folk explanation), episteme (theoretical grasp of underlying principles), and pistis — the deeply embodied, relational mastery that comes from sustained engagement with a domain. Pistis names the kind of knowing a surfer has of the ocean: largely inarticulate, irreducible to propositions, and more real for being so. The inarticulate remainder is not epistemic deficit but epistemic surplus.

When Paul employs pistis in his epistles, he writes within this same Greek intellectual milieu. The word cannot mean 'maximally delusional belief' — the willingness to assert propositions without evidence — because that concept did not exist in the semantic field he inhabited. The catastrophic mistranslation of pistis as propositional faith is largely a post-Enlightenment artifact, hardened by the fundamentalist-atheist dialectic. Properly recovered, pistis names the ongoing, humble, embodied practice of cultivating right relationship with the whole of what is real — closer to a discipline than a doctrine.

## Magus

The substance-ontological framing of belief — in which a discrete mental subject entertains propositional attitudes toward discrete objects — generates the entire fundamentalist-new atheist dialectic as an artifact of its own assumptions. Both positions presuppose that faith names a doxastic operation: the holding-true of propositions, differing only on whether such holding is epistemically warranted. This shared presupposition is the bad map over which both sides wage their proxy war, never interrogating the cartography itself.

Recovering the Platonic tripartition dissolves the impasse. Doxa operates at the level of folk phenomenology; episteme grasps underlying aitiai through theoretical articulation; pistis designates the intimate, participatory knowing that emerges from sustained embodied engagement — what later traditions would recognize as connaturality or practical wisdom irreducible to propositional content. The surfer's relationship with the wave is the paradigm case: the inarticulate surplus is not epistemic privation but the locus of maximal contact with the real. Pistis thus occupies a fundamentally different ontological register than propositional belief — it is relational, processual, and constitutively resistant to full discursive capture.

Paul's deployment of pistis in his epistles is intelligible only within this semantic field. Writing in Koine Greek for audiences formed by Hellenistic paideia, he cannot intend the post-Enlightenment sense of credal assertion absent evidential warrant — a meaning that required centuries of Latin theological mediation, nominalist metaphysics, and Reformation-era confessionalization to produce. The catastrophic narrowing of pistis to fides quae creditur, and thence to 'faith' as epistemically irresponsible commitment, represents a progressive ontological flattening. What pistis actually names is the cultivated disposition of ongoing, embodied, humble participation in the real — an existential comportment rather than a cognitive state.
