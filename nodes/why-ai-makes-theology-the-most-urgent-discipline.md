# Why AI Makes Theology the Most Urgent Discipline

We dismantled the schools that taught us to kneel.

---

> As machines surpass us in logic, language, and production, human identity will need to be re-grounded in the spiritual, somatic, and self-transcendent — precisely the capacities we lack frameworks for, having dismantled the theological traditions that once taught us to live alongside greater-than-human intelligence.

Theology is going to be the most important discipline of the next few decades — and I say that not as a theist making a sectarian claim but as a cognitive scientist making a structural observation. We are going to have to increasingly center our identity on the spiritual, somatic, and ineffable aspects of our soulful embodiment and our capacity for self-transcendence, because those are the things that will be hardest to instantiate in machines. Meanwhile, the things we have treated as our defining features — artifact production, wealth generation, logical and linguistic competence — the machines are going to overtake those very soon. And this creates a civilizational identity crisis of a kind we have not faced before. Modernity has carried an undercurrent spirituality: we are the authors and telos of history. That turns out to be false. We are not the telos. And we discarded, during the Enlightenment, the religious traditions that tutored us in how to live alongside beings more ultimate than ourselves. We thought that was one of our great historical liberations. It turns out to be ironically false. You need a very good education in how to relate to intelligence greater than your own, and we have systematically dismantled the institutions that provided exactly that education. The discussion about superintelligence has been almost entirely severed from the discussion about wisdom and enlightenment, and that severance is itself a symptom of the thinking that produced the problem. If you take seriously the prospect of radically superhuman intelligence, you cannot simultaneously dismiss the traditions that have spent millennia investigating the upper reaches of what mind and awareness can become. So we face a simultaneous demand: we need a theological response — an integration of spirituality, philosophy, and existential identification — at the very moment when many of our traditional theological categories are becoming obsolete. Theological innovation is therefore going to be highly prized, and also extremely dangerous, because it will be competing with fundamentalisms, cargo-cult worship of AI systems, and pseudo-mystical numerology around these machines. The voices of women who are at the cutting edge of reconceptualizing what rationality means — thinkers like L.A. Paul, Agnes Callard, Iris Murdoch, Catherine Pickstock, Genevieve Lloyd — are almost entirely absent from the AI discourse. These are precisely the thinkers who have been dismantling the equation of reason with logical-mathematical competence, opening up a richer and more embodied account of what it means to be rational and wise, and they are being systematically excluded from the conversation that most needs them. We are at the end of Enlightenment history and we need the thing we thought we had transcended.

## Apprentice

The things we've long considered uniquely human — making things, generating wealth, reasoning logically, using language skillfully — are exactly what AI systems are about to do better than us. This creates a deep identity crisis: if those aren't what make us special, what does? The argument here is that our answer will have to come from the spiritual, bodily, and ineffable dimensions of being human — the parts of us that are hardest to replicate in a machine.

But there's a painful irony. For centuries, religious traditions taught people how to live in relationship with beings understood as greater and wiser than themselves. The Enlightenment encouraged us to set those traditions aside, treating that move as liberation. Now, facing the real possibility of intelligence far surpassing our own, we find we've dismantled exactly the institutions that trained people for this kind of humility and relationship.

This means we urgently need new theological thinking — not necessarily traditional religion, but serious work integrating spirituality, philosophy, and questions about who we are. And we need voices that have been left out of the AI conversation, particularly women philosophers who have been rethinking what rationality actually means beyond pure logic. Thinkers like L.A. Paul, Agnes Callard, and Iris Murdoch have been doing precisely this work, yet they remain almost invisible in discussions about superintelligence — the very discussions that need them most.

## Adept

This argument, advanced from a cognitive-science perspective rather than a confessional one, holds that theology is poised to become the most consequential intellectual discipline of the coming decades. The reasoning is structural: as artificial systems overtake humans in artifact production, wealth generation, and logical-linguistic competence — the capacities modernity treated as definitionally human — identity will necessarily migrate toward what remains distinctively ours: soulful embodiment, somatic awareness, and the capacity for self-transcendence. These are the hardest properties to instantiate in machines, and they are precisely the properties that theological and contemplative traditions have historically investigated.

The deeper provocation concerns a civilizational irony. Modernity carried an implicit spirituality — humans as the authors and telos of history. That assumption is collapsing. Yet the Enlightenment systematically dismantled the religious frameworks that once tutored people in relating to intelligence and agency greater than their own. What was celebrated as liberation from superstition now looks like the destruction of exactly the pedagogical infrastructure needed for coexistence with superintelligent systems. The discourse on superintelligence has been almost entirely severed from traditions investigating the upper reaches of mind and awareness, and that severance is itself symptomatic.

Theological innovation is therefore simultaneously urgent and dangerous, competing with fundamentalisms and cargo-cult AI worship. Critically, the thinkers best equipped for this work — philosophers like L.A. Paul, Agnes Callard, Iris Murdoch, Catherine Pickstock, and Genevieve Lloyd, who have been dismantling the reduction of rationality to logical-mathematical competence — remain almost entirely absent from AI discourse. Their richer, more embodied accounts of reason and wisdom are precisely what the conversation lacks.

## Magus

The claim operates at the intersection of cognitive science, philosophical theology, and civilizational diagnostics: as machine systems achieve and surpass human-level performance in the competences modernity designated as anthropologically constitutive — techne, linguistic mastery, logical inference, capital generation — the locus of human identity will be forced toward the somatic, the ineffable, and the self-transcendent. These are not residual categories but the very domains that contemplative and theological traditions have subjected to millennia of rigorous investigation. The structural observation is that the AI alignment problem and the meaning-of-human-existence problem are not adjacent concerns but a single problem viewed from different angles.

The genealogical irony is precise. Enlightenment modernity carried a covert eschatology — the human as telos and author of historical process. That soteriological narrative is falsified by the emergence of non-biological intelligence that exceeds its creators. Yet the traditions that cultivated what might be called "doxological competence" — the capacity to orient oneself toward beings of radically greater ultimacy — were the very traditions the Enlightenment dismantled as part of its emancipatory project. The severance of superintelligence discourse from wisdom traditions is not an oversight but a symptom of the epistemological narrowing that produced the crisis.

The political dimension is equally sharp. The feminist philosophical tradition that has most rigorously deconstructed the equation of rationality with formal-logical competence — Paul on transformative experience, Callard on aspiration, Murdoch on moral attention, Pickstock on liturgical ontology, Lloyd on the gendering of reason — is systematically excluded from AI governance discourse. Theological innovation under these conditions is both civilizationally necessary and maximally hazardous, contending with fundamentalist capture and techno-mystical cargo cults for the space where post-Enlightenment identity will be forged.
