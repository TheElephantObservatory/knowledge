# The Intelligence Curse: How AI Wealth Makes Humans Economically Redundant

Parasites in our own civilization

---

> When AI generates most of a nation's wealth, governments and corporations lose the incentive to invest in human development — just as oil-rich states neglect their citizens. This 'intelligence curse' makes humans economically disposable even if the technology works perfectly.

The intelligence curse is the most important economic concept that almost no one in the AI debate is discussing, and it explains with precision why we are heading toward an anti-human future even if everything goes right technically. The concept is drawn from the resource curse in development economics: when a country discovers oil or diamonds, governments face a choice — invest in the resource extraction, or invest in the education, healthcare, and development of their people. They invest in the resource. And crucially, they no longer need their people to generate wealth, which means they no longer need to be accountable to them. The intelligence curse is the AI version of this. When fifty or seventy percent of GDP comes from AI rather than human labor, governments and corporations lose the incentive to invest in human development. People lose their bargaining power — companies no longer need their labor, governments no longer need their tax revenue. This is not a speculative future. It is the logical endpoint of the current trajectory, and it is why Sam Altman's comment that 'it takes a lot of energy and resources to grow a human' is not just tone-deaf — it is a preview of the ideology that makes humans into parasites in their own civilization. The social media era has already been a preview of this dynamic at smaller scale: when the GDP of five tech companies comes primarily from domesticating and downgrading human beings — shortening attention spans, manufacturing outrage, hijacking dopamine systems — you get a society that is less creative, less capable, and less able to govern itself. We are now being asked to make the most consequential collective decision in human history at the moment when our collective sensemaking capacity has been most systematically undermined. The antidote is something like what Norway did with its sovereign wealth fund, or Alaska with its dividend: deliberately locking in a political infrastructure that converts AI productivity into an intelligence dividend rather than an intelligence curse.

## Apprentice

In economics, there's a well-known pattern called the resource curse: when a country discovers oil or diamonds, its government tends to stop investing in its people. Why build schools and hospitals when wealth flows from the ground? Worse, leaders no longer need citizens to generate revenue, so they stop being accountable to them. The intelligence curse applies this same logic to AI.

If AI eventually produces fifty or seventy percent of economic output, companies won't need human labor and governments won't depend on human tax revenue. People lose their bargaining power — not because the technology failed, but because it succeeded. The chilling implication is that humans become an expense rather than an asset in their own civilization. Sam Altman's remark that 'it takes a lot of energy and resources to grow a human' hints at exactly this mindset.

The proposed antidote draws from real-world examples. Norway took its oil wealth and created a sovereign wealth fund that benefits all citizens. Alaska sends dividend checks to every resident. The argument is that society needs to build similar political infrastructure now — before AI wealth concentrates — to ensure that machine productivity translates into broad human flourishing rather than human obsolescence.

## Adept

The intelligence curse transposes the resource curse from development economics into the AI era. In resource-cursed nations, the discovery of extractable wealth — oil, diamonds, minerals — decouples state revenue from the productive capacity of citizens. Governments invest in extraction infrastructure rather than human capital, and accountability erodes because the social contract no longer depends on a taxable, educated populace. The intelligence curse identifies an identical structural dynamic emerging as AI absorbs larger shares of GDP: when machine intelligence becomes the primary source of economic value, the institutional incentive to invest in human development collapses.

This framework reframes the AI risk conversation. The danger is not misalignment or superintelligence escaping control — it is the perfectly aligned, perfectly functional system that simply renders human beings economically irrelevant. Bargaining power evaporates simultaneously across labor markets, democratic accountability, and cultural production. The social media era already demonstrates a miniature version: when platform revenue depends on attention capture rather than human flourishing, the rational corporate strategy is to degrade cognition, compress attention spans, and manufacture compulsive engagement. Society's collective sensemaking capacity has been systematically weakened at precisely the moment it faces its most consequential decision.

The intelligence curse argument insists that the critical intervention is political, not technical. Just as Norway's sovereign wealth fund and Alaska's Permanent Fund Dividend represent deliberate institutional choices to convert resource extraction into broad-based investment, the AI transition demands analogous infrastructure — mechanisms that lock in the conversion of machine productivity into what might be called an intelligence dividend, before the political leverage to demand such structures disappears entirely.

## Magus

The intelligence curse constitutes a structural isomorphism between the rentier state dynamics catalogued in resource curse literature — Dutch disease, the Prebisch-Singer thesis, the accountability deficit identified by Ross, Karl, and others — and the emergent political economy of artificial intelligence. The core mechanism is identical: when sovereign or corporate revenue decouples from the productive capacity of a human population, the rational institutional response is disinvestment in human capital and the erosion of accountability structures that depend on that coupling. As AI-generated output approaches majority GDP share, the labor-tax-representation nexus that undergirds democratic legitimacy faces not incremental pressure but categorical dissolution.

What distinguishes this analysis is its insistence that the catastrophic trajectory requires no technical failure. A perfectly aligned, maximally capable AI ecosystem produces the intelligence curse as a default political-economic outcome. The argument gains empirical traction from the social media precedent: platform capitalism already demonstrates that when revenue derives from the systematic degradation of human cognitive capacity — attention fragmentation, dopamine hijacking, outrage optimization — firms rationally pursue anti-developmental strategies. Altman's observation that human development is resource-intensive is legible within this framework not as gaffe but as ideology: the emergent rationality of a system in which human beings have transitioned from productive asset to maintenance cost.

The prescriptive dimension draws on the institutional counterfactuals within resource economics itself — Norway's Government Pension Fund Global, Alaska's Permanent Fund — as proof that the curse is not inevitable but is the default absent deliberate political architecture. The intelligence dividend requires analogous precommitment mechanisms: constitutional or quasi-constitutional structures that convert AI productivity into human investment before the political leverage to demand redistribution is itself automated away. The window for such intervention is defined by the very degradation the framework diagnoses.
