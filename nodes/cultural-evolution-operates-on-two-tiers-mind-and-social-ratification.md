# Cultural Evolution Operates on Two Tiers: Mind and Social Ratification

Not all becoming is the same becoming.

---

> Not all evolution is the same process. Tyler Volk argues that conflating galactic, biological, and cultural evolution obscures the specific mechanisms at work — and that cultural evolution specifically operates through a dual-tier system of internal mental selection and social ratification.

Tyler Volk makes a crucial and underappreciated distinction: not all evolution is the same kind of evolution, and conflating them creates serious conceptual confusion. Astrophysicists speak of galactic evolution, biologists of Darwinian evolution, cultural theorists of cultural evolution — but these are not the same process wearing different clothes. Volk identifies a specific family of evolutionary dynamics defined by propagation, variation, and selective retention — a framework drawn from Donald Campbell — and argues that this PVS structure is a major kind of evolution that has manifested at multiple scales: in chemical pre-life, in standard biological evolution, in animal learning through trial and error, and in human cultural systems. What is especially interesting is his claim that cultural evolution operates as a dual-tier PVS: a mental PVS — internal variation and selection within individual minds — coupled to a group PVS, which consists of social agreement systems that function as collective selective retention mechanisms. This maps closely onto Greg Henriques's justification systems framework, which distinguishes internal mental justification from social justification. The implication is that when we ask how cultural forms emerge and stabilize, we need to track both the internal cognitive process and the social ratification process — and neither alone is sufficient. Treating all evolutionary change as one undifferentiated process obscures the specific mechanisms that actually drive transformation at each scale.

## Apprentice

When scientists talk about evolution, they often mean very different things. Stars "evolve," species evolve, and cultures evolve — but these aren't the same process. Tyler Volk argues that lumping them together creates real confusion, and that we need to be precise about what kind of change we're actually describing.

He identifies a specific pattern — propagation, variation, and selective retention (PVS) — that shows up at multiple scales: in the chemistry before life began, in biological natural selection, in how animals learn by trial and error, and in human culture. But culture, he argues, is special. It runs on two connected engines: one inside individual minds, where people generate and test ideas internally, and one at the group level, where social agreement systems decide which ideas get accepted and passed on.

This means that to understand how cultural practices, beliefs, or institutions take shape and persist, you have to look at both what happens inside people's heads and what happens when groups collectively approve or reject ideas. Neither process alone tells the full story. This dual-tier view also connects to Greg Henriques's work on how people justify their beliefs both privately and socially — suggesting these two thinkers are mapping the same territory from different starting points.

## Adept

Tyler Volk draws a critical distinction that much interdisciplinary discourse overlooks: the word "evolution" covers fundamentally different kinds of processes, and treating them as interchangeable generates deep conceptual confusion. Drawing on Donald Campbell's framework, Volk identifies a specific evolutionary structure defined by propagation, variation, and selective retention (PVS). This PVS dynamic is not a metaphor stretched across domains — it is a recurrent mechanism that manifests at distinct scales: prebiotic chemistry, Darwinian biological evolution, operant learning in animals, and human cultural systems.

What makes Volk's analysis especially productive is his claim that cultural evolution operates as a dual-tier PVS. The first tier is a mental PVS: individuals generate internal variations — ideas, hypotheses, imagined possibilities — and subject them to cognitive selection. The second tier is a group PVS, in which social agreement systems function as collective selective retention mechanisms, determining which ideas propagate through communities and which are discarded. Culture stabilizes not through either process alone but through their coupling.

This architecture maps with striking precision onto Greg Henriques's justification systems framework, which similarly distinguishes between internal mental justification (the private filtering of beliefs) and social justification (the public legitimation process). The convergence suggests a robust structural insight: cultural forms emerge through individual cognitive variation but persist only through social ratification. Any account of cultural evolution that attends to only one tier — treating culture as purely individual cognition or purely social dynamics — will systematically misidentify the mechanisms driving cultural transformation and stability.

## Magus

Tyler Volk's intervention into evolutionary theory targets a pervasive equivocation: the unreflective use of "evolution" to designate processes as disparate as stellar nucleosynthesis, Darwinian natural selection, and cultural change. Against this conflation, Volk specifies a particular family of evolutionary dynamics — propagation, variation, and selective retention (PVS), following Donald Campbell — and traces its instantiation across discrete ontological scales: prebiotic chemical systems, biological populations, operant learning in individual organisms, and human cultural formations. The claim is not analogical but structural: PVS constitutes a recurrent dynamical architecture, each instantiation possessing its own substrate-specific mechanisms while sharing a common formal topology.

The most theoretically consequential move is Volk's characterization of cultural evolution as a dual-tier PVS. The mental PVS operates intrapsychically — individuals generate representational variants and subject them to internal selective pressures shaped by prior learning, affect, and cognitive constraints. The group PVS operates through social agreement systems that function as collective retention mechanisms, institutionalizing certain variants while extinguishing others. Cultural stability and change are emergent properties of the coupling between these tiers, irreducible to either alone.

This dual-tier architecture converges with Greg Henriques's justification systems hypothesis, which posits that human behavioral investment is filtered through both intrapersonal justification processes and interpersonal-social justification dynamics. The convergence is non-trivial: it suggests that the PVS framework and the justification systems framework are identifying the same structural bifurcation from complementary theoretical vantage points. The implication for cultural evolutionary theory is that any monistic account — whether purely cognitivist or purely sociological — will systematically fail to capture the generative tension between individual variation and collective ratification that constitutes the actual mechanism of cultural formation.
