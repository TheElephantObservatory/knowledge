# Pre-Tragic, Tragic, and Post-Tragic as Existential Stations

You are what you attend to.

---

> Life moves through innocence, grief, and a hard-won wholeness that holds both. The post-tragic is not optimism recovered but joy deepened by loss — and inhabiting it demands deliberate control over what we attend to.

The pre-tragic, tragic, and post-tragic are not developmental stages to be climbed — they are existential stations, each appropriate to its moment, but not equally close to the whole truth of life. The pre-tragic is the condition of the infant, of innocence, of joy uncomplicated by loss. It is real and beautiful, and there is nothing wrong with inhabiting it when life permits. The tragic arrives when loss does — when a loved one dies, when heartbreak comes, when the weight of the world's predicament lands. There is a case for grief, even for catatonic depression at certain moments. Tragedy is not a failure of resilience; it is a statement that life matters, that there are things worth caring about, that meaning is real. The post-tragic is not the absence of darkness — it is the condition in which the energy and love of the pre-tragic has been informed and deepened by the tragic, and carries that meaning forward into a life that is heavier, more real, but still broadly something you are glad to live. For those working in the metacrisis space, the challenge is that the information environment constantly pulls between a false pre-tragic — the techno-optimist vibe that AI will solve everything — and an overwhelming tragic, as each new data point about ecological collapse or geopolitical instability arrives. Residing in the post-tragic requires agility of attention: knowing when to look clearly at the apparent impossibility of the situation, and when to look into the eyes of a loved one or walk in a park. You are, at some level, what you attend to.

## Apprentice

There are three basic ways a person can stand in relation to suffering. The first is innocence — the joy of someone who hasn't yet been broken by loss. The second is tragedy — the crushing weight that arrives when something you love is damaged or destroyed. The third is what comes after: a life that has absorbed the tragedy but still finds reasons to continue, even to feel glad.

None of these is a rung on a ladder. You don't graduate from one to the next. A moment of uncomplicated happiness isn't a failure of awareness, and a period of grief isn't a failure of resilience. Grief is actually a sign that something mattered. The deeper challenge is reaching a place where you carry the full weight of what you know — about loss, about the state of the world — and still choose to live with energy and love.

This matters especially for people paying close attention to global crises. The information environment constantly whipsaws between false cheerfulness ("technology will fix it") and paralyzing despair ("it's all collapsing"). Living in the post-tragic means developing a kind of agility — knowing when to face the darkness squarely and when to look into the eyes of someone you love. What you pay attention to, moment by moment, shapes who you become.

## Adept

This framework distinguishes three existential stations — pre-tragic, tragic, and post-tragic — while insisting they are not developmental stages arranged on a hierarchy. The pre-tragic is the condition of innocence: joy unmediated by loss, entirely legitimate when life permits it. The tragic is the recognition that meaning entails vulnerability — that grief, even catatonic depression, can be an appropriate response to genuine devastation. Tragedy is not pathology; it is the emotional corollary of caring about things that can be destroyed. The post-tragic is the integration of both: the energy and love characteristic of innocence, now informed and weighted by encounter with loss, carried forward into a life that is heavier but still affirmed.

The insight becomes especially pointed in the context of metacrisis work. The contemporary information environment generates a constant oscillation between a synthetic pre-tragic — techno-optimist narratives that treat existential risk as an engineering problem — and an overwhelming tragic, as ecological, geopolitical, and institutional data accumulates. Neither pole is habitable for sustained, effective engagement. The post-tragic requires something more demanding than balance: it requires agility of attention, the capacity to look unflinchingly at systemic impossibility and then, without denial, to turn toward embodied presence — a loved one's face, a walk in a park.

The concluding claim is phenomenological and ethical at once: "You are, at some level, what you attend to." Attention is not merely a cognitive resource to be allocated; it is constitutive of selfhood. The post-tragic is therefore not a position achieved once but a practice of ongoing attentional discipline, navigating between clarity about the world and fidelity to the life still being lived within it.

## Magus

The tripartite schema of pre-tragic, tragic, and post-tragic resists developmental teleology while preserving evaluative asymmetry: these are existential stations, each appropriate to its occasion, yet not equally proximate to the whole truth of a life that includes both joy and devastation. The pre-tragic — innocence, unmediated delight — is ontologically real, not merely naïve. The tragic is the discovery that meaning and vulnerability are co-constitutive: to care is to be breakable, and grief at its most extreme is not failure but testimony that something mattered. The post-tragic refuses both regression to innocence and permanent residence in despair. It is the condition in which pre-tragic energy has been alchemized by encounter with loss into something denser, more real, and still broadly affirmative.

What gives this framework its contemporary urgency is its application to the epistemic ecology of metacrisis engagement. The information environment enforces a pathological oscillation: on one side, a counterfeit pre-tragic — the techno-solutionist imaginary in which artificial intelligence or green growth dissolves existential risk without remainder — and on the other, an engulfing tragic, as each new data point on biospheric degradation or institutional decay arrives with compounding force. Neither attractor permits sustained coherent agency. The post-tragic, by contrast, demands what might be called attentional sovereignty: the disciplined capacity to hold the apparent impossibility of the macro-situation in view while simultaneously inhabiting the proximate — the face of a beloved, the texture of a living landscape.

The closing aphorism — "You are, at some level, what you attend to" — compresses a phenomenological claim of considerable depth. Attention is not instrumental but constitutive; the self is not a fixed subject directing a spotlight but is continuously enacted through its attentional commitments. The post-tragic is therefore less a achieved state than an ongoing practice of navigating between registers of reality, refusing the comfort of either anesthesia or paralysis.
