# Pre-Industrial Peasants Had More Leisure Than We Do

The ghost that cannot stop eating.

---

> McGilchrist argues that industrialization did not liberate humanity from miserable labor but invented it — pre-industrial peasants enjoyed far more leisure than modern workers, and capitalism replaced livable rhythms with a structurally insatiable demand for endless production.

McGilchrist makes a historically grounded and counterintuitive argument about labor, leisure, and the mythology of progress. We have been taught to believe that life before industrialization was grinding, miserable, and brutish — and that machines liberated us. McGilchrist reports that scholarly research into the actual conditions of peasant life, including the period just before the French Revolution, tells a radically different story. Peasants in the Ancien Régime had approximately 180 days of holiday per year. They took it for granted that they would have a midday rest. Their labor, while real, was not the relentless, unforgiving grind that industrial capitalism introduced. It was the industrial revolution — not feudalism — that first subjected human beings to the kind of exhausting, dehumanizing work schedules we now treat as normal. The inversion is important: capitalism did not liberate people from misery; it created a new and more systematic form of it, justified by the ideology of exponential growth. Once the idea took hold that one could get rich by producing more than one needs and selling the surplus, the logic of endless increase made hard labor not just necessary but virtuous. McGilchrist connects this to the present moment: the 'lord' today is not a feudal landowner but the economic superorganism — a hungry ghost that is structurally incapable of satisfaction, extracting attention and labor in exchange for a representation of life rather than life itself.

## Apprentice

There is a common story we tell ourselves: that life before modern industry was terrible, and that machines set us free. Iain McGilchrist challenges this directly. Historical research shows that peasants before the French Revolution had roughly 180 holidays a year and expected a midday rest. Their work was real, but it followed natural rhythms. It was the industrial revolution that first forced people into the exhausting, unbroken schedules we now consider normal.

The key reversal is this: capitalism did not rescue people from suffering — it introduced a new, more systematic kind of suffering. Once the idea took hold that wealth comes from producing more than you need and selling the surplus, hard labor stopped being a burden to minimize and became something to celebrate. Working yourself to the bone became a virtue.

McGilchrist extends this to the present day. The old feudal lord has been replaced by something harder to see — an economic system that behaves like a creature with an infinite appetite. It can never be satisfied. It demands our time, our attention, and our energy, and in return it offers not life itself but a thin representation of life: consumption, status, distraction.

## Adept

McGilchrist advances a historically grounded inversion of the standard progress narrative. The conventional account holds that pre-industrial life was brutish and that mechanization delivered humanity into relative comfort. But scholarly research into actual conditions of peasant life — including the Ancien Régime period — reveals something strikingly different: approximately 180 days of holiday per year, assumed midday rest, and labor organized around seasonal and communal rhythms rather than maximized output. It was industrialization, not feudalism, that first subjected human beings to the relentless, dehumanizing work schedules now treated as baseline.

This inversion carries significant ideological weight. The mythology of progress serves to retroactively justify the regime of industrial capitalism by making its predecessor appear worse than it was. Once surplus production became the engine of wealth accumulation, the logic of exponential growth transformed ceaseless labor from a misfortune into a moral imperative. Hard work became virtuous precisely because it served the machinery of endless increase.

McGilchrist connects this historical argument to a structural diagnosis of the present. The feudal lord — a specific person with finite appetites — has been replaced by what he characterizes as an economic superorganism: a system with no capacity for satiation. This entity functions as a "hungry ghost," extracting attention and labor while returning only representations of fulfillment — consumption, metrics, simulated experience. The shift from feudal to capitalist domination is not a liberation but a change in the nature of the master, from one that could occasionally be satisfied to one that structurally cannot.

## Magus

McGilchrist's argument operates at the intersection of historical revisionism, political economy, and phenomenology of lived experience. Against the Whig narrative that positions industrialization as emancipation from feudal misery, he marshals evidence — consistent with the work of historians like Juliet Schor and Jacques Le Goff — that pre-industrial labor regimes were substantially less totalizing than their industrial successors. The Ancien Régime peasant's approximately 180 annual holidays and assumed midday rest represent not idyllic nostalgia but documented social fact. The industrial revolution's decisive innovation was not the liberation of labor but its temporal colonization: the subordination of human rhythmicity to the demands of mechanized production.

The ideological architecture supporting this colonization is the mythology of exponential growth. Once surplus production became legible as the mechanism of wealth accumulation, a decisive transvaluation occurred: labor ceased to be a necessity to be minimized and became a virtue to be maximized. This is not merely an economic shift but an ontological one — the replacement of sufficiency as an organizing principle with structural insatiability. The logic of endless increase requires that no quantum of production ever registers as enough.

McGilchrist's most penetrating move is his characterization of the contemporary economic system as a superorganism — a hungry ghost in the Buddhist sense, defined by appetites that are constitutively unsatisfiable. The feudal lord, whatever his cruelties, was a finite being with finite desires. The economic superorganism that has replaced him operates through a fundamentally different logic: it extracts attention, time, and vitality not in exchange for life but for its simulacrum — mediated experience, representational satisfaction, the perpetual deferral of presence. Domination has not ended; it has become systemic, impersonal, and structurally invisible.
