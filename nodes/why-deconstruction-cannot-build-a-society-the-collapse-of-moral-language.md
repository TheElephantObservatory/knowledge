# Why Deconstruction Cannot Build a Society: The Collapse of Moral Language

Irony is not sophistication — it is cowardice.

---

> Postmodernism taught us to see through grand narratives but left us unable to say anything ought to be different. Without recovering moral language, society cannot navigate AI, environmental crisis, or institutional trust — it can only optimize and critique, never choose.

Postmodernism made a crucial philosophical contribution — it allowed us to see through grand narratives, to recognize that the frameworks we swim in are not the water itself. But it cannot serve as the foundation for a society, because deconstruction is not construction. One of its most damaging legacies has been the collapse of the language of 'ought.' Modernity gave us the language of what is possible — production, technology, efficiency. Postmodernism gave us the language of critique. But neither gives us the language of what ought to be. This is not a minor gap. Without the capacity to say 'this is morally wrong' — not just illegal, not just inefficient, but wrong — we lose the ability to discuss how to respond to artificial intelligence, how to steward the environment, how to maintain the social norms that make institutions trustworthy and communities livable. Andersen describes a three-layer model: what is possible here and now, what might be possible, and what ought to be. For decades, investment has flooded into the first layer — technology and production — while the second and third have been starved. Fundamental science, aesthetic institutions, political structures, and moral discourse have all been underfunded and delegitimized. The result is a society that has become, in her words, more one-dimensional and dumber than it realizes. We have confused the removal of indoctrination with the removal of moral formation altogether. Children need boundaries not as a cage but as something to push against — a structure within which a self can form. Adults need the language of ought not to impose values but to negotiate them. The postmodern ironic stance — where nothing is stated earnestly, where every position is held at arm's length — is not sophistication. It is, as Jim Rutt puts it, a form of moral cowardice.

## Apprentice

Postmodernism gave us something genuinely valuable: the ability to question the big stories that cultures tell themselves, to notice when powerful frameworks are being passed off as natural truths. But questioning is not the same as building. Deconstruction taught us how to take things apart, but it never offered tools for putting something better together.

The deeper problem is that we've lost the ability to talk about what ought to be. Modern society is very good at discussing what's technically possible — faster machines, bigger systems, more efficient processes. And postmodern culture is skilled at critiquing all of it. But neither tradition equips us to say something is simply wrong, not just inefficient or illegal, but morally wrong. Without that language, how do we make decisions about artificial intelligence, environmental responsibility, or what makes a community trustworthy?

As the thinker Christine Andersen frames it, society operates on three layers: what is possible now, what might become possible, and what ought to be. Investment has poured into the first layer while the others have been starved. Meanwhile, the postmodern habit of holding every position at an ironic distance — never stating anything earnestly — has been mistaken for sophistication. As Jim Rutt argues, it is closer to moral cowardice. Children need moral structure not as a cage but as something to push against while forming a self. Adults need the language of ought not to impose values but to negotiate them honestly.

## Adept

Postmodernism's philosophical contribution was genuine and necessary: it exposed grand narratives as contingent constructions rather than natural law, enabling critical distance from ideological frameworks. But critique is a solvent, not a foundation. The central problem Andersen identifies is that postmodernism's success in dismantling normative discourse has produced a catastrophic gap — the collapse of the language of 'ought.' Modernity furnished the language of possibility (production, technology, efficiency); postmodernism furnished the language of suspicion. Neither provides the moral vocabulary required to navigate questions about AI governance, ecological stewardship, or institutional legitimacy.

Andersen proposes a three-layer model of societal capacity: what is possible here and now, what might be possible, and what ought to be. For decades, capital and attention have concentrated overwhelmingly in the first layer while fundamental science, aesthetic institutions, political structures, and moral discourse — the infrastructure of the second and third layers — have been systematically defunded and delegitimized. The result is a civilization that is, in her assessment, more one-dimensional and intellectually impoverished than it recognizes.

This analysis reframes the postmodern ironic stance — the refusal to hold any position earnestly, the perpetual arm's-length relationship with commitment — not as intellectual sophistication but as what Jim Rutt calls moral cowardice. The confusion runs deep: the removal of indoctrination has been conflated with the removal of moral formation entirely. Yet moral structure functions not as a cage but as a necessary scaffold — something for a developing self to push against. Recovering the language of ought is not a return to dogma; it is the precondition for genuine ethical negotiation in an era that desperately requires it.

## Magus

The diagnosis advanced here locates postmodernism's pathology not in its epistemological contribution — the denaturalization of grand narratives remains indispensable — but in its metastasis into an ontological condition that forecloses normative discourse altogether. Andersen's three-layer model (the actual-possible, the latent-possible, and the obligatory) provides the structural grammar for this critique: hypertrophic investment in first-layer optimization — technology, production, efficiency — has proceeded in tandem with the systematic defunding of second- and third-layer capacities. Fundamental science, aesthetic institutions, and the discursive infrastructure of moral reasoning have been starved not merely of resources but of legitimacy. The civilization that results is, in Andersen's formulation, more one-dimensional and epistemically degraded than its self-image permits it to recognize.

The crux of the argument is that the Marcusean one-dimensionality has been achieved not through ideological saturation but through ironic evacuation. The postmodern stance — every commitment held provisionally, every assertion bracketed by performative distance — collapses the distinction between critical sophistication and what Jim Rutt identifies as moral cowardice. The language of 'ought' has not been refined or democratized; it has been rendered unspeakable. Without it, adjudication of genuinely novel normative questions — artificial intelligence alignment, intergenerational ecological obligation, the fiduciary conditions of institutional trust — becomes structurally impossible. One can optimize or critique, but one cannot choose.

Andersen's intervention recovers a developmental insight often obscured by libertarian and constructivist framings alike: moral formation requires structure — not as imposition but as resistance, the boundary against which selfhood differentiates. The removal of indoctrination was confused with the removal of formation per se. Restoring the language of ought is therefore not reactionary nostalgia but the precondition for any post-postmodern political and ethical negotiation worthy of the name.
