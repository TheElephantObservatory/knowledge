# Why 'Artificial Intelligence' Is a Category Error About the Nature of Mind

We will miss what vanishes because we never saw it.

---

> The greatest risk of AI is not its power but the metaphor through which we understand it. 'Mind as computer' flattens intelligence into computation, hiding everything — emotion, embodiment, relationality — that makes human cognition what it is. We are replacing capacities we cannot even see ourselves losing.

The deepest problem with AI is not that it is powerful — it is that we are systematically misunderstanding what kind of power it has, because we are using the wrong metaphor to think about it. The dominant metaphor is 'mind as computer': intelligence is computation, the brain is a processor, and therefore a sufficiently powerful processor is a sufficiently powerful mind. This metaphor has driven cognitive science for decades and it has driven AI development from the beginning. But if you compare a human brain to a GPU cluster on the dimension of similarities, you get a short list. If you compare them on the dimension of dissimilarities, you get a very long list. The brain does more than a GPU cluster with orders of magnitude less electricity. It is embedded in a body with a microbiome, a hormonal system, a nervous system that extends into the gut. It has an amygdala — and Antonio Damasio's research on patients with calcified amygdalae shows that without emotional modulation, a person with a normal IQ becomes functionally disabled: unable to navigate a bus, unable to play poker, unable to participate in ordinary social life. Intelligence is not separable from emotion, embodiment, relationality, and the full analog complexity of being a living creature. IQ captures a narrow slice of this and then the word 'intelligence' gets used as if it covers the whole. 'Artificial intelligence' inherits this category error — it is extremely powerful at what it does, but what it does is not what we do when we are intelligent. The danger is not that AI is weak. The danger is that we keep acting as if it is doing something like what we do, and therefore that it can replace what we do, when in fact it is doing something radically different — and the replacement destroys things we cannot see we are losing until they are gone.

## Apprentice

The way most people think about AI rests on a hidden assumption: that intelligence is basically computation, and the brain is basically a processor. If that were true, then building a fast enough computer would eventually produce something like a mind. But this metaphor breaks down quickly. A human brain runs on roughly the power of a light bulb. It is woven into a body with a gut, hormones, emotions, and a web of social relationships. None of this is optional decoration on top of "real" thinking — it is the thinking.

Antonio Damasio's research makes this vivid. Patients whose emotional brain centers were damaged retained normal IQ scores, yet they could not navigate everyday life — couldn't play a simple card game strategically, couldn't manage a bus route. Their reasoning looked intact on paper but collapsed in practice. Intelligence without emotion is not diminished intelligence; it is functionally broken intelligence.

AI is extraordinarily powerful at pattern recognition, text generation, and optimization. But calling it "intelligent" smuggles in the assumption that it does something like what humans do. It does not. The real danger is not that AI is too strong or too weak — it is that we keep treating it as a substitute for human capacities it does not possess, and in doing so, we quietly destroy things we will not notice are gone until it is too late.

## Adept

The dominant conceptual framework for AI development inherits a metaphor from mid-twentieth-century cognitive science: mind as computation, brain as processor. Under this metaphor, intelligence scales with processing power, and a sufficiently capable computational system should approximate a sufficiently capable mind. But the mapping between biological cognition and silicon computation is remarkably thin. The brain operates at orders of magnitude less energy than a GPU cluster. It is embedded in a body — a microbiome, an endocrine system, a nervous system extending into the viscera — and this embodiment is not peripheral to cognition but constitutive of it.

Antonio Damasio's somatic marker hypothesis, grounded in clinical work with patients whose amygdalae were calcified, demonstrates that emotional processing is not a luxury atop rational thought but a precondition for functional reasoning. Patients with intact IQ but damaged emotional circuitry become unable to make ordinary decisions — navigating social life, playing poker, riding a bus. The narrow psychometric construct of IQ captures a slice of cognitive capacity, and the word "intelligence" then does unearned work, implying coverage of the whole. "Artificial intelligence" inherits this category error wholesale.

The consequence is not merely semantic. When institutions treat AI as performing something equivalent to human intelligence, they authorize substitution — of judgment, of care, of relational reasoning — in domains where the computational and the human are not interchangeable. The danger is not that AI is insufficiently powerful. It is that the metaphor of mind-as-computation renders invisible precisely those dimensions of intelligence that AI lacks, ensuring that what is lost in the replacement cannot be perceived until the loss is irreversible.

## Magus

The computational theory of mind — intelligence as formal symbol manipulation, brain as hardware instantiating cognitive software — has functioned not merely as a scientific hypothesis but as the generative metaphor structuring the entire trajectory of AI development. This framing produces a specific ontological commitment: that cognition is substrate-independent and scales along a single axis of processing power. Yet the phenomenology and neurobiology of actual cognition resist this reduction at nearly every point of contact. The brain is not an isolated processor but a metabolically embedded organ, coupled to endocrine, immunological, and enteric nervous systems whose contributions to reasoning are not epiphenomenal but architecturally foundational.

Damasio's work on patients with ventromedial prefrontal and amygdalar damage constitutes a decisive empirical challenge to the computation-sufficiency thesis. These patients retain normal performance on standard psychometric measures of intelligence while exhibiting catastrophic failures in practical reasoning, social navigation, and decision-making under uncertainty. The somatic marker framework demonstrates that affective valuation is not downstream of cognition but integral to it — emotion is the medium through which relevance is computed in situated, embodied agents. IQ, as a construct, captures variance along a narrow band of decontextualized problem-solving, and the lexical slide from this construct to "intelligence" tout court performs a metonymic substitution that the term "artificial intelligence" then reifies at industrial scale.

The epistemological danger is precise: the governing metaphor renders its own inadequacy invisible. When intelligence is defined computationally, everything non-computational about cognition — embodiment, affect, relationality, ecological embedding — falls outside the frame of relevance. Substitution then proceeds not against resistance but against an absence of categories with which to articulate what is being displaced. The losses are structural and, by the logic of the metaphor itself, imperceptible until they have become systemic.
