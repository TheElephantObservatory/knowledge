# Open-Source Religion: Co-Creating Symbols to Prevent Their Calcification

You cannot worship what you helped build.

---

A recurring problem in religious history is what Brendan Graham Dempsey identifies as retroactive canonization — the tendency for institutions to take symbols and interpretations that were actively developed, contested, and evolved over time, and then retrospectively present them as having always meant exactly what they now mean. ## Apprentice

When a religious tradition develops new ideas over centuries, something strange often happens: the institution later claims those ideas were there from the very beginning. Brendan Graham Dempsey calls this "retroactive canonization" — the way living, contested, evolving symbols get frozen in place and presented as eternal truths. The creative process that built the tradition gets hidden, and what was constructed starts to look like something that was simply received.

Dempsey's key move is to ask: what if a community openly acknowledged that it was building its symbols together, rather than pretending they dropped from the sky? He proposes something like an "open-source religion" — a collaborative, digitally-enabled process where people co-create meaningful images, stories, and rituals, and where shared canon emerges from what genuinely resonates rather than from authority declaring what must be believed.

The crucial insight is practical: it is very hard to worship a symbol as a literal, untouchable truth when you helped make it. Participating in the construction keeps the constructedness visible, which acts as a built-in guard against rigidity and idolatry. Collaborative mandalas, evolving images, and meaning extracted from dialogue aren't just creative exercises — they're structural tools for keeping a tradition alive, self-aware, and genuinely shared.

## Adept

Plotinus radically extends Plato, yet the Neoplatonic tradition retroactively collapses this development, claiming mere exegesis of what was always already there. From this diagnosis, Dempsey advances a structural proposal: an "open-source religion" in which symbol generation is explicitly collaborative, digitally distributed, and bottom-up. Canon in such a framework emerges through collective salience — what resonates and persists across participants — rather than through doctrinal imposition. The wiki-generated mandala, the collaboratively evolved sacred image, the meaning-wave distilled from dialogue: these function not as aesthetic experiments but as epistemic mechanisms designed to keep the mythos self-aware of its own provisional character.

The deepest claim is phenomenological: it is structurally difficult to reify or literalize a symbol you participated in constructing. The visibility of the making process serves as what Dempsey frames as a natural prophylactic against idolatry. Participatory construction preserves the tension between continuity and openness that living traditions require — shared reference sufficient for community coherence, combined with explicit awareness that the symbols remain under construction and subject to ongoing collective revision.

## Magus

Brendan Graham Dempsey's concept of "retroactive canonization" isolates a mechanism central to the sociology of religious knowledge: the systematic erasure of diachronic constructedness in favor of synchronic self-presentation as timeless deposit. The paradigm case — Neoplatonism's retrospective identification of its considerable innovations with Plato's original intent — illustrates how traditions metabolize genuine intellectual development into claims of mere recovery. What was contested becomes settled; what was generated becomes received. Dempsey locates the pathological core of institutional religion not in myth-making per se but in the concealment of myth-making, the occlusion of the generative process beneath the authority of the generated product.

The prescriptive extension is architectonic rather than merely attitudinal. Dempsey proposes "open-source religion" as a structural solution: a dispersed, digitally-mediated process of co-creative symbol generation in which canonicity is an emergent property of collective salience rather than an artifact of hierarchical promulgation. Collaboratively constructed mandalas, iteratively evolved iconography, and meaning-waves extracted dialogically function as what one might call epistemically transparent ritual objects — artifacts whose mode of production remains inscribed in their reception. The claim is that participatory construction provides a natural prophylactic against reification: it is phenomenologically resistant to literalization precisely because the constructedness never fully recedes from view.

This positions Dempsey at an interesting intersection of Cassirerian symbolic form theory, Rappaport's analysis of liturgical invariance, and contemporary platform epistemology. The wager is that the continuity and shared reference community requires can be sustained without the ossification that typically accompanies canonization — provided the community's relationship to its own symbol-making remains reflexive, explicit, and structurally embedded in the very media through which symbols circulate.
