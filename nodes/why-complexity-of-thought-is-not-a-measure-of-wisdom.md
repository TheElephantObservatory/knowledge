# Why Complexity of Thought Is Not a Measure of Wisdom

Darth Vader was very smart.

---

> Any serious psychology must distinguish three layers — the natural (biological causation), the normal (cultural averages), and the normative (what is genuinely better) — and collapsing any one into another produces dangerous errors, especially the seductive fallacy that greater cognitive complexity equals greater wisdom.

One of the most important and neglected insights from the developmental tradition — particularly from Piaget and Baldwin — is the distinction between three ontological strata that any adequate metapsychology must navigate: the natural, the normal, and the normative. The natural refers to causal, self-regulating biological processes that can be studied with the hard sciences. The normal refers to what is sociologically predictable within a given culture. And the normative refers to something categorically different — the domain of justification, of normative facts, of claims about what is actually better rather than merely more common. The confusion between these three strata generates catastrophic mistakes in practice. When we medicalize political disagreement, we collapse the normative into the natural. When we universalize WEIRD undergraduate data as universal human cognition, we collapse the normal into the natural. And when we treat developmental complexity as equivalent to moral goodness — the 'growth to goodness' fallacy — we collapse the normative into the normal. This fallacy is especially dangerous because at lower levels of development, feedback loops from reality are direct and unforgiving — a child who misunderstands gravity gets corrected by gravity — so development does track increasing adequacy there. But once abstraction becomes possible, the situation changes radically. Abstraction opens the door to socially constructed simulations that can be internally coherent, increasingly complex, and yet massively disconnected from the world. The Darth Vader move is always available: tremendous cognitive power deployed in the service of catastrophic misjudgment. What this means practically is that we cannot use complexity of reasoning as a proxy for wisdom — it is a necessary but never sufficient condition. Complexification must be coupled to the complexity of reality itself, not merely to the internal elaboration of a simulation. Piaget's structuralism, properly understood, was an attempt to hold all three strata in view simultaneously, to find isomorphic patterns across them without reducing one to another. That project remains unfinished and urgently needed.

## Apprentice

There are three fundamentally different kinds of claims we can make about human minds, and mixing them up causes serious harm. The first is about nature — how biology works, governed by cause and effect. The second is about what's normal — what most people in a given culture actually do or think. The third is about what's genuinely better — what people should do, what reasoning is actually more adequate to reality. These are not the same thing, but they get confused constantly.

When political disagreement gets treated as mental illness, that's collapsing moral questions into biological ones. When psychology studies on Western college students get presented as universal human nature, that's mistaking one culture's normal for what's natural. And when we assume that people who think in more complex ways are automatically wiser or more ethical, that's perhaps the most dangerous confusion of all.

This last mistake — calling it the "growth to goodness" fallacy — is especially tricky. Early in development, reality does correct you directly: misunderstand how a stove works and you get burned. But once people can think abstractly, they can build elaborate mental worlds that are impressively sophisticated yet completely disconnected from how things actually are. Great intellectual power can serve terrible judgment. Complexity of thought is necessary for wisdom, but it is never enough on its own — it has to stay tethered to the complexity of the real world.

## Adept

Drawing on the developmental tradition of Piaget and Baldwin, this insight identifies three ontological strata that any adequate metapsychology must hold distinct: the natural (causal, self-regulating biological processes amenable to natural-scientific investigation), the normal (sociologically predictable patterns within a given cultural context), and the normative (the domain of justification — claims about what is genuinely more adequate, not merely more common). The systematic confusion of these strata generates characteristic errors. Medicalizing political disagreement collapses the normative into the natural. Universalizing WEIRD-sample findings collapses the normal into the natural. And equating developmental complexity with moral goodness — the "growth to goodness" fallacy — collapses the normative into the normal.

The growth to goodness fallacy deserves particular scrutiny because it has a seductive empirical basis at lower developmental levels. Pre-abstract cognition operates under tight feedback constraints: reality corrects misapprehension directly. Development in this range does genuinely track increasing adequacy. But the emergence of abstraction changes the epistemic situation categorically. Abstract thought enables the construction of internally coherent, increasingly elaborate simulations that may be massively decoupled from reality. The "Darth Vader move" — tremendous cognitive sophistication deployed in service of catastrophic misjudgment — becomes a permanent structural possibility.

The practical implication is that structural complexity of reasoning is a necessary but never sufficient condition for wisdom. Complexification must be indexed to the complexity of reality itself, not to the internal elaboration of a self-referential model. Piaget's structuralism, on this reading, was an attempt to find isomorphic patterns across all three strata without reducing any to another — a project that remains both unfinished and urgently needed.

## Magus

The developmental epistemologies of Piaget and Baldwin, properly reconstructed, yield a tripartite ontological distinction that remains largely unmetabolized by contemporary metapsychology: the natural (causal-nomological processes tractable to natural-scientific explanation), the normal (statistically and sociologically predictable regularities within culturally bounded populations), and the normative (the domain of justificatory adequacy — irreducible to either causal determination or statistical prevalence). Each inter-stratal conflation generates a signature pathology: naturalizing the normative produces the medicalization of political and ethical disagreement; naturalizing the normal yields the well-documented WEIRD universalism of mainstream cognitive science; and normalizing the normative produces what can be termed the "growth to goodness" fallacy — the assumption that increasing structural complexity of cognition is isomorphic with increasing normative adequacy.

This last conflation is epistemologically the most insidious precisely because it is locally well-calibrated. At sensorimotor and early concrete-operational levels, feedback loops between organism and environment are tight and unforgiving; developmental progression in this range genuinely tracks increasing adaptive and epistemic adequacy. However, the emergence of formal-operational and post-formal abstraction introduces a phase transition. Abstraction enables the construction of internally coherent, recursively elaborated representational systems that satisfy all structural criteria for complexity while remaining systematically decoupled from the structure of the real. The availability of what might be called the Darth Vader move — maximal cognitive sophistication in service of maximal normative failure — is not an anomaly but a structural feature of post-abstract cognition.

Piaget's genetic structuralism, on this reading, was an attempt to identify cross-stratal isomorphisms without committing reductive collapse — to track how the natural, normal, and normative co-constrain development without any one stratum serving as explanatory ground for the others. That project, barely begun, represents one of the most consequential open problems in the human sciences.
