# How Quine's Critique of Analytic Philosophy Undermines the Is-Ought Distinction

The bedrock was always borrowed.

---

> The famous rule that you can't derive values from facts — the is-ought gap — rests on technical arguments that Quine later dismantled. The prohibition still gets treated as settled, but its philosophical foundations have been quietly knocked out.

One of the most consequential philosophical inheritances of modernity is the is-ought distinction — the claim, rooted in Hume and formalized by G.E. Moore, that no statement of fact can logically obligate any statement of value. This has been treated as a settled conclusion, a kind of philosophical bedrock. But when you trace the dependency relations carefully, the argument doesn't hold. Hume's argument depends on Moore's argument — you need a separate demonstration that statements about goodness are not natural statements. Moore provides that through his open question argument: whatever natural definition you propose for goodness, you can always ask whether it is truly good, which suggests no identity can be established. But Moore's argument depends on two assumptions that were unquestioned at the dawn of analytic philosophy: first, that we can give analytic definitions of things; and second, that there is a clean distinction between analytic truths and synthetic truths. Quine dismantled both. He showed that essences must be discovered empirically, and that the analytic-synthetic distinction cannot be established without circularity. So Hume's argument actually doesn't go through. Crucially, this does not mean every move from is to ought is licensed — but it does mean we cannot invoke the naturalistic fallacy as a settled prohibition. The prohibition was itself only ever a technical argument, and it has been technically undermined.

## Apprentice

There's a widely accepted rule in philosophy that says you can never get from how the world *is* to how it *ought* to be. Facts are one thing, values are another, and no amount of factual knowledge can tell you what's good or right. This idea traces back to David Hume and was reinforced by G.E. Moore in the early twentieth century.

But this argument has a hidden dependency chain. Moore's contribution was to argue that "goodness" can never be defined in terms of natural properties — you can always ask "but is that *really* good?" about any proposed definition. His reasoning, however, relied on assumptions about how definitions work and on a clean separation between truths of meaning and truths of fact. The philosopher W.V.O. Quine later showed that this separation doesn't hold up — it's circular. Without it, Moore's argument loses its force, and without Moore, Hume's prohibition loses its technical backing.

This doesn't mean that every leap from facts to values is suddenly justified. It means the blanket rule against it — often invoked as though it were a logical law — was only ever a technical argument, and that technical argument has been undermined. The question of how facts relate to values is more open than most people realize.

## Adept

The is-ought distinction — the thesis that no descriptive statement can entail a normative one — is typically treated as one of philosophy's most secure results. It originates with Hume's observation that moral conclusions seem to appear without logical warrant from factual premises, and it was given formal teeth by Moore's open question argument, which purports to show that "good" cannot be identified with any natural property. Together, these arguments underwrite the so-called naturalistic fallacy.

The critical insight here is that these arguments form a dependency chain with a fatal vulnerability. Hume's gap requires Moore's demonstration that evaluative predicates are non-natural. Moore's open question argument, in turn, presupposes that we can give analytic definitions of natural kinds and that there is a robust analytic-synthetic distinction — assumptions that were standard at the birth of analytic philosophy but which Quine systematically dismantled. Quine showed that the analytic-synthetic boundary cannot be drawn without circularity, and that essences are discovered empirically rather than stipulated a priori. Without the analytic-synthetic distinction, the open question argument cannot establish that goodness is categorically different from natural properties.

This does not license every naturalistic reduction of value to fact. What it does is reopen a question that has been prematurely closed. The naturalistic fallacy, far from being a logical law, was a technical prohibition resting on technical premises — premises that the subsequent development of philosophy has undermined. The relationship between descriptive and normative claims is a live philosophical problem, not a settled one.

## Magus

The canonical prohibition against deriving ought from is — tracing from Hume's guillotine through Moore's naturalistic fallacy to its status as near-axiomatic in metaethics — conceals a dependency structure that, once made explicit, reveals the prohibition to be far less secure than its institutional authority suggests. The argument's logical architecture requires that Moore's open question argument succeed in demonstrating the non-natural character of evaluative predicates. Moore's argument, however, is parasitic on two commitments characteristic of early analytic philosophy: that definitions are analytic — specifiable through conceptual decomposition — and that the analytic-synthetic distinction is both coherent and philosophically load-bearing.

Quine's demolition of precisely these commitments in "Two Dogmas of Empiricism" propagates upward through the dependency chain with devastating effect. If there is no principled analytic-synthetic boundary, then the open question technique cannot distinguish between a failed analytic identification and a successful synthetic one. The persistent "openness" of the question "but is it *good*?" may reflect not a metaphysical gap between natural and evaluative properties, but the ordinary revisability of empirical identity claims — the same revisability that attends "is water H₂O?" prior to empirical confirmation. Essences, as Quine and later Kripke showed from different directions, are discovered rather than stipulated.

The upshot is not a naive naturalism that collapses value into any convenient empirical predicate. It is the more precise conclusion that the technical apparatus underwriting the prohibition has been technically defeated. The naturalistic fallacy functions in contemporary discourse less as a valid inference rule than as a philosophical shibboleth — invoked to foreclose inquiry that the actual state of the arguments leaves open. The is-ought question must be re-engaged on post-Quinean terms, without the false comfort of a settled impossibility proof.
