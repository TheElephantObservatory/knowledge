# How Human Systems Are Reshaped to Fit Machine Limitations

We didn't build minds. We built mirrors.

---

> The real risk of machine systems is not that they become too intelligent, but that we quietly redesign human environments, social norms, and institutions to accommodate machine limitations — then mistake the flattened result for machine intelligence.

One of the underappreciated civilizational risks of machine systems is not that they will become too intelligent, but that we are systematically restructuring human environments and social protocols to accommodate their profound disabilities — and then mistaking the result for machine intelligence. Luciano Floridi's argument, which Bonnitta Roy draws on, is that machines are not intelligent; they provide agency. The appearance of machine intelligence is produced by a two-step process: first, human systems are standardized — education, medicine, bureaucracy — so that they become more machine-compatible; then machines can perform those standardized functions, and we call that intelligence. The example Roy gives is the DMV: the reason a machine can renew your license is not because the machine is smart, but because we have imposed social protocols that eliminate all the variability — the late arrivals, the crying babies, the edge cases — that a human clerk could handle and a machine cannot. Similarly, a new Volvo's backup safety system repeatedly shut itself off in her driveway because it could not parse a garden with wildflowers and fountains. For that machine to appear safe and functional, the environment must be completely accommodated to it. Roy argues this dynamic — the progressive redesign of human environments, social norms, and behavioral protocols to suit machine limitations — is a form of ontological design that operates largely below the threshold of public deliberation. The counterproject she calls 'naturalizing machine agency': asking not how to make machines more intelligent, but how to use machines to genuinely augment human agency in directions humans actually want to go.

## Apprentice

When a machine renews your driver's license at a kiosk, it looks smart. But Bonnitta Roy, drawing on Luciano Floridi, argues the trick is not in the machine — it is in everything that happened before you arrived. The DMV standardized its forms, eliminated edge cases, and imposed strict protocols so that a machine could handle the interaction. The intelligence is not in the system; the variability was removed from the world.

Roy illustrates this with a personal example: a new Volvo's backup safety system kept shutting itself off in her driveway because it could not make sense of a garden with wildflowers and fountains. For the machine to work, the environment would need to be paved flat and made legible to its sensors. This is the quiet bargain happening everywhere — we reshape schools, hospitals, and bureaucracies to fit what machines can do, and then celebrate the machines for doing it.

The deeper concern is that this redesign happens without public conversation. It is a form of design that changes what counts as normal behavior, acceptable environments, and even valid human needs. Roy's counterproposal is what she calls "naturalizing machine agency" — instead of bending human life to suit machines, we should ask how machines can genuinely support the directions humans actually want to go.

## Adept

Luciano Floridi's distinction — that machines do not possess intelligence but provide agency — anchors Bonnitta Roy's critique of a largely invisible civilizational process. The appearance of machine intelligence, Roy argues, is produced through a two-step mechanism: first, human systems (education, medicine, bureaucracy) are progressively standardized to eliminate variability; then machines perform those flattened functions, and the result is labeled intelligence. The DMV kiosk does not understand your situation — it operates in a world pre-engineered to exclude every situation it cannot parse.

Roy's Volvo example sharpens the point. A backup safety system that repeatedly disabled itself when confronted with a garden of wildflowers and fountains reveals the brittleness that standardized environments conceal. For the machine to function as designed, the driveway would need to be stripped of ecological complexity. This is not a bug in one car — it is the structural logic of machine accommodation scaled across domains. Environments, social norms, and behavioral protocols are redesigned to suit machine limitations, and this redesign constitutes a form of ontological design operating below the threshold of democratic deliberation.

Roy names the counterproject "naturalizing machine agency." Rather than asking how machines can be made more intelligent — a question that implicitly accepts the flattening of human environments as the cost of progress — the question becomes how machine agency can be deployed to genuinely augment human capacities in directions humans reflectively endorse. This reframes the entire discourse: the civilizational risk is not superintelligence but the systematic impoverishment of human lifeworlds in service of machine legibility.

## Magus

Floridi's thesis that machines furnish agency rather than intelligence provides the analytical fulcrum for Bonnitta Roy's diagnosis of a civilizational process she treats as a species of ontological design. The phenomenology of machine intelligence, on this account, is an artifact of environmental pre-processing: human institutions undergo systematic variability reduction — standardization of inputs, elimination of edge cases, behavioral protocol enforcement — until the residual task space falls within machine-tractable parameters. Intelligence is then attributed to the machine through a category error that conceals the prior ontological flattening. The DMV kiosk renews licenses not because it comprehends civic identity but because the entire interaction topology has been pruned to its operational grammar.

Roy's Volvo anecdote functions as a limit case exposing the concealed fragility. A backup safety system that cannot parse a garden with wildflowers and fountains — disabling itself repeatedly in the face of ecological complexity — reveals the degree to which machine functionality presupposes environmental accommodation. Scaled across domains, this logic constitutes what might be called an infrastructure of legibility: the progressive redesign of lifeworlds, social protocols, and normative expectations to satisfy machine-readable constraints, enacted largely without deliberative authorization.

The counterproject Roy designates as "naturalizing machine agency" refuses the implicit telos of this process. Rather than pursuing machine intelligence — a trajectory that structurally demands the impoverishment of human environments as its enabling condition — the question is reframed around genuine augmentation: deploying machine agency in service of human capacities and directions that survive reflective endorsement. This inversion carries significant implications for design epistemology, democratic governance of sociotechnical systems, and the ontological politics of what counts as a well-functioning environment.
