# Why Metamodernism Must Honestly Reckon With Postmodernism

You cannot transcend what you have not understood.

---

> Most critiques of postmodernism — and most developmental frameworks that claim to transcend it — fail the ideological Turing test. You cannot genuinely move beyond a position you have never honestly understood, and authentic metamodern thinking requires engaging postmodern insights rather than caricaturing them.

The ideological Turing test offers a useful standard for intellectual honesty in cultural analysis: you should be able to describe a position in such a way that someone who holds it would recognize themselves in your description. Applied to postmodernism, most of what passes for critique — from Jordan Peterson, from Stephen Hicks, from much of the integral and metamodern community — fails this test badly. Derrida was not an anti-realist. Foucault identified as an Enlightenment thinker. When you build a developmental framework that requires postmodernism as a prior stage to be transcended, and your characterization of postmodernism is a caricature that no actual postmodernist would recognize, you've constructed a straw man and then claimed to have moved beyond it. The genuine metamodern move, at its best, takes postmodern insights seriously — that knowledge is perspectival and socially conditioned, that power is embedded in knowledge production, that the view from nowhere is an illusion — and then asks: can we relate these perspectival systems to each other? Can we find a higher-order framework that doesn't collapse the differences but actually maps the relationships between them? This is why genuine metamodern thought requires actually engaging with postmodernism rather than caricaturing it — you can't make the next move if you haven't genuinely grappled with the move that preceded it.

## Apprentice

There's a useful test for intellectual honesty: can you describe someone else's position so well that they'd recognize themselves in your description? This is sometimes called the ideological Turing test. When applied to how postmodernism gets discussed — by popular critics, by people building big-picture theories of cultural development — the results are embarrassing. Most of what passes for critique is aimed at a version of postmodernism that actual postmodern thinkers wouldn't recognize. Derrida wasn't saying reality doesn't exist. Foucault considered himself part of the Enlightenment tradition.

This matters especially for anyone who claims to have moved beyond postmodernism to some higher stage of thinking. If the version of postmodernism you've "transcended" is a cartoon, then you haven't actually transcended anything — you've just knocked down a scarecrow and declared victory.

The genuinely interesting move is to take postmodern insights seriously — that all knowledge comes from a perspective, that power shapes what counts as truth, that no one has a perfectly neutral view of the world — and then ask: can we map how these different perspectives relate to each other without pretending the differences don't exist? That's a much harder and more honest project, and it can only begin with actually understanding what came before.

## Adept

The ideological Turing test — the standard that you should be able to articulate a position such that its adherents would recognize themselves in your account — exposes a pervasive failure in contemporary cultural criticism. Figures like Jordan Peterson and Stephen Hicks, along with much of the integral and metamodern community, routinely characterize postmodernism as radical anti-realism, nihilistic relativism, or a wholesale rejection of truth. Yet Derrida's project was never a denial of reality but an interrogation of the metaphysics of presence. Foucault explicitly situated himself within the Enlightenment tradition, extending its critical impulse rather than abandoning it. These characterizations fail the ideological Turing test decisively.

The stakes are particularly high for developmental frameworks — Spiral Dynamics, integral theory, metamodernism — that position postmodernism as a necessary but limited stage to be transcended. If the "postmodern stage" in your model is a straw man that no serious poststructuralist would recognize, then the transcendence you claim is illusory. You have not integrated and moved beyond; you have sidestepped.

Genuine metamodern thinking, at its most rigorous, takes the core postmodern insights as non-negotiable starting points: knowledge is perspectival and socially conditioned, power is constitutive of knowledge production, and the view from nowhere is epistemologically untenable. The authentic next move asks whether these irreducibly perspectival systems can be related to one another through a higher-order framework that maps their relationships without collapsing their differences. This move is only available to those who have genuinely grappled with postmodern thought rather than performed its defeat.

## Magus

The ideological Turing test functions here as a diagnostic instrument for epistemic integrity in metatheoretical construction. Applied rigorously, it reveals that the dominant critiques of postmodernism — from Hicks's conflation of poststructuralism with epistemological nihilism to Peterson's reduction of Derrida and Foucault to neo-Marxist anti-realists, to integral theory's staging of "green" as relativistic paralysis — constitute systematic misreadings that no practitioner of the tradition would ratify. Derrida's différance is not a thesis about the nonexistence of reference but an account of the conditions of signification; Foucault's genealogical method extends rather than repudiates the Kantian critical project, as he himself argued in his late engagement with "What Is Enlightenment?" The failure is not incidental but structural: these frameworks require a deficient postmodernism to authorize their own developmental claims.

This structural dependency exposes a paradox at the heart of transcend-and-include models. If the characterization of the stage to be transcended is a caricature — if it does not pass the ideological Turing test — then the claimed transcendence is performative rather than substantive. The framework has not sublated postmodernism but constructed a simulacrum of it as a foil for its own self-legitimation. The developmental claim becomes circular: the necessity of the next stage is demonstrated by the inadequacy of a stage that has been rendered inadequate by design.

The genuinely metamodern operation, properly conceived, begins from the irreducibility of poststructuralist insights — the perspectival constitution of knowledge, the power-knowledge nexus, the impossibility of a non-situated epistemic position — and poses a second-order question: what relational topology can map the connections between incommensurable perspectival frameworks without reducing them to a common denominator or reinstating a covert view from nowhere? This question is only formulable by those who have internalized rather than caricatured the postmodern problematic.
