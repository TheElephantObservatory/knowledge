# How Language Crosses the Boundary Between Private and Shared Minds

The wall that was never there

---

> Consciousness studies forgot self-consciousness. The higher-order, linguistically mediated awareness humans use to reflect on their own minds — Mind 3 — passes between people without changing form, making language a channel for something like direct perception of another's propositional thought. This reshapes the hard problem itself.

One of the most neglected dimensions of human consciousness is Mind 3 — self-conscious, linguistically mediated, propositional awareness — and its relationship to Mind 2. The history of consciousness studies has undergone a strange inversion: in the Freudian era and in Cartesian philosophy, 'consciousness' meant precisely this higher-order self-reflective awareness — the cogito, the ego reflecting on itself. Then, as the hard problem came to dominate the field, attention shifted almost entirely to subjective experience and its relationship to the physical, and self-consciousness was quietly forgotten as a central explanandum. But Mind 3 has a remarkable and underappreciated property: it is a propositional representational system that passes directly through the skin without changing its form. When two people share a language, one person's Mind 3a — their private linguistic-propositional inner speech — becomes directly perceptible to another as Mind 3b — public linguistic expression. There is no semantic interface barrier; the representational structure is the same on both sides. This means that language affords something like direct perception of another's minded experience at the propositional level. Furthermore, Mind 3 connects back into Mind 2 through the phonological working memory loop — the inner speech system — meaning that human subjective consciousness is not just animal sentience but is deeply interpenetrated by the intersubjective linguistic field of the culture-person plane. To understand human Mind 2, you must understand Mind 3, because the two are architecturally coupled. The consciousness literature's near-exclusive focus on the hard problem of subjective experience, while ignoring this entire pathway of intersubjective self-conscious reflection, represents a significant blind spot that the Map of Mind is designed to correct.

## Apprentice

There's a kind of awareness that most consciousness research has quietly stopped talking about: the voice in your head, the capacity to reflect on your own thoughts using language. In the Freudian and Cartesian traditions, this self-reflective awareness was what "consciousness" meant. But as philosophers became consumed by the mystery of why experience feels like anything at all, this higher layer of mind slipped off the agenda.

What makes this neglect so costly is that linguistic self-awareness has a remarkable property. When you speak, the internal structure of your private thought — your inner speech — passes directly to another person without needing to be translated into some different format. The words in your head and the words leaving your mouth share the same representational form. This means language gives us something close to direct access to another person's reflective mind, not just their emotions or body language.

This matters because inner speech isn't separate from felt experience — it loops back into it constantly. The running commentary in your head shapes what it's like to be you. So human consciousness isn't just raw animal feeling; it's feeling thoroughly woven together with language, culture, and other people's minds. Ignoring this dimension means the science of consciousness is working with an incomplete map.

## Adept

The history of consciousness studies contains a peculiar inversion. For Descartes and Freud alike, "consciousness" referred primarily to higher-order self-reflective awareness — the cogito, the ego examining itself. With the ascendancy of the hard problem, however, the field's center of gravity shifted almost entirely to phenomenal experience and its relationship to the physical. Self-consciousness, the very thing that once defined the territory, became a neglected explanandum. The Map of Mind framework identifies this as Mind 3 — linguistically mediated, propositional, self-conscious awareness — and argues that its marginalization constitutes a major blind spot.

Mind 3 possesses a property with profound implications for the problem of other minds. As a propositional representational system, it passes through the boundary of the individual without changing its structural form. Private inner speech (Mind 3a) and public linguistic expression (Mind 3b) share the same semantic architecture. There is no interface problem, no radical translation barrier. Language thus affords something approaching direct intersubjective perception at the propositional level — a point with no analogue in the phenomenal domain, where the explanatory gap remains intractable.

Critically, Mind 3 is not architecturally independent of Mind 2 (subjective phenomenal experience). The phonological working memory loop — the inner speech system — means that propositional self-reflection continuously modulates the stream of conscious experience. Human Mind 2 is not merely animal sentience; it is sentience interpenetrated by the intersubjective linguistic field. Any account of human phenomenal consciousness that ignores this coupling with the culture-person plane is working with a systematically incomplete model.

## Magus

A striking inversion structures the modern consciousness literature. From Descartes through Freud, 'consciousness' designated precisely the higher-order, self-reflective capacity — the cogito's recursive self-apprehension, the ego's propositional self-monitoring. The hard problem's dominance effected a near-total displacement: phenomenal experience became the sole explanandum, and self-consciousness was demoted to an epiphenomenal curiosity or dissolved into theories of higher-order representation that never interrogated its linguistic constitution. The Map of Mind framework reinstates this dimension as Mind 3 — self-conscious, linguistically mediated, propositional awareness — and insists on its architectural coupling with Mind 2.

The central claim is that Mind 3 exhibits a unique semiotic property: as a propositional representational system, it traverses the boundary between interiority and exteriority isomorphically. Mind 3a (private linguistic-propositional inner speech) and Mind 3b (public linguistic expression) share representational structure without requiring transduction across a semantic interface. This is not metaphor: the combinatorial syntax and semantic content are formally continuous across the skin. The implication is that language constitutes a channel of quasi-direct intersubjective access at the propositional level — a partial dissolution of the problem of other minds that operates orthogonally to the explanatory gap governing phenomenal consciousness.

Furthermore, Mind 3 is not epiphenomenal with respect to Mind 2. The phonological loop — Baddeley's articulatory rehearsal system repurposed as inner speech — ensures that propositional self-reflection continuously modulates phenomenal experience. Human subjective consciousness is therefore not animal sentience plus linguistic decoration; it is sentience constitutively interpenetrated by the intersubjective field of the culture-person plane. The field's near-exclusive fixation on qualia and the hard problem, while this entire pathway of intersubjective propositional consciousness goes untheorized, represents not a gap but a systematic distortion of the problem space.
