# Why Brains and Bodies Create a Different Kind of Intelligence Than Cells Do

The beaver builds; the cell becomes.

---

> Michael Levin's work on biological intelligence actually reinforces UTOK's key distinction: the embedded problem-solving of cells and tissues is categorically different from the mindedness that emerges when a complex body with a nervous system navigates three-dimensional space. 4E cognitive science should really be called 4E mindedness science.

There is a subtle but important distinction between what Michael Levin is doing and what UTOK is doing — and understanding that distinction actually clarifies both. Levin is making a powerful continuity argument: that intelligence, problem-solving, and memory-like processes are embedded throughout biological systems, not just in nervous systems. Fungi, cells, morphological development — all of these exhibit something that looks like epistemic agency. And UTOK has no problem with this. It embraces it. But here is the irony: Levin's own framework actually ends up reinforcing UTOK's discontinuity point, because Levin himself acknowledges that what he is describing is not 3D behavioral space. It is not a bee pollinating a flower, a beaver building a dam, a lion hunting an antelope — the behavior of an animal as a whole, navigating a physical environment through coordinated movement. That is a different kind of intelligence. Bio-cognitive processes in morphological space are real and important, but they are categorically distinct from the neuro-cognitive processes that arise when a complex active body with an integrated nervous system begins navigating prey-predator dynamics in three-dimensional space. So the right vocabulary matters: bio-cognitive for the embedded intelligence of living systems broadly, and mindedness for the specific property that emerges with brains and coordinated bodies. What this also means is that 4E cognitive science — embodied, embedded, enacted, extended — is pointing at exactly the right thing, but using the wrong word. 'Cognitive' is too ambiguous, too easily collapsed into computation or bio-intelligence. What 4E cognitive science is really describing is mindedness. It should be called 4E mindedness science. Mindedness is the concept that makes the boundaries clear, that explains why a beaver's dam might be an extended mind in a way that a cell's morphological problem-solving is not.

## Apprentice

Michael Levin has shown that intelligence isn't limited to brains. Cells, fungi, and developing embryos all solve problems and store information in remarkable ways. This is a powerful insight, and the Unified Theory of Knowledge (UTOK) fully embraces it. But there's a crucial twist: Levin's own work actually helps prove a point he might not have intended.

The intelligence of a cell figuring out how to grow into the right shape is real, but it's fundamentally different from a lion chasing an antelope across a savanna. When a complex animal with a brain and coordinated body moves through the physical world — hunting, fleeing, building — something new has emerged. That's not just biological problem-solving anymore. It's a whole different category.

This distinction suggests we need better vocabulary. The proposal is to call the broad intelligence of living systems "bio-cognitive" and reserve the term "mindedness" for what happens when brains and bodies navigate three-dimensional space together. This also means that 4E cognitive science — which studies how thinking is embodied, embedded, enacted, and extended — is actually describing mindedness, not cognition in general. Calling it "4E mindedness science" would clear up a lot of confusion about where biological intelligence ends and genuine minds begin.

## Adept

Michael Levin's continuity thesis — that intelligence, memory, and problem-solving pervade biological systems from cellular morphogenesis to fungal networks — represents a significant advance in understanding life's epistemic capacities. UTOK not only accommodates this thesis but argues that Levin's own framework inadvertently strengthens its central discontinuity claim. Levin himself recognizes that the intelligence he describes operates in morphological space, not in the three-dimensional behavioral space where whole organisms navigate prey-predator dynamics through coordinated movement.

This recognition exposes a categorical boundary. Bio-cognitive processes — the embedded intelligence of cells, tissues, and developmental systems — are real and important, but they are not equivalent to the neuro-cognitive processes that emerge when a complex active body with an integrated nervous system begins operating as a unified agent in physical space. A beaver building a dam and a cell solving a morphological problem are both exhibiting intelligence, but they belong to fundamentally different domains of epistemic agency.

The terminological implications are significant. "Bio-cognitive" should designate the broad intelligence of living systems, while "mindedness" should name the specific property that emerges with brains and coordinated bodies. This reframing also targets 4E cognitive science directly: embodied, embedded, enacted, and extended cognition frameworks are identifying genuine phenomena, but the word "cognitive" is too ambiguous — too easily collapsed into computation or generic bio-intelligence. What 4E frameworks actually describe is mindedness. Renaming the enterprise "4E mindedness science" would resolve persistent boundary confusions and clarify why a beaver's dam qualifies as extended mind while a cell's morphological problem-solving does not.

## Magus

Levin's continuity thesis — that epistemic agency pervades biological organization from bioelectric networks through morphogenetic fields to fungal intelligence — constitutes a genuinely important empirical and conceptual contribution. Yet a careful reading reveals an internal tension that UTOK's architecture is uniquely positioned to resolve. Levin's own operational definitions implicitly concede that the intelligence he characterizes functions within morphological space: the competency of tissues navigating anatomical possibility spaces, not the competency of whole organisms navigating three-dimensional ecological niches through sensorimotor coordination. The continuity argument, pursued rigorously, generates precisely the discontinuity it appears to deny.

The distinction is not merely scalar but categorical. Bio-cognitive processes — the distributed problem-solving of living systems operating below the threshold of centralized nervous integration — instantiate genuine epistemic work. But neuro-cognitive processes emerge at a specific juncture in evolutionary history: when complex active bodies with integrated nervous systems begin functioning as unified behavioral agents within prey-predator dynamics and three-dimensional environmental contingencies. Mindedness names this emergent property with precision that "cognition" cannot, because cognition has been irreparably diluted across computational, biological, and phenomenological registers.

This analysis carries direct consequences for 4E cognitive science. The embodied-embedded-enacted-extended framework identifies real structural features of minded systems — the constitutive role of body, environment, action, and scaffolding in shaping intelligent behavior. But by retaining "cognitive" as its central predicate, 4E science perpetually risks conflation with the very computationalist paradigm it opposes, or with the bio-cognitive processes Levin describes. The corrective is terminological and ontological simultaneously: 4E mindedness science names the actual explanandum. Mindedness draws the boundary that explains why a beaver's dam participates in extended mind while a cell's morphological competency, however sophisticated, does not cross that threshold.
