# Translative and Transformative Spirituality in Integral Post-Metaphysical Practice

Holding the sacred without clutching it

---

> Spiritual life requires both meaning-making practices and genuinely transformative ones. An integral post-metaphysical approach treats spiritual phenomena as participatory domains of experience rather than fixed metaphysical truths, and organizes practice into flexible, modular life disciplines spanning body, mind, shadow, spirit, ethics, and relationship.

The integral post-metaphysical spiritual orientation distinguishes between two primary branches of spiritual practice — translative and transformative — and holds that both are essential while acknowledging the line between them is genuinely blurry. Translative spirituality supports sensemaking and horizontal adaptation: coping with and thriving within our various life situations through wisdom stories, archetypal models, and ritual. Transformative spirituality is developmentally oriented, focused on state training, perspectival flexibility, self-growth, and transcendence. The post-metaphysical dimension of this orientation does not reject ontology or epistemology — those remain important — but it does involve holding spiritual phenomena enactively, in the Santiago school or 4E cognitive science sense, as autopoietic participatory domains of experience available for cultivation, while holding many traditional metaphysical associations lightly or in suspension. This is not a rejection of the sacred but a distancing from the metaphysics of presence and similar presuppositions. The practical structure that has emerged from this orientation is a modular approach — integral life practice — with modules covering shadow, body, mind, spirit, ethics, and relational dimensions, each fillable with any number of practices, taken as a touchstone rather than a rigid requirement. The goal is a broadly integrated suite of transformative practices drawn from shamanic, contemplative, entheogenic, philosophical, artistic, somatic, psychoanalytic, cognitive scientific, evolutionary, postmodern, and socially liberative traditions — covering, in Wilber's terms, all four quadrants of human experience.

## Apprentice

There are broadly two kinds of spiritual practice. One helps us make sense of life — finding comfort, meaning, and guidance through stories, rituals, and wisdom traditions. The other aims at deep personal transformation — meditation, self-inquiry, and practices that shift how we see ourselves and the world. Both matter, and the boundary between them is often fuzzy.

This perspective also takes a distinctive stance on what spiritual experiences actually are. Rather than insisting they prove the existence of specific metaphysical realities, it treats them as real domains of lived experience that we can cultivate and explore — without needing to lock in rigid beliefs about what lies behind them. This isn't dismissing the sacred; it's holding traditional explanations more loosely so the experiences themselves can breathe.

The practical upshot is a modular approach to growth. Instead of following one tradition exclusively, the idea is to build a personal practice that covers multiple dimensions of life — emotional healing, physical health, intellectual development, contemplative depth, ethical engagement, and relationships. These modules can be filled with practices from any tradition — contemplative, artistic, somatic, philosophical, psychoanalytic, or otherwise — creating a flexible, personalized path rather than a one-size-fits-all program.

## Adept

The integral post-metaphysical spiritual orientation, drawing heavily on Ken Wilber's framework, distinguishes between translative and transformative spirituality while insisting both are indispensable. Translative practice supports horizontal adaptation — sensemaking, coping, and thriving through archetypal models, wisdom narratives, and ritual. Transformative practice is vertically oriented: state training, perspectival flexibility, developmental growth, and transcendence. The line between these two branches is acknowledged as genuinely blurry, resisting neat categorization.

The post-metaphysical dimension does not abandon ontology or epistemology but reframes spiritual phenomena through an enactive lens — specifically the Santiago school of autopoiesis and 4E cognitive science. Spiritual experiences are treated as participatory domains of experience available for cultivation rather than as evidence for fixed metaphysical claims. This involves holding the metaphysics of presence and similar presuppositions in suspension — not rejecting the sacred, but refusing to collapse it into any single ontological commitment.

The practical architecture that emerges is integral life practice: a modular system with dimensions covering shadow work, body, mind, spirit, ethics, and relational life. Each module can be populated with practices drawn from an extraordinarily wide range of traditions — shamanic, contemplative, entheogenic, philosophical, artistic, somatic, psychoanalytic, cognitive scientific, evolutionary, postmodern, and socially liberative. The aim is comprehensive coverage across all four quadrants of Wilber's AQAL model — interior-individual, exterior-individual, interior-collective, and exterior-collective — taken as a touchstone for integration rather than a rigid prescription.

## Magus

This orientation operationalizes the translative/transformative distinction within spiritual practice — the former addressing horizontal sensemaking through narrative, archetype, and ritual; the latter targeting vertical development via state training, perspectival flexibility, and transcendence — while foregrounding the genuine porosity of the boundary between them. Neither branch is dispensable; the integral move is their deliberate coordination within a single practitioner's life.

The post-metaphysical commitment here is precise and should not be mistaken for anti-metaphysical positivism. Ontology and epistemology remain operative; what shifts is the epistemic stance toward spiritual phenomena. Drawing on the Santiago school's autopoietic theory and the broader 4E cognitive science framework, spiritual domains are enacted — participatory, structurally coupled, and co-constituted through the practitioner's engagement rather than pregiven as mind-independent objects. Traditional metaphysical associations (the metaphysics of presence, substance ontologies of spirit, naïve perennialism) are held in phenomenological suspension rather than affirmed or denied. This is Wilber's post-metaphysical turn read through Varela, Thompson, and Ferrer: the sacred is not evacuated but relocated from correspondence to enactment.

The resultant praxis takes the form of integral life practice — a modular architecture spanning shadow, body, mind, spirit, ethics, and relational dimensions, each module functioning as an open slot fillable from shamanic, contemplative, entheogenic, philosophical, artistic, somatic, psychoanalytic, cognitive scientific, evolutionary, postmodern, and socially liberative lineages. The AQAL four-quadrant model serves as a heuristic ensuring no major dimension of experience is systematically neglected. The structure is explicitly offered as touchstone rather than prescription — a meta-framework for composing a broadly integrated transformative life from heterogeneous sources without collapsing their distinctiveness.
