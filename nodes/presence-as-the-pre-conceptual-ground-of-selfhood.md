# Presence as the Pre-Conceptual Ground of Selfhood

The world becomes real before you think it.

---

> The feeling of being real — the raw 'I-here-now' of experience — is not a concept or a story about yourself. It is a sub-conceptual co-presencing of self and world that precedes all categories, and its breakdown in depersonalization reveals how foundational it truly is.

The sense of presence — the demonstrative indexicality of the self — is one of the most philosophically precise yet underappreciated features of self-experience. When Nagel asked 'what is it like to be a bat,' he was pointing at something that resists categorical description: the this-here-now-ness of being a subject in a state of consciousness in a world. Demonstratives — 'this,' 'here,' 'now' — do not refer by picking out a category; they operate below categorical identity entirely. They make a salience landscape stand out. And indexicals — 'here,' 'now,' 'I' — only refer relative to the position of the speaker. What the sense of presence involves is exactly this: a co-presencing in which the I-here-now and the world-disclosing-itself are mutually constituting and interwoven, such that the world becomes real. This is why virtual reality researchers pursue presence so deliberately — verisimilitude alone does not produce it. Tetris, with minimal visual fidelity, can generate profound presence; photorealistic environments sometimes fail entirely. The realness of experience is not a function of resemblance but of this co-presencing. And crucially, this demonstrative indexicality falls below conceptual thought — it is the precondition for forming categories at all, since to group things into a category you must first pick them out demonstratively. This means the self, insofar as it is grounded in this sense of presence, is not primarily a conceptual or narrative entity. It is sub-conceptual, pre-categorical, and its disruption — as in depersonalization and derealization — reveals just how foundational this co-presencing is to the coherence of experience itself.

## Apprentice

There is something about experience that is hard to put into words: the sheer sense that you are here, now, in this moment, and that the world around you is real. This feeling of presence is not something you think your way into — it operates beneath thought entirely. It is the raw "this-here-now" quality of being a conscious subject.

Interestingly, this sense of realness has little to do with how detailed or accurate your surroundings appear. Virtual reality researchers have found that a simple game like Tetris can produce a deep feeling of presence, while photorealistic environments sometimes feel hollow. What makes experience feel real is not resemblance to reality but a kind of mutual arising — you and the world showing up together, each making the other vivid.

This matters because it suggests the self is not fundamentally a story you tell about who you are, or a set of categories you fit into. At its deepest level, the self is this pre-verbal sense of being present. When it breaks down — as in depersonalization, where people report feeling unreal or detached from their own experience — the entire coherence of consciousness can unravel. That reveals just how foundational this quiet, overlooked feature of experience actually is.

## Magus

Demonstrative indexicality constitutes the transcendental infrastructure of presence — the "this-here-now" through which a subject and its world co-arise as mutually disclosing. Nagel's celebrated question about bat-experience gestures toward precisely this: not qualia as categorical properties of mental states, but the deictic ground of phenomenality itself. Demonstratives do not refer by satisfying a description; they operate below the level at which categorical identity is constituted, generating a salience landscape from which objects and properties can subsequently be differentiated. Indexicals — "I," "here," "now" — refer only relative to the utterance-position of the speaker. The sense of presence is the lived enactment of this indexical structure: a co-presencing in which the I-pole and the world-pole are neither separable substances nor identical, but reciprocally constituting moments of a single disclosure.

This analysis finds unexpected empirical corroboration in presence research. The persistent failure of verisimilitude to guarantee presence — and the capacity of low-fidelity environments like Tetris to generate it — suggests that presence is not a representational achievement but a structural one, dependent on the coupling dynamics through which agent and environment become mutually salient. Photorealism addresses categorical resemblance; presence requires demonstrative grip.

The deeper philosophical consequence is that demonstrative indexicality is the precondition for categorial thought as such — one must pick something out deictically before subsuming it under a concept. The self, grounded in this co-presencing, is therefore sub-conceptual and pre-categorical in a rigorous sense. It is not the narrative self of Dennett nor the minimal self of Zahavi alone, but the deictic self — the self as indexical anchor of disclosure. Depersonalization and derealization, understood as disruptions of this demonstrative infrastructure, reveal that the coherence of experience depends not on the integrity of concepts or narratives but on the functioning of this prior, constitutive co-presencing.
