# Three Pillars of a New Enlightenment

The lighthouse, the mirror, and the bridge.

---

> Civilization keeps failing because modern education only trains one of three essential ways of knowing. The path forward requires cultivating all three — rigorous reasoning about the world, genuine understanding of others, and clear-eyed knowledge of oneself — at civilizational scale.

Cultural enlightenment or bust. The only answer out of the twin failure modes of oppression on one side and chaos on the other is the comprehensive education of everyone in three distinct epistemic capacities. The third-person epistemic — most familiar from the modern Enlightenment (what Greg Henriques calls MENS, the Modern Enlightenment Natural Science paradigm) — is philosophy of science, formal logic, the ability to make sense of base reality through appropriate methodology and arrive at appropriate confidence margins. The second-person epistemic is the capacity to genuinely inhabit another's perspective — to steelman where someone is coming from, to find the synthesis of a dialectic rather than arguing for one side of partiality, because if you can't do that, you will harm the very thing you care about in the long run by defending only your partial view of it. The first-person epistemic is the capacity to notice your own biases, your own susceptibilities, your own group identity issues, well enough that those aren't the things that run you. Historically, the ancient Greek enlightenment distributed these three across traditions — the Stoics owned the first-person, the Socratics owned the second-person, and the Aristotelians owned the third-person. The modern Enlightenment gave us only the third-person dimension, leaving the first-person and second-person underdeveloped — and that asymmetry is precisely the root of the educational and civilizational crisis we are now in. Psychology as a discipline inherited that same confusion: it tried to become a third-person science of the human being while the human being is irreducibly also a first-person experiencer and a second-person relational creature. The failure to hold all three together is not merely an academic problem — it is deeply dangerous at a civilizational scale. What is needed now is a more comprehensive successor to the modern Enlightenment — one that facilitates an upleveling across all three epistemic modalities through techno-social, education-centric reform at broad scale. This aspiration has deep historical roots: Jan Amos Comenius articulated the basis of a new social reality beginning with culture during the Thirty Years' War, a liminal moment of chaos in which enlightenments arise. The civilizational project on the table right now is to educate everyone so that we can emerge a collective consciousness and collective intelligence that is more than our individual intelligence — something that is neither chaos nor oppression, because it is emergent rather than imposed.

## Apprentice

There are three distinct ways of knowing that a healthy person — and a healthy society — needs to develop. The first is the ability to think clearly about the outside world: to use logic, evidence, and good methods to figure out what is actually true. The second is the ability to genuinely understand other people — not just tolerate them, but actually inhabit their perspective well enough to find the real truth hiding between opposing views. The third is the ability to know yourself honestly: your biases, your blind spots, the group loyalties that quietly shape your thinking without your permission.

The modern world got very good at the first kind of knowing — science, philosophy, formal reasoning — but largely neglected the other two. That imbalance is not a minor gap. When people can reason about the world but cannot understand each other or themselves, the result is exactly the crisis visible today: communities that cannot talk across differences, individuals captured by their own unexamined assumptions, institutions that oscillate between authoritarian control and incoherent fragmentation.

The argument here is that the only real way out is a civilizational-scale educational project — one that develops all three capacities in everyone, not just specialists. This is not a utopian fantasy but a historically grounded possibility. Thinkers like Jan Amos Comenius imagined something similar during the chaos of the Thirty Years' War. The goal is a collective intelligence that is genuinely smarter and wiser than any individual within it — one that emerges from educated people rather than being imposed by powerful ones.

## Adept

Greg Henriques's framing of MENS — the Modern Enlightenment Natural Science paradigm — captures what the Enlightenment actually delivered: a robust third-person epistemology, the capacity to model base reality through controlled methodology, falsifiability, and calibrated confidence. What it did not deliver, and what the ancient Greek traditions had distributed across distinct schools, were the complementary epistemic modalities. The Stoics cultivated first-person epistemics — the disciplined examination of one's own cognition, affect, and identity commitments. The Socratics cultivated second-person epistemics — dialectical engagement aimed not at winning but at reaching synthesis through genuine perspective-taking. The Aristotelians systematized the third-person. Modernity inherited only one leg of that stool.

The consequences are structural, not incidental. Psychology attempted to become a third-person science of the human being while the human being is irreducibly a first-person experiencer and a second-person relational creature. The discipline's internal tensions — between behaviorism and phenomenology, between nomothetic and idiographic approaches — are symptoms of this foundational asymmetry. At the civilizational level, the same asymmetry produces a predictable oscillation between oppression (imposed order substituting for genuine coherence) and chaos (the collapse of shared meaning when no integrative capacity exists).

The thesis is that escaping this oscillation requires a successor to the modern Enlightenment — one that facilitates development across all three epistemic modalities through education-centric, techno-social reform at broad scale. Comenius articulated the seed of this vision during the Thirty Years' War, another liminal civilizational moment. The aspiration is not merely better schooling but the emergence of a collective consciousness and collective intelligence that exceeds the sum of its parts — not through imposition but through the genuine epistemic upleveling of its members.

## Magus

The tripartite epistemic schema advanced here maps onto a deep structure in the history of philosophy. First-person epistemics — the reflexive examination of the knowing subject's own cognitive and affective constitution, its susceptibilities, its identity-laden distortions — corresponds to what phenomenological and contemplative traditions have variously thematized as self-knowledge, apperception, or metacognitive hygiene. Second-person epistemics — the capacity for genuine perspective-taking, for inhabiting the intentional horizon of an interlocutor sufficiently to locate the dialectical synthesis rather than merely advocate a thesis — corresponds to what Gadamer theorized as the fusion of horizons and what Hegel's dialectic presupposes as its operative condition. Third-person epistemics — the modeling of mind-independent reality through intersubjectively validated methodology, falsifiability criteria, and probabilistic confidence calibration — is the explicit achievement of the Enlightenment as institutionalized in MENS.

The historical argument is that the ancient Greek enlightenment distributed these modalities across distinct schools — Stoic askesis for the first-person, Socratic elenchus for the second-person, Aristotelian systematization for the third-person — whereas the modern Enlightenment achieved a decisive but partial advance, hypertrophying the third-person dimension while leaving the other two epistemically underdeveloped and institutionally homeless. Psychology's constitutive tension between Naturwissenschaft and Geisteswissenschaft methodologies is a direct downstream consequence: the discipline attempted a third-person science of an entity that is structurally irreducible to third-person description alone.

The civilizational stakes are not rhetorical amplification. Without first-person epistemic capacity, individuals remain captured by unexamined group-identity dynamics and affective distortions; without second-person epistemic capacity, discourse degenerates into positional advocacy incapable of genuine synthesis. The resulting social epistemology oscillates structurally between imposed order and entropic fragmentation. The Comenian aspiration — a pansophic reform beginning in culture and education during a liminal moment of civilizational crisis — names the only coherent exit: a techno-social, education-centric project aimed at the broad-scale cultivation of all three modalities, producing an emergent collective intelligence that is neither the aggregation of partialities nor the artifact of domination.
