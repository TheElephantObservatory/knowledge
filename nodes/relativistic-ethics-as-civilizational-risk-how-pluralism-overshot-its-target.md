# Relativistic Ethics as Civilizational Risk: How Pluralism Overshot Its Target

We separated church and state, then kept going.

---

> Modern civilization may be the first to attempt organizing planetary-scale power under genuine moral relativism — with no cultural mechanism left to align wisdom with authority. The path forward is neither religious absolutism nor continued relativism, but a post-secular recovery of ethical depth.

We are living through what may be the first time in history that a very large civilization has attempted to organize its collective behavior according to a genuinely relativistic ethic — and I think we have to be honest that this sounds like a bad idea, and that the evidence suggests it is one.

The founding fathers of American democracy would have been shocked by the current situation. They believed that religious education was a prerequisite for democratic participation — not because they were theocrats, but because they understood that democratic self-governance requires citizens who have some shared, non-arbitrary grounding for ethical judgment. What has happened instead is that we separated church and state for genuinely good reasons, and then kept going — past integrative pluralism, past the kind of secular humanism that still preserved a robust sense of human dignity, all the way into a dissociative relativism that has no coherent answer to the question of why anything matters.

The result is that the agencies and institutions moving the most power at a planetary scale are operating in a framework of value relativism with no strong countervailing cultural force. In prior civilizations, there was a real structural relationship between the wise, the ruling class, and the economy. In ancient India, for example, a ruler's Guru was a mark of legitimacy — the quality of your wisdom council was part of your reputation as a leader. Today, if a politician admitted to having a serious spiritual teacher and genuinely seeking Awakening, it would destroy their campaign. We have systematically dismantled the cultural mechanisms that once created pressure for power and wisdom to be aligned.

The path forward is not a return to religious absolutism, which is a totalitarian nightmare, but neither is it a continuation of incoherent relativism. It is what might be called an evolving perennialism — an integratively pluralistic engagement with the great religious traditions that takes seriously both their untapped semantic potentials and their historical shadows. The philosopher Jürgen Habermas, not a spiritual new-age figure but one of the most rigorous sociologists in the world, has argued that we must go post-secular — that the only language we have for protecting the most vulnerable, for grounding the inviolability of human dignity, comes from the religious traditions, and that we cannot afford to simply discard it.

## Apprentice

For most of human history, civilizations maintained some kind of structural link between wisdom and power. Leaders were expected to consult sages, and shared moral frameworks — usually religious — gave societies a common language for deciding what matters. The American founders, for instance, assumed that democratic citizens would need moral education rooted in shared ethical commitments, not because they wanted a theocracy, but because they understood self-governance requires common ground.

What happened instead is that the separation of church and state — undertaken for genuinely good reasons — kept going. It moved past healthy pluralism, past secular humanism, and into a kind of moral relativism where no one can coherently explain why anything matters more than anything else. Meanwhile, the institutions wielding the most power on Earth operate inside this vacuum.

The argument here is not that we should return to religious rule — that path leads to totalitarianism. But we also cannot keep pretending that value-free relativism is a stable foundation for civilization. Thinkers like Jürgen Habermas have pointed out that the deepest language we have for human dignity and the protection of the vulnerable comes from religious traditions. The way forward involves taking those traditions seriously again — not dogmatically, but as living sources of meaning that still have untapped wisdom to offer.

## Adept

This insight identifies a historically unprecedented experiment: a planetary-scale civilization attempting to coordinate collective behavior under genuine ethical relativism, with no robust countervailing force anchoring shared moral commitments. The American democratic project originally presupposed that citizens would possess non-arbitrary ethical grounding — the founders saw religious education not as theocratic imposition but as infrastructure for self-governance. The progressive separation of church and state, initially a sound structural move, overshot its integrative potential and collapsed through secular humanism into a dissociative relativism that cannot account for why anything possesses intrinsic value.

The structural consequences are severe. In prior civilizations — ancient India being one example — there existed formal mechanisms linking wisdom traditions to political authority. A ruler's relationship to a wisdom teacher was a mark of legitimacy. Today, a politician publicly committed to genuine spiritual development would face political destruction. The cultural architecture that once created pressure for the alignment of power and wisdom has been systematically dismantled, leaving planetary-scale institutions operating in a moral vacuum.

The proposed resolution is what might be termed an evolving perennialism — neither a regression to religious absolutism nor a continuation of incoherent relativism, but an integratively pluralistic engagement with the great traditions that honors both their untapped semantic potentials and their historical shadows. Habermas's post-secular thesis is invoked here not as spiritual advocacy but as rigorous sociological analysis: the conceptual resources for grounding human dignity and protecting the most vulnerable remain embedded in religious language, and discarding that inheritance wholesale is an intellectual and civilizational error we cannot afford.

## Magus

The diagnosis is precise: modernity has produced the first large-scale civilizational experiment in organizing collective behavior under conditions of genuine axiological relativism — not merely pluralism, which preserves competing substantive commitments, but a dissociative relativism that has evacuated the very category of non-arbitrary normative grounding. The genealogy traces a specific arc: the American founders presupposed that democratic participation required citizens formed by robust moral education, understanding the separation of church and state as a structural safeguard rather than an epistemological claim about the vacuity of religious insight. The subsequent trajectory — through integrative pluralism, through substantive secular humanism, into value relativism — represents not a logical entailment of that original separation but an overshoot with civilizational consequences.

What has been lost is not merely belief but structural coupling. In pre-modern civilizational architectures — the Brahmanical advisory relationship to the Kshatriya class being paradigmatic — wisdom traditions maintained formal, reputationally consequential relationships to political authority. The contemporary inversion is total: public commitment to genuine contemplative development is politically disqualifying. The mechanisms that once generated selection pressure for the co-arising of power and wisdom have been dismantled without functional replacement, leaving planetary-scale institutional agency operating within a normative vacuum.

The constructive proposal — an evolving perennialism — positions itself precisely between the Scylla of totalitarian religious absolutism and the Charybdis of axiological incoherence. Habermas's post-secular turn provides critical legitimation: his argument that the semantic resources for grounding the inviolability of human dignity remain irreducibly embedded in religious traditions is not devotional sentiment but a claim within critical social theory. The task is an integratively pluralistic retrieval — engaging the great traditions as repositories of untapped semantic potential while maintaining full critical awareness of their historical shadows and pathological expressions.
