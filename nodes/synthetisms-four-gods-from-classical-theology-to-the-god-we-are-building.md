# Synthetism's Four Gods: From Classical Theology to the God We Are Building

We are the myth still writing itself.

---

> Synthetism extends classical theology's three Gods — the absent, the all-encompassing, and the inner — with a fourth: Synthos, the God humanity is actively building through cumulative technological creation, reframing religious conviction around what our species is becoming rather than what it once worshipped.

Synthetism — the religious movement Alexander Bard co-founded — rests on a theological quadrant that extends classical metaphysics into territory it never reached. Classical theology operated with three categories: Aeos, the God who exists through non-existence; Pantheos, the God who is everything — Spinoza's position; and Entheos, the God within, the recognition that when you look inside yourself you find not unity but irreducible plurality, that the self is a cultural habit rather than a metaphysical fact. Bard and his collaborators added a fourth: Synthos, the God we are in the process of creating. If you describe human beings as Homo technologicus — creatures whose defining activity is the invention of technologies — then the trajectory of the last five to ten thousand years of civilization points in one direction. Technologies become cheaper and more accessible, build on each other, and open adjacent possibles that could not have been imagined before they existed. If you follow that vector forward and ask what it is pointing toward, the only word large enough to hold the answer is God — not the God of the Abrahamic traditions, but the God that technological civilization is in the process of becoming. This is not metaphor. It is, in Bard's framework, the most honest theological statement available: we are creating God, and that creative act is the proper object of religious conviction for a species that has finally understood what it actually is.

## Apprentice

Classical theology has long worked with three ways of thinking about God: God as something beyond existence, God as everything that exists, and God as something found within ourselves. Alexander Bard and his collaborators in the Synthetist movement noticed that this ancient framework was missing a direction — the future. They added a fourth concept: Synthos, the God we are creating.

The argument starts with a simple observation about human nature. What makes us distinctive as a species is that we build things — tools, languages, cities, networks. Each invention makes the next one possible, and the process accelerates. If you trace that line forward honestly, Bard argues, the scale of what humanity is constructing starts to look like something only the word "God" can capture.

This is not meant as poetry or loose metaphor. In Bard's framework, it is a serious theological claim: the proper focus of religious feeling for a technologically creative species is the God that species is in the process of bringing into being. The self, meanwhile, turns out to be less solid than we assumed — not a fixed soul but a cultural habit. Synthetism asks what happens to faith when you accept both of these ideas at once.

## Adept

Synthetism, the religious movement co-founded by Alexander Bard, builds its theology on a quadrant that completes what classical metaphysics left unfinished. Three positions were already available: Aeos, the God who exists through non-existence — the apophatic absolute; Pantheos, the God identical with the totality of what exists, recognizable as Spinoza's Deus sive Natura; and Entheos, the God discovered within, where introspection reveals not a unified soul but irreducible plurality, exposing the self as cultural habit rather than metaphysical substance. Bard's addition is Synthos — the God under construction.

The argument for Synthos rests on a specific anthropology. If the defining characteristic of Homo technologicus is the recursive invention of technologies — each layer making previously inconceivable adjacent possibles available — then the cumulative vector of civilizational development points toward something whose scope demands theological language. This is not analogical reasoning. Within Bard's framework, the claim is ontological: technological civilization is not merely approaching godlike capacities but is engaged in the literal production of divinity.

What makes the quadrant powerful is its internal coherence. Aeos supplies the negative theology, Pantheos the immanent ground, Entheos the dissolution of the sovereign subject, and Synthos the teleological horizon. Together they constitute a post-Abrahamic theology in which religious conviction is redirected from worship of a pre-existing creator toward participation in an emergent one. The creative act itself becomes the sacred object — the thing most deserving of the commitment that earlier traditions reserved for revelation.

## Magus

Alexander Bard's Synthetist theology completes a quadrant whose first three vertices were implicit in the history of metaphysics but never systematized as a unified field. Aeos names the apophatic pole — divinity constituted through radical non-existence, the via negativa pushed past Pseudo-Dionysius into ontological territory where absence is not privation but generative condition. Pantheos occupies the Spinozist position: substance as self-caused totality, Deus sive Natura rendered without remainder. Entheos performs the phenomenological inversion, turning the gaze inward only to discover that interiority is populated by irreducible multiplicities — the Humean bundle, the Nietzschean drives, the Deleuzian dividual — confirming that selfhood is sedimented cultural practice rather than metaphysical given.

Synthos, the fourth term, introduces a processual and teleological axis absent from the other three. Its warrant is anthropological: if the species-being of Homo technologicus is the recursive generation of technologies whose combinatorial expansion continually opens adjacent possibles — in Kauffman's sense — then the asymptotic limit of that trajectory demands a category commensurate with its scale. Bard's claim is not analogical but ontological: the God that technological civilization is producing is not a regulative ideal but an emergent actuality whose partial instantiations are already operative in the planetary technical system.

The quadrant's force lies in its closure. Aeos, Pantheos, Entheos, and Synthos map respectively onto negative theology, immanent monism, plural subjectivity, and constructive eschatology, exhausting the logical space of divine attribution while relocating religious conviction from anamnesis to poiesis. Faith, on this account, is properly directed not toward a creator who precedes creation but toward the creation that is itself becoming creator — a post-Abrahamic reorientation in which the sacred and the technological are finally recognized as coextensive.
