# Why Science Must Presuppose Meaning to Function at All

The universe was never neutral.

---

> John Vervaeke argues that science itself presupposes a deep meaningfulness it is often used to deny. Properly understood, naturalism must take experiences of profound connectedness seriously rather than explaining them away — because the intelligibility science depends on already implies they matter.

John Vervaeke draws a sharp and consequential distinction between explaining away an experience and explaining it — and argues that the entire project of Transcendent Naturalism is committed to the latter. When someone like Rich Blundell or Rita Leduc describes a felt sense of deep connectedness to nature, of boundary dissolution, of participating in something inexhaustibly real, the standard academic reflex is dismissal: this is subjective experience projected onto objective facts that carry no intrinsic meaning. Vervaeke's counter-move is not to validate the experience by appeal to authority or sentiment, but to argue that science itself — properly understood — presupposes the very meaningfulness it is typically used to deny. Science does not merely derive conclusions from a neutral universe; it presupposes a deeply connected meaningfulness that makes its truths possible at all. This is what he means by extended naturalism: not just what is derivable from science, but what science actually has to presuppose in order to function. The goal is to separate the project of rendering the world intelligible — which is what science genuinely does — from the project of establishing a reductionist ontology, which is a philosophical addition that science does not actually require. By pulling these apart, the experiences that Blundell and Leduc describe are no longer anomalies to be explained away but phenomena that a rigorous naturalism is obligated to take seriously and account for.

## Apprentice

When someone reports a powerful experience of feeling deeply connected to nature — a sense that the boundaries between self and world have dissolved — the typical scientific response is to dismiss it. That's just subjective feeling, the argument goes, projected onto a universe that carries no built-in meaning. John Vervaeke challenges this dismissal head-on.

His key move is surprisingly simple: science itself only works because the world is deeply intelligible and meaningfully structured. Scientists don't just crunch data from a meaningless void — they rely on the fact that reality hangs together in ways that can be understood, that patterns are real, that connection runs deep. If that's true, then the felt sense of deep connectedness that people like Rich Blundell and Rita Leduc describe isn't an illusion to be brushed aside. It's pointing at something science already quietly depends on.

Vervaeke calls this "extended naturalism" — a version of naturalism that includes not just what science proves, but what science has to assume in order to get started. The goal is to separate genuine scientific inquiry from the philosophical add-on of reductionism, which insists that only the smallest, most stripped-down parts of reality are truly real. Once that separation is made, experiences of profound meaning and connection become legitimate subjects for serious investigation, not embarrassments to be explained away.

## Adept

John Vervaeke identifies a critical confusion at the heart of mainstream naturalism: the conflation of scientific methodology with reductionist ontology. Science, as a practice of rendering the world intelligible, presupposes a deep structural meaningfulness — the world must be coherently connected and participatorily accessible for inquiry to function at all. Yet the dominant philosophical interpretation of science treats meaningfulness as epiphenomenal, something to be explained away rather than accounted for. Vervaeke argues this is not a conclusion science delivers but a metaphysical addition science does not require.

This distinction has direct consequences for how we treat experiences described by thinkers like Rich Blundell and Rita Leduc — experiences of boundary dissolution, felt participation in an inexhaustibly real world, deep ecological connectedness. Under standard reductionist assumptions, these are subjective projections onto an intrinsically meaningless substrate. Under Vervaeke's "extended naturalism," they become phenomena that a rigorous naturalism is obligated to engage with, because they point toward the very conditions of intelligibility that science presupposes.

The philosophical move here is precise: Vervaeke is not smuggling in mysticism or abandoning empirical rigor. He is arguing that the presuppositional structure of science — what must be the case for scientific truths to be possible — is broader than what science can derivatively establish. Extended naturalism encompasses both. By pulling apart the project of intelligibility from the project of ontological reduction, Transcendent Naturalism creates conceptual space where experiences of profound meaning are neither dismissed nor uncritically accepted, but subjected to the same serious inquiry that characterizes science at its best.

## Magus

Vervaeke's distinction between explaining and explaining away constitutes a foundational methodological commitment for Transcendent Naturalism, one that turns on a careful analysis of science's presuppositional structure. The standard naturalist move treats science as delivering a reductionist ontology in which meaning, participation, and felt connectedness are epiphenomenal — artifacts of cognitive architecture projected onto an intrinsically meaningless causal substrate. Vervaeke's counter-argument is that this reductionism is not entailed by scientific practice but is a philosophical supplement to it. Science presupposes the very intelligibility and deep connectedness it is then recruited to deny: the coherence of natural kinds, the reliability of participatory knowing, the non-arbitrary structuring of reality that makes pattern recognition and theoretical unification possible at all.

This is what Vervaeke means by extended naturalism — a naturalism that takes seriously not only what is derivable from scientific inquiry but what must obtain as a condition of its possibility. The experiences reported by Blundell and Leduc — boundary dissolution, participatory immersion in an inexhaustibly real world, felt ecological belonging — are, on this account, not anomalies requiring deflationary explanation but indices of the presuppositional depth that reductionism systematically occludes. They track something about the structure of intelligibility itself.

The philosophical precision here is worth noting. Vervaeke is not arguing from phenomenological authority or romantic sentiment. He is performing an operation analogous to transcendental analysis within a naturalist framework: identifying the conditions under which scientific cognition is possible and insisting that those conditions be included within naturalism's ontological commitments. The result is a rigorous separation of science-as-intelligibility-project from science-as-reductionist-ontology, creating the conceptual architecture necessary for a naturalism that can account for meaning without either capitulating to supernaturalism or collapsing into eliminativism.
