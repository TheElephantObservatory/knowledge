# Equality and Liberty: Co-Constitutive, Not Competing

Danielle Allen represents a crucial corrective to a dominant tendency in modern liberal political thought, which has so thoroughly privileged liberty that it has allowed equality to atrophy as a living political value. Her book Our Declaration makes the argument — grounded in a close, almost philological reading of the Declaration of Independence — that equality and liberty are not in tension but are co-constitutive. The Declaration does not subordinate equality to liberty; it presents them as mutually dependent. And yet the dominant tradition of liberal political philosophy, from Locke through much of contemporary libertarianism, has treated liberty as the master value and equality as, at best, a constraint on it. Allen's correction matters not just as a historical or textual point but as a philosophical and political one: a democracy that loses its grip on equality as a substantive value — not merely formal equality before the law, but genuine egalitarianism in the conditions of life and participation — is a democracy that is hollowing itself out. Her later work, Justice by Means of Democracy, extends this into a full account of how democratic practice is itself the mechanism of justice — not merely the arena in which justice is pursued, but the very means by which it is achieved. This is a deeply important idea because it refuses the technocratic temptation to outsource justice to experts, courts, or algorithms, and insists instead that the hard, antagonistic, cooperative work of democratic conversation is irreplaceable.

## Apprentice

Most people think of freedom and equality as being in tension — give people more freedom, and some will end up with more than others; push for equality, and you might have to limit what people can do. Danielle Allen challenges this assumption directly. Reading the Declaration of Independence very carefully, she argues that the document's authors did not see freedom and equality as opposites. They saw them as depending on each other. A society where some people are genuinely excluded from full participation is not truly free — it is only free for some. Allen's point is not just historical. She is making a claim about what democracy actually requires: not just the right to vote or equal treatment under the law, but real equality in the conditions that allow people to show up and participate. Her later work pushes this further, arguing that democracy is not just a system for making decisions — it is the actual process through which a society achieves justice. Justice does not come from judges or experts alone. It comes from people working through disagreements together. That hard, sometimes frustrating process of democratic conversation is not a problem to be solved. It is the solution itself.

## Adept

A persistent tension in liberal political philosophy pits liberty against equality, treating them as values that must be traded off against one another. Danielle Allen's work constitutes a serious challenge to this framing. In Our Declaration, she undertakes a close reading of the Declaration of Independence to argue that the text does not subordinate equality to liberty — rather, the two values are presented as co-constitutive. The dominant liberal tradition, running from Locke through Rawls and into contemporary libertarianism, has tended to treat liberty as the foundational value and equality as either derivative or constraining. Allen's intervention reframes this: equality is not a limit on liberty but a precondition for it. A political order in which substantive equality — equality of standing, of voice, of the conditions enabling participation — has eroded is one in which liberty itself becomes a privilege rather than a universal condition. This is not merely a textual argument about what the founders intended. It is a normative claim about what democratic legitimacy requires. Her subsequent work, Justice by Means of Democracy, extends the argument institutionally. Justice, on this account, is not a standard applied to democracy from the outside — by courts, technocrats, or philosophical principles — but something that democratic practice itself generates. The deliberative, agonistic, cooperative work of democratic engagement is not instrumental to justice; it is constitutive of it. This position resists both judicial supremacy and technocratic governance as substitutes for democratic agency, insisting that the irreducible complexity of political life cannot be outsourced.

## Magus

Allen's project can be situated within a broader effort to recover the egalitarian strand of republican thought that liberalism's liberty-centered turn effectively suppressed. The Lockean inheritance, as it passed through classical liberalism and into the libertarian tradition, performed a conceptual operation that Allen's philological recovery seeks to undo: it installed liberty as the lexically prior value and reconceived equality in purely formal, procedural terms — equality before the law as the exhaustion of egalitarian obligation. Against this, Allen's reading of the Declaration restores equality to a substantive and generative role. The document's architecture, she argues, does not present equality as a background condition against which liberty operates but as a mutually constituting principle: the equal standing of persons is what makes the claim to liberty politically intelligible and morally binding. This is a move with significant implications for political ontology. It challenges the liberal tendency to treat the individual rights-bearer as the pre-political atom from which legitimate order is constructed, and instead insists on a relational account of political standing in which equality of condition and participation is constitutive of the subject who can meaningfully exercise liberty at all. Justice by Means of Democracy then performs a further operation: it refuses the Rawlsian gesture of grounding justice in principles derivable prior to or independent of democratic practice. Justice is not applied to democracy; it is produced through it. This positions Allen against both the proceduralism of high liberal theory and the epistocratic temptations of technocratic governance, insisting on the irreplaceable epistemic and moral work performed by agonistic democratic engagement itself — a position with affinities to Dewey's democratic experimentalism and Arendt's account of political action, but distinctively grounded in the American republican tradition.
