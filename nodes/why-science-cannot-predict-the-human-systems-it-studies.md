# Why Science Cannot Predict the Human Systems It Studies

The comet knows nothing of itself.

---

> Science has never fully reckoned with the fact that it is itself a historical process shaped by unconscious forces — and the more powerful its technologies become, the more urgent that philosophical confrontation grows.

There is a structural argument here about the relationship between science and its own historical and unconscious conditions that goes deeper than the familiar critique of scientific bias. The claim is not merely that scientists have unconscious motivations — that is trivially true. The deeper claim is that the entire project of applying scientific prediction to human society rests on a category error that has never been fully reckoned with. The early sociologists — Marx, Durkheim, Weber — imported the predictive logic of physics into the study of human civilization, and it failed. Not because they were bad thinkers, but because human social systems are constituted by billions of forms of subjectivity unfolding through history, and that kind of system does not submit to the prediction horizons that work for comets or chemical reactions. Complexity science and chaos theory have begun to reconcile science with nonlinearity and irreducible uncertainty, but the deeper reconciliation still hasn't happened: science has not yet fully confronted itself as a historical process, embedded in and shaped by the unconscious dynamics it studies. The technological singularity discourse is a vivid example of this unresolved tension. The exponential predictions about computation may be technically sound, but the anthropological, phenomenological, and psychoanalytic consequences of those predictions — what they mean for love, politics, education, subjectivity — cannot be derived from the same exponential models. This is not a relativization of science. The universality of scientific products is real: genetic engineering and artificial intelligence affect all humans regardless of cultural horizon. But that universality makes the philosophical reckoning more urgent, not less. The more powerful the technology, the more necessary it becomes to confront the historical and unconscious field within which that technology is being produced and deployed.

## Apprentice

There's a deep problem hiding inside modern science that goes beyond the familiar complaint about bias. When the founders of sociology tried to study human civilization, they borrowed the predictive tools of physics — expecting that societies could be forecast the way we forecast eclipses. It didn't work, not because they weren't brilliant, but because human life is made up of billions of subjective experiences unfolding through history, and that kind of system simply doesn't behave like planets or chemical reactions.

Complexity science has started to address this by taking chaos and unpredictability seriously. But there's a deeper issue still unresolved: science has never fully confronted the fact that it is itself a product of history, shaped by the same unconscious forces it tries to study. It operates as though it stands outside the world it examines.

This matters enormously right now. When people make exponential predictions about artificial intelligence or genetic engineering, the math might be sound — but what those technologies mean for love, education, politics, and identity can't be calculated with the same equations. The more powerful the technology, the more we need to understand the human world that's producing and absorbing it. This isn't an attack on science. It's a call for science to take its own situation seriously.

## Magus

What is at stake here is not another iteration of the sociology-of-knowledge critique but a structural diagnosis: the predictive logic imported from natural science into the study of human civilization by its founding figures — Marx, Durkheim, Weber — failed not contingently but categorically. Human social systems, constituted by billions of forms of subjectivity unfolding diachronically, are ontologically incommensurable with the prediction horizons that govern cometary orbits or reaction kinetics. Complexity science and deterministic chaos have begun to formalize nonlinearity and sensitive dependence, but they have not yet accomplished the more radical task: compelling science to confront itself as a historically embedded process, shaped by the unconscious dynamics it purports to objectify.

The technological singularity discourse serves as a particularly vivid symptom of this unresolved tension. The exponential extrapolations concerning computational capacity may be internally coherent, yet the anthropological, phenomenological, and psychoanalytic ramifications — the transformation of subjectivity, eros, pedagogy, political form — are not derivable from the same formal models. There is a constitutive gap between the technical prediction and the field of meaning within which that prediction lands, a gap that no amount of additional data can close because it is not epistemic but ontological.

This argument does not relativize science. The universality of its products — genetic engineering, artificial intelligence — is real and operates across every cultural horizon. But precisely this universality intensifies the demand for a philosophical reckoning with science's own historicity and its embeddedness in unconscious processes. The more potent the technological output, the more the unexamined conditions of its production become consequential — not as external critique but as an internal requirement of intellectual coherence.
