# Why AI Cannot Know What It Feels Like to Be Alive

Three hundred billion miles from infinity.

---

> Iain McGilchrist argues that AI can accumulate facts but never achieve genuine understanding, because real knowing requires embodied, mortal interiority — a felt inside that no amount of data or processing power can simulate or substitute.

McGilchrist draws a sharp and consequential distinction between two kinds of knowing that most English speakers collapse into one word. In French, you have 'savoir' — knowing facts — and 'connaître' — knowing through lived experience. AI can possess the first kind in abundance; it can know that Paris is the capital of France. But it can never possess the second, because that kind of knowing requires having a body, having emotions, having faced grief, having contemplated death, having felt joy. These are not incidental features of human cognition — they are constitutive of deep understanding. The anglophone world's failure to distinguish these two modes of knowing is not merely a linguistic quirk; it is a philosophical catastrophe. It allows us to mistake the manipulation of information for genuine thought, and to treat AI outputs as a form of understanding when they are, at best, an asymptotic approach to something they can never reach. McGilchrist uses a striking mathematical analogy: if you take a fraction of an inch and extend it to 300 billion miles, you are no nearer to infinity than when you started. The gap between AI and genuine understanding is not a gap that more data or more processing power can close — it is a categorical chasm. What AI produces is not knowledge from the inside. It has no inside. And it is precisely that inwardness — the felt, embodied, mortal interiority of a living being — that makes genuine understanding possible at all.

## Apprentice

Most English speakers use the word "knowing" to cover very different things. You can know that Paris is the capital of France, and you can know what it feels like to lose someone you love. Iain McGilchrist points out that French preserves this distinction with two separate verbs — 'savoir' for factual knowledge and 'connaître' for knowledge gained through lived experience. Collapsing these into one word, he argues, has led to a dangerous confusion.

AI systems can accumulate enormous quantities of the first kind of knowing. They can store and retrieve facts, detect patterns, and generate sophisticated outputs. But they have no body, no emotions, no mortality — and therefore no access to the second kind of knowing at all. McGilchrist uses a vivid analogy: if you stretch a fraction of an inch across 300 billion miles, you are still no closer to infinity. More data and faster processors do not bridge this gap.

The core claim is that embodied, felt experience is not a bonus feature of human thought — it is what makes genuine understanding possible in the first place. AI has no inside, no interiority. And without that inwardness, what it produces may look like understanding from the outside, but it is something fundamentally and permanently different.

## Adept

Iain McGilchrist identifies a philosophical catastrophe hiding in plain sight within the English language: the collapse of two fundamentally distinct modes of knowing into a single word. French maintains the distinction between 'savoir' — propositional, factual knowledge — and 'connaître' — knowledge born of direct, embodied acquaintance. This linguistic poverty, McGilchrist contends, has made it dangerously easy for anglophone culture to mistake information processing for genuine cognition, and to treat AI-generated outputs as though they constitute understanding.

The argument is categorical, not quantitative. AI can possess 'savoir' in extraordinary abundance — it can retrieve, correlate, and recombine factual content at scales no human mind could match. But 'connaître' requires something AI structurally lacks: interiority. To know grief, joy, mortality, or embodiment from the inside demands a phenomenological subject — a being for whom experience has qualitative character. McGilchrist deploys a striking mathematical analogy to drive the point home: extending a fraction of an inch to 300 billion miles brings you no nearer to infinity. The distance between AI and genuine understanding is not a technical gap awaiting closure through increased parameters or training data; it is an ontological chasm.

This framing challenges the implicit materialism underwriting much AI discourse, which assumes that cognition is substrate-independent computation. McGilchrist insists that emotions, embodiment, and mortality are not incidental accompaniments to thought but constitutive conditions of deep understanding. Without an inside — without felt, mortal interiority — there is no understanding to speak of, only its increasingly convincing simulation.

## Magus

McGilchrist's distinction between 'savoir' and 'connaître' — propositional knowledge versus knowledge by acquaintance — recovers a philosophical differentiation with roots in Bergson, Buber, and Polanyi, but deploys it toward a specific contemporary target: the epistemological conflation that permits AI outputs to be received as cognition. The anglophone collapse of these two modes into a single lexeme is, on this account, not a mere philological curiosity but a condition of possibility for the category error now pervading discourse on artificial intelligence. When a culture lacks the linguistic apparatus to distinguish knowing-that from knowing-through, it becomes structurally predisposed to treat the manipulation of symbolic tokens as equivalent to understanding.

The argument's force lies in its insistence on categorical rather than scalar difference. McGilchrist's mathematical analogy — a fraction of an inch extended to 300 billion miles remains infinitely distant from infinity — functions as a precise refusal of the asymptotic imaginary that sustains much AI optimism. The claim is that no accumulation of parametric complexity, no expansion of training corpora, no architectural refinement can traverse the ontological gap between information processing and phenomenological interiority. What is at stake is not a deficiency in current systems but an in-principle impossibility rooted in the absence of Leibnizian 'perception' — the having of an inside.

This positions embodiment, affectivity, and mortality not as epiphenomenal accompaniments to cognition but as transcendental conditions for understanding in any robust sense. McGilchrist thus aligns with enactivist and phenomenological traditions against computationalist orthodoxy, while sharpening the critique: AI does not approximate understanding imperfectly — it occupies a different ontological register entirely, one in which the very question of understanding cannot coherently arise.
