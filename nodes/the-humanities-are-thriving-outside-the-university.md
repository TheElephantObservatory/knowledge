# The Humanities Are Thriving Outside the University

The river has no gates.

---

> The humanities are experiencing a genuine renaissance — but it is happening outside universities, through online reading groups, essay culture, and great-books communities. This extra-institutional flowering may be closer to the original democratic spirit of humanistic inquiry than the credentialed academy ever was.

A humanities revolution is already underway — but it is happening almost entirely outside the university, and that may be precisely what makes it vital. The institutionalization of the humanities in the 20th century was a historical anomaly: Homer was not a professor, Thoreau was not a professor, Melville was not a professor. The great works were always public property, recited by bards to common audiences, not sequestered behind credentialing systems. What is now emerging — through platforms like the Catherine Project, through Substack essay culture, through great-books discussion groups on Zoom with participants from around the world — resembles something closer to the original democratic spirit of humanistic inquiry. It is amateur in the best sense: earnest, bottom-up, ungated. Several forces are converging to drive this: disillusionment with universities whose ideological capture alienated a generation of serious thinkers who redirected their intellectual energy elsewhere; digital technology that makes global, decentralized reading communities possible; and AI, which is forcing urgent philosophical questions about what it means to be human at precisely the moment when machines are beginning to outperform us cognitively in certain domains. The result is a flowering that is less scholarly but more alive — and the important practical implication is that no one should gatekeep themselves out of it. The great books are a river you can enter at any point in your life, and they will hit you differently each time.

## Apprentice

The great works of literature and philosophy were never meant to live behind locked doors. Homer recited his poems to common audiences. Thoreau and Melville were not professors. For most of human history, the humanities belonged to everyone. The twentieth-century university claimed ownership of these traditions, but that was the anomaly, not the norm.

Something interesting is now happening. Around the world, people are forming reading groups on Zoom, writing long essays on Substack, and joining communities like the Catherine Project to read classic texts together — no credentials required. Several forces are driving this: many serious thinkers grew disillusioned with universities and took their energy elsewhere; digital tools made it possible to build global communities of readers; and artificial intelligence is raising urgent questions about what makes us human, questions the great books have always explored.

The result is a movement that is less polished than academic scholarship but more alive. It is amateur in the best sense — earnest, bottom-up, and open to anyone. The practical takeaway is simple: no one should wait for permission to engage with these texts. The great books are a river you can step into at any point in your life, and they will meet you differently each time.

## Adept

The institutionalization of the humanities within the modern research university was a historical anomaly, not a natural state. The canonical figures — Homer, Montaigne, Thoreau, Melville — operated outside any credentialing apparatus. Their works circulated as public property, recited by bards, debated in coffeehouses, read aloud in parlors. The twentieth-century university absorbed this tradition, professionalized it, and in doing so subtly transformed humanistic inquiry from a democratic practice into a disciplinary one governed by specialization, peer review, and tenure incentives.

What is now emerging outside the academy represents something closer to the original spirit. Platforms like the Catherine Project, the Substack essay ecosystem, and globally distributed great-books discussion groups are reconstituting humanistic community on amateur — in the etymological sense, love-driven — terms. Three converging forces accelerate this shift: ideological capture within universities that alienated a generation of intellectually serious people; digital infrastructure enabling decentralized, borderless reading communities; and the rise of AI, which is forcing genuinely urgent philosophical questions about cognition, consciousness, and human distinctiveness at precisely the moment when machines begin to rival us in certain cognitive domains.

The resulting landscape is less methodologically rigorous than the academy but arguably more vital. It is ungated, earnest, and participatory in ways the credentialed humanities largely ceased to be. The important implication is normative as much as descriptive: self-gatekeeping — the assumption that one needs institutional permission to engage seriously with foundational texts — is the real barrier. The great books remain a river one can enter at any point, and they reward each encounter differently.

## Magus

The absorption of humanistic inquiry into the twentieth-century research university represents what might be called a credentialing parenthesis — a historically bounded period in which traditions that had always been fundamentally public and participatory were recodified as disciplinary knowledge subject to professional gatekeeping. The canonical figures of the Western humanistic tradition operated almost entirely outside institutional frameworks: Homer was a bardic performer addressing common audiences; Montaigne wrote from voluntary retirement; Thoreau and Melville were emphatically non-academic. The professionalization of literary and philosophical study introduced enormous gains in rigor but also effected a subtle ontological shift, recasting the encounter with great texts as a credentialed activity rather than an existential one.

The contemporary extra-institutional humanities revival — visible in the Catherine Project's seminars, in the long-form essay culture of Substack, in globally distributed great-books reading groups — is not merely a populist reaction but a structural reversion toward older norms of humanistic practice. Three forces converge to make this possible: the ideological narrowing of university humanities departments, which displaced a generation of serious but heterodox thinkers into alternative intellectual ecosystems; digital infrastructure that enables decentralized, asynchronous, and geographically unbounded interpretive communities; and the emergence of artificial intelligence as a philosophical provocation of the first order, forcing questions about consciousness, meaning, and cognitive distinctiveness that the disciplinary humanities have been slow to address.

The result is a landscape that is less methodologically disciplined but phenomenologically richer — amateur in the root sense of the term, driven by love rather than credentialing incentive. The normative implication cuts against both institutional gatekeeping and self-gatekeeping: the great texts function as what one might call permanently open hermeneutic systems, yielding different readings at different life stages, resistant to final interpretation, and belonging irreducibly to anyone willing to engage them.
