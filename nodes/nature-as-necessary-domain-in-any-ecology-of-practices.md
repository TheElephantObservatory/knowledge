# Nature as Necessary Domain in Any Ecology of Practices

You cannot learn the forest from inside.

---

> Nature is not a backdrop for spiritual practice but a central domain of practice itself. Evolutionary history, the lived understanding of what an ecology actually is, and the urgency of civilizational risk all demand that practices be situated within natural environments.

The ecological and natural environment is not merely a pleasant backdrop for spiritual practice — it is a proper domain of practice in its own right, and its absence from any proposed ecology of practices is a failing feature. Three distinct arguments converge on this claim. First, the natural environment functions as a living template for what an ecology actually is — encountering real ecologies in nature is what properly tempers, in both senses of the word, our normativity on ecologies of practices. You cannot fully understand what an ecology of practices should be by reading about it inside buildings; you need situated, enacted encounter with actual ecological complexity. Second, connection to the natural environment plugs into our deepest evolutionary history — it is the normative mode of human ritual and religion, extending back far further than indoor, literate, or institutionalized spirituality. Third, and most urgently, the pertinence of ecological connection to facing existential and civilizational risk cannot be overstated. The anthropological relationship to the ecological must be reset, and that resetting cannot happen abstractly — it must happen through practices situated in, oriented toward, and emergent within natural environments. Rituals should arise within this domain for communities practicing ecologies of practices. This is not a peripheral concern. It is central.

## Apprentice

There is a common tendency to treat the natural world as a nice setting for spiritual or contemplative practice — a scenic bonus, but not essential. This argument pushes back hard: nature is not optional. It is one of the most important arenas in which transformative practice should actually happen, and leaving it out is a serious gap.

Three reasons support this claim. First, if we want to understand what a healthy "ecology of practices" looks like, we need direct experience with real ecologies — forests, rivers, living systems — not just abstract models. Second, humans have been practicing ritual and religion in natural settings for hundreds of thousands of years. Indoor, text-based spirituality is the newcomer, not the norm. Third, the environmental crises we face demand a fundamental shift in how humans relate to the natural world, and that shift cannot be achieved through ideas alone. It has to be lived, practiced, and felt in actual natural environments.

The upshot is that communities developing serious practices of meaning-making and transformation should treat nature-based ritual and engagement not as a pleasant add-on but as a core commitment. This is central, not peripheral.

## Adept

The claim here is that the natural environment constitutes a proper domain of practice within any ecology of practices, and its omission represents a structural deficiency rather than a minor oversight. Three converging lines of argument establish this. First, real ecological systems serve as living templates for understanding what an ecology of practices should be. The concept of an "ecology" of practices is not merely metaphorical — it draws normative force from actual ecological complexity. Situated encounter with natural systems tempers our theorizing in ways that purely discursive engagement cannot. One cannot fully grasp the relational, emergent, interdependent character of a practice ecology without enacted experience of biological ones.

Second, connection to the natural environment is the normative mode of human ritual and religion across deep evolutionary time. The vast majority of human spiritual history unfolded outdoors, embedded in landscapes, attuned to seasonal and ecological rhythms. Indoor, literate, institutionalized spirituality is a recent and narrow slice of this heritage. Ignoring this is not just historically incomplete — it severs practices from the embodied, evolutionary grounding that gives them participatory depth.

Third, and most pressing, the existential risks facing civilization are fundamentally ecological in character. The anthropological relationship to the natural world must be reset, and this resetting cannot proceed through abstraction alone. It requires practices that are situated in, oriented toward, and emergent within natural environments. Rituals and communal practices should arise from this domain organically. This is not a peripheral enrichment of an ecology of practices — it is a central, load-bearing element without which the entire framework is impoverished.

## Magus

The argument advances a strong normative claim: the ecological and natural environment is not ancillary to an ecology of practices but constitutive of it, and any framework that omits this domain suffers from a failing feature — not merely an incompleteness but a structural deficiency that distorts the framework's own self-understanding. Three lines of reasoning converge. The first is epistemological and phenomenological: natural ecologies function as the living referent from which the very concept of an "ecology of practices" draws its normative content. The metaphor is not decorative; it is load-bearing. Without situated, enacted encounter with actual ecological complexity — the interdependencies, feedback loops, emergent properties, and fragilities of living systems — the practitioner's grasp of what an ecology of practices demands remains abstract and undertempored. The natural environment tempers normativity in both senses: it moderates and it hardens.

The second line is evolutionary and anthropological. Human ritual and religious practice are overwhelmingly outdoor, landscape-embedded, and ecologically attuned across deep time. Literate, indoor, institutionalized spirituality represents a vanishingly thin stratum of this history. To construct an ecology of practices without this domain is to sever the participatory and embodied roots that connect transformative practice to its phylogenetic ground — the very ground from which relevance realization and enacted cognition draw their evolutionary warrant.

The third argument is existential and civilizational. The pertinence of ecological reconnection to confronting anthropogenic risk is not supplementary but urgent. The anthropological relationship to the ecological must be fundamentally reset, and this resetting demands practices that are not merely about nature but situated within and emergent from natural environments. Communal rituals should arise in this domain as a matter of course. This is the center, not the periphery, of any serious ecology of practices adequate to the present crisis.
