# How Ritual Was Misclassified as Irrationality — and Why That Error Costs Us

You cannot think your way across the threshold.

---

> Ritual is not the opposite of rationality but structurally required by it. Because genuine self-transformation cannot be reached by inference alone, rationality depends on serious play, imaginal engagement, and non-propositional knowing — exactly the capacities ritual has evolved to activate.

Ritual has been progressively delegitimized — first by the Protestant Reformation's suspicion of embodied practice, then by the Scientific Revolution's reduction of knowing to propositional inference, then by the Enlightenment's equation of ritual with primitive superstition, and finally by Freud's pathologizing of ritual behavior as neurotic or psychotic. The result is a vast gulf between rationality, understood as computation, and ritual, understood as irrational or sick behavior. But this entire framing rests on a category error: it evaluates ritual exclusively through the propositional lens, missing that ritual is primarily addressed to non-propositional kinds of knowing — skills, perspectival states, participatory identity, and character. Once you recognize that rationality itself requires aspiration — the project of becoming normatively superior to who you currently are — you face a paradox: you cannot infer your way into a transformation you haven't yet undergone. The resolution is serious play: a liminal state in which you rehearse a perspective or identity without fully committing to it, allowing the transformation to begin. This serious play depends on the imaginal — not the imaginary, which severs you from reality, but imagination that puts you into deeper contact with reality, augmenting your perception and conception. Crucially, this imaginal augmentation is not peripheral to rationality; it is structurally required by it. Hirschfield's research shows that people will not save for retirement when given inferential and empirical arguments, but will do so when asked to imaginally inhabit their future self as someone they love and feel responsible for. The imaginal is also central to deep learning systems, embedded in predictive processing as the generative fantasy that improves recognition. Ritual, then, is the culturally evolved technology for activating exactly these capacities — non-propositional knowing, serious play, imaginal augmentation, and distributed cognition — and it can be evaluated by a rational standard: does what is cultivated in the ritual transfer broadly and deeply outside the ritual context? If it does, the ritual is enhancing relevance realization and is therefore rational. If it traps the learning within its own context, it is irrational. This gives us not a dismissal of ritual but a normative framework for evaluating and choosing rituals — and a strong argument that rationality not only permits ritual but depends on it.

## Apprentice

For centuries, ritual has been pushed to the margins of serious thought. The Protestant Reformation questioned embodied religious practice, science narrowed knowledge to what can be stated in propositions, the Enlightenment labeled ritual as primitive, and Freud treated it as a symptom of mental illness. The cumulative effect is a culture that sees ritual as the opposite of rational thinking.

But this dismissal rests on a mistake. It judges ritual only by whether it produces correct beliefs or logical arguments, ignoring that much of what we know and become cannot be captured in statements at all — things like skills, shifts in perspective, and changes in character. If you want to become a better version of yourself, you face a real problem: you cannot reason your way into a transformation you have not yet experienced. You need a way to rehearse being someone you are not yet, without pretending. This is serious play — a state where imagination does not disconnect you from reality but actually deepens your contact with it.

Research supports this. People who are asked to vividly imagine their future selves make better financial decisions than people given logical arguments alone. Ritual is the ancient, culturally refined technology for generating exactly this kind of engagement. And it can be evaluated rationally: does the growth cultivated inside the ritual carry over into the rest of life? If yes, the ritual is genuinely enhancing how someone thinks and lives. If not, it is trapping them. This reframes the question from whether ritual belongs in a rational life to which rituals best serve one.

## Adept

The delegitimization of ritual follows a clear historical arc: Protestant suspicion of embodied practice, the Scientific Revolution's reduction of knowing to propositional inference, Enlightenment dismissal of ritual as superstition, and Freud's pathologizing of ritual behavior as neurosis. The cumulative result is a deeply entrenched assumption that rationality and ritual are opposed — that rationality is computation and ritual is irrational residue. This framing, however, commits a category error. It evaluates ritual exclusively through a propositional lens while ritual is primarily addressed to non-propositional forms of knowing: procedural skill, perspectival transformation, participatory identity, and character development.

The deeper argument turns on a paradox internal to rationality itself. Rationality is aspirational — it involves the normative project of becoming superior to one's current cognitive and existential state. But one cannot infer one's way into a transformation not yet undergone. The resolution lies in serious play: a liminal engagement where one rehearses a new perspective or identity without full commitment, enabling transformation to begin. This serious play depends on the imaginal — not the imaginary, which severs contact with reality, but imaginative engagement that augments perception and conception, placing one in deeper contact with the real. Hirschfield's research on retirement savings demonstrates this concretely: inferential arguments fail where imaginal inhabitation of one's future self succeeds.

Ritual, understood through this framework, is the culturally evolved technology for activating non-propositional knowing, serious play, imaginal augmentation, and distributed cognition simultaneously. Crucially, it admits rational evaluation: a ritual is rational if what it cultivates transfers broadly and deeply beyond the ritual context, enhancing relevance realization. If it traps learning within its own frame, it is irrational. This yields not a blanket endorsement but a normative framework — and the striking conclusion that rationality does not merely tolerate ritual but structurally depends on it.

## Magus

The progressive delegitimization of ritual traces a precise genealogy: the Reformation's hermeneutics of suspicion toward embodied practice, the Scientific Revolution's collapsing of knowing into propositional inference, the Enlightenment's taxonomic equation of ritual with primitive cognition, and psychoanalysis's subsumption of ritual under obsessional neurosis. Each move narrows the space of legitimate knowing until rationality is identified with computation over propositions and ritual is rendered its pathological other. The central claim here is that this entire trajectory rests on a category error — the evaluation of a fundamentally non-propositional epistemic technology by exclusively propositional criteria. Ritual addresses procedural, perspectival, and participatory knowing, and the cultivation of character — dimensions of cognition irreducible to inferential structure.

The argument's pivot is a paradox intrinsic to rational aspiration. If rationality involves the normative project of self-transcendence — becoming epistemically and existentially superior to one's current state — then the agent faces a bootstrapping problem: the inferential resources available are precisely those of the self to be transcended. Resolution requires serious play, a liminal state structurally analogous to what Winnicott identified in transitional phenomena, where one rehearses an identity or perspective without ontological commitment. This play is powered by the imaginal — distinguished sharply from the imaginary — functioning as augmented contact with reality rather than withdrawal from it. Hirschfield's empirical findings on temporal self-continuity and financial decision-making provide convergent evidence; predictive processing architectures in deep learning instantiate the same generative logic computationally.

Ritual emerges as the culturally evolved technology integrating these capacities: non-propositional knowing, serious play, imaginal augmentation, and distributed cognition within enacted, embodied frames. The normative criterion proposed is transfer — whether capacities cultivated within the ritual context generalize broadly, enhancing relevance realization across domains. This criterion is internal to rationality, not imposed from outside it. The conclusion is therefore not a romantic rehabilitation of ritual but a structural argument: rationality, fully understood, requires ritual as a constitutive element rather than tolerating it as an optional supplement.
