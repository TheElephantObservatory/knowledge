# Global Commons as Framework for Governing Shared Civilizational Problems

The map we need before the territory arrives

---

> The Global Commons reframes politics around shared problems — automation, pandemics, ecological collapse — rather than shared identities or ideologies. The 21st century will generate these common crises faster than existing political frameworks can handle, making speculative thinking about commons governance a practical necessity, not utopian fantasy.

The concept of the Global Commons points toward a reorientation of political thinking that is neither a return to communism nor a defense of neoliberal capitalism, but something more structurally precise. The key move is to shift from thinking about communities — shared identities, shared values, shared institutions — to thinking about shared negativities: problems, tensions, and resource crises that affect everyone regardless of political affiliation or cultural identity. Elinor Ostrom won a Nobel Prize for developing the commons framework precisely because it sidesteps the ideological deadlock between collective ownership and private property by focusing on the practical governance of things we objectively share. The wager of the Global Commons as a political concept for the 21st century is that the list of such shared negativities is going to grow faster than our existing political frameworks can process. Mass automation eliminating entire labor categories, genetic engineering of human traits, global pandemics, ecological thresholds, the concentration of productive capital in entities that no longer require human workers — these are not problems that can be addressed by nation-state politics or by the ideological vocabulary of the 20th century. The tragedy is that you need prior agreement that something is a common problem before collective political action becomes possible, and that agreement is itself contested — as we saw with both climate change and COVID-19. But the deeper point is that the 21st century is not going to produce fewer of these common problems; it is going to produce more of them, and at accelerating speed. The political imagination that will be required to meet them does not yet exist, which is precisely why speculative thinking about the commons is not utopian fantasy but practical necessity.

## Apprentice

Most political arguments today still run along familiar lines: more government or less, collective ownership or private property, left or right. The concept of the Global Commons suggests a different starting point entirely. Instead of asking what values or identities people share, it asks: what problems do they share? Climate change, pandemics, mass automation — these affect everyone regardless of political beliefs.

Elinor Ostrom won a Nobel Prize for showing that communities can govern shared resources without choosing between full government control and pure private ownership. The Global Commons extends this thinking to the planetary scale. The argument is straightforward: the 21st century is producing shared crises faster than our political systems were designed to handle. Entire job categories may vanish through automation. Genetic engineering raises questions no single nation can answer alone. Ecological limits don't respect borders.

The catch is that before people can act together on a problem, they first have to agree it is a problem — and as climate change and COVID-19 showed, even that basic agreement is fiercely contested. Still, the deeper point holds: these shared challenges are multiplying, not shrinking. The political thinking needed to address them hasn't been invented yet, which is exactly why working through the idea of the commons now is not wishful thinking but urgent preparation.

## Adept

The Global Commons as a political concept performs a precise structural move: it shifts the foundation of collective politics from shared identities and values to shared negativities — problems, resource crises, and systemic risks that bind populations together regardless of ideological affiliation. This is neither a revival of communist collectivism nor a concession to neoliberal market logic. It draws instead on Elinor Ostrom's Nobel-winning work on commons governance, which demonstrated that practical management of shared resources can operate outside the binary of state ownership versus private property.

The 21st-century wager is that the catalogue of genuinely common problems — mass automation eliminating labor categories, genetic engineering of human traits, ecological threshold crossings, the concentration of productive capital in entities that no longer require human workers — will expand faster than existing political frameworks can metabolize. Nation-state politics and the ideological vocabulary inherited from the 20th century were not designed for problems at this scale or speed. The structural mismatch between the pace of emerging crises and the capacity of current institutions to recognize and respond to them constitutes the core political challenge.

The deepest difficulty is epistemological and political simultaneously: collective action on a common problem requires prior agreement that the problem is common, and that agreement is itself a site of contestation. Climate denialism and pandemic polarization are not aberrations but symptoms of this structural gap. The insight here is that speculative political thinking about the commons is not utopian indulgence but practical necessity — the political imagination adequate to these challenges does not yet exist, and the rate at which new shared negativities emerge is accelerating.

## Magus

The concept of the Global Commons enacts a categorial shift in the grammar of political collectivity: from the community — constituted through shared identity, shared values, shared institutional belonging — to the commons, constituted through shared negativity. What binds political subjects in this framework is not what they affirm together but what they cannot escape separately. Ostrom's contribution was to demonstrate empirically that commons governance operates in a space irreducible to the Hardin-Hobbes dilemma of privatization or Leviathan; the extension to the global scale transforms this from a resource-management insight into a fundamental reorientation of political ontology.

The structural wager is that modernity's acceleration of systemic risk — mass automation decoupling productive capital from human labor, germline genetic intervention, planetary ecological thresholds, pandemic vectors amplified by global connectivity — generates shared negativities at a rate that exceeds the metabolic capacity of existing political frameworks. The nation-state system and the ideological lexicon of the 20th century (organized around the capital-labor dialectic, the public-private distinction, and sovereignty as territorial jurisdiction) lack the conceptual architecture to process problems whose causal structure is transnational, whose temporality is nonlinear, and whose affected constituency is species-wide.

The recursive difficulty is that the commons requires a moment of prior political recognition — agreement that a given negativity is genuinely common — and this recognition is itself the most contested political act. The polarization around climate change and COVID-19 reveals not failures of communication but the structural absence of institutions capable of producing legitimate epistemic consensus at the requisite scale. The political imagination adequate to these conditions does not yet exist. This is precisely what renders speculative work on the commons not a utopian exercise but a form of anticipatory political construction under conditions of accelerating necessity.
