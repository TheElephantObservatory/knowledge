# Why Biological and Economic Evolution Cannot Be Predicted by Physics

The future has no sample space.

---

> The biosphere and the economy evolve by generating genuinely novel possibilities that cannot be listed in advance. Because the space of what could happen next is open-ended, no fixed laws, probability measures, or equations can entail what will come. The only way to find out is to let it unfold.

A major claim running through this conversation is that the evolution of the biosphere and the global economy lies beyond the Newtonian paradigm — and that this constitutes a genuine third transition in the history of science. In the Newtonian paradigm, you pre-state the objects, the relevant variables, the laws, and the boundary conditions, fixing the phase space and integrating equations forward to get an entailed trajectory. Quantum mechanics does the same with the Schrödinger equation. All of physical science is Newtonian in this sense: the phase space is fixed and pre-statable. The claim is that the evolution of the biosphere cannot be handled this way, and the proof turns on exaptation — Darwin's observation that a part used for one function can come to be used for another. The set of possible uses of any object is indefinite — not merely large, but genuinely open-ended, because new uses will emerge from future co-evolutionary contexts that do not yet exist. This matters mathematically because the first axiom of set theory requires that two sets be identical if and only if they contain the same members — but you cannot complete the list of uses of an engine block, so you cannot apply set theory to reason about what the biosphere will become. There is no sample space. Without a sample space, there is no probability measure. This same logic applies to the adjacent possible in both chemistry and economics. The core mathematical observation is that if you have N things, the number of pairs grows as N(N-1)/2 — already much larger than N — and the number of possible combinations grows combinatorially faster still. Kauffman's equation capturing this has a remarkable property: starting from a small number of things, very little happens for a long time, and then the system explodes hyperbolically — faster than exponential — reaching infinity in finite time. This is offered as a structurally correct model of why the number of goods and services in the human economy has gone from perhaps a few hundred in the Paleolithic to billions today, with the rate of increase itself accelerating. Brian Arthur applied the same logic to technology: new technologies are recombinations of existing ones, and each new technology expands the adjacent possible for further recombination. The critical point is that the adjacent possible is not a pre-statable set — what becomes possible depends on what has already been actualized, in a path-dependent, open-ended cascade. This is why there is no probability measure over the future states of the economy or the biosphere, why the Arrow-Debreu foundation of microeconomics cannot apply to an economy building genuinely novel goods, and why evolution cannot be entailed by any law. The only way to find out what it will do is to let it run.

## Apprentice

In all of standard physics, you can write down the rules, set up the starting conditions, and calculate what happens next. The future is, in principle, contained in the present. Stuart Kauffman argues that biological evolution and the economy do not work this way — and that recognizing this amounts to a third major transition in the history of science.

The key idea is "exaptation": any object can be repurposed in ways nobody anticipated. A feather evolved for warmth can become useful for flight. An engine block can be used as a paperweight, a weapon, or an anchor. The list of possible future uses is not just long — it is genuinely open-ended, because uses depend on contexts that do not yet exist. If you cannot complete the list, you cannot assign probabilities to what comes next.

This has a dramatic practical consequence. When things combine to make new things, and those new things combine further, the number of possibilities grows faster than exponentially — it explodes. Kauffman's mathematical model shows that starting from very few items, almost nothing happens for a long time, then suddenly the system races toward infinite variety in finite time. This pattern matches what we see in the real economy, which went from a few hundred goods in the Stone Age to billions today, accelerating all the while. The future of such a system cannot be predicted from any equation. You simply have to watch it happen.

## Adept

Stuart Kauffman's central claim is that the evolution of the biosphere and the global economy constitutes a genuine departure from the Newtonian paradigm — not merely in complexity, but in kind. In Newtonian mechanics, quantum mechanics, and all of standard physical science, the phase space is pre-stated: you fix the objects, variables, laws, and boundary conditions, then integrate forward. The trajectory is entailed. Kauffman argues that biological and economic evolution cannot be cast in this form, and that the proof turns on exaptation — Darwin's preadaptation — whereby a structure serving one function is co-opted for another. The set of possible uses of any object is not merely large but indefinite, because future uses depend on co-evolutionary contexts that do not yet exist.

This indefiniteness has precise mathematical consequences. The first axiom of set theory (extensionality) requires that a set be defined by its members. If the members cannot be listed, set theory cannot be applied. Without a well-defined sample space, there is no probability measure. This means no stochastic model can capture what the biosphere or economy will become. Brian Arthur's parallel insight — that technologies are recombinations of existing technologies, each expanding the adjacent possible — reinforces the point: the space of possibilities is constructed as the system evolves, not given in advance.

Kauffman formalizes the combinatorial engine driving this process. If N items exist, the number of possible pairwise combinations scales as N(N−1)/2, and higher-order combinations grow combinatorially faster. His equation exhibits a striking property: from a small seed, growth is negligible for extended periods, then detonates hyperbolically — reaching infinity in finite time. This provides a structurally correct account of why economic diversity has accelerated from hundreds of goods in the Paleolithic to billions today, and why the Arrow-Debreu framework, which presupposes a fixed commodity space, cannot describe an economy that generates genuinely novel goods.

## Magus

Kauffman's thesis is that the evolution of the biosphere and the economy marks a third transition in the foundations of science, following Newtonian mechanics and quantum theory. Both prior paradigms share a deep structural commitment: the phase space is pre-stated and fixed, laws operate over that space, and trajectories are entailed by integration from boundary conditions. The claim is that no such entailment is possible for the biosphere or the economy, and the argument is grounded in the ontological status of exaptation. A causal consequence of a feature — Kauffman's preferred term over "function" — can be co-opted into a novel causal role within a context that did not previously exist. The set of possible causal consequences of any object is therefore not completable: it is not a set in the Zermelo-Fraenkel sense, because extensionality requires enumerable membership. No sample space, no sigma-algebra, no measure — the Kolmogorov axioms have no purchase.

The combinatorial substrate of this open-endedness admits partial formalization. Given N existing entities, the number of pairwise combinations scales as N(N−1)/2; k-wise subsets grow as binomial coefficients; and if even a small fraction of combinations yield genuinely novel entities that re-enter the combinatorial pool, the dynamics become autocatalytic. Kauffman's governing equation for this process exhibits finite-time singularity: from a small seed, growth is sub-perceptible across extended intervals, then detonates hyperbolically. This is not exponential growth but something categorically faster — the system reaches formal infinity in bounded time. The empirical signature is the accelerating diversification of goods and services from the Paleolithic to the present, a pattern Arthur independently derived for technology through his combinatorial theory of technological evolution.

The implications propagate into economic theory with destructive force. Arrow-Debreu general equilibrium presupposes a pre-stated commodity space — precisely the Newtonian move. If the economy continuously generates goods whose possibility could not have been specified in advance, the commodity space is not pre-statable, contingent contracts over future states are undefined, and the equilibrium concept loses its mathematical foundation. Evolution — biological and economic — is not entailable. It is radically emergent, and the only oracle is the process itself.
