# Game B as Evolutionary Experiment, Not Civilizational Blueprint

Don't quote this book as catechism.

---

> Game B is not a blueprint but an evolutionary process: proto-communities run experiments, share replicable solutions horizontally, and pursue metastability rather than utopia — all while designing institutions that compensate for the severe cognitive limitations of human general intelligence.

Game B is not a utopian doctrine and must never become one. The book I'm writing will be the first comprehensive account of the movement, but I intend to put in the introduction something like: if I hear anyone quoting this book as catechism, I'm going to kick their ass. That's not just rhetorical color — it reflects something structurally important about what Game B actually is. We are not proposing a finished blueprint for a new civilization. We are proposing a new social operating system that must be built through variation, selection, and retention — the same logic that drives biological evolution and that I spent years studying in evolutionary computation. The proto-B communities that are now forming — some have already bought land, others are close — are not implementations of a theory. They are experiments. Some will fail. Some will be captured by charismatic bad actors. Some will discover that our theories were simply wrong. That's fine. What matters is that they remain in horizontal communication with each other, sharing what works, building what I call 'X in a box' — replicable solutions to concrete problems like governance, sewage, financing, agriculture. The goal is metastability, not stability. Stability is how you get China in 1471, burning the sailing ships and turning inward, only to be conquered later. You cannot stop. You have to go forward — but forward in a way that understands the carrying capacity of the earth, that is compatible with actual human cognitive limitations, and that builds collective sense-making capacity rather than assuming individuals can navigate an information environment of forty or fifty thousand messages a month on their own. We are, to a first approximation, the stupidest possible general intelligence that could still be called generally intelligent. Our institutions need to compensate for that, not pretend otherwise.

## Apprentice

Game B, as articulated by Jim Rutt, is explicitly not a finished plan for a better civilization. It is closer to a new operating system for society — one that has to be built the way evolution builds things: through trial, error, and keeping what works. The small communities now forming around these ideas are experiments, not implementations of doctrine. Some will fail, some will be hijacked, and some will prove existing theories wrong. That failure is not a bug; it is the process working as intended.

What holds the effort together is not ideology but horizontal communication between these experiments. The concept of "X in a box" captures this: replicable, practical solutions to real problems like governance, water treatment, or financing that any community can adopt and adapt. The goal is metastability — a system that keeps moving and adjusting — rather than a locked-in stability that eventually becomes brittle and collapses.

Underneath all of this sits a frank admission about human beings: we are, roughly speaking, the least capable general intelligence that still qualifies as generally intelligent. We cannot individually process the flood of information modern life throws at us. So institutions must be designed not around idealized rational actors but around actual human cognitive limits, building collective sense-making rather than assuming individuals can manage alone.

## Adept

Jim Rutt's framing of Game B insists on a structural distinction between doctrine and process. Game B is not a utopian endpoint but a social operating system that must emerge through variation, selection, and retention — the same evolutionary dynamics Rutt studied extensively in evolutionary computation. The proto-B communities now acquiring land and forming governance structures are not instantiations of a theory; they are experiments subject to failure, capture by charismatic bad actors, and outright theoretical falsification. This is by design. The anti-catechistic stance is not rhetorical posturing but a load-bearing feature of the architecture.

Coherence across these distributed experiments is maintained not through doctrinal alignment but through horizontal communication networks. Rutt's concept of "X in a box" — modular, replicable solutions to concrete civilizational problems such as governance, sanitation, agriculture, and financing — functions as the unit of cultural transmission between communities. The operative goal is metastability rather than stability. Rutt invokes Ming Dynasty China's 1471 decision to burn its sailing fleet as the canonical example of stability's terminal failure mode: lock-in followed by eventual conquest by more adaptive competitors.

The epistemological foundation is equally important. Rutt argues that humans are, to a first approximation, the minimal general intelligence — the stupidest cognitive architecture that still qualifies as generally intelligent. This is not cynicism but a design constraint. Any viable Game B institution must compensate for severe individual cognitive limitations, particularly the inability to navigate information environments generating tens of thousands of signals monthly. Collective sense-making infrastructure is therefore not optional but architecturally necessary, and institutions that assume rational individual navigation of complexity are building on false premises.

## Magus

Jim Rutt's anti-catechistic framing of Game B encodes a deeper structural commitment: the movement's coherence must be procedural rather than doctrinal, governed by the same variation-selection-retention dynamics that underpin both biological evolution and the evolutionary computation tradition from which Rutt draws. Proto-B communities function as parallel experiments in a fitness landscape whose topology is largely unknown. Failure, capture by defectors, and theoretical falsification are not pathologies but expected outcomes within an evolutionary search process. The explicit prohibition against treating any canonical text as catechism is itself a selection pressure against memetic lock-in — the very failure mode Rutt identifies in stable civilizational configurations like Ming China's 1471 retreat from maritime exploration.

The coordination mechanism across these distributed experiments is horizontal rather than hierarchical, operating through what Rutt terms "X in a box" — modular, replicable solution-packages addressing concrete infrastructural problems (governance, sanitation, finance, agriculture). These function as transferable phenotypic innovations within a cultural evolutionary framework, enabling retention without requiring convergence on a single organizational genotype. The target attractor is metastability: a regime that maintains adaptive capacity under perturbation rather than optimizing for equilibrium, which inevitably produces brittleness at civilizational timescales.

The anthropological premise is equally foundational and deliberately deflationary. Humans constitute, in Rutt's formulation, the minimal viable general intelligence — the lowest-capacity cognitive architecture still capable of domain-general reasoning. This is treated not as a lament but as a binding design constraint. Information environments generating on the order of tens of thousands of signals per month per individual exceed unaided cognitive bandwidth. Institutional architectures must therefore embed collective sense-making as a first-class design requirement, not an afterthought. Any framework that presupposes individually rational navigation of high-dimensional information spaces is, on this account, building on a false model of the agent it purports to serve.
