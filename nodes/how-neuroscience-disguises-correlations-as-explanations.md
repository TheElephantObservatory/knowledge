# How Neuroscience Disguises Correlations as Explanations

The diagram that was never drawn

---

> Neuroscience routinely disguises correlations as explanations by using filler verbs like 'regulates' and 'underlies' that imply mechanism where none has been demonstrated — a habit that reveals deep confusion about what genuine neural explanation actually requires.

Neuroscience is riddled with what might be called filler verbs — words like 'involves,' 'regulates,' 'underlies,' and 'produces' — that appear to add explanatory content beyond a simple correlation but actually do no extra work. When you find that a neuron fires in association with a behavior, the honest statement is: we found a correlation. But that doesn't scratch the itch. It doesn't feel like an explanation. So researchers reach for these surplus-meaning words that give the impression of mechanism without actually providing one. This is not merely a stylistic complaint. It reflects a deeper confusion about what counts as explanation in neuroscience. The stretch reflex is the gold standard: you can draw a diagram — sensory fibers, spinal synapse, motor neuron, muscle contraction — and someone looks at it and says, 'Ah, I see how that works.' The diagram is the explanation. The question is whether every behavior admits of that kind of neural circuit diagram that produces genuine comprehension. The argument is that it does not — and that the thirst for such diagrams leads scientists to dress up correlations as mechanisms using filler verbs. The related error is the identity fallacy: equating a psychological state with its neural correlate. 'Pain is just C-fiber firing' sounds rigorous, but it loses all the surplus meaning of pain — the phenomenology, the behavioral flexibility, the motivational weight. The word doing the real work is always the psychological one. Saying 'my dopamine is low' when you mean 'I'm unhappy' is the same move in reverse: borrowing the credibility of neuroscience to dress up a psychological observation without actually explaining it.

## Apprentice

When neuroscientists discover that a brain region lights up during a particular experience, the honest thing to say is: we found these two things happening together. But that feels unsatisfying — it doesn't feel like an explanation. So researchers reach for words like "produces," "regulates," or "underlies," which sound like they're telling you how something works without actually doing so. These filler verbs create an illusion of understanding.

The gold standard for real neural explanation is something like the stretch reflex — you can literally draw a diagram showing how a tap on the knee leads to a kick, tracing the signal from sensor to spinal cord to muscle. That diagram genuinely explains the mechanism. The uncomfortable question is whether most of what the brain does can ever be captured in such a tidy circuit diagram. The argument here is that it often cannot, and the gap between correlation and mechanism gets papered over with impressive-sounding language.

A related trap is treating a brain state and a mental experience as the same thing. Saying "pain is just C-fiber firing" sounds scientific, but it throws away everything that makes pain meaningful — how it feels, how it changes behavior, why it matters. And the reverse move — saying "my dopamine is low" when you mean "I'm sad" — borrows neuroscience's authority without adding any real explanation. In both cases, the psychological word is doing the heavy lifting.

## Adept

A persistent critique of neuroscientific discourse targets what might be called filler verbs — terms like "involves," "regulates," "underlies," and "produces" — that occupy the space between correlation and mechanism without earning their place there. When an experiment reveals covariation between neural activity and behavior, the epistemically honest report is a correlation. But correlations feel explanatorily thin, so researchers deploy these surplus-meaning verbs to simulate mechanistic depth. This is not a pedantic stylistic objection; it indexes a genuine confusion about the explanatory standards appropriate to neuroscience.

The benchmark for authentic mechanistic explanation is the stretch reflex: a complete circuit diagram — sensory afferents, spinal synapse, alpha motor neurons, muscle contraction — that produces genuine comprehension in the viewer. The diagram itself constitutes the explanation. The critical question is whether complex behaviors and cognitive phenomena admit of analogous circuit-level accounts that yield the same sense of transparent understanding. The argument is that most do not, and the resulting explanatory gap is systematically concealed by filler verbs that gesture toward mechanism without instantiating it.

A closely related error is the identity fallacy: collapsing a psychological state into its neural correlate. The claim that "pain is C-fiber firing" appears to gain precision but sacrifices the surplus meaning of the psychological concept — its phenomenological character, behavioral flexibility, and motivational valence. The psychological term invariably carries the explanatory load. The converse move — substituting "my dopamine is low" for "I'm unhappy" — performs the same conflation in reverse, borrowing neuroscientific credibility to redescribe a psychological observation without advancing the explanation one step.

## Magus

The critique isolates a class of lexical items in neuroscientific prose — "involves," "regulates," "underlies," "produces" — functioning as what might be termed filler verbs: operators that occupy the syntactic position of causal connectives while performing no additional explanatory work beyond the correlation they nominally transcend. Their prevalence is not incidental but symptomatic. They arise precisely at the juncture where empirical findings (covariation between neural and behavioral variables) must be transmuted into explanatory claims, and they serve as the rhetorical solvent for that transmutation. The epistemic cost is substantial: the community's capacity to distinguish genuine mechanistic accounts from redescriptions of correlational data is progressively eroded.

The proposed criterion for authentic neural explanation is the stretch reflex paradigm — a complete, traversable circuit diagram whose inspection yields comprehension of the phenomenon's production. This is explanation as diagrammatic transparency: the representation does not merely describe the system but renders its operation intelligible. The deeper claim is that this standard is met only in a narrow class of cases, predominantly spinal and peripheral, and that its extension to cortical and cognitive phenomena is not merely incomplete but may be structurally inappropriate. The filler verb, on this analysis, is the linguistic trace of an explanatory aspiration that outstrips the available inferential machinery.

The identity fallacy — equating a psychological predicate with its neural correlate — represents the ontological counterpart of this linguistic maneuver. "Pain is C-fiber firing" achieves apparent parsimony by eliminating the psychological term, but the eliminated term was carrying all the surplus meaning: phenomenal quality, context-sensitive behavioral modulation, motivational force. The reverse substitution ("low dopamine" for "unhappiness") reveals the same asymmetry from the opposite direction, borrowing neurobiological authority to redescribe rather than explain. In both directions, the psychological vocabulary remains indispensable and irreducible.
