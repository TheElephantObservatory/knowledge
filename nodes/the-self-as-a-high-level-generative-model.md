# The Self as a High-Level Generative Model

The zip file we call "I"

---

> The self is not a fixed reality but a high-level inference the mind constructs — a useful story that can, under the right conditions, be seen through entirely.

The deepest, most abstract construct in any agent's generative model is the self. The sense of self is not a metaphysical given but a very high-level inference — a story we learn. We do not arrive with it: at birth there is no notion of me or mother, just an oceanic universe where needs are met. It takes months to work out that mother is separate, that she has intentions not always responsive to what I sense myself doing, and from that — if mother is a thing in her own right, perhaps I am too — a sense of self slowly develops. Like any inference, it can be dissolved. Experienced meditators aspire to this through years of practice; psychedelics can induce it pharmacologically. What remains when the self-model dissolves is what some call non-dual awareness — lucid awareness without content, without a subject — the ground from which the self-story is normally constructed. Selfhood, like matter, like space and time, is not a metaphysical given but a useful story, a zip file, that we use to navigate our lived world.

## Apprentice

Most of us assume the sense of 'me' is simply given — a bedrock fact about existence. But it is actually something the mind builds up slowly, from scratch. Newborns have no clear boundary between themselves and the world; it takes months of experience before a child works out that its mother is a separate being with her own intentions, and from that discovery, the idea of a self gradually crystallises.

Because the self is constructed rather than given, it can also come undone. Long-term meditators sometimes report states in which the usual sense of being a someone dissolves, leaving behind a kind of clear, open awareness with no particular centre. Certain psychedelic substances can produce the same effect more quickly. What people describe in these states is not blankness or unconsciousness, but awareness itself — without the usual story of 'me' running on top of it.

The implication is striking: selfhood is less like a solid object and more like a very convincing working model — practical, useful for navigating daily life, but not the deepest truth about what is happening when we are aware.

## Adept

Within predictive processing and active inference frameworks, the self is understood as the most abstract and high-level construct in an agent's generative model. Rather than a metaphysical given, it is a hierarchical inference — a compressed representation that the nervous system learns to maintain because it reliably predicts a vast range of sensorimotor contingencies. Developmental evidence supports this: neonates exhibit no clear self-other boundary, and the gradual recognition of the caregiver as an independent intentional agent appears to be a precondition for the reciprocal inference that 'I, too, am a bounded agent.'

Because the self-model is an inference, it is subject to revision or dissolution under conditions that disrupt normal predictive hierarchies. Contemplative traditions have long described such states — samadhi, rigpa, non-dual awareness — as the experiential residue when the self-as-construct falls away, leaving what some phenomenologists call 'pure' or 'minimal' consciousness: awareness without an object-subject structure. Psychedelic compounds, particularly classical serotonergic psychedelics, appear to pharmacologically replicate this by transiently destabilising high-level priors, including those that constitute the self-model.

The theoretical upshot is that selfhood occupies the same epistemic category as other high-level constructs — space, time, causality — that the brain deploys not because they are metaphysically primitive but because they are extraordinarily useful compressions. The self is, in information-theoretic terms, a lossy but efficient encoding of an agent's history, affordances, and predicted trajectories.

## Magus

In the architecture of hierarchical generative models, the self-representation occupies the apex of the predictive hierarchy — the most abstract, most temporally extended, and most heavily weighted prior in the system. This is not merely a philosophical claim but an empirical one: the sense of personal identity functions as a hyperprior that constrains inference at every subordinate level, from interoceptive prediction to social cognition. Its developmental emergence tracks the infant's progressive differentiation of self from world — a process contingent on the recognition of the primary caregiver as a separate intentional system, which then licenses the reflexive inference of one's own agentive boundedness.

The dissolution of this hyperprior — whether through sustained contemplative practice or pharmacological perturbation of high-level cortical priors — reveals what nondual traditions variously term rigpa, turiya, or pure consciousness events: a condition of reflexive awareness no longer organised around a subject-pole. Phenomenologically, this is not absence but a kind of contentless luminosity; structurally, it may correspond to a collapse of the precision-weighting that normally privileges self-referential predictive loops. Thomas Metzinger's self-model theory of subjectivity and Karl Friston's free-energy formulations converge here: the phenomenal self-model is a tool, not a substrate.

The broader implication is that selfhood belongs to the same class of high-level, instrumentally indispensable fictions as Kantian categories of experience — not metaphysical primitives but evolved epistemic compressions. Like a zip archive, the self-model trades fidelity for efficiency, enabling rapid navigation of a social and physical world at the cost of systematically obscuring the inferential machinery that generates it. Its occasional transparency, in contemplative or psychedelic states, constitutes a kind of meta-cognitive audit of the model's own scaffolding.
