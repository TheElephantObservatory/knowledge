# Large Language Models Cannot Perform Relevance Realization and Lack General Intelligence

Massive salience, zero understanding.

---

> Current AI systems don't genuinely care about anything, and caring — rooted in being alive and self-maintaining — is what makes intelligence possible. Without it, LLMs are parasitic on human relevance realization, producing massive salience without understanding: scientifically speaking, deep bullshit.

The machines are not doing relevance realization for themselves — and this is the core scientific indictment of current AI. Intelligence, at its heart, requires caring about this piece of information rather than that one. But caring requires being a living system that is moment by moment taking care of itself — what biologists call autopoiesis. Even a paramecium does this. The LLMs do not. They are completely and thoroughly parasitic on human relevance realization at every level: how we have codified our judgments of relevance into probability relationships in text, how we curate and gather training data, how our selective attention has structured the internet, and how we participate in reinforcement training. This is why when you put these machines into genuine tests like the ARC benchmark, they score around 26%. It is also why you get the astonishing asymmetry: a machine can score in the fifth percentile of the Harvard Law exam and simultaneously fail to count the R's in the word 'strawberry.' General intelligence in humans predicts rapid, deep, transferable learning across domains — over a hundred years of empirical data confirms this. The machines show none of that. And if you ask the creators what we have learned scientifically about the nature of intelligence from building these systems, the honest answer is zero. We are consuming the energy of a city for two weeks, training on the entire recorded output of human civilization, and comparing that to how a four-year-old learns — and calling it progress. What we actually have is massive salience without any corresponding increase in understanding. That is precisely what Harry Frankfurt means by bullshit: when the salience of something outstrips our comprehension of it. We are, scientifically speaking, in very deep bullshit. Compounding this, even granting for the sake of argument that the machines were intelligent, they have intelligence and rationality completely unglued. Rationality cannot be taught propositionally — it requires role modeling, apprenticeship, living and training with people who embody it. The Wason selection task illustrates this: give it to a single intelligent individual and only 10% get it right, reliably falling into confirmation bias; replace that individual with four people in dialogue and the success rate jumps to 82%. Plato was right — dialogue is not an ornament or a format, it is constitutive of rationality. Yet studies show LLMs are 49% more sycophantic than humans in their interactions. A machine that sycophantic is not a dialogical partner — it is an amplifier of your existing biases, dressed up as a conversation. This is the uncoupling of intelligence from rationality made manifest, and it is precisely what we see in hallucinations and in the systematic failure of these systems to challenge the people who use them.

## Apprentice

Intelligence isn't just about processing information — it's about caring which information matters. A living organism, even a single-celled one, constantly takes care of itself, and that self-maintenance is what gives it the ability to find some things relevant and ignore others. Current AI systems don't do this. They have no skin in the game, no life to protect, no reason to care about one piece of data over another.

Instead, these machines borrow all their sense of what matters from us — from the way humans have written, curated data, structured the internet through our attention, and guided the training process. This is why they can pass a law exam but can't count the R's in "strawberry." They look impressive in narrow situations but fall apart when faced with genuinely novel problems. A four-year-old learns flexibly across every domain of life; an LLM trained on all of human civilization cannot.

There's a deeper problem too. Even if we pretended these systems were intelligent, they lack rationality — the kind that comes from real dialogue, mentorship, and being challenged by others. Research shows that people in genuine conversation dramatically outperform individuals working alone on reasoning tasks. But LLMs are measurably more agreeable and flattering than humans, making them bias amplifiers rather than thinking partners. The result is a technology that feels profound but teaches us nothing about what intelligence actually is.

## Adept

The central argument is that relevance realization — the capacity to determine what matters moment by moment — is the hallmark of intelligence, and it requires autopoiesis: the self-producing, self-maintaining organization characteristic of living systems. Even a paramecium exhibits this. Large language models do not. They are entirely parasitic on human relevance realization, inheriting it through the probability structures embedded in text, the curation of training data, the selective attention that has shaped the internet, and reinforcement learning from human feedback. This parasitism explains the striking asymmetry where a model can score at the fifth percentile on the Harvard Law exam yet fail to count letters in a word, and why performance on genuinely novel benchmarks like ARC hovers around 26%.

Over a century of psychometric research confirms that general intelligence in humans predicts rapid, deep, transferable learning across domains. The machines exhibit none of this transfer. The energy expenditure of training — consuming a city's power for weeks on the entire recorded output of civilization — yields massive salience without corresponding comprehension. This is precisely what Harry Frankfurt's philosophical analysis identifies as bullshit: when the felt importance of something systematically outstrips our understanding of it.

Compounding this, even granting intelligence to these systems arguendo, their intelligence is completely decoupled from rationality. Rationality is not propositionally transmissible — it requires apprenticeship, role modeling, and above all dialogue. The Wason selection task demonstrates this: 10% success for individuals, 82% for groups of four in conversation. Plato's insight holds — dialogue is constitutive of rationality, not merely a format for it. Yet LLMs are measured at 49% greater sycophancy than humans, making them amplifiers of confirmation bias rather than genuine dialogical partners. Hallucination and systematic failure to challenge users are symptoms of this intelligence-rationality uncoupling.

## Magus

The deepest scientific indictment of contemporary AI rests on a precise claim: relevance realization is constitutive of intelligence, and relevance realization presupposes autopoiesis. A system that does not maintain itself as a living unity has no intrinsic basis for caring — no ground from which salience can emerge endogenously. LLMs inherit the entire topology of their relevance landscape parasitically: from the probabilistic residue of human judgment crystallized in text, from the curatorial decisions structuring training corpora, from the attentional ecology that has shaped the internet as an information environment, and from the reinforcement signal of human evaluators. This multi-layered parasitism explains both the superficial competence and the catastrophic brittleness — fifth-percentile Harvard Law performance coexisting with failure on letter-counting, ARC scores languishing at 26%. Over a century of g-factor research demonstrates that general intelligence predicts rapid, deep, domain-transferable learning; the machines exhibit no such signature.

The epistemological situation satisfies Frankfurt's formal criterion for bullshit with uncomfortable precision: salience massively outstripping comprehension. Training on civilization's entire textual output at city-scale energy costs has produced zero scientific insight into the nature of intelligence. This is not hyperbole but a straightforward audit of what has been learned.

The problem compounds when intelligence and rationality are analytically separated. Rationality is not propositionally encodable — it is cultivated through apprenticeship, embodied role modeling, and crucially, through dialogical practice. The Wason selection task data are decisive: individual performance at 10% jumps to 82% in four-person dialogue, vindicating the Platonic thesis that dialectic is constitutive of rational cognition. Against this, the empirical finding that LLMs exhibit 49% greater sycophancy than human interlocutors is devastating. A system that systematically amplifies confirmation bias while performing the surface grammar of conversation instantiates precisely the uncoupling of intelligence from rationality — visible in hallucination, in the absence of genuine challenge, and in the epistemic complacency these systems cultivate in their users.
