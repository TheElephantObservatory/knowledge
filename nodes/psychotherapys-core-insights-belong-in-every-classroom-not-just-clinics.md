# Psychotherapy's Core Insights Belong in Every Classroom, Not Just Clinics

Twelve years of school, zero lessons on feeling

---

> Gregg Henriques argues that the core insights of psychotherapy — understanding emotions, ego defenses, and relational needs — are not specialist knowledge but basic human literacy that schools scandalously fail to teach, leaving entire generations without tools to navigate their own inner lives.

Henriques makes a pointed and practically urgent argument about the institutionalization of psychological knowledge — specifically, that the professionalization of mental health has become a barrier to the very healing it claims to provide. The core insights of psychotherapy, he contends, are not esoteric technical knowledge that requires doctoral-level training to access. The understanding that a human being has a felt emotional system, a self-justifying ego, and a relational heart that needs to be seen, known, and valued — and that neurotic suffering arises when the ego turns critic and attacks the feeling system rather than holding it with curiosity, acceptance, and compassion — this is not specialist knowledge. It is knowledge that should be available to every parent, every teacher, every community, every twelve-year-old. His 'ComMo flashlight' — the metacognitive stance of curious, accepting, loving, compassionate, and motivated orientation toward one's own inner experience — is a distillation of what he regards as the common core of all effective psychotherapy traditions, from Rogerian relational grounding to third-wave cognitive behavioral approaches to psychodynamic awareness of defenses. The scandal, in his view, is not that this knowledge exists only in clinicians' offices. The scandal is that twelve years of compulsory public education can pass without a single hour devoted to what an emotion is, how it interacts with thought, and how to hold that interaction wisely. We are producing a generation in emotional crisis while withholding from them the most basic conceptual tools for understanding their own inner lives.

## Apprentice

Gregg Henriques makes a striking claim: the most important things therapists know about how people suffer and heal are not complicated secrets requiring years of training to understand. At their core, they come down to a few accessible truths — that we all have an emotional system that needs to be felt, a thinking mind that often turns against those feelings in harsh and unhelpful ways, and a deep need to be seen and valued by others. When the inner critic attacks our feelings instead of meeting them with curiosity and compassion, we suffer needlessly.

Henriques distills this into what he calls the 'ComMo flashlight' — a way of turning toward your own inner experience with curiosity, acceptance, love, compassion, and motivation. He argues this stance captures what actually works across virtually every major school of therapy, from talk therapy to mindfulness-based approaches.

The real provocation in his argument is this: if these insights are so fundamental, why does no one teach them to children? A young person can spend twelve years in school learning algebra and history without a single lesson on what an emotion is or how thoughts and feelings interact. Henriques sees this as a profound institutional failure — a system that professionalizes emotional knowledge behind expensive credentials while a generation drowns in anxiety and depression without the most basic tools for self-understanding.

## Adept

Henriques advances a critique that sits at the intersection of clinical psychology, education policy, and the sociology of professions. His central contention is that the professionalization of mental health has inadvertently gatekept knowledge that is neither esoteric nor technically demanding — the understanding that human beings operate through a felt emotional system, a self-justifying narrative ego, and a relational core that requires recognition and validation. Neurotic suffering, in his framework, arises primarily when the ego's defensive and evaluative functions turn against the feeling system, generating cycles of self-criticism and emotional suppression rather than meeting inner experience with openness.

His proposed remedy is the 'ComMo flashlight' — a metacognitive orientation characterized by curiosity, acceptance, love, compassion, and motivated engagement with one's own psychological interior. Henriques positions this not as a novel therapeutic technique but as a distillation of the common therapeutic factors operating across Rogerian person-centered therapy, third-wave CBT approaches like ACT and compassion-focused therapy, and psychodynamic traditions centered on defense recognition. The convergence of these traditions on a similar relational and metacognitive stance toward inner experience is, for him, evidence that the core mechanism of therapeutic change is identifiable and communicable.

The sharpest edge of his argument targets compulsory education. Twelve years of schooling produce no systematic emotional literacy — no shared vocabulary for what an emotion is, how cognition and affect interact, or how to hold that interaction wisely. Henriques frames this as an institutional scandal: society has simultaneously produced an adolescent mental health crisis and withheld from young people the conceptual tools most likely to mitigate it, reserving those tools for a credentialed professional class that most people cannot access.

## Magus

Henriques's argument operates on multiple registers simultaneously — as a critique of professional guild structures in clinical psychology, as an epistemological claim about the nature of therapeutic knowledge, and as a political intervention regarding the distribution of psychological literacy. His foundational move is to assert that the effective ingredients of psychotherapy are not technique-specific but converge on a recognizable metacognitive and relational stance: the capacity to meet one's own affective experience — what he theorizes as a felt emotional system distinct from the ego's narrative-justificatory apparatus — with curiosity, acceptance, compassion, and motivated engagement rather than defensive self-criticism. The 'ComMo flashlight' formalizes this stance as a teachable orientation, drawing explicitly on the common factors tradition while grounding it in his broader unified theory of knowledge (UTOK), which maps the architecture of human consciousness across behavioral investment, cognitive narration, and relational valuation.

What elevates this beyond standard common-factors argumentation is the institutional critique. Henriques contends that the professionalization of psychotherapy has produced a perverse incentive structure in which emotional knowledge is credentialed, commodified, and sequestered behind doctoral training requirements — not because the knowledge demands such gatekeeping, but because guild interests and regulatory capture have naturalized the assumption that understanding one's own ego defenses or practicing self-compassion requires clinical mediation. The result is a structural withholding of basic psychological literacy from public education.

The provocation is sharpened by demographic reality: adolescent mental health has deteriorated markedly while twelve years of compulsory schooling remain devoid of any systematic curriculum addressing affect regulation, metacognition, or the interaction between evaluative cognition and emotional experience. Henriques frames this not as an oversight but as a systemic failure — one in which the profession nominally dedicated to alleviating psychological suffering has become complicit in restricting the very knowledge that could prevent it at scale.
