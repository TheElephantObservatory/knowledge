# The Continuity of Meaning from Matter to Culture

Purpose is a property of the storm

---

> Meaning and purpose aren't uniquely human — they extend down through animal minds, living cells, and even swirling matter. A whirlpool already knows something about its environment. Life may be physics continuing a process it started long before biology.

If meaning arises from the mutual information correlating entities and environments, then the learning process of entities in the universe extends across all levels of complexity — from human culture down through animal cognition, biological organisms, and even into inanimate matter. To delineate these distinct levels, we can speak of four kinds of learning: Structural Learning connected to matter, Genetic Learning connected to life, Cognitive Learning connected to minds, and Symbolic Learning connected to culture. This framework reveals that meaning is not a uniquely human invention or a subjective social construct, but rather something that connects down into the very rudiments of reality. Even a whirlpool — a dissipative structure that maintains its informational organization against entropy — exists in an entity-field relationship whose correlation defines a crude but real form of meaningful knowledge. More broadly, dissipative structures like whirlpools, hurricanes, and tornadoes exhibit teleological, goal-directed behavior emerging entirely on the plane of matter, prior to any biology: they arise and persist because they more effectively dissipate free energy through the spontaneous emergence of greater order, a process Jeremy England and others call dissipative adaptation. Environmental conditions select for which structures emerge, meaning a natural pressure to minimize free energy spontaneously creates ordered structures with an objective function — purpose arising immanently from physics, not imposed from outside. This offers a promising bridge toward understanding abiogenesis, since biological organisms are clearly a subset of dissipative structures, suggesting life itself may be a continuation of a process that begins deep in the material world. Far from diminishing the concept of meaning or purpose, this continuity elevates it — demonstrating that teleology is a genuine feature of nature, not an arbitrary projection of the human mind.

## Apprentice

Most people assume meaning is something humans invented — a story we tell ourselves. But there's a compelling case that meaning is built into the fabric of reality at every level, from culture and thought down through living cells and even into ordinary matter like whirlpools and hurricanes.

The key idea is that when any entity — a mind, an organism, or even a spinning vortex of water — maintains a stable relationship with its surroundings by tracking and responding to information about that environment, something like knowledge is already present. A whirlpool persists because it effectively channels energy; in doing so, it embodies a crude but real relationship with its surroundings. Physicist Jeremy England and others have shown that physical systems naturally tend to organize themselves in ways that more efficiently disperse energy — a process called dissipative adaptation. The environment, in effect, selects for which structures survive, giving rise to something that looks remarkably like purpose, with no mind required.

This matters enormously for understanding life's origins. Biological organisms are themselves dissipative structures — they maintain order against entropy by processing energy. If purpose and organization arise naturally in physics before any biology exists, then life may not be a miraculous exception but a continuation of something the universe was already doing. Meaning, on this view, is not a human projection onto a cold cosmos — it is woven into nature itself.

## Adept

The standard view treats meaning as either a uniquely human cognitive achievement or a subjective social construction. This framework challenges both positions by grounding meaning in mutual information — the statistical correlation between an entity's internal states and its environment. Where such correlation exists and is maintained against entropic dissolution, a functional analog of knowledge is present. This allows a taxonomy of learning that spans all complexity levels: Structural Learning in matter, Genetic Learning in organisms, Cognitive Learning in minds, and Symbolic Learning in culture.

Central to this account is the physics of dissipative structures. Whirlpools, hurricanes, and tornadoes are not passive formations — they arise and persist precisely because they more effectively dissipate free energy gradients than the unstructured states they replace. Jeremy England's work on dissipative adaptation formalizes this: thermodynamic boundary conditions select for structures that minimize free energy, spontaneously generating ordered systems with what amounts to an objective function. Teleology, on this reading, is not imported from biology or intentionality — it emerges immanently from statistical mechanics.

The implications for abiogenesis are significant. If biological organisms are a subset of dissipative structures, then the transition from chemistry to life is not a leap across an explanatory chasm but a continuation along a gradient of increasing informational organization. This reframes the hard problem of life's origin: rather than asking how purpose entered a purposeless universe, the question becomes how a universe already exhibiting proto-teleological dynamics at the physical level gave rise to its more complex biological and cognitive expressions. Meaning is not diminished by this naturalization — it is shown to have genuine ontological depth.

## Magus

The proposal advanced here situates meaning within an information-theoretic ontology: meaning supervenes on mutual information between system and environment, such that wherever stable correlational structure is maintained against entropic degradation, a functional form of semantic content obtains. This move dissolves the traditional bifurcation between objective, meaningless nature and subjective, meaning-laden mind by identifying a continuous gradient of semiotic complexity. The resulting taxonomy — Structural, Genetic, Cognitive, and Symbolic learning — maps onto increasing orders of representational fidelity and adaptive plasticity, but shares a common formal substrate.

The physics underwriting the lower registers of this hierarchy draws on non-equilibrium thermodynamics and, specifically, on dissipative adaptation as articulated by Jeremy England. Far-from-equilibrium dissipative structures — Bénard cells, Belousov–Zhabotinsky reactions, atmospheric vortices — are selected by environmental free-energy gradients for their capacity to accelerate entropy production in the surroundings. The system thereby acquires an immanent objective function: persistence through efficient dissipation. This constitutes a naturalistic, substrate-independent account of teleology that requires no appeal to intentionality, normativity, or biological function — purpose is a thermodynamic attractor property, not an externally imposed category.

The abiogenetic implications are considerable. If the canonical features of living systems — bounded organization, metabolic throughput, environmental responsiveness, heritable variation — are continuous with dissipative-structural dynamics rather than discontinuous from them, then the explanatory gap between chemistry and biology narrows substantially. The hard question shifts from how life introduced purpose into a purposeless substrate to how dissipative adaptation, already exhibiting proto-intentional structure at the physical stratum, underwent the phase transitions in informational complexity that constitute Genetic and, subsequently, Cognitive learning. This continuity thesis does not deflate meaning; it demonstrates that teleology has genuine nomological purchase, resisting eliminativist reduction while remaining fully immanent to the natural order.
