# The Imaginal as Bridge Between Bodily Experience and Propositional Thought

Where gesture becomes the grammar of the soul

---

> The imaginal — the domain of gesture, metaphor, and inner drama — is not decorative but functional: it is the bridging field where bodily, participatory knowing and propositional, narrative knowing meet and coordinate with each other.

The imaginal — understood not as ornamental or merely metaphorical but as a genuine functional domain — may be the bridging field between the propositional and the non-propositional dimensions of selfhood. Primates have rich mental lives without narrative. They have sophisticated gestural languages, complex social intelligence, and powerful general problem-solving capacities — all without propositional justification systems. What bridges the primate perspectival-participatory mode and the person propositional mode is something like pantomime, gesture, and the mimetic function — what Arbib calls pantomime — which takes sensorimotor schemas of action and transforms them into intersubjective communication. This is the imaginal register. The imaginal, as Corbin understood it, glues together the sensual and the conceptual, the internal and the external. When we use spatial metaphors to describe inner states — speaking of things being 'inside' or 'outside,' 'above' or 'below' — we are already operating imaginally, using the imagery of space to pantomime something that resists direct propositional capture. This is why practices like Internal Family Systems work not through narration but through dramatization. You do not stand back and narrate the parts of yourself — you create an imaginal field in which the drama can unfold and dialogue between the parts becomes possible. The imaginal is the phenomenological and possibly functional field within which narratives evolve, within which the propositional and the participatory coordinate with each other. Dialogos — genuine philosophical dialogue — is itself an attempt to take language and return it to that imaginal field, so that gesture, drama, movement, and musical elements are brought forward, and the logos that gathers things together into intelligibility can operate not just propositionally but participatorily.

## Apprentice

Humans are not the only creatures with rich inner lives. Primates navigate complex social worlds, solve problems, and communicate through gesture — all without using language or building narratives about themselves. So what changed when humans developed the capacity for storytelling and reasoning in words? Something had to bridge the gap between the older, embodied way of knowing and the newer, language-based way. That bridge is the imaginal — the capacity to use images, spatial metaphors, and inner drama to make sense of experience.

When someone says a memory is "buried deep" or a realization hit them "from above," they are using the language of space and movement to express something that resists straightforward description. This is imaginal thinking in action. It is also why therapeutic approaches like Internal Family Systems do not ask people to simply narrate their problems. Instead, they invite people to dramatize inner conflicts — to let different parts of the self speak, move, and interact in an imagined space.

The imaginal, then, is not mere fantasy or decoration. It is the living field where bodily experience and abstract thought meet. Genuine philosophical dialogue may work the same way — not as a dry exchange of propositions, but as a dramatic, gestural, almost musical event where meaning comes alive between people.

## Adept

The imaginal, as articulated in the tradition running from Corbin through contemporary cognitive science, is not an ornamental or merely metaphorical register but a genuine functional domain — the bridging field between propositional and non-propositional dimensions of selfhood. Primates possess rich perspectival and participatory cognition: sophisticated social intelligence, gestural communication, and powerful problem-solving, all without narrative or propositional justification systems. The question is how the transition to propositional personhood occurred. Drawing on Arbib's work on pantomime, the answer lies in the mimetic function — the capacity to take sensorimotor schemas of action and transform them into intersubjective communication. This mimetic capacity is the imaginal register in its most basic form.

Corbin's insight was that the imaginal glues together the sensual and the conceptual, the internal and the external. Spatial metaphors for inner states — "inside," "outside," "above," "below" — are not loose analogies but imaginal operations, pantomiming realities that resist direct propositional capture. This explains the efficacy of practices like Internal Family Systems, which work not through narration but through dramatization: creating an imaginal field in which parts of the self can dialogue and coordinate.

The deeper implication is that the imaginal is the phenomenological and possibly functional field within which narratives themselves evolve — the medium in which propositional and participatory knowing coordinate. Dialogos, genuine philosophical dialogue, can be understood as an attempt to return language to this imaginal field, reactivating gesture, drama, and musicality so that logos operates not merely propositionally but participatorily, gathering things into intelligibility through embodied, intersubjective encounter.

## Magus

The imaginal — mundus imaginalis in Corbin's precise sense — emerges here not as epiphenomenal ornamentation but as a functionally irreducible domain mediating between the propositional and the perspectival-participatory dimensions of cognition. The phylogenetic evidence is instructive: primate cognition exhibits sophisticated social intelligence, gestural proto-communication, and general problem-solving without recourse to propositional justification systems. The transition to propositional personhood therefore requires a bridging mechanism. Following Arbib's analysis of pantomime as the transformation of sensorimotor action schemas into intersubjective communicative acts, the mimetic function occupies precisely this mediating position — and it is, in its essential structure, imaginal.

Corbin's insistence that the imaginal is the isthmus (barzakh) binding the sensible to the intelligible finds unexpected confirmation in the phenomenology of spatial metaphor. When inner states are rendered through the imagery of interiority, exteriority, elevation, or depth, cognition is operating imaginally — pantomiming what resists propositional determination. The therapeutic efficacy of Internal Family Systems instantiates this principle: transformation occurs not through third-person narration but through the constitution of an imaginal field within which sub-personal parts dramatize, dialogue, and reorganize. The imaginal is thus the phenomenological — and plausibly the functional — medium within which narrative structures themselves crystallize and within which propositional and participatory knowing achieve mutual coordination.

Dialogos, understood as genuine philosophical practice, represents the deliberate reinhabitation of this imaginal field through language. By foregrounding gesture, dramatic structure, rhythmic and musical elements, dialogical practice returns logos to its gathering function — intelligibility achieved not through propositional abstraction alone but through participatory enactment, where the mimetic and the conceptual interpenetrate within a shared imaginal space.
