# Rationality Requires Meaning: How Agents Construct Relevant Worlds

The dog that did not bark was always the point.

---

> Rationality cannot be separated from meaning-making. Because any agent in a complex world must choose what information to seek, truth and value collapse into a single question: what is relevant to this agent, in this context, for this purpose?

A profound reframing emerges when we stop treating rationality as a 'view from nowhere.' Information is always perspectival and relational: you cannot separate what is true from what is relevant, because in an open-ended world you must actively choose which information to seek — and that choice is already a value judgment. This collapses the two classical questions of rationality — what is true, and what should I do — into a single question: what information is meaningful to this agent, in this context, for this purpose?

Meaning is therefore not a luxury added on top of cognition — it is the very structure of cognition. An agent embedded in a complex world cannot process everything; it must constantly realize relevance, narrowing an intractable environment into a tractable 'small world.' But this small world is not given — it is actively brought forth. The Sherlock Holmes example is instructive: the meaningful clue was the dog that did not bark — an absence, not a pregiven feature of the environment. No purely stimulus-response model can account for this; the agent had to construct an interpretive frame in which the absence became significant.

This is what researchers call 'generative rationality': value and meaning are not objective properties waiting to be read off, but are actively created through the agent's history, knowledge, and first-person perspective. The efficient market hypothesis assumes a hundred-dollar bill on the ground would be visible to everyone — but generative rationality shows that what counts as a hundred-dollar bill depends entirely on the meaning-making capacity of the observer. The same logic applies to entrepreneurial opportunity, scientific discovery, and ethical perception: different agents, standing in different relationships to the world, will literally see different possibilities.

## Apprentice

There is a common assumption that being rational means seeing the world as it objectively is — a neutral "view from nowhere." But this framing misses something fundamental: in a world of infinite information, you always have to choose what to pay attention to. That choice — what to look for, what to ignore — is already shaped by what matters to you. Truth and values are not separate questions; they are entangled from the start.

Consider Sherlock Holmes noticing that a dog did not bark during a crime. The clue was an absence — something no one else thought to look for. Holmes didn't just passively receive information; he actively constructed a way of seeing that made the silence meaningful. This is not a quirk of detective fiction. It describes how all thinking works: we bring frameworks to the world that determine what counts as important.

This perspective, sometimes called "generative rationality," suggests that meaning is not a decoration on top of clear thinking — it is the engine of clear thinking. Different people, with different histories and knowledge, will literally perceive different opportunities and problems. What looks like a hundred-dollar bill on the sidewalk to one person is invisible to another, not because of eyesight, but because of the interpretive lens they carry.

## Adept

A powerful reframing of rationality emerges once the "view from nowhere" is abandoned. Information is always perspectival and relational: an agent embedded in an open-ended environment cannot separate questions of truth from questions of relevance, because the very act of selecting which information to seek already encodes a value judgment. The two classical pillars of rationality — epistemic (what is true?) and practical (what should I do?) — collapse into a unified question: what information is meaningful to this agent, in this context, for this purpose? Meaning, on this account, is not an affective overlay on cognition but its constitutive structure.

The concept of "generative rationality" captures this shift. Rather than treating value and significance as objective properties waiting to be discovered, generative rationality holds that they are actively brought forth through an agent's history, knowledge, and first-person situatedness. The Sherlock Holmes case — where the meaningful datum was the dog that did not bark — illustrates the point precisely: no stimulus-response architecture can account for the detection of a salient absence. The agent must construct an interpretive frame within which the absence becomes informative. This is the process of "realizing relevance," converting an intractable open world into a tractable small world that is not pregiven but enacted.

The implications extend across domains. The efficient market hypothesis presumes that opportunities are equally visible to all rational agents, but generative rationality reveals that what counts as an opportunity depends on the meaning-making capacity of the observer. Entrepreneurial discovery, scientific insight, and ethical perception all exhibit this structure: different agents, differently situated, will perceive genuinely different possibility spaces.

## Magus

The dissolution of the "view from nowhere" yields a radical entanglement of the epistemic and the practical. If information is constitutively perspectival and relational — if what is true cannot be disentangled from what is relevant — then rationality can no longer be parsed into a factual register and a normative register operating in sequence. The selection of which information to seek in an open-ended environment is itself a value-laden act, collapsing the is/ought distinction at the level of cognitive architecture. Meaning is not epiphenomenal to cognition; it is its operative structure, the mechanism by which an embedded agent realizes relevance and converts an intractable state space into a navigable small world.

The framework of generative rationality formalizes this insight. Value and significance are not objective properties awaiting extraction but are actively constituted through the agent's embodied history, accumulated knowledge, and irreducible first-person perspective. The canonical illustration — Holmes detecting the salience of the dog's silence — is not merely heuristic but structurally diagnostic: no architecture confined to stimulus-response contingencies can register a meaningful absence. The agent must enact an interpretive frame within which non-occurrence becomes informative, a move that presupposes precisely the kind of constructive, meaning-generating cognition that standard decision-theoretic models bracket out.

The consequences ramify across epistemology, economics, and ethics. The efficient market hypothesis operates under the assumption of homogeneous perceptual access to value — the proverbial hundred-dollar bill visible to all. Generative rationality dismantles this assumption: what constitutes a hundred-dollar bill is a function of the observer's meaning-making capacity. Entrepreneurial alertness, paradigm-shifting scientific perception, and moral discernment all instantiate the same deep structure — differently situated agents inhabit genuinely different possibility landscapes, not by error or bias, but by the generative logic of perspectival cognition itself.
